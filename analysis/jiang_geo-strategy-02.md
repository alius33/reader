# Christian Zionism and the Middle East Conflict

## Core Question

Why will the United States invade Iran — and what is the religious mechanism that drives American Middle East policy?

Prof. Jiang frames the lecture around three reasons the US will invade Iran: (1) the US must defend its empire, (2) allies (Saudi Arabia and Israel) will pressure the US into it, and (3) the Israel lobby. The lecture focuses on reason #3, but to explain the Israel lobby, Prof. Jiang first builds the entire theological architecture behind it — from the origins of Christianity through the Reformation, the founding of America as a Christian nation, the emergence of dispensationalist premillennialism, and the invention of Christian Zionism. The core question is not merely political but theological: why does a significant group of American Christians actively want war in the Middle East?

## Lecture Type

Geopolitical analysis — with an extended theological-historical foundation. The lecture is structured as an explanatory chain: Christianity's origins → the Second Coming debate → the Reformation → America's founding as a Christian nation → dispensationalist premillennialism → Christian Zionism → Zionism → the Israel-Iran conflict → why this ideology will grow.

## Central Thesis

The United States will invade Iran because its foreign policy is driven — at a deep structural level — by a religious ideology: dispensationalist premillennialism and its political expression, Christian Zionism. This is not a fringe belief that happens to influence some voters. Prof. Jiang argues it is woven into the founding DNA of the United States as a Christian nation, that the descendants of its founding dissenters now control the military and foreign policy apparatus, and that the ideology will only grow more popular as inequality increases under the Pax Americana. The result is a self-reinforcing system: American Christians believe they have a religious duty to support Israel and promote Middle East conflict because it advances biblical prophecy; Israel exploits this religious commitment to advance its geopolitical interests, knowing America will fight its wars; and growing inequality creates more believers who want war because peace means continued hopelessness. The driving force behind a potential US invasion of Iran is not rational strategic calculation but a theological worldview that sees Middle East war as a step toward the Second Coming of Jesus.

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: Christianity's Revolutionary Origins and the Second Coming Problem

**The argument:** Christianity began as a revolutionary, egalitarian, anti-authority religion that promised the oppressed a better world through the Second Coming. When it became the establishment religion of the Roman Empire, this promise became dangerous — so Augustine reinterpreted the Second Coming as metaphorical.

**Evidence for:**
- Christianity's early followers were slaves, peasants, women — marginalised people
- Christianity offered hope, salvation, and redemption to outcasts
- The "free lottery ticket" analogy — it costs nothing to believe, and the payoff (heaven) is infinite
- Augustine's City of God and Confessions explicitly adapted Christianity for establishment use

**Evidence against:** None presented — this is treated as established historical fact

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang presents this as uncontroversial background. The key insight is the structural tension: a revolutionary religion becoming the establishment creates the need for theological innovation (Augustine's reinterpretation).

### Argument 2: The Four Views of the End Times

**The argument:** The question of when and how Jesus will return has produced four major theological positions, and the most dangerous — dispensationalist premillennialism — is also the most organised and fanatical.

**The four positions:**
1. **Amillennialism** — the 1000 years is a metaphor (Augustine's position; the Catholic Church's view)
2. **Historic premillennialism** — Jesus promised to return; be patient and trust God
3. **Postmillennialism** — humans must build 1000 years of Christian civilisation first, then Jesus returns
4. **Dispensationalist premillennialism** — the Bible contains a literal plan (roadmap) for bringing Jesus back: Israel reconstituted → Temple rebuilt → Antichrist → nations attack Israel → Jesus returns → 1000 years of peace → Last Judgement

**Evidence for dispensationalism's danger:**
- Most Christians consider it evil/blasphemous — it attempts to manipulate God
- But dispensationalists are the most organised and most fanatical
- History shows that the most united and fanatical groups achieve their objectives
- The belief thrives in times of uncertainty and inequality (which are increasing)

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang endorses the view that dispensationalist premillennialism is both dangerous and growing in influence. He explicitly says most Christians reject it but that doesn't prevent a fanatical minority from driving policy.

### Argument 3: America as a Christian Nation

**The argument:** The United States was not founded as a secular multicultural nation (though it appears that way from outside). It was founded as a Christian nation by religious dissenters who put the Bible as supreme authority and were dedicated to building the kingdom of God on earth.

**Chain of reasoning:**
- The Reformation allowed Protestants to read the Bible → thousands of dissenter groups in England
- Dissenters believed the Bible was supreme authority above the king → constant conflict → English Civil War
- The king sent dissenters to America to build their own kingdom of heaven (no one else was there)
- America's founding was explicitly Christian — the Bible as supreme authority and guide
- The descendants of these founders now control the military and foreign policy apparatus

**Evidence for:** The historical narrative of the Reformation → English dissent → American colonisation

**Evidence against:** None presented. Prof. Jiang acknowledges students may think the US is secular and multicultural, but argues this is a superficial impression — "inside its soul, its history, it is a Christian nation."

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang presents this as historical fact with contemporary implications. The key claim is continuity: the Christian founding isn't just history, it still shapes who controls American foreign policy today.

### Argument 4: Christian Zionism as the Mechanism Driving Middle East Conflict

**The argument:** Christian Zionism — the belief that Christians must help Jews return to Israel — is the mechanism connecting American religious ideology to Middle East conflict. It preceded and enabled Jewish Zionism, and it is ultimately cynical because it uses Jewish people as tools in a Christian eschatological plan.

**Evidence for:**
- Christian Zionism emerged after the Reformation (about 200 years ago)
- It intersects with dispensationalist premillennialism — Israel's reconstitution is step one of the plan
- Christianity's own prophecy says two-thirds of Jews die in the final battle, the rest convert
- Before Christian Zionism, Zionism was extremely unpopular among Jews — they saw themselves as nationals of their countries, not a separate race
- The Holocaust was the catalyst that made Zionism viable (Jewish migration to Palestine → 1948)

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang considers Christian Zionism "a pretty evil idea" because of its instrumental use of Jewish people. He argues it is the foundational ideology driving US support for Israel and thus driving the Israel-Iran conflict.

### Argument 5: Why This Ideology Will Only Grow

**The argument:** Dispensationalist premillennialism will become more popular in America (and globally) because the same conditions that made early Christianity popular in the Roman Empire are recurring under the Pax Americana.

**The Pax Romana parallel:**
- Pax Romana → peace → no wars → no social mobility → massive inequality → hopelessness among the poor → Christianity as a "free lottery ticket"
- Pax Americana → 70 years of peace → growing inequality → billionaires hoard wealth → millions of young people with no hope → dispensationalist premillennialism as a "free lottery ticket" offering a new world through prophecy

**Supporting evidence:**
- The delivery person analogy: millions of young men in China (and America) with no hope, no future, no marriage prospects, stuck in permanent poverty
- War becomes attractive when peace means continued hopelessness — "what good does peace do you?"
- The government cannot easily fix this: social welfare costs money, and rich people (who have the money) are more organised and more dangerous than the poor

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang believes this is a structural trend driven by inequality, not a passing fad. The ideology will grow because the conditions producing it (inequality, hopelessness) are worsening.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### 1. Dispensationalist Premillennialism
- **Definition:** The belief that the Bible contains a literal plan (not just a promise) for bringing about the Second Coming of Jesus. The plan requires specific steps to be completed: Israel reconstituted as a Jewish nation, the Temple rebuilt, the Antichrist appears, all nations attack Israel, Jesus returns to defeat the Antichrist and usher in 1000 years of peace, followed by the Last Judgement.
- **How it works:** Believers see themselves as active participants in a divine plan — they must work to fulfil the biblical prophecy, not passively wait for God.
- **What it explains:** Why a subset of American Christians actively promotes war in the Middle East — conflict advances the prophecy toward the Second Coming.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2).
- **Relationship to other views:** Contrasted with historic premillennialism (passive waiting), amillennialism (metaphorical interpretation), and postmillennialism (human effort to build a good world first). Most Christians consider dispensationalism blasphemous because it attempts to manipulate God.

### 2. Christian Zionism
- **Definition:** The belief that Christians have a religious duty to support Jews in returning to and maintaining control over Israel.
- **How it works:** Provides the political-theological bridge between dispensationalist premillennialism and real-world Middle East policy. Christians support Israel not out of affinity for Judaism but because Israel's existence and conflicts are necessary steps in the prophecy.
- **What it explains:** Why the US provides unconditional support to Israel regardless of Israel's actions.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2).
- **Key detail:** Prof. Jiang calls it "a pretty cynical and a pretty evil idea" because Christianity's own prophecy predicts two-thirds of Jews die and the rest convert — Jews are instruments, not beneficiaries.

### 3. Zionism
- **Definition:** The belief that Jews are a chosen people, Israel is their promised land, and Jews must return to Israel. Reframes Jewish identity from a religion to a race — claiming direct descent from the biblical Hebrews.
- **How it works:** Provides the ideological basis for the State of Israel. Originated about 200 years ago, was extremely unpopular among Jews until the Holocaust.
- **What it explains:** Why Israel exists and why it claims the territory it does.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2).
- **Key detail:** Prof. Jiang says the racial claim "is complete nonsense" — Jews historically saw themselves as members of their national communities who happened to have a different religion.

### 4. The Four Views of the End Times
- **Definition:** A taxonomy of Christian eschatological positions: amillennialism, historic premillennialism, postmillennialism, dispensationalist premillennialism.
- **How it works:** Each position has different implications for political behaviour — especially regarding Israel and Middle East conflict.
- **What it explains:** The internal theological diversity within Christianity and why only one specific subset (dispensationalists) drives pro-war Middle East policy.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2).

### 5. Pax Americana (parallel to Pax Romana)
- **Definition:** The period of relative global peace under American hegemony (past ~70 years), paralleled to the Pax Romana under the Roman Empire.
- **How it works:** Peace → no wars → no social mobility through war → massive inequality → hopelessness among the poor → fertile ground for radical religious movements.
- **What it explains:** Why dispensationalist premillennialism will grow in popularity — the same structural conditions that made early Christianity explosive are recurring.
- **Introduced:** Lecture 1 mentioned American empire; this lecture develops the Pax Americana concept and its religious consequences.
- **Connected to Lecture 1:** The three causes of imperial collapse (overextension, debt, civil unrest) are the structural drivers of the inequality that feeds dispensationalism.

### 6. The "Free Lottery Ticket" Model of Religious Adoption
- **Definition:** Prof. Jiang's analogy for why marginalised people adopt radical religious beliefs: it costs nothing to believe, and if the belief is correct, the payoff is infinite.
- **How it works:** Applied to early Christianity (slaves, peasants, women under the Pax Romana) and to modern dispensationalist premillennialism (hopeless young people under the Pax Americana).
- **What it explains:** The economic logic of religious radicalisation — it's a rational choice for people with nothing to lose.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2).

### 7. The Three Reasons for US Invasion of Iran
- **Definition:** Prof. Jiang's framework for why the US will eventually invade Iran: (1) defence of empire, (2) ally pressure (Saudi Arabia and Israel), (3) the Israel lobby / Christian Zionism.
- **How it works:** The three reasons are complementary and mutually reinforcing — strategic, diplomatic, and ideological drivers.
- **What it explains:** The invasion is overdetermined — multiple independent causes all point to the same outcome.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2) as a framing device; reasons 1 and 2 are explored in Lectures 1, 3, 4.

### 8. Augustine's Theological Innovation
- **Definition:** Augustine's reinterpretation of the Second Coming as metaphorical (amillennialism), transforming Christianity from a revolutionary religion into an establishment religion compatible with the Roman Empire.
- **How it works:** Solved two problems: (a) if the world is ending, no one works; (b) the Second Coming implies the Catholic Church is failing.
- **What it explains:** Why mainstream Christianity defers the Second Coming and why dispensationalism is considered a return to "dangerous" pre-Augustinian theology.
- **Introduced:** This lecture (Lecture 2); referenced from the Civilization series (City of God and Confessions were discussed in a previous semester).

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### 1. Jesus and the Origins of Christianity (~4 BCE - 31 CE)
- **Who:** Jesus of Galilee
- **What:** A man about whom very little is known — born ~4 BCE, crucified ~31 CE by the Romans, from Galilee
- **Key details:** Believers say he was God on earth, preached that the kingdom of heaven was coming, was crucified, resurrected, ascended to heaven
- **The promise:** Jesus will return (the Second Coming) and bring heaven onto earth — 1000 years of peace
- **Early followers:** Slaves, peasants, women — marginalised people
- **Why it worked:** Christianity was revolutionary, egalitarian, anti-authority; it promised hope, salvation, and redemption to people with none
- **The analogy:** "A free lottery ticket" — costs nothing, infinite payoff if true
- **Supports argument:** Christianity's revolutionary origins and why it appealed to the oppressed

### 2. Augustine and the Establishment Problem (~5th century CE)
- **Who:** Augustine (Church Father)
- **What:** Christianity became the official religion of the Roman Empire — creating a contradiction (revolutionary religion = the establishment)
- **Augustine's problem:** The Second Coming was dangerous for two reasons: (a) if the world is ending, why work? (b) Jesus returning implies the Catholic Church is failing
- **Augustine's solution:** The 1000 years of peace is "just a metaphor" — it's already happening through the Catholic Church. Don't worry about the Second Coming; the Church will take care of everything
- **Works referenced:** City of God, Confessions — studied in the Civilization series
- **Supports argument:** Origin of the amillennialist position and why the Second Coming became politically contested

### 3. The Reformation and the English Dissenters
- **What:** The Reformation challenged Catholic authority — Protestants must read the Bible themselves
- **England's trajectory:** King supports Reformation → creates Anglican Church (king has authority instead of Pope) → but Bible reading spawns thousands of new dissenter religions → dissenters believe the Bible is supreme authority above the king → conflict → English Civil War → Puritans kill the king → king reestablished → decades of fighting
- **Resolution:** The king sends dissenters to America — "if you really believe these things, go to America, there's nobody there, build your own kingdom of heaven"
- **Key detail:** Dissenters were characterised by strict biblical obedience — no drinking, no dancing, no fun, focus on spiritual improvement, no slaves
- **Supports argument:** America's founding as a Christian nation — not secular but explicitly dedicated to achieving the kingdom of God on earth

### 4. The Establishment of Israel (1948)
- **What:** Zionism — the belief that Jews must return to Israel — was extremely unpopular until the Holocaust
- **Before the Holocaust:** Jews saw themselves as nationals of their respective countries (an Iraqi Jew was Iraqi; a Chinese Jew was Chinese) — religion was not above nationality
- **The catalyst:** The Holocaust drove mass Jewish migration to Palestine → establishment of the State of Israel in 1948
- **Christian Zionist reaction:** Celebrated because Israel's reconstitution fulfilled the first step of the dispensationalist prophecy
- **Key context:** Romans destroyed the Temple in 70 CE → Jews had not controlled Israel for ~2000 years
- **Supports argument:** Christian Zionism preceded and enabled Zionism; Israel's establishment was interpreted through a dispensationalist lens

### 5. The Pax Romana / Pax Americana Parallel
- **Pax Romana:** Roman peace → no wars → no social mobility → massive inequality → poor people hopeless → Christianity appealed as a free lottery ticket
- **Pax Americana:** 70 years of American peace → growing inequality → billionaires accumulate wealth → millions of young people with no future → dispensationalist premillennialism appeals as a free lottery ticket
- **The delivery person analogy:** Millions of young men in China working as delivery drivers — no hope of marriage, children, housing, education for children. "Your life is pointless and hopeless." Same dynamic in America.
- **The war attraction:** For hopeless people, peace = continued poverty; war = opportunity for a new world. "What good does peace do you?"
- **Supports argument:** Dispensationalist premillennialism will grow because the structural conditions producing it (inequality under imperial peace) are worsening

### 6. The Destruction of the Temple (70 CE)
- **Who:** The Romans
- **What:** Burned down the Jewish Temple in Jerusalem (~70 CE, Prof. Jiang says "70 BCE" but this is a verbal slip — the Temple was destroyed in 70 CE)
- **Consequence:** Jews lost control of Israel for approximately 2000 years
- **Significance for dispensationalism:** The Temple must be rebuilt as part of the prophecy — this is one of the steps toward the Second Coming
- **Supports argument:** Why rebuilding the Temple is a live political issue, not just theology
