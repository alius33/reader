# Noise — Daniel Kahneman, Olivier Sibony & Cass R. Sunstein

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Argument — a systematic case that noise (unwanted variability in judgments) is as damaging as bias but almost entirely ignored.
**Authors:** Daniel Kahneman (Nobel laureate, author of Thinking Fast and Slow), Olivier Sibony (McKinsey partner turned professor), Cass R. Sunstein (Harvard Law professor, former head of OIRA).

## Central Thesis
When professionals make judgments — judges sentencing criminals, doctors diagnosing patients, underwriters pricing insurance — their judgments vary far more than anyone expects or accepts. This unwanted variability is "noise." Bias has received enormous attention; noise has received almost none. Yet noise causes as much error as bias, sometimes more. The book diagnoses the problem and prescribes decision hygiene — structured protocols that reduce noise without requiring identification of its specific causes.

## Key Frameworks
- Noise vs Bias (bias = systematic error in one direction; noise = scatter/variability)
- MSE = Bias² + Noise² (total error is the sum of both)
- Noise Audit (the simple test: have multiple people judge the same case independently)
- Occasion Noise (the same person gives different judgments on different days)
- Pattern Noise (different people weight the same factors differently)
- Decision Hygiene (structured procedures that reduce noise: checklists, independent judgments, aggregation)

## Best Stories
1. Insurance underwriters — asked to price the same case, their judgments varied by 55% (executives expected 10%)
2. Criminal sentencing — identical cases received wildly different sentences depending on the judge
3. Asylum judges — one judge granted asylum in 5% of cases, another in 88%
4. Performance reviews — more about the rater than the ratee
5. Medical diagnoses — doctors disagree with themselves 20-40% of the time when shown the same case twice
