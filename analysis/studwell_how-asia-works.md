# Analysis: How Asia Works — Joe Studwell

## Source
- **Title:** How Asia Works: Success and Failure in the World's Most Dynamic Region
- **Author:** Joe Studwell
- **Year:** 2013
- **Pages:** ~322
- **Type:** Argument/comparative economics
- **Source:** Training knowledge (extract file unavailable)

## Book Structure

Three-part structure organised around three policy interventions:

1. **Part 1: Land (Chapters 1-3)** — Land reform and smallholder agriculture
2. **Part 2: Industry (Chapters 4-6)** — Manufacturing, industrial policy, and export discipline
3. **Part 3: Finance (Chapters 7-9)** — Financial policy in service of development
4. **Epilogue** — Lessons and implications

## Core Thesis

- Successful Asian development (Japan, South Korea, Taiwan, China) followed a specific three-stage recipe:
  1. Maximise agricultural output through land reform (redistribution to smallholders)
  2. Direct capital and entrepreneurs into export-oriented manufacturing with strict discipline
  3. Keep the financial system subordinate to industrial policy goals
- Failed Asian economies (Philippines, Indonesia, Thailand to varying degrees) skipped or botched one or more of these steps
- The recipe contradicts free-market orthodoxy promoted by the IMF, World Bank, and mainstream Western economics

## Key Countries Compared

### Success Stories
- **Japan** — Post-WWII land reform under US occupation; MITI-directed industrial policy; financial repression
- **South Korea** — Park Chung-hee era land reform; chaebol system with export discipline; directed credit
- **Taiwan** — KMT land reform (1949-53); SME-driven manufacturing; state-directed banking
- **China** — Household Responsibility System (1978); SEZs and export manufacturing; state banking

### Cautionary Tales
- **Philippines** — Failed land reform (landlord class blocked it); premature deregulation; financial liberalisation
- **Indonesia** — Suharto-era crony capitalism; resource extraction over manufacturing; banking failures
- **Thailand** — Partial reforms; 1997 crisis exposed financial vulnerabilities
- **Malaysia** — Mixed results; resource wealth masked policy weaknesses

## Key Concepts

### Agriculture
- **Gardening vs. farming** — Intensive smallholder cultivation (gardening) produces dramatically higher yields per hectare than large-scale mechanised farming
- Land reform is politically painful but economically essential as a first step
- Generates rural surplus that funds industrialisation
- Japan's post-war land reform created the foundation for the economic miracle

### Industrial Policy
- **Export discipline** — The critical mechanism that separates successful industrial policy from crony capitalism
- Governments must subsidise and protect infant industries BUT condition support on export performance
- Companies that fail to export lose government support — Darwinian selection within a protected system
- Park Chung-hee's South Korea as the exemplar: chaebol received cheap credit but had to meet export targets or face consequences
- Contrast with Philippines: protection without discipline created rent-seeking, not competitive firms

### Financial Repression
- **Development finance** — Keeping interest rates low and directing credit to strategic industries
- Banks as servants of industrial policy, not profit-maximising independent actors
- Premature financial liberalisation (as advocated by IMF/World Bank) undermines the entire development model
- The 1997 Asian Financial Crisis as a case study in what happens when financial deregulation precedes institutional readiness

## Major Stories/Examples

1. **Japan's post-WWII land reform** — US occupation forces redistributed land; MacArthur's role
2. **Park Chung-hee and the chaebol** — Export-or-die discipline for Korean conglomerates
3. **Taiwan's KMT land reform** — Mainlanders redistributing Taiwanese landlords' property
4. **Deng Xiaoping's agricultural reform** — Household Responsibility System and its effects
5. **Marcos-era Philippines** — Why land reform failed; sugar barons and crony capitalism
6. **Suharto's Indonesia** — Resource curse and industrial policy without export discipline
7. **1997 Asian Financial Crisis** — Thailand, Indonesia, South Korea — different causes, different outcomes
8. **Malaysia's Proton** — National car project as case study in industrial policy with mixed results

## Intellectual Context

- Challenges neoclassical economics (free markets, comparative advantage, minimal state intervention)
- Draws on Friedrich List's infant industry argument
- Influenced by Ha-Joon Chang's "Kicking Away the Ladder" thesis
- Engages with the "developmental state" literature (Chalmers Johnson, Alice Amsden)
- Critiques Washington Consensus prescriptions

## Themes for Summary

- The recipe is sequential and non-negotiable: agriculture → industry → finance
- Politics determines policy — land reform requires overcoming entrenched elites
- Export discipline is the mechanism that prevents industrial policy from becoming crony capitalism
- The free-market advice given to developing countries by international institutions often contradicts what the successful developers actually did
- Geography and natural resources are less important than policy choices
