# The Like Switch — Jack Schafer

## Central Thesis

An ex-FBI agent's guide to turning strangers into allies. Schafer developed rapport-building techniques for recruiting foreign spies and informants. The core framework is the **Friendship Formula**: Friendship = Proximity + Frequency + Duration + Intensity. Combined with "friend signals" (eyebrow flash, head tilt, genuine smile, open body language) and conversational techniques (empathic statements, the art of compliments, active listening), anyone can systematically build rapport.

**Friendship = Proximity + Frequency + Duration + Intensity**

| Variable | Definition | FBI Application | Everyday Application |
|----------|-----------|----------------|---------------------|
| **Proximity** | Physical nearness to the target | Schafer would walk past a spy suspect's office daily | Sit near someone at meetings, use the same coffee shop |
| **Frequency** | Number of contacts over time | Gradually increase "accidental" encounters | Be seen regularly — don't disappear for weeks |
| **Duration** | Length of each contact | Start with brief exchanges, gradually extend | Short conversations first, then longer ones |
| **Intensity** | Depth/quality of the interaction | Move from small talk to personal topics | Use empathic statements, share appropriately |

The key insight: you can manipulate each variable independently. Schafer would spend weeks just establishing proximity before saying a single word.

### Friend vs Foe Signals

The brain classifies every person it encounters as friend, foe, or neutral — usually within seconds, based on nonverbal cues. These are hardwired survival responses.

| Signal | Friend Version | Foe Version |
|--------|---------------|-------------|
| **Eyebrows** | Quick flash (raise and lower in ~¼ second) | Furrowed, compressed |
| **Head position** | Tilted (exposes carotid artery = "I trust you") | Straight or turned away |
| **Smile** | Genuine/Duchenne (eyes crinkle) | Absent, forced, or asymmetric |
| **Torso** | Open, facing toward | Turned away, crossed arms |
| **Touch** | Brief, appropriate (handshake, shoulder) | Absent or aggressive |
| **Eye contact** | Mutual, comfortable duration (1–2 sec) | Staring (>3 sec = threat) or avoiding |
| **Personal space** | Gradually decreasing with comfort | Invaded or excessively maintained |

### The Golden Rule of Friendship

**Make the other person feel good about themselves — not about you.**

This is the single most powerful rapport-building principle. Every technique in the book serves this rule. People are drawn to those who make them feel valued, understood, and appreciated.

### Empathic Statements

The FBI's conversational workhorse. Instead of asking questions (which feel like interrogation), make statements that reflect observed feelings:

- "So you must have felt really frustrated when that happened."
- "It sounds like that was a really important moment for you."
- "That must have taken a lot of courage."

These statements accomplish three things simultaneously: they show you're listening, they validate the person's experience, and they invite further disclosure without the pressure of a question.

### Laws of Attraction

| Law | Mechanism | Application |
|-----|-----------|-------------|
| **Similarity** | We like people who are like us | Mirror language, posture, values |
| **Reciprocity** | We like people who like us | Show genuine interest and approval |
| **Misattribution** | We attribute arousal to the nearest person | Meet people during exciting activities |
| **Curiosity** | We're drawn to things that pique interest | Be slightly unpredictable, use curiosity hooks |
| **Scarcity** | We value what's rare | Don't be always available |

## Best Stories

1. **Charles — The Spy Who Came In** — Schafer's opening story. A foreign spy walked into FBI headquarters and volunteered to become a double agent. Schafer spent months establishing proximity (walking past him at a shop), then frequency, then duration, before the man ever spoke to him. The Friendship Formula in its purest form.
2. **Seagulls and Rapport** — Schafer uses the metaphor of feeding seagulls at the beach. First they're wary (foe signals). You toss bread far away (proximity without threat). Gradually they come closer (frequency + duration). Eventually they eat from your hand (intensity). Human rapport works the same way.
3. **The Prisoner Interview** — When standard interrogation failed, Schafer sat silently in the room reading a newspaper. After hours, the prisoner started talking — because the absence of foe signals and the presence of proximity eventually triggered the brain's social need for connection.

## Key Insights for Summary

1. The Friendship Formula gives structure to an intuitive process — you can deliberately adjust proximity, frequency, duration, and intensity
2. Friend/foe signals are processed by the brain before conscious thought — you must send the right signals before opening your mouth
3. The Golden Rule of Friendship (make THEM feel good) is the operating principle behind every technique
4. Empathic statements are more powerful than questions — they validate without interrogating
5. Schafer's FBI background gives unique credibility — these techniques were tested in the highest-stakes rapport situations
6. The book is lighter and more practical than academic social psychology but less comprehensive than Carnegie
