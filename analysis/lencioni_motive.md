# Analysis: The Motive — Patrick M. Lencioni

## Source
- **Title:** The Motive: Why So Many Leaders Abdicate Their Most Important Responsibilities
- **Author:** Patrick M. Lencioni
- **Year:** 2020
- **Publisher:** Jossey-Bass / Wiley
- **Pages:** ~240
- **Format:** Leadership fable (Part One) + conceptual framework (Part Two)
- **Source type:** Training knowledge (no extraction available)

## Book Structure

The book follows Lencioni's standard two-part format:

**Part One: The Fable** (~150 pages)
- A fictional story about two CEOs: Shay Davis (protagonist, reward-centred leader discovering his failures) and Liam Alcott (antagonist, another reward-centred leader whose company is failing)
- Set in a technology company (Golden Gate Alarm) and a competitor (Del Mar Analytics)
- Shay's coach/foil is his board member and former CEO mentor who forces him to confront his motives
- The fable dramatises each of the five omissions through Shay's realisations

**Part Two: The Model** (~70 pages)
- Lays out the two fundamental motives for leadership
- Details five specific responsibilities that reward-centred leaders avoid
- Provides diagnostic questions and warning signs

---

## Core Thesis

There are only two fundamental reasons people pursue leadership positions:

1. **Responsibility-centred leadership** — The leader sees the role as a responsibility to serve the organisation. The work is often uncomfortable, tedious, and thankless. They do it because it must be done and they are the only ones who can do it.

2. **Reward-centred leadership** — The leader sees the role as a reward for years of hard work. They enjoy the status, compensation, and perks. They unconsciously avoid the uncomfortable parts of the job and gravitate toward the enjoyable, intellectually stimulating parts.

Lencioni's argument: the motive matters more than the skills. A leader with mediocre skills but the right motive will outperform a brilliant leader with the wrong motive — because the right motive drives you toward the uncomfortable work that actually makes an organisation healthy.

## The Five Omissions

Reward-centred leaders consistently avoid five specific responsibilities:

### 1. Developing the Leadership Team
- Reward-centred leaders treat their team meetings as information-sharing sessions
- They avoid the hard work of building genuine trust and vulnerability among the leadership team
- They don't invest time in team-building because it feels soft and unproductive
- They allow silos to persist because breaking them down requires uncomfortable confrontation

### 2. Managing Subordinates (and Making Them Manage Theirs)
- This is the responsibility leaders most desperately want to avoid
- Managing means having difficult conversations about behaviour, performance, and accountability
- Reward-centred leaders rationalise their avoidance: "I hired good people, I should trust them," "I don't want to micromanage"
- The result: underperformance festers, cultural norms erode, best performers leave

### 3. Having Difficult and Uncomfortable Conversations
- Separate from (but related to) managing subordinates
- Includes confronting peers, addressing interpersonal conflicts, delivering unwelcome feedback
- Reward-centred leaders delay these conversations hoping problems resolve themselves
- Problems almost never resolve themselves — they metastasise

### 4. Running Great Team Meetings
- Most leaders treat meetings as a necessary evil and run them poorly
- Reward-centred leaders see bad meetings as inevitable rather than as their personal failure
- Lencioni argues meetings are the most important activity a leader engages in — the place where the leadership team makes decisions, debates priorities, and aligns
- A leader who doesn't invest in meeting structure and energy is abdicating leadership

### 5. Communicating Constantly and Repetitively to Employees
- Leaders get bored saying the same things over and over
- Reward-centred leaders assume that saying something once (or twice) means everyone heard it, understood it, and internalised it
- Lencioni argues that employees need to hear a message seven times before it sticks
- Repeating the message is tedious for the leader but essential for the organisation

## Key Story Elements (Fable)

### Shay Davis
- CEO of Golden Gate Alarm, a mid-sized technology/security company
- Promoted to CEO after years of strong functional performance
- Discovers he has been leading for the wrong reasons — enjoying the status and intellectually stimulating parts while avoiding the uncomfortable work
- His journey is one of self-confrontation and gradual recognition

### Liam Alcott
- CEO of Del Mar Analytics, a competitor/peer company
- Serves as a mirror — Shay initially admires Liam but comes to recognise that Liam embodies the reward-centred model even more extremely
- Liam's company is suffering from the consequences of reward-centred leadership: poor team dynamics, unmanaged conflict, strategic drift

### The Confrontation
- The fable's climax involves Shay recognising that Liam's impressive external persona masks deep leadership failures
- Shay must choose: continue enjoying the rewards of the CEO title, or embrace the uncomfortable responsibilities

## Connections to Other Lencioni Works

- **The Five Dysfunctions of a Team** — The Motive explains WHY leaders fail to address the five dysfunctions: they have the wrong motive
- **The Four Obsessions of an Extraordinary Executive** — The Motive is essentially a prequel; if the leader's motive is wrong, none of the four obsessions will be pursued
- **The Ideal Team Player** — complements by addressing individual contributor virtues (humble, hungry, smart) vs. leader virtues
- **Death by Meeting** — The Motive's fourth omission (running great meetings) directly connects

## Analytical Notes

### Strengths
- The motive concept is genuinely diagnostic — it explains patterns that skills-based frameworks miss
- The fable format makes the ideas emotionally resonant
- The five omissions are specific and actionable
- Links clearly to Lencioni's broader body of work

### Weaknesses
- No empirical research — all claims are from consulting observation
- The binary framing (reward vs. responsibility) may oversimplify — most leaders are a mix
- The fable characters are somewhat schematic
- Light on structural/systemic factors that shape leadership behaviour

### Comparable Works
- Drucker's "The Effective Executive" — similar emphasis on what leaders SHOULD spend time on
- Coyle's "The Culture Code" — both argue culture is the leader's primary job
- Pfeffer's "7 Rules of Power" — provides a contrasting lens (power as necessary tool vs. servant leadership)
