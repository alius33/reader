# Analysis: Getting to Yes — Roger Fisher & William Ury

## Central Thesis

Most people negotiate by positional bargaining — taking a position, arguing for it, and making concessions. This approach is inefficient, produces unwise outcomes, and damages relationships. There is a third alternative: **principled negotiation** (developed at the Harvard Negotiation Project), which decides issues on their merits rather than through a contest of wills. It rests on four pillars: (1) separate the people from the problem, (2) focus on interests not positions, (3) invent options for mutual gain, and (4) insist on using objective criteria. The method is hard on the merits and soft on the people. It works whether the other side is more powerful, refuses to play, or uses dirty tricks. It is an all-purpose strategy that becomes easier — not harder — if the other side also learns it.

## Structural Map

The book is organized in five parts:

**Introduction** — Why everyone negotiates; the problem with soft vs. hard positional bargaining; overview of the four principles

**Part I: The Problem (Chapter 1)** — Why positional bargaining fails on all three criteria: wise outcomes, efficiency, and relationship preservation

**Part II: The Method (Chapters 2-5)** — The four pillars of principled negotiation:
- Ch. 2: Separate the People from the Problem (perception, emotion, communication)
- Ch. 3: Focus on Interests, Not Positions (identifying, communicating, and reconciling interests)
- Ch. 4: Invent Options for Mutual Gain (brainstorming, broadening options, dovetailing differences)
- Ch. 5: Insist on Using Objective Criteria (fair standards, fair procedures, principled resistance to pressure)

**Part III: Yes, But... (Chapters 6-8)** — Handling real-world obstacles:
- Ch. 6: What If They Are More Powerful? (BATNA — Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)
- Ch. 7: What If They Won't Play? (Negotiation Jujitsu and the One-Text Procedure)
- Ch. 8: What If They Use Dirty Tricks? (Taming the Hard Bargainer)

**Part IV: Conclusion** — Three closing points: you knew it already, learn from doing, what "winning" means

**Part V: Ten Questions People Ask** — Extended FAQ covering fairness, dealing with difficult people, practical tactics, and power dynamics

## Key Frameworks

1. **Principled Negotiation (The Four Pillars)** — People, Interests, Options, Criteria. The irreducible method for reaching wise agreements efficiently and amicably. Each pillar addresses a basic element of negotiation.

2. **Positional Bargaining Spectrum** — Soft (make concessions, trust, yield) vs. Hard (demand concessions, distrust, apply pressure) vs. Principled (explore interests, reason, yield to principle not pressure). The first two are zero-sum; the third is collaborative.

3. **BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)** — The only legitimate measure for evaluating any proposed agreement. Replaces the "bottom line" which is rigid, arbitrary, and inhibits creative solutions. Developing your BATNA is the most effective source of negotiating power.

4. **The People Problem Trinity** — Perception, Emotion, Communication. All people problems in negotiation fall into one of these three categories. Each requires specific psychological techniques rather than substantive concessions.

5. **The Circle Chart (Four Types of Thinking)** — Problem → Diagnosis → Prescription → Action. A method for generating creative options by shuttling between specific problems and general theories.

6. **Dovetailing Differences** — Agreement is often made possible precisely because interests differ. Differences in interests, beliefs, time preference, forecasts, and risk aversion create opportunities for mutual gain rather than obstacles.

7. **Negotiation Jujitsu** — When the other side uses positional bargaining, don't push back. Sidestep their attack and deflect it against the problem. Use questions instead of statements, look behind positions for interests, invite criticism of your ideas.

8. **The One-Text Procedure** — A mediator (or one party acting as mediator) prepares a draft, asks both sides for criticism, improves it iteratively, and finally presents a take-it-or-leave-it recommendation. Used at Camp David in 1978 with 23 drafts leading to the Egyptian-Israeli peace treaty.

9. **Dirty Tricks Taxonomy** — Three categories: deliberate deception (phony facts, ambiguous authority, dubious intentions), psychological warfare (stressful situations, personal attacks, good-guy/bad-guy, threats), and positional pressure tactics (refusal to negotiate, extreme demands, escalating demands, lock-in tactics, hardhearted partner, calculated delay, take-it-or-leave-it).

10. **Six Sources of Negotiating Power** — (1) Good working relationship, (2) Understanding interests, (3) Elegant options, (4) External standards of legitimacy, (5) Strong BATNA, (6) Carefully crafted commitments.

## Key Principles

1. **Separate the people from the problem** — Deal with human beings as human beings and with the problem on its merits; never trade substantive concessions for relationship repair
2. **Focus on interests, not positions** — Positions are what you decided; interests are why you decided; multiple positions can satisfy the same interest
3. **Invent options for mutual gain** — Separate inventing from deciding; brainstorm first, evaluate later; the key to wise decisions is selecting from a great number of options
4. **Insist on using objective criteria** — Be open to reason, closed to threats; never yield to pressure, only to principle
5. **Know your BATNA** — The only standard against which any proposed agreement should be measured; the better your BATNA, the greater your power
6. **The relationship and the substance are separate** — A good working relationship tends to make good substantive outcomes easier, not harder
7. **Vive la différence** — Differences in interests, beliefs, time horizons, forecasts, and risk aversion create the building blocks for agreements, not obstacles
8. **Invent first, decide later** — Judgment hinders imagination; the creative act must be separated from the critical one
9. **Make their decision easy** — Focus on one person, draft a "yesable proposition," use precedent and legitimacy to make agreement painless
10. **Don't push back — use jujitsu** — When attacked, sidestep and redirect energy toward the problem; use questions and silence as your primary weapons
11. **Negotiate the process, not just the substance** — Every move in a negotiation is also a move about how you negotiate; meta-negotiate the rules of the game
12. **Prevention beats cure** — Build relationships before problems arise; structure the process to protect egos from substantive disagreement

## Major Stories & Examples

1. **The Library Window** — Two men quarrel over whether a window should be open or closed. The librarian asks "why?" — one wants fresh air, the other wants no draft. She opens a window in the next room. Classic illustration of interests vs. positions.

2. **Sinai Peninsula / Camp David** — Egypt wanted sovereignty; Israel wanted security. Positions were incompatible (keep vs. return the Sinai). Solution: full Egyptian sovereignty with demilitarization. Egyptian flag everywhere, Egyptian tanks nowhere near Israel. The defining case study of the book.

3. **Nuclear Test Ban Talks** — US insisted on 10 inspections, Soviet Union agreed to 3. Talks broke down because no one defined what an "inspection" meant. Classic failure of positional bargaining.

4. **Sadat's Visit to Jerusalem (1977)** — To change Israeli perceptions of Egypt as enemy, Sadat flew to their capital. Acting inconsistently with the enemy perception opened the path to peace.

5. **The Orange Quarrel** — Two sisters split an orange in half. One ate the fruit and discarded the peel; the other discarded the fruit and used the peel for baking. Both could have had all of what they wanted. Failure to explore interests.

6. **Jones Realty / Frank Turnbull** — Extended dialogue demonstrating principled negotiation in action. Tenant discovers he's been overcharged rent; negotiates reimbursement using every technique in the book without damaging the relationship.

7. **Tom and the Insurance Adjuster** — Tom's car is destroyed; using objective criteria (blue book value, replacement cost, depreciation) he negotiates $8,024 settlement. Shows principled negotiation producing concrete results.

8. **Nasser and the American Lawyer (1970)** — Lawyer asks Nasser to imagine Golda Meir promising unconditional withdrawal on TV. Nasser laughs — "she'd have trouble at home." Hypothetical questioning reveals the unrealism of the Egyptian position.

9. **Small Town vs. Corporation** — Town negotiates a factory from $300,000 to $2,300,000 in annual payments by developing a strong BATNA (expanding town limits to include the factory). Demonstrates that resources don't equal power.

10. **Law of the Sea / MIT Model** — India proposed $60M mining fee; US proposed none. An MIT economic model provided objective criteria that shifted both positions. Shows the power of independent standards.

11. **Malta vs. Britain (1971)** — Prime Minister of Malta kept escalating demands after each apparent agreement. Classic example of the escalating demands dirty trick.

12. **Camp David One-Text Procedure** — US mediators prepared 23 successive drafts, asking Egypt and Israel to critique each one. Neither side had to make concessions — they just critiqued. Final draft was accepted by both.

## Tensions & Counterarguments

1. **Rationality assumption** — The method assumes both sides can be persuaded by reason and principle. Chris Voss (Never Split the Difference) argues humans are primarily emotional decision-makers and the principled approach targets the wrong cognitive system.

2. **Power asymmetry** — When one side has overwhelming power, objective criteria may be irrelevant to them. The book addresses this via BATNA but acknowledges limits: "No method can guarantee success if all the leverage lies on the other side."

3. **Cultural applicability** — The framework reflects Western, particularly American, communication norms. The emphasis on directness, explicit interest-sharing, and brainstorming may not translate well in high-context cultures.

4. **Strategic withholding** — Good faith negotiation "does not require total disclosure." There's tension between being open about interests and maintaining strategic advantage.

5. **When the other side won't play** — Despite the jujitsu chapter, some counterparties simply refuse to engage on the merits. The book's answer (one-text procedure, third parties) may not always be available.
