# Podcast Analysis: Frontline Manager at Meta to Senior Director at Snapchat in 3 Years

## Podcast Type

**Interview** — Ryan Peterman interviews Rome, who progressed from PhD researcher to IBM research scientist to Facebook engineer to Square director to Snapchat senior director (250 engineers) to CTO of HeyGen, an AI video generation startup.

## Core Topic

The career journey of an engineering leader who made unconventional choices at every stage — leaving academia for industry, choosing an unfamiliar domain over comfort, and anchoring every decision against a long-term northstar goal of becoming a CTO at an AI-first company.

## Host & Guest

- **Host:** Ryan Peterman — ex-Instagram Staff Engineer, career content creator
- **Guest:** Rome — CTO of HeyGen. Background: PhD in machine learning (CMU), 3 years at IBM Research (computer vision/ML), joined Facebook ~2009 during fast-growth era, transitioned to management at Facebook, took a director offer at Square (25→50 engineers in ~1 year), joined Snapchat as ~100th employee and grew to senior director managing 250 engineers, then moved to startup world, now CTO of HeyGen (AI video generation platform)

## Main Arguments & Claims

### 1. Directing is fundamentally different from managing
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** The jump from manager to director requires a completely different philosophy — "directing instead of managing." Managers know the details and use that as leverage. Directors provide strategic direction, cross-department resource allocation, and solve the organisation's hardest problems.
- **Evidence:** Personal experience at Square — first 6 months were rough because he tried to manage like a line manager. Reports started questioning why his layer existed.
- **Strength:** Strong — lived experience with specific failure mode described

### 2. Trust is the foundational layer of any team
- **Who:** Rome (citing The Five Dysfunctions of a Team)
- **Claim:** When hired above existing leaders, the first priority must be building trust. The new director must prove they are a career promoter, not a career blocker.
- **Evidence:** Square experience where reports were passed over. He had to demonstrate value through unblocking problems they couldn't solve alone.
- **Strength:** Strong — specific anecdote plus book reference

### 3. Every career decision should anchor against a personal northstar
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** Having a long-term northstar (his: become CTO of an AI company) makes every career decision clearer. He chose the camera team at Snapchat over the data team because it filled a product gap in his CTO preparation.
- **Evidence:** Multiple examples — CMU over Princeton, Facebook over IBM/academia, camera over data, startups over big-company VP track
- **Strength:** Very strong — consistent thread across entire career

### 4. Titles belong to the company, not to you
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** When senior executives leave their companies, you immediately hear less about them. People say "the company did this" not "the VP did this." Impact and product building are more fulfilling than title accumulation.
- **Evidence:** Observing role models — former VPs/SVPs who became invisible after leaving
- **Strength:** Moderate — observational, but resonant

### 5. Being technical at every level is a critical success factor
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** Learned from Facebook's culture (VP doing 6-week bootcamp coding) that staying technical is essential even as a senior leader. Still writes 2-3 pull requests per week as CTO.
- **Evidence:** Facebook VP doing bootcamp, personal practice of weekly code reviews
- **Strength:** Strong — backed by specific cultural practice at a successful company

### 6. Promotions should never be the objective
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** Checking level rubrics and asking "when's my next promotion" creates unhappiness because promotions depend on organisational factors you can't control. Focus on controllable impact instead.
- **Evidence:** Mentoring experience — seeing mentees obsess over rubric checkboxes
- **Strength:** Moderate — common advice but well-articulated

### 7. Radical honesty in recruiting produces the best hires
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** Telling candidates there's a 1% chance of success filters for the right people — those who won't leave at the first sign of difficulty.
- **Evidence:** Current HeyGen hiring philosophy, military analogy (soldiers who know the difficulty but are pumped for it)
- **Strength:** Moderate — philosophy stated but no outcome data

### 8. Manager career growth is largely situational
- **Who:** Rome
- **Claim:** The more senior you become, the more your growth depends on organisational structure and company strategy — things you can't control. The only thing you can control is performing well in your current role.
- **Evidence:** Reflection on own career — Snapchat's explosive growth enabled his team to grow from a handful to 250
- **Strength:** Strong — honest admission that undermines "hustle culture" narrative

## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### 1. The Three-Path Fork (Career Origin)
- After PhD + 3 years at IBM Research, Rome faced three options: faculty at a top school, quant trader at a top firm, or software engineer at a fast-growing company
- Chose software engineering for broader impact; chose Facebook specifically because engineering was a "first-class citizen"
- In financial firms, engineering was always "second class"
- Illustrates: **Northstar-driven decision-making from the very beginning**

### 2. The Facebook VP in Bootcamp
- Facebook had a 6-week bootcamp where every new hire — including a VP — sat next to Rome finding bugs, fixing bugs, writing pull requests
- At IBM, VPs "probably only write PPTs"
- This deeply planted the belief that being technical at all levels is critical
- Illustrates: **Culture shapes leadership philosophy**

### 3. The Invisible Director (First 6 Months at Square)
- Joined Square as a director, but his reports were more senior in tenure and knew more details
- First 6 months, he wasn't adding value — reports started questioning why his layer existed
- Had to pivot from "managing details" to "directing strategy, unblocking, cross-department resources"
- Illustrates: **The manager-to-director skill gap is real and dangerous**

### 4. Choosing Camera Over Data at Snapchat
- Had two options at Snapchat: director of data (comfortable, his background) or director of camera (the first page of Snapchat, but required learning iOS/Android from scratch)
- Spent 2 months learning iOS programming
- The first 2 years at Snapchat were "one of the happiest periods" of his career because he was learning every day
- Illustrates: **Northstar thinking trumps comfort — chose product experience over domain comfort**

### 5. Evan Spiegel's Pixel-Level Reviews
- In product reviews, Evan would point out pixel-level issues in demos
- Team learned to build "a ready demo, not a normal demo"
- His attention to detail echoed Steve Jobs
- Illustrates: **Founder obsession with craft drives product quality**

### 6. Never Fire the First 15 Engineers
- Evan told leaders that the first 15 engineers should never be fired — they are founding members
- After 4 years of growth, he bought back the original "blue house" in Venice Beach and hosted all-hands meetings there
- Illustrates: **Founder who values personal connection and loyalty**

### 7. The Cryptographer Neighbour
- In LA, Rome's neighbours included a medical doctor and a cryptographer for Michael Jackson
- In San Francisco, every dinner conversation was about tech, stocks, and startups
- Illustrates: **Geographic diversity broadens product thinking**

### 8. CMU Over Princeton
- Rome's father asked why he didn't choose Princeton (more prestigious in China)
- Rome chose CMU because it was better for computer science specifically
- Illustrates: **Don't follow conventional wisdom — optimise for your specific goal**

### 9. The 1% Pitch to Candidates
- When recruiting for HeyGen, Rome tells every candidate there's only a 1% chance the company will grow into something big
- Wants people who believe in that 1% and won't leave at the first difficulty
- Uses military analogy: "you want soldiers who understand the difficulty but are pumped for it"
- Illustrates: **Radical honesty as a filtering mechanism**

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. Directing vs Managing
- **Managers** know the details and use that knowledge as their anchor
- **Directors** provide strategic direction, cross-department resource allocation, and solve the organisation's hardest problems
- The word "director" literally means "one who directs" — this isn't about managing people anymore

### 2. The Northstar Framework
- Define a long-term career goal (Rome's: CTO of an AI company)
- Evaluate every career decision against it: does this move me closer?
- Accept discomfort if it fills a gap (e.g., learning iOS at Snapchat)
- Periodically reassess the gap between current state and northstar

### 3. Trust as Foundation (from Five Dysfunctions of a Team)
- Trust is the base layer — everything else builds on it
- When hired above existing people: first priority is building trust, not executing strategy
- Show you are a career promoter, not a blocker
- Unblock things your reports can't unblock themselves

### 4. The Manager as Psychological Doctor
- Understand what each direct report is looking for in their career
- Align your work towards helping them grow to the next level
- When you do this, you earn respect and trust

### 5. Weekly Top 3 Prioritisation
- At the beginning of every week, ask: what are the top 3 things I need to achieve?
- Focus only on those three things
- Everything else is less important
- Sometimes one of those three is getting into technical details — that's fine
- Recalibrate weekly against your northstar

### 6. The Regret Minimisation Test
- "15 years from now, if I choose to retire, will I feel regret for not doing this?"
- If yes, do it now
- This drove Rome to eventually pursue the AI company CTO role

### 7. Lagging Indicators Philosophy
- Promotions, titles, team size — these are lagging indicators of impact
- Focus on what you can control: making impact in your current position
- "If you're lucky, you get the promotion. If you're not lucky, don't worry about it."
