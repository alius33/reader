# Made to Stick — Chip Heath & Dan Heath: Analysis

## Book Overview

**Full Title:** Made to Stick: Why Some Ideas Survive and Others Die
**Authors:** Chip Heath (Stanford professor) & Dan Heath (education entrepreneur)
**Published:** 2007, Random House
**Pages:** ~291 + endnotes
**Genre:** Communication/persuasion framework

**Central Thesis:** Sticky ideas — those that are understood, remembered, and change behavior — share six common traits (SUCCESs: Simple, Unexpected, Concrete, Credible, Emotional, Stories). These traits can be learned and applied systematically, and the primary obstacle to creating sticky ideas is the Curse of Knowledge.

**Core Argument Structure:** The book is an argument/framework book built around one villain (Curse of Knowledge) and six principles (SUCCESs). Each chapter introduces one principle, provides research backing, gives real-world examples, and includes "Idea Clinics" showing before/after message improvements.

---

## Thematic Architecture

### The Villain: Curse of Knowledge
- Once you know something, you can't imagine not knowing it
- Illustrated by Elizabeth Newton's 1990 Stanford experiment: tappers predicted 50% recognition but achieved only 2.5%
- CEOs, teachers, experts all suffer from it — they hear a "song" in their head that listeners can't hear
- Only two ways to beat it: don't learn anything, or transform your ideas using SUCCESs

### The Framework: SUCCESs

1. **Simple** — Find the core + make it compact
2. **Unexpected** — Get attention (surprise) + keep it (curiosity/mystery)
3. **Concrete** — Sensory, tangible language; avoid abstraction
4. **Credible** — External authorities, internal details/statistics, testable credentials
5. **Emotional** — Make people care through individuals, identity, self-interest
6. **Stories** — Simulation (how to act) + inspiration (energy to act)

### Meta-Framework
The SUCCESs checklist maps to a communication pipeline:
- Pay attention → Unexpected
- Understand & remember → Concrete
- Believe/agree → Credible
- Care → Emotional
- Act → Stories
- Simple tells you *what* to say (finding the core)

- The Curse of Knowledge as the central villain

- Proverbs — "A bird in the hand" traced across 55+ languages back to Aesop (570 BC)

- High-concept Hollywood pitches — "Die Hard on a bus" (Speed), "Jaws on a spaceship" (Alien)

**Key Concepts:**
- Simple = Core + Compact (not "dumbing down")
- Forced prioritization — "If you say three things, you don't say anything"
- Schemas enable profound simplicity
- Generative analogies create platforms for novel thinking
- Two steps: (1) Find the core, (2) Translate it using SUCCESs

**Idea Clinic:** Sun exposure messaging — reordered to unbury the lead, added aging analogy

### Chapter 2: Unexpected

**Key Stories/Examples:**
- Karen Wood's humorous flight safety announcement — scattered applause
- Enclave minivan ad — Ad Council's fake car commercial ending in deadly crash
- Surprise brow (Paul Ekman) — biological purpose of surprise
- HENSION/PHRAUG word exercise — surprise without insight vs. surprise with insight
- Nordstrom stories — tire chain refund, gift-wrapping Macy's purchases, warming cars
- Nora Ephron's journalism teacher — "The lead is 'There will be no school next Thursday'"
- Saturn's rings mystery — Robert Cialdini's discovery of mystery as teaching tool
- Gap Theory of Curiosity — George Loewenstein (1994), Carnegie Mellon
- Roone Arledge and ABC college football — "take the viewer to the game" memo at age 29
- Sony's "pocketable radio" — Masaru Ibuka (1953), defying Bell Labs skepticism
- JFK's "man on the moon" speech (1961)

**Key Concepts:**
- Two questions: How to *get* attention (surprise) and *keep* it (interest/curiosity)
- Surprise = schema violation; must be "postdictable" not gimmicky
- Break guessing machines, then fix them — target core message dimensions
- Common sense is the enemy of sticky messages
- Gap Theory: curiosity = pain of missing knowledge; gaps start with knowledge
- Sequencing information like treasure maps, not MapQuest directions
- Long-term unexpectedness drives years of effort (pocketable radio, moon mission)

**Idea Clinics:** Foreign aid spending (unbury the lead, soft drinks comparison); Fund-raising presentation (mystery/gap approach)

- Velcro Theory of Memory — multiple hooks (David Rubin's memory exercise)
- Jane Elliott's blue eyes/brown eyes prejudice simulation (1968) — effects 20 years later
- Engineers vs. manufacturers at silicon chip firm — Beth Bechky's research
- Boeing 727 — "seat 131 passengers, land on Runway 4-22 at La Guardia"
- HP/Disney Ferraris family exhibit — Stone Yamashita Partners' 6,000 sq ft simulation
- White things exercise — refrigerator constraint improves brainstorming
- Jerry Kaplan's Kleiner Perkins pitch — maroon portfolio as "model of future computing" ($4.5M)
- UNICEF's James Grant — salt/sugar packet for oral rehydration therapy
- Hamburger Helper's "Fingertips" in-home visits — Melissa Studzinski
- Saddleback Church's "Saddleback Sam" persona — Rick Warren

**Key Concepts:**
- Concrete = examine with your senses; specific people doing specific things
- Abstraction is the luxury of the expert
- Concreteness creates shared turf for coordination
- Concreteness brings knowledge to bear (refrigerator constraint)
- Universal language is always concrete — engineers must come to the factory floor
- Memory is like Velcro: more hooks = better sticking

**Idea Clinic:** Oral Rehydration Therapy — Grant's salt/sugar packet vs. scientific description

### Chapter 4: Credible

**Key Stories/Examples:**
- Barry Marshall & Robin Warren's ulcer bacteria discovery — Nobel 2005; Marshall drank H. pylori
- Flesh-eating bananas email — fake "Manheim Research Institute" authority
- Pam Laffin — 29-year-old emphysema patient as antismoking antiauthority
- Doe Fund's Dennis — formerly homeless driver as living proof
- The Boyfriend's Death urban legend — localized details add credibility
- Jurors and the Darth Vader toothbrush — Shedler & Manis (1986), vivid irrelevant details swayed judgments
- Liz Lerman Dance Exchange — 73-year-old Thomas Dwyer as proof of diversity
- Beyond War's BBs — 1 BB = Hiroshima, 5,000 BBs poured into bucket
- Human-scale principle — sun-to-earth vs. NYC-to-LA accuracy comparison
- Stephen Covey's soccer team analogy for workplace dysfunction statistics
- Sinatra Test — Safexpress delivering Harry Potter books and board exams in India
- Bill McDonough's edible textile — 38 of 8,000 chemicals; factory water cleaner on exit
- Wendy's "Where's the Beef?" — testable credentials, audience as source of credibility
- Reagan's "Are you better off?" — 1980 debate question
- Positive Coaching Alliance's Emotional Tank exercise
- NBA rookie orientation — women from bar revealed as HIV positive

**Key Concepts:**
- Three sources of credibility: External (authorities/antiauthorities), Internal (details, statistics, Sinatra Test), Audience (testable credentials)
- Vivid details boost credibility — even irrelevant ones (Darth Vader toothbrush)
- Statistics should illustrate relationships, not stand alone
- Human-scale principle — make numbers relatable
- Testable credentials = "try before you buy" for ideas
- Antiauthorities can be more powerful than authorities

**Idea Clinic:** Shark attack hysteria (deer 300x more deadly); Availability bias (testable credential quiz)

### Chapter 5: Emotional

**Key Stories/Examples:**
- Mother Teresa: "If I look at the one, I will act"
- Carnegie Mellon Save the Children study — Rokia ($2.38) vs. statistics ($1.14) vs. both ($1.43)
- Truth antismoking campaign vs. Philip Morris's "Think. Don't Smoke" — 66% less likely vs. 36% MORE likely to smoke
- Semantic stretch — "relativity," "unique" (73% usage increase), "sportsmanship" dilution
- Jim Thompson's Positive Coaching Alliance — "Honoring the Game" replaces hollow "sportsmanship"
- John Caples — "They Laughed When I Sat Down at the Piano" (1925), WIIFY principle
- Cable TV in Tempe study (1982) — "imagine yourself" → 47% subscription vs. 20%
- Maslow's hierarchy (not really a hierarchy) — people operate on all levels simultaneously
- "Maslow's basement" — we assume others are motivated by base needs, but we're not
- Floyd Lee's Pegasus mess hall in Baghdad — "I am in charge of morale" (Transcendence)
- Popcorn Popper and Political Science — firefighters rejected gifts, self-interest sometimes backfires
- Donald Kinder's 30-year survey — self-interest "trifling" in predicting political views
- James March's two decision models — consequences vs. identity
- "Don't Mess with Texas" antilitter campaign — identity appeal to "Bubba," 72% litter reduction in 5 years
- Murray Dranoff Duo Piano Foundation — "the sound of the orchestra but the intimacy of chamber music"
- IDEO's hospital patient-perspective video

**Key Concepts:**
- Analytical thinking suppresses emotional giving (mere calculation reduced charity)
- Associations: piggyback on existing emotions, but beware semantic stretch/arms race
- Self-interest works but it's a limited palette; spell out WIIFY, use "you" language
- Get out of Maslow's basement — aesthetic, learning, transcendence motivations
- Identity-based decisions: Who am I? What kind of situation? What do people like me do?
- Group interest predicts political behavior better than self-interest
- Three Whys technique to bypass Curse of Knowledge and reach core emotional truth

**Idea Clinics:** Why study algebra (weight lifting analogy); Don't Mess with Texas deep dive

### Chapter 6: Stories

**Key Stories/Examples:**
- NICU nurse overrides heart monitor to save baby — pneumopericardium diagnosis
- Xerox copier repairmen's shop talk — E053 code story during cribbage
- Sweatshirt spatial simulation study — readers track objects within story geography
- UCLA problem-solving study — event simulation beats outcome simulation
- Mental practice meta-analysis — 2/3 the benefits of physical practice from mental rehearsal alone
- Jared Fogle's Subway weight loss — 425 lbs to 180 lbs; spotted by franchise owner Bob Ocwieja
- Three inspirational plot types: Challenge (David & Goliath, Rose Blumkin), Connection (Good Samaritan), Creativity (Ingersoll-Rand Drag Test, Shackleton)
- Stephen Denning's World Bank springboard story — Zambia health worker finding CDC info on Internet
- Conference storybook — presenters furious when their stories extracted from abstractions
- Stanford classroom exercise — 63% remember stories vs. 5% remember statistics

**Key Concepts:**
- Stories provide dual power: simulation (how to act) + inspiration (energy to act)
- Stories are flight simulators for the brain — mental practice improves real performance
- Three plot templates: Challenge, Connection, Creativity
- Spotting stories is as valuable as creating them — know what to look for
- Springboard stories combat skepticism, create buy-in, mobilize action
- Stories engage the "little voice" in harmony rather than trying to silence it
- Stories naturally embody most of SUCCESs (Concrete, Emotional, Unexpected)
- The hardest part: ensuring stories are Simple — reflecting core message

### Epilogue: What Sticks

**Key Stories/Examples:**
- "Nice guys finish last" — Leo Durocher's quote evolution
- "Elementary, my dear Watson" — Holmes never said it; combined from two separate phrases
- Stanford "Making Ideas Stick" class exercise — polished speakers scored well but ideas didn't stick

**Key Concepts:**
- Audience gets a vote — ideas evolve, simplify, improve in the marketplace
- Four villains: burying the lead, focus on presentation over message, decision paralysis, Curse of Knowledge
- Answer stage vs. Telling Others stage — expertise helps the first but hinders the second
- SUCCESs checklist as a practical substitute for abstract communication framework
