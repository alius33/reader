# Running on Empty — Jonice Webb

## Classification & Context

- **Category:** Awareness & Protection / Psychology / Self-Help
- **Book type:** Manual / how-to — clinical framework + recovery programme
- **Target reader:** Adults who feel "something is missing" but can't identify what; therapists treating stuck patients
- **Author credentials:** PhD clinical psychologist, private practice, coined "Childhood Emotional Neglect" (CEN) as a distinct clinical concept
- **Co-author:** Christine Musello, PsyD — contributed initial sections and clinical vignettes
- **Published:** 2012 (Morgan James Publishing)
- **Companion:** *Running on Empty No More* (2017) — focuses on relationships
- **Unique contribution:** First book to isolate emotional neglect from abuse and name it as a standalone invisible force; prior literature bundled neglect with trauma/abuse

## Central Thesis

Childhood Emotional Neglect (CEN) is an invisible force defined not by what happened in childhood but by what *didn't* happen. When parents fail to respond adequately to a child's emotional needs — even while providing excellent physical care — the child grows up with a structural flaw: an inability to recognise, value, or manage emotions. This manifests as emptiness, counter-dependence, alexithymia, self-blame, poor self-discipline, and difficulty with intimacy. CEN is self-propagating across generations because emotionally neglected parents cannot give what they never received. The damage is reversible through deliberate emotional re-education: learning to identify feelings, practise self-compassion, build self-discipline, and break the intergenerational cycle.

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Chapter 1: Why Wasn't the Tank Filled?
- Establishes Winnicott's "Good Enough Mother" as baseline
- Three essential emotional parenting skills: (1) feel connection, (2) pay attention to child as unique person, (3) respond competently to emotional needs
- Zeke vignette: recurring character (3rd grader sent home with teacher note) used throughout book to illustrate each parent type
- Kathleen vignette: subtle neglect — mother interrupts father-daughter play on beach, neither parent attuned
- Key distinction: acute empathic failure (one critical moment) vs chronic empathic failure (ongoing tone-deafness)

### Chapter 2: Twelve Ways to End Up Empty
12 parent types, each illustrated with case vignettes + Zeke variation:
1. **Narcissistic** — child as extension of self (Sid, Beatrice, Gina)
2. **Authoritarian** — obedience = love (Sophia, Joseph, Renee)
3. **Permissive** — path of least resistance (Samantha, Audrey, Eli)
4. **Bereaved/Divorced/Widowed** — grief absorbs parenting energy (Sally)
5. **Addicted** — two-parent phenomenon: kind sober, unpredictable using (Richard, Elsie)
6. **Depressed** — missing in action, low energy (Margo)
7. **Workaholic** — material success masks emotional absence (Sam)
8. **Special Needs Family Member** — healthy child expected to be mini-adult (Stuart, Miranda, Jack)
9. **Achievement/Perfection Focused** — future > feelings (Tim)
10. **Sociopathic** — no conscience, control-driven (Wallace)
11. **Child as Parent** — role reversal due to hardship
12. **Well-Meaning-but-Neglected-Themselves (WMBNT)** — largest category; loving but blind to emotions

### Chapter 3: The Neglected Child, All Grown Up
10 adult characteristics of CEN:
1. **Feelings of Emptiness** (Simon — skydiver, empty rich man)
2. **Counter-Dependence** (David — youngest of 7, cordial family)
3. **Unrealistic Self-Appraisal** (Josh — square peg, directionless)
4. **No Compassion for Self, Plenty for Others** (Noelle — harsh inner critic)
5. **Guilt and Shame** (Laura — friend suicides, parents dismissed feelings)
6. **Self-Directed Anger/Self-Blame** (Laura continued — cutting, overdose)
7. **The Fatal Flaw** — "If people really know me they won't like me" (Carrie)
8. **Difficulty Nurturing Self and Others** (David continued)
9. **Poor Self-Discipline** (William — unstructured childhood)
10. **Alexithymia** — poor emotional awareness (Cal — engineering degree, repairman, destroyed photos)

### Chapter 4: Cognitive Secrets — Suicidal Feelings
- Robyn: detailed case study — "Zero Tolerance" household, sensitive child, hermit mode, overdose triggered by Waltons rerun
- Links suicidal feelings to emptiness + shame + isolation, NOT dramatic events
- Reviews Simon, David, Laura, Cal through suicide-risk lens
- Common factors: emptiness/numbness, suffering in silence, questioning life's meaning, escape fantasy

### Chapter 5: How Change Happens
Three enemies of change:
1. **False Expectations** — change isn't linear; setbacks ≠ failure
2. **Avoidance** — comfort of not trying
3. **Discomfort** — the alien feeling when change starts working

### Chapter 6: Why Feelings Matter
Six-skill emotion programme:
1. Understanding purpose/value of emotions (evolutionary function table)
2. Identifying and Naming (blank screen meditation exercise)
3. Self-Monitoring (Feelings Sheet — 3x/day tracking)
4. Accepting and Trusting (3 rules: no bad emotion; feelings always have reason; emotions manageable)
5. Expressing Effectively (assertive + compassionate expression)
6. Recognising Emotions in Relationships
- **IAAA Framework**: Identify → Accept → Attribute → Act

### Chapter 7: Self-Care (4 parts)
**Part 1 — Nurturing Self:**
- Step A: Putting yourself first (saying no, asking for help, discovering likes/dislikes, prioritising enjoyment)
- Step B: Eating (childhood-adult habit comparison)
- Step C: Exercise
- Step D: Rest & Relaxation

**Part 2 — Improving Self-Discipline:**
- **Three Things Programme**: daily do 3 things you don't want to do OR stop 3 things you shouldn't
- Inner voice rewiring: harsh ↔ indulgent → loving-but-firm

**Part 3 — Self-Soothing:**
- Comprehensive strategy list (bubble bath to meditation)
- IAAA + physical strategies

**Part 4 — Self-Compassion:**
5 principles:
1. Treat yourself as you'd treat a friend
2. Stop comparing (everyone has flaws)
3. Talk to yourself compassionately (give yourself own advice)
4. Develop inner loving-but-firm voice
5. Allow yourself to be human

### Chapter 8: Ending the Cycle
- Addresses parental guilt directly (5 principles)
- Maps each of 10 adult CEN characteristics to specific parenting challenges
- Key insight: you can't give your child what you don't have — fill your own tank first
- Oxygen mask metaphor: secure your own before helping child

- Clinical identification markers
- Treatment considerations

## Key Principles (15)

1. What didn't happen has more power than what did happen
2. Emotional neglect is invisible — it hides in omission, not commission
3. You can love your child and still emotionally neglect them
4. CEN is self-propagating across generations
5. The fuel of life is feeling — emptiness is worse than pain
6. Emotions evolved before thought — they are more fundamental to who we are
7. There is no bad emotion — judge actions, not feelings
8. Every emotion exists for a good reason, even if it seems irrational
9. Feelings that are hidden gain power; acknowledged feelings lose potency
10. Self-compassion is not selfishness — it's the oxygen mask principle
11. Change comes in fits and starts, not linear progression
12. Self-discipline is wired through practice, not willpower
13. The Fatal Flaw is not real, but the feeling is real
14. Emptiness at its worst drives suicidal ideation
15. It is never too late — CEN scars don't have to be permanent (Cal's story)
