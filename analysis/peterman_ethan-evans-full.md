# Analysis: Amazon VP On Stack Ranking & PIPs, Working With Bezos, His Promotions

## Podcast Type

**Interview** — Ryan Peterman (host, ex-Instagram Staff Engineer) interviews Ethan Evans (retired Amazon VP). Conversational, question-driven format with Ethan providing most of the substance through stories and hard-earned principles.

## Core Topic

The full arc of a career from startup engineer to Amazon VP — covering early failures, the transformation from abrasive individual contributor to politically skilled leader, the mechanics of big-company promotions, working with Bezos and Jassy, and the brutal realities of stack ranking and PIPs.

## Host & Guest

- **Host:** Ryan Peterman — ex-Instagram Staff Engineer, tech career content creator
- **Guest:** Ethan Evans — retired Amazon VP, managed 800+ engineers, now career coach and writer. Known for straight talk about corporate realities. Built Prime Video, oversaw Twitch integration, launched Prime Gaming.

## Main Arguments & Claims

### Claim 1: Being abrasive works short-term but is career-fatal long-term
- **Who:** Ethan Evans (personal experience)
- **Evidence:** Fired twice from startups despite strong technical skills. Rose to VP-level in startups but couldn't sustain it. First review said "solution is to knock heads together."
- **Strength:** Very strong — autobiographical with clear cause-and-effect chain

### Claim 2: You must be "strategically annoying" to get promoted
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** Got director promotion by politely threatening to leave ("my career is very important to me"). Managers admit promoting the squeaky wheel over the silent hard worker.
- **Strength:** Strong — supported by his own behaviour as VP making the same choices

### Claim 3: Managers want to promote you (it's in their interest)
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** "The better my team is, the easier my job is." But they can't do it for you — you must reach the bar.
- **Strength:** Moderate — true in principle but contradicted by his own admission about promoting the aggressive person over the team player

### Claim 4: PIPs are functionally death sentences
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** Coached half a dozen Amazon PIP recipients — all got fired. Those he didn't coach also got fired. Manager's cognitive bias becomes self-fulfilling. HR and boss pressure to "exit" the person.
- **Strength:** Very strong — both personal observation and systemic analysis

### Claim 5: A manager can fire any single employee they want
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** Same performance data can be narrated positively or negatively. Manager gets the preemptive narrative strike. HR hears "it's unfair" from every bad performer too. Pattern only caught if manager does it repeatedly.
- **Strength:** Strong — nuanced (can't fire everyone, just any one person)

### Claim 6: Big companies confuse hitting goals with creating value
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** Amazon App Store — hit all goals for years, eventually shut down. Prime Gaming — hit all interim goals, never achieved the vision. Evans himself got good reviews while running zombie products.
- **Strength:** Strong — multiple examples from direct experience

### Claim 7: Bezos as founder could take risks Andy Jassy as hired CEO cannot
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** CFO budget story — Bezos told CFO "how much is that? I might want to spend it." Jassy must answer to board. Bezos emotionally supportive; Jassy scrutinising.
- **Strength:** Moderate — personal experience, may reflect Evans' relationship more than objective truth

### Claim 8: High growth companies are career escalators
- **Who:** Ethan Evans
- **Evidence:** Amazon grew 100x (10K to 1M employees) during his tenure. Revenue grew 80x. "The escalator went up and I rode it."
- **Strength:** Strong — widely supported principle

## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### Story 1: The Apple Letter
- Young Evans wrote a physical letter to Apple asking how to work there
- Apple said they only hired from 5 universities
- He went to Carnegie Mellon specifically to fulfil this dream
- Never actually joined Apple — went to startups instead
- **Illustrates:** Intentional career planning from a young age, but plans change

### Story 2: Getting Fired as a Layoff of One
- Second startup: Evans led engineering, sales VP was promising features they hadn't built
- Evans fought the sales VP over honesty — didn't realise it was "promise or go bankrupt"
- Company called it a layoff but Evans was the only one laid off
- "I'd call that being fired"
- **Illustrates:** Being right doesn't protect you if you fight the wrong battle

### Story 3: The Interviewer's Wake-Up Call
- After being fired twice, Evans struggled to find work post-9/11
- An interviewer said: "If you were that valuable, they would have found a way to keep you"
- This broke through Evans' self-narrative and forced self-examination
- Led to deep study of workplace psychology
- **Illustrates:** External feedback can shatter self-serving narratives

### Story 4: The TiVo Partnership Gamble
- A project came up that Evans' management advised against
- Evans told his SVP: "Unless you order me not to do this, I'm going to do it"
- The project succeeded — became a key line in his promotion case
- "Without Ethan, we wouldn't have had TiVo"
- **Illustrates:** Calculated risk-taking is essential for rapid advancement

### Story 5: The Polite Threat for Director Promotion
- Evans told his VP: "My career is very important to me, and I need to know if it's as important to Amazon"
- Never explicitly threatened to leave, but the implication was clear
- VP was leaving — promoting Evans would secure the team
- Promotion went through
- **Illustrates:** You can raise any topic if you find civil wording

### Story 6: The VP Promoting the Squeaky Wheel
- Evans admits as VP, when faced with two equal candidates, he promoted the one threatening to leave
- "I'd like to tell you I always did the right thing... I can't actually tell you that's true"
- "Almost everyone's doing what I'm doing. They're just not telling you."
- **Illustrates:** The uncomfortable truth that advocacy beats patience

### Story 7: The Broken Promise to a Star Engineer
- Star engineer made a deal: ship the product, get promoted SDE1 to SDE2
- Evans (new to Amazon) didn't understand the promotion cycle process
- Missed the window — engineer waited, then left
- Evans: "It was my ignorance" — has written about it publicly
- **Illustrates:** Know the promotion process, verify your manager does too

### Story 8: Twitch Integration — Influence Without Authority
- Evans gave up his 800-person team to advise Twitch CEO Emmett Shear
- Had no authority — was advisor to a founder 15 years younger
- Twitch team initially saw no reason to pursue profitability ("Amazon has money")
- Evans had to bridge Twitch culture and Amazon/Jassy's profitability demands
- **Illustrates:** Soft skills as the ultimate career tool

### Story 9: Bezos vs. the CFO
- CFO told Bezos "we only have so much money in the bank" to slow spending
- Bezos replied: "How much is that? Because I might want to spend it."
- **Illustrates:** Founder mentality vs. steward mentality

### Story 10: Narrative Spinning — Same Facts, Two Stories
- Evans demonstrates how a manager can describe the same engineer's code review work as either "upleveling the whole team" or "nitpicking and not contributing real code"
- Same data (700 pull requests), opposite conclusions
- **Illustrates:** Performance measurement in software is never objective

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### The Magic Loop
- Partnership with your manager: "I'll do everything you need. You make sure I'm rewarded."
- Not transactional in a crude sense — built over 2.5 years of trust
- Manager eventually stakes their reputation on your promotion

### Strategically Annoying
- Not abrasive or confrontational — but not compliant either
- Have an opinion, fight for it, but fight well
- The sweet spot between pushover and bully

### Career Ladder vs. Career Escalator
- Choose high-growth companies — the company's growth lifts you
- Evans: "I could climb, but it was also moving up for me"
- Amazon grew 100x during his tenure

### The Preemptive Narrative Strike
- In manager-report conflicts, the person who tells HR/leadership their story first wins
- Managers have structural advantage: higher level, more tenure, tell the story before the report knows it's happening

### Hitting Goals vs. Creating Value
- Big companies confuse interim metric achievement with meaningful progress
- "Zombie products" — nobody wants to shut them down (admission of failure) but nobody knows how to make them big

### The Chickens and Pigs Principle (adapted)
- In your promotion, you're the pig (committed) and your manager is the chicken (involved)
- Your life is on the line — so YOU must know the promotion process, deadlines, requirements
- Don't trust your manager to track it — verify
