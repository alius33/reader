# Analysis: Revolution — Peter Ackroyd

## Classification & Context

- **Book type:** Narrative social and political history — structured chronologically across 32 thematic chapters covering 1688-1815, with frequent cultural and literary excursions
- **Audience:** General readers interested in the long eighteenth century; accessible, anecdotal, personality-driven
- **Ackroyd's approach:** Impressionistic and panoramic — weaves political events, social change, literary culture, and economic transformation into a continuous prose narrative. Heavy use of contemporary quotation (Defoe, Swift, Pope, Johnson, Horace Walpole). Less interested in causal argument than in evoking atmosphere and texture of the period. Each chapter reads almost as a self-contained essay on a theme
- **Scope:** England (and after 1707 Great Britain) from William III's accession through Waterloo. Covers the Glorious Revolution settlement, the War of the Spanish Succession, Walpole's ascendancy, the Seven Years War, the loss of America, the French Revolution, and the Napoleonic Wars — all interwoven with chapters on the agricultural revolution, industrialism, consumer culture, Methodism, the novel, the arts, gin, and the condition of the working poor
- **Key sources discussed:** Defoe's Tour and Robinson Crusoe, Swift's Gulliver's Travels, Pope, Gay's Beggar's Opera, Hogarth's prints, Johnson's Dictionary, Boswell, Adam Smith's Wealth of Nations, Burke's Reflections, Paine's Rights of Man, Wordsworth and Coleridge's Lyrical Ballads. Ackroyd uses literary works as primary evidence for the mood of the age

---

## Central Thesis

Ackroyd does not advance a single thesis but traces two interlocking revolutions across the long eighteenth century. The first is political: the transfer of sovereignty from Crown to Parliament, beginning with the Bill of Rights in 1689 and confirmed through the crises of the American and French revolutions. The second is economic and social: the transformation of England from an agrarian society into the world's first industrial and commercial power, driven by enclosure, the Bank of England, colonial trade, the steam engine, and the factory system.

The overarching narrative arc follows England's rise from a second-rank European power under William III to global maritime and commercial supremacy after Waterloo — achieved through perpetual war against France (fifty-eight years of conflict between 1689 and 1815), the creation of a fiscal-military state, and the unleashing of industrial capitalism. Along the way, Ackroyd shows how the "age of reason" was also an age of gin, mob violence, child labour, slavery, and the Gordon Riots — that the polished surface of Georgian civilization concealed depths of suffering and exploitation.

The book ends on a deliberately ambivalent note: victory at Waterloo left Britain the world's foremost power, but "weariness and hardship were as ever part of daily life" and it was "not at all clear, in 1815, that such problems could be resolved."

## Key Frameworks

1. **The fiscal-military state** — England's transformation through perpetual war against France, with the Bank of England, national debt, and parliamentary taxation creating a war machine that consumed 83% of public revenue
2. **The culture of commerce** — trade as national religion, from Defoe's paeans to the invisible hand of Adam Smith; the merchant class displacing the landed aristocracy as the engine of national life
3. **The transfer of sovereignty** — from divine right through parliamentary supremacy to the question of popular sovereignty raised by Wilkes, the Americans, and the French Revolution
4. **The dual revolution** — political revolution (1688, 1776, 1789) running in parallel with industrial revolution (enclosure, steam, factory system) — both transforming England irreversibly
5. **Satire as the voice of the age** — Pope, Swift, Gay, Hogarth as the true chroniclers of a society that preferred ridicule to earnest argument

---

## Key Principles

- The Glorious Revolution was engineered by an "organized elite" who retained power for 200 years
- The financial revolution (Bank of England, national debt) changed politics as much as any military victory
- The long war against France (1689-1815) was the driving force of state formation, taxation, and national identity
- Consumer culture, fashion, and emulation were as powerful as any political ideology in shaping eighteenth-century society
- The Industrial Revolution was not a sudden event but an incremental, evolutionary process spanning a century
- England avoided revolution because of pragmatism, religious pluralism, the myth of English liberty, and the conservative instincts of its people

## Stories Worth Preserving (12)

1. **William III at the Boyne (1690)** — grazed by a cannonball the day before battle, steadied himself and said "There is no harm done, but the bullet came quite near enough." James II watched from a distance, then fled to France — earning the Irish nickname "Seamus a' chaca" (James the Shit)
2. **Marlborough's march to Blenheim (1704)** — 20,000 men across 250 miles in six weeks, absorbing 20,000 more along the route, concealing his intentions from allies as well as enemies. Wrote to his wife: "within the memory of man there had been no victory so great as this"
3. **The South Sea Bubble (1720)** — £100 of government stock worth £1,050 at peak; schemes advertised for making quicksilver malleable, importing jackasses from Spain, and "a wheel of perpetual motion." A ballad captured the crash: "Hubble Bubble; all is broke"
4. **Sacheverell's trial (1710)** — a fiery Anglican cleric whose prosecution by the Whigs backfired spectacularly, provoking riots, bonfires, and a Tory landslide — demonstrating that the London mob was instinctively loyalist
5. **Walpole and the Excise Crisis (1733)** — the master politician misjudged the mood; his effigy burned in markets, cockades worn with "Liberty, property, and no excise." At supper he told colleagues: "this dance it will no farther go"
6. **The Beggar's Opera (1729)** — Gay's musical ran 62 nights when most plays lasted six; Walpole attended the first night and with "characteristic sang-froid applauded the references to himself, even asking for an encore"
7. **Wolfe at Quebec (1759)** — scaled the Heights of Abraham like "a thief in the night"; both he and the French commander Montcalm were killed. Voltaire derided the war as a fight over "a few acres of snow"
8. **Wilkes and Number 45 (1763-1769)** — the cross-eyed radical turned his seditious libel trial into a cause celebre; 45 became a national craze — forty-five diners, forty-five gills of wine, a sirloin weighing precisely 45 pounds
9. **The Boston Tea Party (1773)** — Bostonians disguised as Mohock Indians gave a "war whoop" then systematically destroyed 342 chests of tea; Lord North's response with the "intolerable Acts" turned protest into revolution
10. **The Gordon Riots (1780)** — the worst mob violence of the century; Newgate stormed and burned, the Bank of England threatened, Bedlam nearly emptied. Blake was caught up in the rush. Edward Gibbon observed "a dark and diabolical fanaticism which I had supposed to be extinct"
11. **Pitt and Fox's final confrontation** — at Trafalgar's celebration dinner, Pitt was toasted as "saviour of Europe"; he replied: "England has saved herself by her exertions, and will, as I trust, save Europe by her example." He died three months later
12. **Waterloo (1815)** — Wellington called it "the nearest run thing you ever saw in your life." Napoleon's Imperial Guard wavered and the cry went up: "La garde recule. Sauve qui peut." Wellington waved his hat to signal the general advance

## Critique & Cross-Reference

- **Strengths:** Ackroyd's panoramic sweep and ear for quotation create a vivid texture; the interweaving of political, cultural, and economic history is genuinely illuminating; the chapters on gin, consumer culture, and the factory system are outstanding
- **Weaknesses:** No sustained argument or thesis; chronology sometimes jumps confusingly within chapters; Ireland and Scotland receive limited attention; little analysis of why events happened — more atmosphere than explanation. Military campaigns are often treated cursorily
- **Compared to [[A History of Britain Vol 2 - Simon Schama]]:** Schama is more dramatic and thesis-driven; Ackroyd more panoramic and discursive. Schama focuses on political narrative; Ackroyd gives equal weight to culture, industry, and social conditions
- **Compared to [[A History of Britain Vol 3 - Simon Schama]]:** Schama covers some of the same ground (Industrial Revolution, empire, reform) with more emotional intensity and political argument
- **Compared to [[The English and Their History - Robert Tombs]]:** Tombs is more analytical, more interested in what made England distinctive; Ackroyd is more literary and impressionistic
- **Compared to [[Tudors - Peter Ackroyd]]:** Same panoramic style and anecdotal method; Revolution covers a longer period with even more cultural and economic material
