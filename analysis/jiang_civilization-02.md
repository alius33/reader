# Religion and the Dawn of Society

## Core Question

Has religion always been fundamental to being human — and if so, what role does it play in creating society itself?

Prof. Jiang frames it as three sub-questions about Ice Age cave paintings: (1) How were they painted? (2) Why were they painted? (3) What do they represent? But the lecture's real driving question is larger: he argues that religion is not an invention of civilisation but a defining feature of humanity itself — present for at least 30,000-40,000 years — and that without religion, there could be no society.

## Lecture Type

Single-topic deep dive with theory construction — builds a comprehensive argument from physical evidence (cave paintings) through theological reconstruction (animism) to philosophical synthesis (Kant's construction of reality, Durkheim's definition of religion as collective consciousness). Unlike Lecture 1's theory-evaluation format, this lecture constructs a positive case step by step.

## Central Thesis

Religion is not a product of civilisation — it is what makes civilisation (and humanity itself) possible. The evidence from Ice Age cave paintings going back 30,000-40,000 years proves that religious belief preceded agriculture, cities, and even permanent settlement by tens of thousands of years.

The first religion was **animism**: the belief that every living thing has a soul, all are interconnected through the Mother Goddess, and the world operates as a cycle requiring balance and harmony. Cave paintings were not art — they were religious expression, part of rituals (with music, dancing, and possibly psychedelics) that maintained the cosmic balance.

Prof. Jiang's deepest claim is philosophical: drawing on Immanuel Kant, he argues that humans do not see reality — they imagine it. Religion was the first system by which humans collectively imagined reality. Emile Durkheim completed this argument: religion is "a system of ideas by which men imagine the Society of which they are members." Without religion, there is no collective consciousness; without collective consciousness, there is no society; without society, there is no humanity. Therefore: humans are, first and foremost, religious animals.

The chain of reasoning:
1. Ice Age cave paintings prove religion existed 30,000-40,000 years ago
2. The paintings were part of religious rituals (location = best acoustics, instruments found)
3. The religion they expressed was animism — universal interconnection, Mother Goddess, cyclical balance
4. Symbols in the paintings suggest proto-written language, mythology, and possibly psychedelic visions
5. Kant: reality is constructed by the mind, not passively received
6. Durkheim: religion is collective reality-construction — the foundation of society itself
7. Therefore: religion is what makes us fundamentally human — not economics (Marx) or biology (Darwin)

## Argument Structure

### Main Argument: Religion Has Always Been There

This is not a theory-evaluation lecture. Prof. Jiang builds a single cumulative case:

**Claim:** Religion is not a late development in human history — it has been present since at least 30,000-40,000 years ago and is the defining characteristic of humanity.

**Evidence chain:**
- Cave paintings found worldwide dating back 30,000-40,000 years
- Paintings located in sections with best acoustics → part of rituals with music
- Musical instruments (flutes) found alongside paintings
- Paintings depict animals not realistically but imaginatively → expressing a worldview, not documenting nature
- Recurring symbols found worldwide → suggests shared symbolic/religious language
- Disabled individuals (dwarfs) given equal food, elaborate burials → valued as shamans who communicate with the spirit world
- Parallels with Göbekli Tepe from Lecture 1 (both are periodic religious gathering places)

**Philosophical grounding:**
- Kant: reality is imagined/constructed, not passively seen (time and space are inventions of the mind)
- Neuroscience confirms Kant: the brain projects reality
- Durkheim: religion is "a system of ideas by which men imagine the Society of which they are members"
- From religion came philosophy; from philosophy came science. Science is our modern religion.

**Verdict:** We are first and foremost religious animals. Economics (Marx) and biology (Darwin) matter but are secondary. The interplay between religion, economics, and biology drives human history.

### Sub-Argument: The Content of the First Religion (Animism)

**Claim:** The first religion was animism — the belief that every living thing has a soul, all are interconnected, and the world operates as a balanced cycle.

**Evidence for:**
- Cave paintings show animals depicted with respect and wonder, not domination
- Shamans dressed as animals (human-animal hybrid figures in paintings)
- Mother Goddess appears as bird-like being herding animals between worlds
- Disabled people treated as special/sacred → different = closer to the spirit world
- Trees communicate through fungal networks (modern science confirming the interconnectedness animism posited)
- Found in indigenous religions worldwide (North America, South America, Australia) and in Buddhism
- Consistent with Çatalhöyük evidence from Lecture 1 (mother goddess, sky burials, harmony with nature)

**Theological reconstruction (Prof. Jiang's first-principles thought experiment):**
- Things that amaze early humans: childbirth, stars, healing, nature
- Childbirth → womb is a portal from the spirit world to our world
- Death/burial → portal back to the spirit world (symmetry: both involve darkness)
- Stars → souls of the dead or other worlds
- Disease → soul and body out of alignment (parallels Chinese medicine)
- Cave → natural womb, portal between worlds
- Killing animals → must be compensated by summoning them back from the spirit world
- Mother Goddess gives life to everything → all creatures are equal (humans, animals, trees)
- Each creature has a function in maintaining balance → killing is permitted within the cycle
- Violation of the cycle (incest, killing without sacrifice) causes things to go wrong

### Sub-Argument: Gender Equality in Pre-History

**Claim:** For most of human history, women were either equal to or more powerful than men.

**Evidence:**
- Childbirth is mysterious and sacred → women are sacred and divine
- The god is a Mother Goddess, female → women are more special than men
- Only women can give birth → men cannot perform the most sacred act
- Male dominance is a recent historical development

**Promise:** How men came to dominate women will be explained in the next lecture (or future lectures).

### Sub-Argument: Symbols as Proto-Language and Mythology

**Claim:** The symbols found in cave paintings represent something beyond simple pictures — they are an early form of written language or sacred encoding.

**Three theories presented (not evaluated — all considered possible):**
1. **Visualising ideas:** Some concepts cannot be drawn (energy, love, cycle, balance, harmony) → symbols represent abstract ideas → each painting is a mythology/story, not just a picture
2. **Language of the spirit world:** Psychedelics produce geometric visions → shamans accessed the spirit world through drugs and saw symbols → symbols are the language of the gods
3. **Sacred mystery:** Symbols make the content mysterious → mystery makes things sacred and divine → symbols create a boundary between profane and sacred knowledge

**Source:** Genevieve von Petzinger, Canadian anthropologist, documented recurring symbols in Ice Age cave paintings worldwide (hand, spiral, quadrangle, circle, asterisk).

### Sub-Argument: Humans as Religious Animals (vs. Economic or Biological)

**Claim:** The dominant modern theories of human motivation — Marx's economic animal and Darwin's biological animal — are incomplete. Humans are primarily religious animals.

**Marx's position:** Humans are driven by the need for money. School → university → job. Economic motivation.
**Darwin's position (evolutionary biology):** Humans are driven by the need to spread genes. Men want many partners; women want loyal partners (high cost of childbearing).
**Prof. Jiang's position:** Humans are driven first by the need to understand why they are here and to connect with others. Religion serves this need. But economics and biology also matter — all three interplay throughout history. When religion fails to meet economic/biological needs, it must change or be abandoned. When economic needs are met but religion is absent, something is still missing.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

- **Animism:** Probably the first religion. Belief that every living thing (tree, mosquito, person) has a soul, all are interconnected, and balance/harmony must be maintained. Found in indigenous religions worldwide and in Buddhism. Introduced in this lecture.
- **Mother Goddess:** The force that gives life to everything. Appears as a bird (sky = mother goddess's domain). All creatures are her children and therefore equal. Introduced in Lecture 1 (Çatalhöyük), expanded here.
- **Portal (womb/cave/burial):** Womb = portal from spirit world to our world. Burial = portal back. Cave = natural portal (resembles a womb). Mountaintops and rivers are also portals. Early settlements formed around these portals.
- **Shamans:** People who communicate with the spirit world. Often physically different (disabled, dwarfs) — difference = special, favoured by the Mother Goddess. Dressed as animals. May have used psychedelics. Created cave paintings. Introduced in Lecture 1, expanded here.
- **Immanuel Kant's constructivism:** Reality is not passively seen but actively imagined/constructed by the mind. Time and space do not exist in nature — they are inventions of the mind. Described as "the greatest philosopher who ever lived." Neuroscience has confirmed this — the brain projects reality. Psychedelics change brain structure → different reality.
- **Emile Durkheim's sociology of religion:** Founder of sociology. Religion is "a system of ideas by which men imagine the Society of which they are members." Religion creates collective consciousness → collective consciousness creates society. Without religion, no society; without society, no religion. From religion came philosophy, then science. Science is our modern religion.
- **Religious animal (vs. economic/biological):** Prof. Jiang's thesis for the series. Humans are driven primarily by the need to understand why they are here and to connect with others. Marx's economic animal and Darwin's biological animal are secondary. All three interplay to drive history.
- **Cyclical worldview:** Early humans understood life as a cycle (born → die → reborn), not a hierarchy. No power pyramid — "a river, not a pyramid." The Mother Goddess is not the "greatest god" hierarchically — she is the giver of life in our world, but other gods may give life in other worlds. Monotheism and hierarchy are recent innovations.

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### Ice Age Cave Paintings (30,000-40,000 years ago, worldwide)
- Found in caves across Europe and the world — Chauvet Cave (~30,000 ya), Lascaux (~20,000 ya, France), Altamira (34,000 BCE, Spain)
- Different people at different times painted in the same caves — continuous process over millennia
- Animals depicted: bisons, rhinoceros, lions, horses, gazelles — nature as one interconnected picture, no single focus
- NOT realistic depictions — imaginative representations of the natural world
- Painted using red (ochre clay) and black (charcoal)
- Caves were dark → used animal fat lamps for lighting
- Crucially: paintings found where acoustics were BEST in the cave → part of rituals including music (flutes found)
- Interpretation: these were periodic religious festivals, like Göbekli Tepe — hunter-gatherers came together for ritual celebrations with music, dancing, and viewing the paintings
- Pablo Picasso, invited into one of these caves, said: "We learned nothing in 10,000 years" — artistically, humans have not improved since the Ice Age

### The Dwarf Skeleton (Paleolithic burial)
- A skeleton from ~10,000 years ago identified as a dwarf
- As a hunter-gatherer, a dwarf cannot contribute much to hunting/survival
- In theory, the community should have discarded, killed, or abandoned this person given the harsh conditions
- DNA and bone analysis showed this person ate the same quality and quantity of food as everyone else — no discrimination
- Granted an elaborate burial at end of life — highly valued by the community
- Best explanation: the dwarf was a shaman — physical difference = special, favoured by the Mother Goddess, able to communicate with the spirit world
- Cited from David Graeber and David Wengrow, *The Dawn of Everything*: "Neither is [this] an isolated case. When archaeologists undertake balanced appraisals of hunter-gatherer burials from the Palaeolithic, they find high frequencies of health-related disabilities" and "high levels of care until the time of death. Sometimes their funerals were remarkably lavish."
- These disabled individuals were prioritised by their communities — likely shamans who could access the spirit world

### Human Migration Out of Africa (300,000 ya - 20,000 ya)
- Humans originated in Africa ~300,000 years ago
- ~100,000 years ago, extreme cold caused scarcity of resources → forced migration
- Africa → Europe → Asia → across the Bering Strait (ice bridge) → North America → South America
- From Asia → Australia
- Only ~1 million humans worldwide 20,000 years ago
- Encountered other human species (Neanderthals, Denisovans ["the necess" in transcript]) — interbred with them until Homo sapiens became dominant

### Trees Communicating Through Fungal Networks (modern science)
- Recent discovery: trees in forests are connected through underground fungal networks (mycorrhizal networks)
- Trees share nutrients — if one tree lacks water, others send nutrients to it
- Trees warn each other of threats — if pests attack one tree, it communicates to others, which begin preparing defences
- Trees recognise their own offspring — mother trees send nutrients preferentially to their children
- The forest functions as "one big brain or one big living organism"
- Prof. Jiang's connection: if you lived back then, immersed in nature daily, you could sense this interconnectedness — this lived experience of nature's communication may be the empirical basis for animism

### Shamans as Animal-Human Hybrids (cave painting evidence)
- Cave paintings show figures that are clearly human in body shape but dressed/depicted as animals
- Two bird-like beings depicted herding gazelles — interpreted as the Mother Goddess (bird = sky = mother goddess)
- Shamans dressed as birds to channel the energy of the Mother Goddess and control animals
- Shamans also dressed as other animals to communicate with those species and with the spirit world
- Multiple clear examples in cave paintings across different sites

### Genevieve von Petzinger's Symbol Research
- Canadian anthropologist who travelled the world documenting symbols in Ice Age cave paintings
- Found recurring symbols across geographically distant sites: hand, spiral, quadrangle, circle, asterisk
- This recurrence suggests either: a shared proto-language, a common psychedelic experience producing similar visions, or a universal sacred encoding system
- Implies cave paintings are not just pictures but mythologies — stories with abstract ideas that cannot be visually drawn

## Summarisation Notes

### Recommended Structure

Follow the professor's argument flow — it builds cumulatively and the payoff (Kant → Durkheim → "religious animal") depends on the groundwork laid earlier:
