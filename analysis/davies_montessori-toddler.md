# The Montessori Toddler — Simone Davies
## Classification & Context
Practical guide / Montessori philosophy for the home. Davies is an AMI (Association Montessori Internationale) certified Montessori teacher who runs Jacaranda Tree Montessori in Amsterdam — parent-toddler classes helping families apply Montessori at home. The book translates Montessori classroom principles into accessible, illustrated guidance for parents of 1-3 year olds. Illustrated by Hiyoko Imai with warm, inviting drawings throughout.
## Central Thesis
Toddlers are misunderstood humans. They are not "terrible twos" — they are curious, capable, innocent, and driven to learn. The Montessori approach treats the child as their own person on their own unique path, with the parent as a gentle guide rather than a controller. The method rests on three pillars: (1) a carefully prepared environment that invites independence, (2) activities matched to the child's developmental needs and sensitive periods, and (3) a respectful adult-child relationship that uses neither threats, bribes, nor punishments, but instead sets clear limits with kindness. When these three elements align, toddlers thrive — and the chaos that most parents associate with this age diminishes dramatically.
## Key Frameworks
- The Montessori Triangle: Child ↔ Adult ↔ Environment (all three interact dynamically)
- Prepared Environment: spaces set up for the child's independence — accessible, beautiful, uncluttered, with everything at their height
- Sensitive Periods: windows of intense interest (language, movement, order, small objects, toileting) during which learning is effortless
- The Absorbent Mind: children under 6 take in everything without effort, like a sponge
- Maximum Effort: the toddler's drive to challenge themselves physically — carry heavy objects, climb, push limits
- Crisis of Self-Affirmation: the developmental phase (18 months–3 years) when children realize they are separate beings and begin saying "no" — not defiance, but identity formation
- Five Ingredients for Curiosity: trust in the child, rich learning environment, time, safe/secure base, fostering wonder
- Seven Principles for Curious Humans: follow the child, encourage hands-on learning, include the child in daily life, go slow, help me to help myself, encourage creativity, observation
- Allow All Feelings But Not All Behaviour: emotions are always welcome; some behaviours need limits
- Setting Limits Without Threats/Bribes/Punishments: be clear on the limit, follow through with kind/clear action, acknowledge negative feelings, deal with tantrums, reconnect, help them make amends
## Best Stories
1. The repairman shocked by the under-2-year-old who independently changed clothes, handled laundry, and walked off — demonstrating toddler capability
2. The "let's change the way we see toddlers" reframe — what looks like stubbornness is sense of order, what looks like slowness is exploration, what looks like a tantrum is a child feeling safe enough to release emotions
3. Davies hearing a radio interview about time-outs being harmful but the interview ending without alternatives — sparking her lifelong mission to find another way
4. The home tours at the end — real Montessori families from Australia, Mongolia, Canada, and the USA showing how they adapted the approach
