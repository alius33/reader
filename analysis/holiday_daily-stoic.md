# The Daily Stoic — Ryan Holiday
## Classification & Context
Daily devotional / curated anthology. Holiday is a modern populariser of Stoicism who previously wrote The Obstacle Is the Way and Ego Is the Enemy. This book distills the three major Stoic thinkers — Marcus Aurelius, Seneca, and Epictetus — into 366 daily meditations, one per day, each with a translated quote and a short interpretation for modern life.
## Central Thesis
Stoic philosophy is not an academic exercise — it is a daily operating system. Each day presents a specific challenge to perception, action, or will, and the ancient Stoics left instructions for all of them. The book organises the year into twelve monthly themes: January (Clarity), February (Passions & Desires), March (Awareness), April (Unbiased Thought), May (Right Action), June (Problem Solving), July (Duty), August (Pragmatism), September (Fortitude & Resilience), October (Virtue & Kindness), November (Acceptance), December (Meditation on Mortality).
## Key Frameworks
- The Three Disciplines (Epictetus): Desire (what you want), Action (what you do), Assent (what you accept as true)
- The Dichotomy of Control as daily habit — morning check-in on what is and isn't in your power
- Premeditatio Malorum (morning negative visualisation) as recurring practice
- Memento Mori as December's culminating theme
- Amor Fati — accepting and embracing fate as a daily attitude
## Best Stories
1. Seneca practising voluntary poverty for a week each month to reduce fear of losing wealth
2. Epictetus's lesson on the broken cup — practise loss on small things before large ones
3. Marcus Aurelius writing Meditations during the Marcomannic Wars while surrounded by death
4. Cato walking barefoot and underdressed through Rome to train indifference to public opinion
5. Seneca's evening review — each night, interrogate yourself: what weakness did I resist? What virtue did I gain?
## Cross-References
- Meditations - Marcus Aurelius (the primary source for roughly a third of the daily entries)
- Discourses - Epictetus (the most practical and systematic source Holiday draws from)
- Man's Search for Meaning - Frankl (Frankl's logotherapy echoes the Stoic discipline of desire)
