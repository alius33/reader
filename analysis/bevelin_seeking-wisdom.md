# Seeking Wisdom — Peter Bevelin

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Compilation of mental models and thinking tools drawn from Charles Darwin, Charlie Munger, and multiple scientific disciplines. Structured as a reference manual for clear thinking rather than a linear argument.
**Author:** Peter Bevelin is a Swedish businessman and thinker deeply influenced by Warren Buffett and Charlie Munger. He spent years distilling Munger's approach to multidisciplinary thinking into a single practical volume.

## Central Thesis
Most bad decisions come from a small number of predictable psychological misjudgements and a failure to think across disciplines. By studying how evolution shaped our brains, how psychology produces systematic errors, and how mathematics and physics describe reality, you can build a latticework of mental models that dramatically improves judgement. The book is organised as Munger's toolkit made explicit: here are the biases, here are the models, here is how to combine them.

## Key Frameworks
- Munger's 25 Causes of Human Misjudgement (reward/punishment super-response, liking/disliking tendency, doubt-avoidance, inconsistency-avoidance, reciprocation, social proof, contrast-misreaction, authority-misinfluence, deprival super-reaction, envy/jealousy, etc.)
- Evolutionary biology of behaviour (why our brains are wired for Stone Age survival, not modern decision-making)
- Mathematics of decision-making (probability, base rates, regression to the mean, compounding)
- Physics and engineering analogies (feedback loops, redundancy, critical mass, equilibrium)
- The Two-Track Analysis: what are the relevant factors, and what are the psychological biases likely distorting my thinking?
- Checklist approach: systematically run through models before deciding

## Best Stories
1. Munger's "Psychology of Human Misjudgement" speech at Harvard — the origin of the 25 biases framework
2. Darwin's method of deliberately writing down every argument against his own ideas
3. Pavlov's dogs and how reward/punishment conditioning shapes human behaviour without our awareness
4. Coca-Cola thought experiment — Munger reverse-engineers how to build a trillion-dollar brand using mental models
5. The surgeon who washes hands vs the one who doesn't — base rates and evidence over authority
