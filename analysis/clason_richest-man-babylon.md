# The Richest Man in Babylon — George S. Clason

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Collection of financial parables set in ancient Babylon. Originally published as a series of pamphlets distributed by banks and insurance companies in the 1920s, then compiled into a book. Each chapter is a self-contained story teaching one or two principles of wealth-building.
**Author:** George Samuel Clason was an American businessman and writer. He founded the Clason Map Company and wrote these parables to teach ordinary people the basics of financial management through storytelling.

## Central Thesis
The principles of building wealth are timeless and simple — so simple they can be taught through stories set 4,000 years ago. Wealth comes not from earning more but from keeping what you earn, making it multiply, and protecting it from loss. The man who saves at least one-tenth of his earnings, invests wisely under guidance from those who know, and lives below his means will inevitably become wealthy. Poverty is not a lack of income — it is a lack of discipline.

## Key Frameworks
- Seven Cures for a Lean Purse: (1) Pay yourself first — save 10%, (2) Control expenditures, (3) Multiply savings through investment, (4) Guard against loss, (5) Own your dwelling, (6) Ensure future income, (7) Increase your ability to earn
- Five Laws of Gold: Gold comes to those who save it, invest it wisely, seek expert counsel, avoid unfamiliar schemes, and refuse get-rich-quick temptations
- The Debt Repayment Plan: Arkad's formula — 70% to live on, 20% to repay debts, 10% to save — even when in debt, you save first

## Best Stories
1. Arkad (the richest man in Babylon) explaining to childhood friends Bansir and Kobbi why he's rich and they're not — despite equal starting points
2. Dabasir the camel trader — a man drowning in debt who claws back through discipline and the 70/20/10 formula
3. The walls of Babylon — how protection of what you already have is as important as earning more
4. Sharru Nada and the slave who worked his way to freedom through initiative and savings
