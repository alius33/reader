## Main Arguments

### 1. Project Taste > Intelligence
- Carey repeatedly argues that intelligence is necessary but insufficient for career success
- The differentiator is "project taste" — picking projects with big business impact where gaps exist
- Brilliant L4 at Google X illustrates: 200+ IQ, still L4 due to poor project taste, communication, collaboration

### 2. Imposter Syndrome as Career-Limiting Force
- Kept him at Symantec years too long despite unhappiness
- Recurred at Google — fear of high expectations at senior levels prevented him from tackling big problems
- At Lyft — Waymo rejected him internally because of domain mismatch at senior level
- Needed external pull (someone inviting him) to overcome it each time

### 3. Outcomes > Activity
- Focus on company/customer outcomes, not your own technical interests
- Understand how stakeholders measure success, speak to their metrics
- Present business impact to leaders, not algorithmic details
- Generalises this to the future of software engineering

### 4. The Future of Software Engineering is Product Thinking
- Two scenarios: (a) LLMs stay good-but-not-AGI → engineers still needed to supervise, (b) AGI programming → role shifts to understanding customer outcomes
- In AGI world, the best "engineers" will be domain experts who understand customer pain points
- Jobs To Be Done / Outcome-Driven Innovation as repeatable methodology
- Most product managers are incompetent because they think in features, not customer pain points

### 5. Communication is a Career Multiplier
- People associate good communication with intelligence
- The Stuxnet talk at Google X example — people thought he was brilliant based on presentation skill
- Practice is the only path — speaking deteriorates without use

### 6. Collaboration > Being Right
- At Lyft, the architecture transformation succeeded through influence, not authority
- He had no robotics credentials — had to work WITH roboticists, not tell them what to do
- Let them take ownership — but this backfired on credit attribution

## Key Stories (8)

### 1. First Intern at Peter Norton Group (1992)
- No desk, worked in QA lab with a former knife salesman
- QA manager had no formal CS training — early industry was the wild west
- Rose from first intern to most senior engineer (Fellow) over 21 years

### 2. Polymorphic Virus Detection (Master's Thesis)
- Self-mutating malware with quadrillions of variants
- Teams wrote handwritten assembly to detect each variant — took 6 months per virus
- Next day, 3 new variants made all work obsolete
- Carey identified the gap, built algorithmic detection, transferred to product

### 3. Assembly to C Rewrite
- First antivirus engines written in assembly for speed
- Carey ported to C with proper algorithms (hash tables/binary search vs linear search over 60K signatures)
- Result: less code AND 5x faster — algorithms beat hand-optimised assembly

### 4. The Brilliant L4 at Google X
- Over 200 IQ, astoundingly brilliant in conversation
- Still at L4 because: poor communication, worked on personally interesting but low-impact projects, didn't collaborate well
- Illustrates that intelligence alone doesn't drive career success

### 5. Chronicle/Backstory at Google X
- Stealth "Project Lantern" — started from zero, 6 months figuring out what to build
- Competing products: customers waited 30 minutes for a single query
- Chronicle indexed petabytes, returned results in 2 seconds
- Spun out as Alphabet company (the "C" in Alphabet), then reacquired by Google Cloud

### 6. Lyft AV Architecture Transformation
- Classic robotics stack: hand-coded algorithms for each manoeuvre, 100+ tweaked parameters
- Carey designed neural network-based end-to-end architecture with safety guardrails
- Had to influence roboticists who didn't want to change and didn't respect his non-robotics background
- Someone else took credit — their manager claimed they "initiated" the project

### 7. UCLA Lecturer Call (2001)
- Applied on a whim, heard nothing for months
- Two weeks before fall quarter: frantic call, lecturer bailed
- Had two weeks to plan entire curriculum
- 25 years later, still teaching part-time

### 8. Stuxnet Deep-Dive
- Multi-platform malware (Windows + microcontrollers for centrifuges)
- Used 6 zero-day vulnerabilities to spread
- Stealthed itself in file browsers AND in controller logic
- Would remove itself when logic was downloaded for inspection, reinsert when uploaded back
- Attribution: Israeli and American governments (coding style markers)

## Frameworks & Concepts

| Framework | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Project Taste** | Ability to identify high-impact projects where gaps exist — the primary career differentiator |
| **Outcomes Focus** | Understand stakeholder/customer metrics of success; speak to outcomes, not technology |
| **Intelligence Baseline** | Need baseline intelligence, but beyond that threshold, other skills matter far more |
| **The BS Tax** | At senior levels, strategic meetings, posturing, and politics consume time; must actively resist |
| **External Pull** | Overcoming imposter syndrome often requires someone else pulling you into an opportunity |
| **Jobs To Be Done / Outcome-Driven Innovation** | Repeatable methodology for understanding customer pain points vs touchy-feely product intuition |
| **AGI Programming Threshold** | The dividing line: can LLMs handle arbitrarily complex projects? If yes, role shifts to product thinking |
| **Greedy Breadth-First Search** | Career advice: explore broadly early (internships), go deep once you find passion |
| **Communication-Intelligence Heuristic** | People equate presentation skill with intelligence — a career multiplier |
| **Credit Attribution Trap** | At senior levels, influence work is invisible; must actively document and claim contributions |
