# Psychohistory (The Science of Imagining the Future)

## Core Question

Can the future be scientifically predicted — and if so, can humanity use that predictive power to build a better world?

Prof. Jiang frames this final lecture as a message of hope after eleven lectures of increasingly dark predictions (Trump's election, war with Iran, collapse of the American empire, multipolar chaos, mass death). He introduces **psychohistory** — a concept from Isaac Asimov's *Foundation* series — as the idea that human behaviour follows mathematical patterns over civilisational timescales, and that if those patterns can be modelled, the future can be predicted and potentially redirected. He then proposes that modern AI (specifically supervised machine learning) could be the tool to operationalise psychohistory, while identifying the fundamental challenges: clean data, working algorithms, edge cases (the Great Man problem), and the need for 50-100 years of validation.

The deeper argument beneath the surface: the principles discovered throughout both semesters — the human need for structure, meaning, and purpose; the dynamics of elite overproduction; the pattern of imperial collapse — are not just historical observations but potential inputs for a mathematical model of human civilisation.

## Lecture Type

Series finale — synthesis and forward projection. The lecture synthesises themes from both semesters (the great books course on Homer, Dante, Plato, the Bible, and the geopolitics course on Iran, empire, Russia, Trump) into a single unifying framework. It is part explanation of AI/machine learning, part intellectual manifesto, and part farewell address to the students.

## Structural Map

1. **Opening — message of hope** (0:01-1:30): Prof. Jiang declares this is the final class. Recaps both semesters: last semester = great books (Iliad, Odyssey, Bible, Republic, Divine Comedy); this semester = geopolitics (how dark and hopeless the world is). Invokes Dante's Divine Comedy: "The future is not what happens to you. It's what you imagine and fight for." States the thesis: God gave us imagination and the capacity to love, and these will guide us to a better world.

2. **Semester summary and predictions recap** (1:30-3:00): Summarises the semester's predictions: (a) Trump will be elected again in November; (b) Trump will declare war on Iran; (c) the war will be a disaster for the US; (d) it will mean the end of the American empire; (e) a multipolar world will emerge; (f) this means endless war and millions/billions of deaths; (g) climate change will eventually cause civilisational collapse. Acknowledges this is an "extremely hopeless and extremely bleak" picture, but says "where there is darkness, there can also be light."

3. **Introduction of psychohistory — Asimov's Foundation** (3:00-6:00): Defines psychohistory: the idea that the future can be predicted, and if predicted, controlled for humanity's benefit. Source: Isaac Asimov's *Foundation* series (1950s). The premise: set a million years in the future, a galactic empire is collapsing, which will lead to 30,000 years of barbarism. A new science called psychohistory mathematically maps human behaviour over a million years, discovers patterns, enables prediction, and allows manipulation of events to shorten the dark age. Notes there is now a TV adaptation ("it's terrible").

4. **Cliodynamics — Peter Turchin** (6:00-8:00): Introduces **Peter Turchin** [transcription: "Peter Turkin"], a real historian who founded **Cliodynamics** (Clio = goddess of history + dynamics = movement). Turchin attempts to create mathematical models of historical patterns. His key discovery: **the overproduction of the elites** as the primary cause of societal collapse.

5. **The elite overproduction theory** (8:00-10:00): Detailed exposition. Society produces increasing numbers of elites over time — wealthy people, famous people, entrepreneurs. Problem: wealth and fame are infinite resources, but **power is a finite, zero-sum resource**. Too many elites competing for limited power leads to internal struggle and social collapse. Chinese example: the imperial examination system (keju) produced too many candidates for too few official positions. The failed candidates became angry and led revolutions. Most famous example: **Hong Xiuquan** — a failed keju candidate who became a Christian and led the **Taiping Rebellion**. Also why the Roman Empire collapsed. Prof. Jiang claims this was discovered through mathematical modelling of historical data.

6. **AI as the path to real psychohistory** (10:01-15:00): Proposes that AI can take Turchin's work further — not just analysing the past but predicting the future. Explains what AI actually is: **supervised machine learning** (the technical term, not "AI" which is the popular term). Explains the traditional programming model (algorithm + input = output) vs. supervised machine learning (use output to refine the algorithm). Detailed example: facial recognition technology — creating a topological mathematical model of faces based on measurements (distance between eyes, nose size, etc.), building a database of 1 billion faces, then iteratively refining the model until it can match faces with precision. Key insight: "if you're not in this database, you can't be recognised."

7. **Three requirements for any AI system** (15:56-20:00): Prof. Jiang identifies three things you must solve to create any AI: (a) **Clear metrics/outputs** — you need a mathematically definable output (e.g. maximum profit per flight for an airline); (b) **Clean data** — data must be labelled and objective, not subjective ("I can't make an AI that says this is the best ice cream in the world because I can't find data on the best ice cream"); (c) **A working algorithm structure** — the computer can refine and optimise the algorithm but cannot create it from scratch. If you can solve all three, you can use AI. If not, the problem is unsolvable by AI.

8. **What AI can and cannot solve** (20:00-25:00): Easy problems: facial recognition, translation software, recommendation engines (Netflix). Impossible problem: **self-driving cars** — because of the **edge case** of a human who intentionally wants to crash into the car (e.g. a taxi driver whose livelihood is threatened). "AI is a scam. It does not exist. What exists is supervised machine learning." AI has no self-awareness; it can only do what the algorithm tells it to do. AI is "an extremely limited tool." Cars with self-driving features are not 100% self-driving — they tell you not to sleep in the car.

9. **Applying AI to geopolitical prediction** (25:00-28:00): Links back to the semester's predictions. The model: lobby + Saudi Arabia + American empire + petrodollar → forces pushing America to war with Iran. No counteracting forces: military believes in shock and awe, Iran wants revenge, Trump wants to cement power. This prediction can be turned into an AI model. The model is refined by testing against every historical war: the 415 BCE Athenian invasion of Sicily, 1941 Operation Barbarossa, hundreds of others. The model self-refines with each test. Multiple models (war prediction, Trump election prediction, etc.) can be combined to eventually create psychohistory.

10. **Game theory as underlying framework** (28:00-28:55): Reaffirms game theory analysis as foundational: no good and evil, only players who are self-interested and develop strategies to optimise their chances of winning. But game theory alone is insufficient — new ideas are needed.

11. **The European elections — right-wing shift** (28:55-33:00): Uses the June 2024 European Parliament elections as a case study. Results: Marine Le Pen's party gained votes in France; AfD (Alternative for Germany) gained votes in Germany. Macron called early elections; Le Pen might govern France within three months. Three reasons for the shift: (a) **Anti-immigration** — Muslim immigrants threatening Christian social fabric over past 10-20 years; (b) **Anti-federalism/anti-bureaucratism** — Brussels mandating rules felt anti-democratic; (c) **Anti-pan-Europeanism** — no European identity exists; only German, French, Italian, Spanish identities. The EU project is dead — Prof. Jiang bets it will collapse within five years. The fundamental driver: people want **structure, meaning, and purpose** in their lives — love, create, and grow. The EU's secular liberalism destroys local identity, community, and relationships. This connects to Brexit, Trump, and worldwide movements. The human urge for **agency**.

12. **The human heart as the fundamental principle** (33:33-37:00): Student (Eric) asks what the source of all this is. Prof. Jiang: **the human heart** — "this is just who we are." Homer and Dante would agree. If we cannot fulfil our human heart, "we will rebel, we will destroy, we will want to kill." This becomes a principle for the AI model: **there is a fundamental underlying structure to human society.** If society conforms to the structure of the human heart → society prospers (eudaimonia, as in Athens). If society represses the human heart → collapse is inevitable.

13. **Synchronicity — social cohesion** (35:00-37:00): Introduces **synchronicity** as a measurable concept: how willing are people to follow rules? Do drivers follow traffic rules? Do people give up subway seats for the elderly? Do people pick up garbage? Synchronicity = cohesion and trust = resilience and capacity for growth. High synchronicity societies: Japan, Germany. Low synchronicity societies: India, Brazil, China. This becomes another input variable for the AI model.

14. **Mass society and the artificial elite** (37:04-40:00): Student (Saleen/Celine) asks a question. Prof. Jiang: **mass society** is "a complete deviation from human history" — an accident. Mass society requires an elite, but the elite are no better than anyone else ("we in this classroom could swap for the leadership of the world — nothing would change"). The elite create fictions to maintain their power: credentials (Yale, Harvard), IQ, hereditary lineage. These fictions eventually fall apart due to incompetence — visible in global management of the economy, COVID, and war. The elite then resort to **suppression** — using force to enforce obedience. To control people, they must repress the human heart by redirecting attention from thinking to consuming. This repression can last a long time but eventually fails.

15. **Modelling the human heart** (40:23-42:50): Student (Ali) asks if the model analyses beliefs, wants, and needs. Prof. Jiang: yes, the human heart has to become a mathematical model, like the facial recognition model. Student (Sally) asks: aren't individuals unique? Don't people change? Prof. Jiang: Homer and Dante say no — at the fundamental level, all humans share the same desires. Everyone wants structure, meaning, and purpose. Everyone seeks to love, create, and grow. When you achieve all three = **eudaimonia** — the Greek concept of a happy and self-fulfilled life. Methods differ, but fundamental motivations are universal.

16. **Implementation timeline and challenges** (42:52-47:00): Student (Sally) asks how to construct this AI. Prof. Jiang: it requires **50-100 years** minimum. Reasons: (a) predictions must be validated over decades — "we won't know if my predictions are fully correct until 50 years from now"; (b) you must constantly make predictions and test them; (c) you must **rewrite history** — "most of history is actually not correct." The AI model must challenge the entire discipline of history and reconstruct more factually correct accounts. Example: "You're telling me that this illiterate guy named Jesus was able to spread Christianity all by himself after he died — I don't believe that. A much easier explanation is that it became co-opted by the Roman Empire to control the Empire." The project requires mathematicians, computer programmers, and historians collaborating together. The potential payoff: "if this project actually works, it would change the fate of humanity forever" — because humanity repeats the same mistakes due to inaccurate history.

17. **Edge cases and the Great Man Theory** (47:30-53:00): Student (Eric) asks the best question of the lecture: will this model encounter edge cases? Prof. Jiang: yes — the **Great Man Theory**. Underlying historical forces control humanity, but occasionally a great individual appears who steps outside of history and directs it. Examples: Homer, Dante, Plato, Jesus, Julius Caesar, Octavius (Augustus), Putin. "You cannot ever predict these people." Asimov's solution in *Foundation*: the **Second Foundation** — a team of specialists who observe the AI and correct it for edge cases. In the novel, these specialists are **telepaths** — they can read and control minds. Prof. Jiang semi-seriously proposes that great men of history might actually be telepaths: Putin, for example — "Why is Putin able to do what he does? He has almost some telepathic abilities. He can read other people's minds. He can control other people's minds." Homer and Dante had "God-given gifts" or were "speaking for God." The model cannot account for these individuals; only human observers can correct for them in real time.

18. **Modelling love, creation, and growth** (53:39-56:50): Student (Celine) asks how to model love, learn, and create. Prof. Jiang identifies three variables to model: (a) **Agency/freedom** — people need autonomy to pursue what they want, and what they want is to love, create, and grow; (b) **Social interaction** — love, creation, and growth all require others ("you can't love yourself, you can't create by yourself, you can't learn by yourself"); (c) **Compassion** — if people are united by necessity (survival on an island), they become compassionate; if incentivised by competition (bank bonuses), they hate each other. "We have all these theories. It's just like no one's turning them into a mathematical model for society."

19. **Understanding human behaviour — social science vs. literature** (56:50-60:00): Student (Eric) asks if it is possible to completely understand human behaviour. Prof. Jiang identifies two approaches: (a) **Social science** — economics, psychology, anthropology, sociology try to figure out mathematically what drives us; (b) **Literature** — some truths can only be expressed through literature, not mathematics. Example: the scene in the Iliad where Priam sneaks into Achilles' tent and kisses his hand — "you can never mathematically express it, but think about how much truth and power is in that one scene." Homer and Dante's mission: to express human truths "in the most beautiful way." Dante is explicit: what drives us is **love and the willingness to imagine**. This must be the underlying truth of any AI model.

20. **Why societies destroy themselves — love/hate duality** (60:00-61:20): Student (Celine) asks: if humans want to love, create, and grow, why do we destroy ourselves? Prof. Jiang: love and hate are **one force** — "if I can't love you, I'm going to hate you." Creation and destruction are one force — "if I can't create, then I want to destroy." A society that nurtures love and creation (like the Greeks) flourishes. But eventually elites consolidate power, turn everyone into slaves. Slaves cannot love, create, or grow → they rebel → society is destroyed. The cycle: nurture → flourishing → elite consolidation → repression → rebellion → collapse → restart.

21. **Who controls the AI — the democratic imperative** (1:01:24-1:06:00): Student (Sally) asks who would control this AI and how to prevent elite exploitation. Prof. Jiang: the Second Foundation — a group of scholars and experts dedicated to the AI. Critical requirement: the AI must be **open and transparent**. If open, everyone must agree it will be used for good. The AI would present different scenarios for different questions, and society collectively agrees on which scenario to pursue and what action to take. It must be part of a **democratic system** — otherwise, as Sally points out, it will only be used to oppress. Example applications: modelling consequences of US intervention in Ukraine or US attack on Iran. This creates a "powerful argument for correct and proper behaviour." Counterpoint: different nations want different outcomes (America wants hegemony, Russia wants hegemony, China wants hegemony). Prof. Jiang's response: the current structure is unsustainable; population will collapse from 8 billion to 1 billion; when everyone is fighting for survival, humanity will be united in the goal of rebuilding civilisation "in a more progressive, more democratic, and more prosperous way." The AI can only work if it "steps outside of history and human interest" — speaking for all humanity, not one nation.

## Central Thesis

The future can be scientifically predicted using a combination of mathematical modelling, AI (supervised machine learning), and the fundamental principles of human nature that great literature and historical analysis have revealed. Isaac Asimov imagined this as "psychohistory" in his *Foundation* series; Peter Turchin has begun the work through Cliodynamics; and the principles discovered in this course — game theory, elite overproduction, the human heart's need for structure, meaning, and purpose, synchronicity, the love/hate duality, and the cycle of flourishing and collapse — can serve as the foundation for an AI model that predicts civilisational trajectories. The model will take 50-100 years to build, requires open and democratic governance (the "Second Foundation"), and will always face edge cases in the form of great individuals who step outside historical forces. But the project is worth pursuing because it could break humanity's cycle of repeating the same mistakes — and because the alternative (the dark future predicted in this semester's lectures) is too terrible to accept without resistance.

The chain of reasoning:
1. The semester has made dark predictions: Trump's election, war with Iran, end of American empire, multipolar chaos, mass death, climate collapse
2. But the future is not what happens to you — it's what you imagine and fight for (Dante)
3. Asimov imagined psychohistory: mathematically predicting civilisational trajectories
4. Peter Turchin's Cliodynamics has begun this work — discovering patterns like elite overproduction
5. AI (supervised machine learning) could operationalise psychohistory by iteratively refining models against historical data
6. The principles for the model already exist: game theory, elite overproduction, the human heart (structure, meaning, purpose), synchronicity, the love/hate-creation/destruction duality
7. The model requires 50-100 years of validation, collaboration across disciplines, rewriting of inaccurate history, and open democratic governance
8. Edge cases (great men) cannot be predicted by the model — only corrected by human observers (the Second Foundation)
9. This is speculative but worth pursuing because the cost of inaction is civilisational collapse

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: The Future Can Be Predicted Through Pattern Recognition

**The theory:** Human behaviour follows mathematical patterns over civilisational timescales. If these patterns can be modelled, the future can be predicted.

**Evidence for:**
- Asimov's *Foundation* series imagined this as fiction in the 1950s — it resonated because the idea feels intuitively true
- Peter Turchin's Cliodynamics has already discovered real patterns (elite overproduction) through mathematical modelling of historical data
- The predictions made in this course (Trump's election, war with Iran, end of American empire) were derived from pattern recognition — identifying forces and counter-forces
- Historical patterns repeat: the Athenian invasion of Sicily (415 BCE), Operation Barbarossa (1941), and hundreds of other wars share structural similarities
- The keju system / Hong Xiuquan example demonstrates that elite overproduction is a recurring pattern across civilisations

**Evidence against:**
- Edge cases (great men) defy prediction — Homer, Dante, Plato, Jesus, Caesar, Putin
- 50-100 years of validation needed before the model can be trusted
- History itself is inaccurate, so training data is corrupted
- Subjective concepts (the "best" ice cream, the "right" policy) cannot be quantified

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang believes the project is feasible but will take generations. The semester's predictions serve as an early, pre-AI version of psychohistory.

### Argument 2: AI is a Limited but Powerful Tool

**The theory:** AI (supervised machine learning) can be harnessed to build psychohistory, but only if its limitations are understood.

**Evidence for:**
- Facial recognition technology demonstrates that complex patterns can be modelled mathematically
- AI excels at problems with clear metrics, clean data, and working algorithm structures
- Translation software and recommendation engines show AI's pattern-recognition capability
- The geopolitical prediction model (push factors for Iran war) can be formulated as an AI model and refined against historical data

**Evidence against:**
- Self-driving cars demonstrate the edge case problem — a human who intentionally crashes into you cannot be predicted
- "AI is a scam — what exists is supervised machine learning" — AI has no self-awareness
- The computer can refine an algorithm but cannot create one from scratch
- Data must be clean and objective, but history is messy, biased, and often incorrect
- Subjective judgments cannot be turned into data sets

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang demystifies AI while arguing it could still be the tool for psychohistory — if combined with human judgment, literature's insights, and decades of validation.

### Argument 3: The Human Heart is the Fundamental Input Variable

**The theory:** At the deepest level, human behaviour is driven by universal needs — structure, meaning, purpose, love, creation, and growth — and any model of human civilisation must encode these as fundamental variables.

**Evidence for:**
- Homer and Dante agree: the human heart drives everything
- Eudaimonia (Greek concept of flourishing) = achieving love + creation + growth
- The European right-wing shift is driven by people wanting local identity, community, family — not abstract liberalism
- Brexit, Trump, and populism worldwide are expressions of the human heart rebelling against systems that deny it
- When societies nurture these needs (Athens), they flourish; when they repress them, they collapse
- The love/hate duality explains destruction: if love is blocked, hate emerges; if creation is blocked, destruction follows

**Evidence against:**
- Individual variation (Sally's objection) — people are unique and change over time
- The concept may be too abstract to quantify mathematically
- Different cultures may express these needs differently

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang treats the universal human heart as non-negotiable — the fundamental truth on which any model must be built. He uses Homer and Dante as his authorities.

### Argument 4: Society's Collapse Pattern — Elite Consolidation and the Destruction of Human Agency

**The theory:** Societies follow a cycle: flourishing → elite consolidation → repression → rebellion → collapse → restart. This cycle is driven by the elite's need to maintain power by suppressing the human heart.

**Evidence for:**
- Peter Turchin's elite overproduction theory — too many elites competing for limited power creates internal struggle
- The keju system and Hong Xiuquan / Taiping Rebellion
- The Roman Empire's collapse
- Mass society is an accident of history — it requires an artificial elite whose fictions eventually fail
- When fictions fail, elites resort to force; force represses the human heart; repression triggers rebellion
- The global elite's incompetent management of the economy, COVID, and war is causing people to "wake up"
- The elite redirects attention from thinking to consuming — but this cannot suppress the human heart forever
- Current prediction: global population collapse from 8 billion to 1 billion

**Evidence against:**
- Some societies have maintained stability for centuries (Japan, China's dynastic cycle included long stable periods)
- The specific mechanism linking "too many elites" to collapse needs more rigorous evidence
- The 8 billion to 1 billion prediction is extreme and unsupported

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang presents this as the pattern that psychohistory must model and that great literature has already identified.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### Psychohistory
- **Definition:** The science of mathematically predicting civilisational trajectories — predicting the future based on mathematical patterns in human behaviour over long timescales
- **Origin:** Isaac Asimov's *Foundation* series (1950s science fiction)
- **How it works:** Map human behaviour mathematically → discover patterns → predict future events → manipulate events for better outcomes
- **What it explains:** Why the dark predictions of this course could be avoided if humanity builds the tools to anticipate and redirect
- **Connected to:** Cliodynamics (Turchin's real-world attempt), game theory (method used in this course)

### Cliodynamics
- **Definition:** The mathematical study of historical dynamics — attempting to identify mathematical patterns in the rise and fall of civilisations
- **Named for:** Clio (Greek goddess of history) + dynamics (movement)
- **Founder:** Peter Turchin
- **Key discovery:** The overproduction of the elites as a primary driver of societal collapse
- **Connected to:** Lecture 6 of the Civilization series (elite overproduction and the Bronze Age collapse)

### The Overproduction of the Elites
- **Definition:** As societies mature, they produce increasing numbers of elites (wealthy, famous, entrepreneurial). But power is a finite, zero-sum resource. Too many elites competing for limited power creates internal struggle → collapse.
- **Key example:** The Chinese imperial examination system (keju) producing more candidates than positions. Failed candidates (Hong Xiuquan) led revolutions.
- **Also explains:** The fall of the Roman Empire
- **Connected to:** Lecture 6 of the Civilization series (explicitly about elite overproduction)

### Supervised Machine Learning
- **Definition:** The correct technical term for what is popularly called "AI." Instead of writing an algorithm and feeding it input, you use the output to iteratively refine the algorithm.
- **Three requirements:** (a) clear metrics/outputs, (b) clean data, (c) a working algorithm structure
- **Key limitation:** Cannot create an algorithm from scratch, only refine one. Cannot handle edge cases (self-driving cars and intentional human interference).
- **Prof. Jiang's verdict:** "AI is a scam. What exists is supervised machine learning."

### The Human Heart (Structure, Meaning, Purpose)
- **Definition:** The fundamental human needs that drive all behaviour: structure, meaning, and purpose in life — expressed as the capacity to love, create, and grow
- **Source:** Homer, Dante, Greek philosophy (eudaimonia)
- **Principle:** If society conforms to the human heart → eudaimonia / flourishing. If society represses the human heart → inevitable collapse.
- **Measurable through:** Agency/freedom, social interaction, compassion

### Synchronicity
- **Definition:** The degree to which people in a society voluntarily follow rules and norms — a measure of social cohesion and trust
- **Examples of indicators:** Traffic rule compliance, giving up subway seats, picking up garbage
- **High synchronicity:** Japan, Germany → resilient, capable of growth
- **Low synchronicity:** India, Brazil, China → less resilient
- **Model input:** Societies with higher synchronicity will outperform over the long term

### The Great Man Theory (as Edge Case)
- **Definition:** Occasionally, individuals appear who step outside historical forces and redirect history. These are the "edge cases" that no mathematical model can predict.
- **Examples:** Homer, Dante, Plato, Jesus, Julius Caesar, Augustus/Octavius, Putin
- **Solution (from Asimov):** The Second Foundation — a team of human observers who correct the model in real time
- **Prof. Jiang's speculation:** Great men may actually be "telepaths" — possessing an ability to read and control minds that transcends normal human capacity

### The Second Foundation
- **Definition:** A team of scholars, experts, and observers who monitor the psychohistory AI model and correct it for edge cases and unpredictable events
- **Origin:** Asimov's *Foundation* series
- **Requirements:** Must be open and transparent; must serve all humanity, not one nation; must be part of a democratic system
- **Prof. Jiang's requirement:** The AI and its governance must "step outside of history and human interest"

### The Love/Hate Duality
- **Definition:** Love and hate are one force; creation and destruction are one force. If positive expression is blocked, negative expression inevitably emerges.
- **How it works:** Society nurtures love/creation → flourishing. Elite consolidation blocks love/creation → hate/destruction → rebellion → collapse.
- **Connected to:** The entire cycle of civilisational rise and fall

### Eudaimonia
- **Definition:** The Greek concept of a happy, self-fulfilled life — achieved when a person is able to love, create, and grow
- **Connected to:** Civilization lecture series (Athens as the model of eudaimonia), the human heart principle
- **How it fits the model:** Eudaimonia is the output variable — the measure of whether a society is conforming to the human heart
