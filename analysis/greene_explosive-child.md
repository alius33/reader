## Central Thesis

Ross Greene argues that behaviourally challenging children are not manipulative, attention-seeking, coercive, or unmotivated. They are **lacking the skills** of flexibility, frustration tolerance, and problem solving that most people take for granted. The central philosophy is captured in five words: **"Kids do well if they can."** Challenging behaviour occurs when the demands placed on a child exceed the skills he has to respond adaptively. The solution is not more punishment, firmer limits, or better reward systems — it is **Collaborative & Proactive Solutions (Plan B)**: identifying the specific unsolved problems that predictably trigger challenging episodes, then solving those problems together with the child through a structured three-step process (Empathy, Define the Problem, Invitation). This replaces the conventional imposition of adult will (Plan A) with collaborative problem-solving that simultaneously resolves the triggering problems, teaches the lagging skills, and rebuilds the parent-child relationship.

## Structural Map

The book is organized in 12 chapters plus supplementary material, following a progressive logic:

- **Chapter 1 — The Waffle Episode:** Opens with a vivid scene of Jennifer's family to illustrate the cycle of explosive behaviour and parental helplessness. Establishes running family narratives (Jennifer/Debbie/Kevin; Frankie/Sandra).
- **Chapter 2 — Kids Do Well If They Can:** The philosophical foundation. Contrasts "kids do well if they can" with "kids do well if they want to." Reframes challenging behaviour as a developmental delay in skills rather than a motivational deficit.
- **Chapter 3 — Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems:** The diagnostic framework. Lists specific cognitive skills that behaviourally challenging kids lack (transition-making, problem-solving, emotion regulation, flexibility, social cognition). Introduces "unsolved problems" — the specific, predictable situations that trigger episodes.
- **Chapter 4 — Getting Started:** The Assessment of Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems (ALSUP). Four guidelines for identifying unsolved problems. Extended examples with Jennifer, Frankie, Zach, Mitchell, and Ana.
- **Chapter 5 — The Truth About Consequences:** Why conventional reward-and-punishment programs fail for these children. The two things consequences do well (teach right from wrong, provide incentive) and why neither is the bottleneck.
- **Chapter 6 — The Three Plans:** Introduces Plan A (unilateral imposition of adult will), Plan B (collaborative problem-solving), and Plan C (strategically setting aside expectations). Clarifies that the plans are not a ranking system.
- **Chapter 7 — Plan B:** Deep dive into the three ingredients: Empathy step (gathering the child's concern), Define the Problem step (articulating the adult's concern), Invitation step (brainstorming realistic, mutually satisfactory solutions). Proactive vs. Emergency Plan B. Five drilling strategies.
- **Chapter 8 — Trouble in Paradise:** Troubleshooting guide for common Plan B failures. Covers stuck Empathy steps, skipped ingredients, imposed solutions disguised as collaboration, exhaustion, and defensive children.
- **Chapter 9 — Got Questions?:** Extended Q&A addressing accountability, safety, medication, real-world readiness, non-verbal children, and how Plan B teaches skills indirectly. Includes adaptations for children with severe language delays.
- **Chapter 10 — Family Matters:** Sibling dynamics, maladaptive communication patterns (speculation, overgeneralization, perfectionism, sarcasm, one-upmanship), grandparents, and partner disagreements.
- **Chapter 11 — The Dinosaur in the Building:** Application to schools. Critiques zero-tolerance policies and school discipline rubrics. Covers student-teacher, student-student, and parent-teacher Plan B.
- **Chapter 12 — Better:** Closing reflection. Defines "better" as incremental progress. Final updates on Jennifer and Frankie's families.

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Chapter 1 — The Waffle Episode
Jennifer (11) erupts over waffles, terrorising her brother Riley and mother Debbie. Sets up the core family narrative. Jennifer has received multiple diagnoses (ODD, bipolar, intermittent explosive disorder), been medicated, had sticker charts, time-outs — none have worked. Introduces Sandra and Frankie as a parallel narrative: single mother, severe aggression, multiple hospitalisations.

### Chapter 2 — Kids Do Well If They Can
The philosophical reframe. Challenging kids lack skills in flexibility, adaptability, frustration tolerance, and problem-solving. They're challenging only sometimes — specifically when demands exceed their skills. Those situations (unsolved problems) are predictable, not random. The primary strategy will be problem-solving, not rewarding/punishing. Debunks conventional labels: attention-seeking, manipulative, unmotivated, bad choices, bad attitude, button-pushing.

### Chapter 3 — Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems
Detailed walk-through of specific lagging skills: difficulty handling transitions, considering solutions, expressing concerns in words, managing emotions, black-and-white thinking, deviating from routine, handling unpredictability, shifting from original plans, social cognition deficits. Complete list of ~20 lagging skills. Introduces the concept that unsolved problems (the when) are more important than lagging skills (the why) for reducing episodes.

### Chapter 4 — Getting Started
The ALSUP tool. Four guidelines: (1) Start with "difficulty," (2) Split don't clump, (3) Keep theories out, (4) Keep solutions out. Extended worked examples for Jennifer, Frankie, Zach (autism spectrum, 3 years old), Mitchell (15, Tourette's, bright but struggling), and Ana (7, intense, perfectionistic, anxious). Prioritising: pick top 3 problems, put the rest on Plan C.

### Chapter 5 — The Truth About Consequences
Deconstructs the conventional assumption that challenging behaviour is learned and can be unlearned through better discipline. Consequences do two things: teach right/wrong and provide incentive. These kids already know right from wrong and are already motivated. The problem is lagging skills, which consequences can't teach. Extended narrative of how Jennifer's reward/punishment program backfired dramatically.

### Chapter 6 — The Three Plans
Plan A: Unilateral imposition of adult will. Heightens likelihood of episodes. Uninformed solutions. Plan B: Collaborative problem-solving. Three steps. Proactive preferred over Emergency. Plan C: Strategically setting aside expectations. Not capitulating — prioritising. "Giving in" is starting with Plan A and capitulating. Plan C is intentional.

### Chapter 7 — Plan B
Three steps detailed: (1) Empathy step — "I've noticed that..." + "What's up?" Then drilling (not grilling) using five strategies: reflective listening, who/what/where/when questions, conditions analysis, asking what the child is thinking, breaking the problem into components. (2) Define the Problem — "My concern is..." Two categories: how it affects the child, how it affects others. (3) Invitation — "I wonder if there's a way..." Give kid first crack. Solution must be realistic AND mutually satisfactory. Full worked example with Ana's homework.

### Chapter 8 — Trouble in Paradise
Systematic troubleshooting: haven't tried Plan B yet; relying on Emergency instead of Proactive; using Plan B as last resort; old lenses still on; entering Empathy with preconceptions; preordained solutions; unrealistic/unsatisfactory solutions; skipping ingredients; stuck on "I don't know"; difficulty drilling; not believing the child; discouraged when child doesn't care about your concern; child has no ideas; child blows up at Plan B initiation; too exhausted to try.

### Chapter 9 — Got Questions?
Accountability through Plan B is real accountability. Plan B sets limits. Safety issues best handled proactively. Medication discussion: stimulants for inattention, SSRIs for irritability, atypical antipsychotics for extreme reactivity — conservative approach recommended. Plan B teaches skills indirectly. Extended section on adapting Plan B for non-verbal or language-delayed children using picture cards and problem-solving binders (Zach's story).

### Chapter 10 — Family Matters
Siblings: protect them, explain the challenging child's difficulties, "fair does not mean equal," use Plan B as facilitator between siblings. Communication patterns: speculation, overgeneralization, perfectionism, sarcasm, put-downs, catastrophising, interrupting, lecturing, dwelling on past. Extended Mitchell family therapy scene. Grandparents. Spouse disagreements. Single parents.

### Chapter 11 — The Dinosaur in the Building
School discipline programs are the "dinosaur." Zero-tolerance has made things worse. Key components for school implementation: Awareness, Urgency, Mentality ("kids do well if they can"), Expertise, Abandoning Blame, Time, Assessment tools (ALSUP), Practice/Coaching, Communication, Continuity, Perseverance. Examples of student-teacher, student-student, and group Plan B. Parent-teacher collaboration.

### Chapter 12 — Better
"Better" is defined as incremental progress. Final narrative updates: Sandra reports Frankie is in partial hospitalisation, participating in Plan B, his bedroom door isn't closed all the time. Debbie reports Jennifer is talking, letting herself be hugged (with advance warning), but still rigid. Closes with a parent's email about how the approach transformed his 12-year-old daughter in 20 months.

## Key Frameworks

1. **"Kids Do Well If They Can"** — The foundational philosophy replacing "kids do well if they want to." Children already know right from wrong and are already motivated. The problem is skills, not will.
2. **Lagging Skills** — The specific cognitive capacities that are underdeveloped: transition-making, emotion regulation, flexibility, problem-solving, language processing, social cognition, etc.
3. **Unsolved Problems** — The specific, predictable situations where lagging skills meet demands that exceed capacity. Written with "Difficulty..." format.
4. **The ALSUP** — Assessment of Lagging Skills and Unsolved Problems. The diagnostic instrument. Four guidelines for wording problems.
5. **The Three Plans** — Plan A (impose will), Plan B (collaborate), Plan C (set aside). Not a ranking system but three distinct responses to unsolved problems.
6. **Plan B Three Steps** — (1) Empathy step: gather child's concern. (2) Define the Problem: articulate adult's concern. (3) Invitation: brainstorm realistic, mutually satisfactory solutions.
7. **Proactive vs. Emergency Plan B** — Proactive (solve before it erupts) is far superior to Emergency (solve in the heat).
8. **Five Drilling Strategies** — Reflective listening, who/what/where/when, conditions analysis, what are you thinking, break into components.
9. **Inflexibility + Inflexibility = Meltdown** — The equation showing that adult rigidity compounds child rigidity.
10. **Three General Solution Categories** — Ask for help, meet halfway, do it a different way.

## Key Principles

1. **Challenging behaviour is a learning disability** — a developmental delay in flexibility, frustration tolerance, and problem-solving, not a character flaw
2. **Unsolved problems are predictable** — the same 5-12 problems trigger episodes every day/week; they are not random
3. **Rewards and punishments don't teach lagging skills** — they were never designed to, and they often make things worse
4. **Plan A (imposition of adult will) is the primary cause of challenging episodes** — when you "rewind the tape," an adult using Plan A is almost always found
5. **You don't lose authority by using Plan B** — you gain a problem-solving partner and set limits more effectively
6. **Fair does not mean equal** — different children need different approaches
7. **Solutions must be realistic AND mutually satisfactory** — the first solution often needs refinement
8. **Problem-solving is incremental** — rarely a one-shot deal; good solutions are refined versions of earlier ones
9. **The child's concerns are legitimate** — hunger, fatigue, fear, desire, avoidance of incompetence are all real concerns
10. **It takes two to tango** — the adult's response is half the equation

## Best Stories

1. **The Waffle Episode** (Ch 1) — Jennifer counts six frozen waffles, plans three today and three tomorrow. When her brother Riley wants waffles, she erupts, pushes her mother aside, seizes the container, and stalks to her room. A seemingly absurd trigger that illustrates the real pattern: inflexibility meeting an unexpected change.

2. **Frankie Hits Sandra** (Ch 8-9) — Sandra tells Frankie the in-home therapist is coming. He gets angry. She escalates by telling him he'll get her fired. He punches her in the mouth and disappears overnight. Returns the next day, sees her lip, becomes remorseful. Reveals to the therapist that he hates school, feels out of control, has been smoking marijuana, and is scared. Had never communicated any of this.

3. **The Mitchell Family Therapy Scene** (Ch 10) — A fifteen-year-old and his attorney father engage in rapid-fire sarcasm, one-upmanship, and speculation. The therapist names each pattern in real-time. Mitchell — the "explosive child" — is the first to identify his father's sarcasm correctly, and grins. The family dynamics are immediately illuminated.

4. **Ana's Homework Drilling** (Ch 7) — Through persistent drilling, parents discover Ana's science homework is hard not because she's lazy but because she writes so slowly that she forgets what she wanted to say by the time she gets the words down. She thinks she's stupid. The real problem was invisible until someone asked.

5. **Frankie and Ms. Brennan** (Ch 10) — The inpatient social worker facilitates a conversation between Frankie and Sandra. Frankie reveals he feels like a "speddie," wants to be normal, can't do math and writing, and gets suspended because teachers embarrass him. Sandra cries; Frankie says "I knew you couldn't do it" — but lets her stay. First real conversation in years.

6. **Jennifer's TV Schedule Solution** (Ch 8-9) — After a brief, aborted first Plan B attempt, Jennifer spontaneously comes into the kitchen the next day with a solution: a TV schedule so she and Riley don't fight. She even has a backup plan (recording her shows). Debbie gives her a hug; Jennifer pushes her away — but with the slightest hint of a smile.

7. **Rickey and George Washington Carver** (Ch 11) — A teacher uses Emergency Plan B when Rickey refuses to do a social studies worksheet. Discovers the real issue is spelling frustration. Rickey suggests DeJuan (not Darren) as a helper because "he won't rag on me." A five-minute intervention that prevents a daily crisis.

8. **Chris and the Parking Lot** (Ch 9) — Plan B applied to a safety issue. Chris doesn't want to hold hands because "that's for babies." Solution: he'll hold Mom's belt loop instead. A durable, realistic, mutually satisfactory solution that a child helped create.

## Critique

**Strengths:**
- The reframe from "won't" to "can't" is genuinely paradigm-shifting for most parents
- Running narratives (Jennifer/Debbie/Kevin, Frankie/Sandra) make the theory emotionally real
- The ALSUP tool is immediately actionable and well-structured
- Plan B is clearly defined with concrete steps, drilling strategies, and extensive examples
- The troubleshooting chapter (Ch 8) anticipates and addresses virtually every objection
- Adaptations for non-verbal children prevent the approach from seeming limited to articulate kids
- Critique of school discipline is timely and evidence-based

**Weaknesses:**
- The book is highly repetitive — the same core ideas are restated in nearly every chapter
- Almost all examples follow the same conversational format, which can feel monotonous
- The fictional narrative dialogue, while helpful, sometimes feels stilted and overly neat
- Limited discussion of cultural contexts where the approach may need significant adaptation
- The book underplays the difficulty of the emotional work required from parents who were themselves raised with Plan A
- Could benefit from more empirical data and outcome studies

## Summarisation Notes

### Tone and Voice
Greene writes in an accessible, conversational tone with frequent direct address ("you," "your child"). The running family narratives provide emotional grounding. He is passionate about debunking conventional wisdom and occasionally repetitive in driving home key points. The extended dialogue examples are a hallmark of the book.

### Structure Recommendations
- Lead with the Waffle Episode as hook, then the philosophical reframe
- Group lagging skills and unsolved problems as a single diagnostic section
- Give Plan B (the three steps) the most space — it's the operational heart
- Use the family narratives selectively (the most powerful moments)
- Troubleshooting and school application can be condensed
- Include the drilling strategies with examples — they're the most practically useful element

### Key Vocabulary to Preserve
- Kids do well if they can, lagging skills, unsolved problems, ALSUP, Plan A, Plan B, Plan C, Empathy step, Define the Problem step, Invitation step, drilling (not grilling), realistic and mutually satisfactory, Proactive Plan B, Emergency Plan B, inflexibility + inflexibility = meltdown

### Length Target
600-1000 lines. This is a single-framework book with clear structure. The content is practical rather than conceptually dense, so the summary should prioritise the actionable elements (ALSUP, Plan B steps, drilling strategies) over extensive theoretical discussion.

### Special Considerations
- The running family narratives are important for emotional resonance — include key moments from Jennifer and Frankie's stories
- The dialogue examples are central to understanding how Plan B actually works in practice
- The school chapter contains important material but can be summarised more concisely
- The book is the 5th edition (2014) and incorporates decades of refinement
