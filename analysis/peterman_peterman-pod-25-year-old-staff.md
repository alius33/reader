## Podcast Type

**Interview** — dual-perspective format. Ryan Peterman (host, ex-Instagram Staff Eng) interviews Evan King (guest, ex-Meta Staff Eng turned startup founder), but uniquely both share their parallel promotion stories at each career stage. This creates a richer dataset than a standard one-way interview.

## Core Topic

How two engineers grew from new grad to Staff (IC6) at Meta in roughly three years each — the specific decisions, behaviours, luck, and relationships that enabled that trajectory — followed by Evan's post-Meta startup journey and the lessons from leaving Big Tech.

## Host

**Ryan Peterman** — ex-Instagram Staff Engineer (IC6), now content creator and manager at Meta. Grew from new grad to Staff at Instagram. Transitioned to management (TLM → org leader). Provides his own parallel career stories throughout.

## Guest

**Evan King** — ex-Meta Staff Engineer (IC6) at age 25. Grew from IC3 to IC6 in three years on Content Integrity (terrorism, graphic violence, real-time integrity). Left Meta to co-found startups, including Hello Interview (current company). Cornell grad, founded the Cornell Hacking Club.

## Structural Map

### 1. Origins & Education (~0:00–~10:00)
- Both share how they got into CS — neither was a prodigy
- Evan: AP CS in Seattle, felt behind peers with Microsoft parents; Cornell, switched from soccer to hacking club
- Ryan: didn't know CS existed until UCLA, started as EE, switched to CS junior year
- **Key theme:** You don't need to be a prodigy to succeed in industry

### 2. Side Projects & Preparation (~10:00–~18:00)
- Evan: founded Cornell Hacking Club (200 attendees), CTF competitions, always loved teaching
- Ryan: built occupancy sensor for lab (Raspberry Pi + Slack bot)
- **Key insight:** Build side projects that solve real problems around you, not Redis clones

### 3. Interviewing & Luck (~18:00–~28:00)
- Evan: LeetCode was hard, got lucky — skimmed the exact problem night before Meta on-campus interview
- Ryan: ground LeetCode all summer at Bloomberg internship, failed every onsite except Amazon (SAT-style puzzle test)
- Ryan's Palantir heartbreak — thought he crushed it, was "released" in first group, heartbroken
- **Key theme:** Luck is a massive factor — one lucky night of studying changed Evan's entire trajectory

### 4. Team Selection at Meta — Boot Camp (~28:00–~38:00)
- Evan: expected cybersecurity, found it "stoic and unwelcoming"; discovered Content Integrity (terrorism team), fell in love with the mission
- Ryan: chose Instagram infra despite no headcount — begged manager, showed earnestness, got in
- **Key insight:** The best opportunities are ones you seek out, not the ones that seek you; agency turns NOs into YESes

### 5. The "Penis Story" & Team Culture (~38:00–~42:00)
- Evan's jarring first days: colleague on porn detection team had explicit content filling entire monitor
- Content Integrity teams hidden in corner of Seattle office
- Illustrates: you become desensitised; team culture was vibrant and young despite dark content

### 6. IC3→IC4 (New Grad to Mid-Level) — First Promo (~42:00–~50:00)
- Evan's first promo happened in six months — never asked, didn't expect it, manager pulled him into room
- **Pivotal confidence moment** — "from that moment on I had the assurance that I was good"
- Prior confidence boost: Zillow internship manager called him "best intern he'd ever worked with"
- **Key behaviours:** Inquisitive, not bounded by assigned scope, built fun internal tools (security game at Zillow)

### 7. IC4→IC5 (Mid to Senior) — Tech Lead Growth (~50:00–~68:00)
- Manager gave Evan tech lead of Graphic Violence team (3 people) — even though he didn't know what tech lead meant
- Set up weekly meetings, one-on-ones, road maps — effectively managing without the title
- **Territorial tension:** senior engineer joined, Evan felt threatened, had to "slap himself" and be humble — ended up becoming best professional relationship
- Ryan parallel: tech lead of backend work stream, had an amazing intern who taught him leverage/delegation
- **Key frameworks:** Context is king (be the go-to person); delegation is the gateway to senior+; 1+1=3 when you share scope

### 8. IC5→IC6 (Senior to Staff) — Christchurch & Real-Time Integrity (~68:00–~86:00)
- **Christchurch mosque shooting (March 15, 2019)** — 51 killed on live stream, Facebook missed it for 9.5 minutes
- Evan in war room with VP of Integrity, head of content policy; his manager on paternity leave
- Led creation of Real-Time Integrity team from scratch — greenfield, just him and a PM
- **Golden Set Recall:** built infrastructure to restream past atrocities on isolated Facebook to measure detection — found only 9% recall on live suicides
- **The breakthrough:** everyone focused on pixel/audio ML models; Evan noticed user comments ("don't do it", "your family loves you") were the obvious signal — comments + temporal modelling took recall from 9% to 55% overnight, eventually to mid-90s
- Team grew from 3 to 16 engineers
- Ryan parallel: compute efficiency at Instagram during COVID surge; most impactful optimisation was trivially simple — just eliminating redundant work
- **Key principle:** The simplest, most obvious solution often has the biggest impact; brilliant people with "horse blinders" miss the holistic view

### 9. Considering IC7 vs Management (~86:00–~98:00)
- Evan considered management but opportunity for IC7 was clear; stayed IC
- Ryan chose management despite manager warning IC7 path was more deterministic
- Ryan's reasoning: IC7/8 makes you a "snowflake" — only useful at a handful of companies, handcuffed by equity
- Ryan on TLM (Tech Lead Manager): 70% IC / 30% manager with small team
- **Key insight:** Manager career proportional to recursive reports; IC career more deterministic but less transferable

### 10. Leaving Meta — Startup Journey (~98:00–~140:00)
- Evan's trusted ex-manager/co-founder started messaging about Web3
- Built side project after hours — got 10K users; Evan initially refused to leave until funding
- Eventually: side project more exciting than day job — "I would wake up thinking about when work ended"
- Web3 social intelligence company → 100K users → acquired during market downturn
- Period of trying random ideas (truck factoring, design tools, website-to-Figma converter → acquired)
- Finally: Hello Interview — AI mock interviews failed, in-person mock interviews took off ("do what people are willing to pay for")
- **Key learning:** Left Meta as "idiot" — never set up caching, never wrote frontend, never configured database. Startups give breadth; Big Tech gives depth

### 11. Reflections & Advice (~140:00–end)
- **Biggest regret:** Not slowing down to understand WHY things work — "ship it and run" culture at Meta encouraged this
- **Career hours:** Meta ~50hrs/week with flexibility (snowboard Wednesdays); early startup days: 7am–2am, couldn't leave house without laptop
- **Final advice to younger self:** Invest in relationships outside work — career optimisation at expense of friendships and romantic relationships was the biggest mistake
- Was "underinvested in friendships and romantic relationships" — only realised after moving to LA and making non-tech friends

## Main Arguments & Claims

### 1. You don't need to be a prodigy to succeed in tech
- **Claim:** Neither a CS background from childhood nor natural brilliance is required for rapid career growth
- **Who:** Both Ryan and Evan
- **Evidence:** Personal experience — both started CS late (Evan: felt behind in AP CS; Ryan: didn't know CS existed until college, started as EE)
- **Strength:** Moderate (anecdotal but consistent across two data points)

### 2. Agency and seeking out opportunities is the primary driver of career acceleration
- **Claim:** The best opportunities don't come to you — you must aggressively pursue them
- **Who:** Both, but primarily illustrated through Ryan's team selection story
- **Evidence:** Ryan begged a manager with no headcount; Evan chose an obscure terrorism team over "obvious" cybersecurity
- **Strength:** Moderate (anecdotal, but pattern repeated across multiple career stages)

### 3. The simplest solution often has the biggest impact
- **Claim:** Engineers with deep technical expertise develop "horse blinders" that cause them to miss obvious, high-impact solutions
- **Who:** Evan (primary), Ryan (confirming)
- **Evidence:** Comments-as-signal for detecting live suicides (9% → 55% recall overnight); Ryan's trivial compute efficiency fix
- **Strength:** Strong (quantitative evidence from Meta's production systems)

### 4. Your manager is the single largest factor in career acceleration (beyond your own performance)
- **Claim:** Having a great manager who advocates for you behind the scenes is essential for rapid promotion
- **Who:** Both
- **Evidence:** Evan's manager advocated for Real-Time Integrity team creation, gave him tech lead role early; Ryan's manager was supportive when asked
- **Strength:** Moderate (consistent pattern but survivorship bias)

### 5. Speed of execution creates a "budget" for growth
- **Claim:** Completing assigned work fast creates discretionary time to do high-visibility, career-accelerating work
- **Who:** Evan (primary)
- **Evidence:** Finished projects quickly at Zillow and Meta, used extra time for side projects and broader exploration
- **Strength:** Moderate (anecdotal but widely confirmed pattern)

### 6. Delegation and working through others is the gateway behaviour from senior to staff
- **Claim:** The biggest behavioural shift from IC5→IC6 is learning to scale yourself through others
- **Who:** Both
- **Evidence:** Evan leading 3-person then 16-person team as IC tech lead; Ryan's intern experience
- **Strength:** Moderate (consistent with known industry patterns)

### 7. Startups teach breadth that Big Tech cannot
- **Claim:** Leaving Big Tech for startups produces dramatically faster skill growth despite lower compensation
- **Who:** Evan
- **Evidence:** Had never set up caching, databases, or written frontend at Meta; forced to learn everything at startup
- **Strength:** Moderate (single data point but acknowledged as common pattern)

### 8. Relationships matter more than career optimisation
- **Claim:** Over-optimising for career growth at the expense of friendships and romantic relationships is the biggest career regret
- **Who:** Evan
- **Evidence:** Personal reflection — only realised after moving to LA and building non-tech friendships
- **Strength:** Weak (purely personal reflection, no external evidence)

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. The Speed Budget
- **How it works:** Complete assigned work faster than expected → creates discretionary time → use that time for high-visibility, career-accelerating work outside your scope
- **When to apply:** Early career (IC3–IC5) when assigned scope is narrow
- **Two levers:** (a) Dial in workflow (keybindings, code search, memorise codebase); (b) Work more hours (more honest but less glamorous)

### 2. Impact Throughput (Impact/Effort Ratio)
- **How it works:** Prioritise like a fraction — numerator is impact, denominator is effort/complexity. A one-line change with huge impact beats a year-long complex project
- **When to apply:** Choosing projects at any level, especially staff+
- **Meta's culture advantage:** Meta rewards impact regardless of effort; other companies reward proportional to difficulty

### 3. Context Accumulation → Influence
- **How it works:** Be the first person on a growing area → accumulate context → become the go-to person → this context gives you de facto authority regardless of level
- **When to apply:** Team selection (join growing areas); early career positioning

### 4. The Two Paths of Life Fulfilment
- **How it works:** Either (a) find work that's so passionate it fills all your boxes (relationships, fulfilment, money), or (b) do good work 9-5 and find fulfilment outside work
- **When to apply:** Career/life planning, especially after achieving senior/staff level
- **Evan's arc:** Planned for option (b), accidentally fell into option (a) with startups

### 5. Influence Without Authority
- **How it works:** At Meta levels aren't public → can't rely on "I'm IC6, listen to me" → must earn trust daily by being right consistently and being genuinely on everyone's side
- **When to apply:** Tech lead roles, cross-team collaboration, any leadership without direct reports

### 6. The Startup Learning Inversion
- **How it works:** Ideal order is startup first (breadth) → Big Tech (depth), though career-wise people do the reverse. Big Tech creates specialists who are "handcuffed" to large companies; startups create generalists who are transferable
- **When to apply:** Career planning at senior+ level
