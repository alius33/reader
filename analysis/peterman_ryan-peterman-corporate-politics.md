# Podcast Analysis: How Corporate Politics Work And How To Win

## Podcast Type

**Interview** — Second-time guest appearance. Ryan Peterman (tech content creator, software engineer) hosts Ethan Evans (retired Amazon VP) for a deep-dive on corporate politics. Builds on a first episode about transparency in tech. The conversation is unusually candid because Ethan is retired and "immune" — no boss to worry about.

## Core Topic

How corporate politics actually work inside big tech companies (primarily Amazon), covering: empire building, reorgs, promotions, influence vs manipulation, scope wars between orgs, firing mechanics, and how to navigate all of it without becoming either a victim or a villain.

## Host

**Ryan Peterman** — Tech content creator and software engineer. Positions himself as the engineer asking the questions his audience would ask. Draws on his own experience but primarily serves as interviewer, drawing out Ethan's stories and frameworks. Second time interviewing Ethan.

## Guest

**Ethan Evans** — Retired Amazon Vice President. Key credentials:
- Became Amazon director early in the company's history (with only 22 people, when thresholds were different)
- At peak managed ~800 people across diverse functions (Prime Video, Amazon App Store, reverse logistics, B2B, custom T-shirt printing)
- Ran Prime Video engineering team
- 15 years at Amazon during its growth from 14,000 to 1.4 million employees
- Now coaches tech leaders and executives
- Admits to being fired twice early in career for volatility and having a temper
- Retirement gives him freedom to be unusually candid

## Main Arguments & Claims

### Empire Building & Incentive Structures
- Empire building exists because **headcount is the easiest metric to measure** — impact is subjective but "no one can debate Ethan has 42 people and Ryan has 17" (Ethan, observational)
- Amazon established written director thresholds of **80-90 people** after Ethan left (Ethan, secondhand from coaching clients)
- The "no bonus for additional headcount" principle **directly contradicts** these thresholds — "there damn sure is a bonus, several hundred thousand dollars a year" (Ethan, personal experience)
- Leaders **game headcount thresholds** by temporarily transferring reports — "move people for six months, check the box, give them back" (Ethan, told by his VP)
- No company systematically **down-levels** people when their team shrinks (Ethan, career-wide observation)

### Reorgs
- Every reorg has **hidden secondary motives** beyond the stated business rationale — retention, promotion setup, managing people out (Ethan, career-wide)
- Reorgs are triggered by: (a) business change, (b) someone quit/was moved (cascade), (c) accumulated deferred changes (Ethan)
- Reorgs are fundamentally an exercise in **narrative construction** — "you're a storyteller coming up with your narrative about why this is the best possible reorg" (Ethan)
- Reorgs can deliberately manage unwanted leaders out via **undesirable assignments** — the "window seat" from Japanese corporate culture (Ethan)
- Reorgs cause roughly a **six-month productivity hit** — widely known, used as defensive argument (Ethan)

### Career Self-Advocacy
- The **squeaky wheel gets the grease** — vocal people receive more attention and resources; quiet high performers get deprioritised (Ethan, coaching practice)
- Being "too nice" is a **career liability** — "that is not a compliment in this context" (Ethan, coaching)
- You can express career ambitions firmly without being threatening using **pre-planned "polite fictions"** — statements that are simultaneously honest and strategically calibrated (Ethan, personal success)
- The best leverage comes from having a **genuine outside option**, not from bluffing (Ethan, personal VP negotiation)
- If a demanding high performer is told no, they will usually **quit on their own** — this can be a feature, not a bug (Ethan)

### Promotion Mechanics
- **"Solve problems for your boss"** — the five-word promotion strategy. Works even with bad bosses because even selfish managers want to keep helpers (Ethan, attributed to SVP lineage at Amazon/Walmart)
- Forward-looking **promotion slates** exist where names are queued 6-24 months out (Ethan, Amazon/Google practice)
- **Quotas and lines** become more important at senior/expensive levels — "those positions cost a million dollars a piece" (Ethan)
- What gets people **stuck at senior levels** is doing the current skill harder instead of learning the next level's skill (Ethan, coaching observation; Marshall Goldsmith reference)
- **Trust is earned more in bad times than good** — the "war buddy" effect from surviving crises together (Ethan)
- At equally qualified levels, **likeability is the tiebreaker** — "do you suck all the oxygen out of the room?" (Ethan, attributed to Bezos)
- You **cannot get promoted over your manager's objection** — Ethan has never seen it happen (Ethan, entire career)
- For senior promotions, **over-subscribe on champion requests** — ask 6-7 for 4 needed (Ethan)

### Influence & Politics
- Influence and politics use **identical skill sets**; only motive distinguishes them — "Darth Vader had executive presence in spades" (Ethan)
- Three categories of skilled operators: altruistic (Jedi), purely self-serving (Sith), and **negotiable middle** (most people) (Ethan)
- Even unethical people have a **self-narrative** where they're "practical, not evil" — work with their narrative (Ethan)
- People reach **emotional conclusions first**, then rationalise with facts — being a better storyteller beats having better facts (Ethan, psychology reference)
- **Soft power** (allies, reputation, relationships) is the primary defence against political attacks — the porcupine strategy (Ethan)
- Entry-level employees can have **outsized influence** with the right idea + storytelling + enthusiasm + access to power (Ethan, Cloud Drive and Fire TV stories)

### Scope Wars
- **Respect between engineering teams is inversely proportional to their distance** (Ethan, attributed to unnamed leader)
- Attempted internal scope raids follow **coup dynamics** — succeed and you win, fail and you're burned (Ethan)
- **Cannibalism becomes more common** as company growth slows — confirmed by both Ryan and Ethan
- Defence tactics: skunk trick, thorny target, delay, storytelling (Ethan)
- Attack tiers: (1) genuine value creation, (2) efficiency/cost arguments, (3) highlighting flaws. Plus the "offer to help" Trojan horse (Ethan)

### Firing & Being Fired
- The real reason most people get fired is **style incompatibility**, not the performance issues in their file (Ethan, career-wide)
- It's harder to fire managers because: (1) subtle mistakes, (2) hard to measure, (3) they know the system (Ethan)
- Three pieces of **leverage when being fired**: time/hassle cost, departure narrative, manager's self-image (Ethan)
- By the time a manager decides to act, they've already decided it's over — **PIPs are formalities** (Ethan, coaching hundreds of people)
- "**Constructive termination**" is the legal tripwire both sides dance around (Ethan, Amazon HR practice)
- **IQ without EQ is a waste of IQ** — attributed to Satya Nadella (Ethan)

### Avoiding Politics
- Two strategies: (1) choose a leader who shares your disdain, (2) work on the **hardest technical problems** where expertise truly matters (Ethan)
- If you have an **irreplaceable skill**, you get exceptions — Andy Jassy absolved certain principal engineers of all mentoring duties (Ethan, firsthand Amazon)
- **Growing companies have less politics**; stagnant ones are zero-sum (Ethan)

## Key Stories & Anecdotes

### Story 1: "My Career Is Very Important to Me" — Director Promotion
- **Who:** Ethan and his manager Neil, at Amazon
- **Situation:** Senior manager with 22 people, wanting director promotion
- **What happened:** Pre-crafted diplomatic statement: "Neil, I need to understand how important my career is to Amazon, because my career is very important to me. And if it's not as important to Amazon as it is to me, I need to think about that."
- **Outcome:** Neil pushed the promotion through
- **Illustrates:** The polite fiction — simultaneously honest and strategically calibrated. "No one can argue with it."

### Story 2: The India Dev Center Deal
- **Who:** Ethan (director) and a senior manager wanting director promotion
- **Situation:** VP told Ethan to open offshore dev center; Ethan didn't want to
- **What happened:** Found an Indian senior manager living in the US. Deal: go build the dev center, use it as platform for director promotion
- **Outcome:** SM moved to India for 3 years, built the center, got promoted. His son learned Hindi, saw grandparents
- **Illustrates:** Deal-making between managers and reports. Solving the boss's problem creates promotion leverage

### Story 3: The "Other" / Garbage Can Org
- **Who:** Early Amazon, during Prime Video/Kindle formation
- **Situation:** Teams organised, one leader got the leftovers — "database maintenance, QA, and a couple small things"
- **What happened:** Leader joked "my group's Other — it's the garbage can." VP was upset about the label
- **Outcome:** That leader was reportedly still at Amazon 20 years later
- **Illustrates:** Team-player penalty (nice people get worst assignments) but patience can mean longevity

### Story 4: Ethan's Random Empire — Saying Yes to Everything
- **Who:** Ethan, under a VP with no other engineering leaders
- **Situation:** VP kept receiving engineering teams with no one else to hand them to
- **What happened:** Ethan said yes to everything — ended up managing 8 functions: reverse logistics, video games, App Store, B2B business. ~200 people from wildly diverse functions
- **Illustrates:** "Solve problems for your boss" in practice. Willingness builds trust and accumulates scope

### Story 5: The Billion-Dollar T-Shirt Business
- **Who:** Ethan, his manager, at Amazon
- **Situation:** Ethan ran App Store (800 people). Believed in custom-printed T-shirts on Amazon
- **What happened:** Allocated 10 people (1% of resources) over manager's objections. Manager called it irrelevant micromanagement fight
- **Outcome:** Amazon now sells over a billion dollars a year of custom-printed T-shirts
- **Illustrates:** The "inventor" path alongside the "helpful" path. How to frame pushback on micromanagement

### Story 6: Forcing the VP's Hand with an SVP Offer
- **Who:** Ethan (VP) and his VP boss
- **Situation:** Wanted a different role. VP kept saying no because current arrangement suited him
- **What happened:** Obtained genuine job offer from a Senior VP in different org. Said: "I have an offer. If I give my word, I have to go. You've told me no several times. What do you want me to do?"
- **Outcome:** "I was in the new role the next morning"
- **Illustrates:** Power of genuine alternatives. "Once I give my word" framing — honour, not ultimatum

### Story 7: The Leader Mistreating Women
- **Who:** A leader under Ethan, unnamed women on the team
- **What happened:** Took a long time for different rumours to bubble up. When Ethan investigated by talking to women directly, they confirmed problems
- **Outcome:** Ethan had "been blind to a problem." Any single woman alone probably wouldn't have been believed
- **Illustrates:** "Never mutiny alone" — multiple corroborating reports force action

### Story 8: Cloud Drive — Entry-Level Engineer Pitches Bezos
- **Who:** New college grad engineer at Amazon
- **What happened:** Had idea for cloud storage. Fought into invite-only engineering conference by submitting a proposal. Emailed Bezos asking him to stop by his poster — "a very small ask of the CEO." Bezos liked it and emailed Ethan's VP
- **Outcome:** VP didn't like the idea but because Bezos said "look into it," project got funded and built
- **Illustrates:** Outsized influence from the bottom with the right idea + storytelling + access to power

### Story 9: The C-Level Sexual Harasser
- **Who:** Very high-level executive hired at Amazon
- **What happened:** Immediately began harassing women. So brazen he harassed an HR representative on his own team
- **Outcome:** Terminated quickly. No legal proof existed, so references only confirmed employment dates. He deleted Amazon from LinkedIn entirely
- **Illustrates:** How serial harassers survive the reference check system. The gap between internal knowledge and what's legally shareable

### Story 10: The PIP Coaching Pattern
- **Who:** Ethan's coaching clients
- **What happened:** People constantly write saying "I've been put on a PIP, can you help?" Ethan tells them honestly: "No, you're most likely going to be fired"
- **Outcome:** Three months later they email back: "You were right"
- **Illustrates:** By the time managers act, they've already decided. PIPs are formalities

### Story 11: Ethan's Star Employee — Three Promotions in Eight Years
- **Who:** Ethan's employee, started as mid-level engineer (L5)
- **What happened:** Promoted to senior engineer → lateral to manager → senior manager → director — catching up to Ethan himself
- **Outcome:** After 8 years and 3 promotions, left to found his own company. "He powered my career and I powered his"
- **Illustrates:** Ethan's selling point as a manager: "come work for me and I'll get you promoted." Mutual career acceleration

### Story 12: Andy Jassy and the Principal Engineers
- **Who:** Andy Jassy (AWS), principal engineers
- **What happened:** Jassy absolved certain engineers working on the hardest AWS problems of all mentoring, advising, and architecture review duties
- **Illustrates:** If you have an irreplaceable skill, you get exceptions to all social rules

### Story 13: Andy Jassy and the Low-Effort Employee
- **Who:** Andy Jassy, a high-level employee
- **What happened:** People complained the employee wasn't working hard. Jassy said: "Yeah, you're right. But he's in a role where we need him and I think he knows we need him. So we're just gonna have to live with it"
- **Illustrates:** Irreplaceable leverage. Even leadership accepts it when someone has genuine monopoly on a capability

## Frameworks & Mental Models

### 1. The Polite Fiction
A statement simultaneously true at face value AND communicating an unspoken message both parties understand. Surface meaning is unattackable; subtext is clear.
- "My career is very important to me" = I might leave
- "If you have a need, I'll be there for you" = I want any available scope
- "Once I give my word, I can't go back on that" = I'm about to accept the other offer

### 2. The Three-Problem Framework (Why Skip-Level Complaints Fail)
When someone complains about their manager to the skip:
- **Option A (dismiss):** One problem — the complainer is "high maintenance"
- **Option B (believe):** Three problems — (1) decide what to do with the bad manager, (2) hire/train replacement, (3) cover manager's work in interim
- Asymmetry makes Option A far more attractive

### 3. Interpersonally Warm, Professionally Firm
Separate emotional delivery from message content. Be friendly, smile, don't get agitated, but clearly state what you need. Same words delivered differently produce completely opposite results.

### 4. The Chess Analogy for Difficult Conversations
- **Book openings:** Pre-planned phrases rehearsed before entering the room
- **General principles:** Move toward common ground, understand emotional state, find shared interests

### 5. Solve Problems for Your Boss
Five-word promotion strategy. Identify what your manager needs → deliver it → express what you want. Works even with bad bosses because even selfish managers want to keep helpers.

### 6. Defence Tactics Against Org Takeovers
1. **Skunk Trick:** Surface unsexy maintenance work nobody wants
2. **Thorny Target:** Overreact early to signal retaliation capability
3. **Delay:** Argue six-month productivity hit from reorgs
4. **Storytelling:** Emotional narratives beat facts in decision-making
5. **Performance:** High performance is the best platform for all defences

### 7. Attack Tactics for Scope Acquisition (Three Tiers)
1. **Tier 1 (legitimate):** Genuine value-creation story with metrics
2. **Tier 2 (grey area):** Efficiency, cost savings, simplification
3. **Tier 3 (aggressive):** Highlight the other org's flaws — "dumpster fire"
- **Trojan Horse:** Offer to "help" → embed → propose consolidation

### 8. Jedi vs. Sith Assessment
Three categories of skilled operators:
- **Category 1 (Jedi):** Altruistic — combine forces
- **Category 2 (Sith):** Self-serving — "get your lightsaber up"
- **Category 3 (Negotiable):** Interests alignable in limited scope — make deals but don't trust beyond them

### 9. Umbrella vs. Funnel Managers
When pressure comes down, managers either shield the team (umbrella) or channel it onto the team (funnel). Evaluate your manager by which one they are.

### 10. Balance of Safety
To change a yes-man manager: either help them feel safe pushing back up, or help them feel unsafe pushing down on you. Script during a calm moment (Tuesday), not during a crisis (Friday).

### 11. Three Pieces of Leverage When Being Fired
1. Time/hassle cost of the formal process
2. The story you'll tell the departing team
3. Manager's desire to feel good about themselves (spending company money, not their own)

### 12. What Got You Here Won't Get You There
Each career level requires fundamentally different skills. Early = hard work/delivery. Senior = delegation, communication, letting go. Reference: Marshall Goldsmith.

### 13. Champion Over-Subscription Strategy
For senior promotions requiring N champions, ask N+2 or N+3. Some will leave, get reorged, or say no. Like over-provisioning capacity.

### 14. The "Do People Want You in the Room?" Test
When you walk in, does energy go up or down? Attributed to Bezos: "Do you suck all the oxygen out of the room?"
