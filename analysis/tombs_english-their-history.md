# The English and Their History — Robert Tombs

## Classification & Context

**Book type:** Argument/narrative hybrid — a comprehensive single-volume history of England from prehistory to c.2014, but with a distinctive argumentative thread throughout. Not a textbook — it has a thesis.

**Author background:** Robert Tombs is Professor of French History at Cambridge. He is an Englishman who spent most of his life studying France, which gives him an outsider's perspective on his own country. He explicitly positions himself as looking at England comparatively, not insulary. His expertise on France means he can constantly contextualise English developments against Continental experience.

**Audience:** Educated general readers and students. Accessible prose but serious scholarship. Assumes some basic historical literacy but explains most concepts. Written during the 2014 Scottish independence referendum debate, which clearly influenced the book's engagement with questions of English identity and the UK's future.

**Key innovation:** This is the first full-length single-volume history of England (not Britain) in decades. Tombs argues that England has a distinct history that is NOT simply subsumed into "British history," and that writing it matters.

## Central Thesis

Tombs advances three interconnected arguments:

1. **England is one of the world's oldest nation-states** — the concept of "Englishkind" (Angelcynn) emerged in the 8th-9th centuries, making England a pioneer of the nation-state form. Unlike most nations whose identities are modern constructs, England's identity predates its political unity.

2. **History and memory are inseparable** — What happened to the English and what they believed happened are equally important. The book tracks four "memory themes" that form an arc of rise and fall: the aftermath of the Norman Conquest, the "Whig history" of liberty, the contested memory of empire, and the recent narrative of decline.

3. **The "decline" narrative is largely an illusion** — Tombs systematically demolishes the idea that post-1945 Britain has been in decline. He argues that Britain remains among the richest and most powerful nations, that the empire's loss was mutual liberation, and that "declinism" has been a corrosive and largely unfounded national neurosis.

**Underlying model:** England's unusual longevity as a nation is explained by geography (island position), institutional continuity (never conquered since 1066), and the development of a powerful central state with broad participation — representative government, common law, effective taxation. The sea is both highway and moat: it connects England to the world while making invasion difficult, and this combination explains most of England's distinctive trajectory.

## Structural Map

### Introduction: Who Do We Think We Are?
- Sets up the dual themes: what happened AND how the English remembered it
- Argues that England (not Britain) deserves its own history — this is unusual and somewhat controversial among historians
- Introduces four memory themes: Norman Conquest aftermath, Whig history, empire, decline
- Notes England has rarely been autonomous — always attached to wider political units (Scandinavian, French, British, European)

### Prelude: The Dreamtime
- Prehistory through Roman Britain to Saxon arrival
- 800,000 years of habitation; Channel formation ~6,000 years ago
- Roman conquest (AD 43), Boudicca's revolt, Hadrian's Wall
- Roman withdrawal (c.409), Saxon invasions, cultural catastrophe
- The Arthur myth — invented by Geoffrey of Monmouth c.1136, became foundation of English/British royal mythology
- Key point: invasions were always by relatively small warrior bands who became overlords, not mass population replacements

### Part One: The Birth of a Nation (c.600-1066)
**Chapter 1: This Earth, This Realm**
- The idea of "the English" preceded political unity — Pope Gregory's "not Angles but Angels" (c.580)
- Bede's Ecclesiastical History (731) defined English identity as religious: the gens Anglorum, one people in God's eyes
- Christianity brought back literacy, created Old English as a written language, produced Beowulf
- Viking invasions from 793 (Lindisfarne) transformed the political landscape
- Alfred of Wessex: survived Danish invasion, created the first English kingdom (seized London 886), commissioned English-language scholarship, began the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle
- Alfred as nation-builder: invented the English as a political community, not just a religious one
- The English kingdom consolidated under his successors (Athelstan, Edgar), then tested by renewed Danish invasion under Cnut
- 1066: Harold defeated at Hastings by William of Normandy — the last successful invasion, and the most transformative

**Chapter 2: The Conqueror's Kingdom**
- Norman Conquest was the most important single event in English history
- Rewired language (French-speaking aristocracy), law, land ownership, class structure, and the Church
- Created the fundamental English class system that persisted for centuries
- Yet English institutions (shires, courts, taxation) survived because they were useful to the conquerors
- Domesday Book (1086): unprecedented survey of national wealth — no equivalent anywhere in Europe
- Common Law developed from 12th century: Henry II's legal reforms created a system that eventually covered all freemen
- Magna Carta (1215): not a charter of liberty for the people, but a negotiation between king and barons — yet it established the principle that the king is bound by law
- Parliament emerged from the 13th century as a mechanism for raising taxes and securing consent
- Historiographical essay: how Shakespeare and other writers shaped memory of this period

### Part Two: The English Unleashed (c.1200-1485)
**Chapter 3: A Well Good Land**
- England by 1300: population ~6 million, 800+ towns, dense road network, wool as major export
- Open-field agriculture at its peak; peasant society surprisingly litigious and rights-conscious
- Social structure: lords, knights, esquires, yeomen, villeins — but English peers had no special legal status unlike Continental nobility
- Church owned half of all land; 50,000 male clergy; great cathedrals in "Decorated" Gothic
- Common Law extending to every village — cheap, accessible, a last resort when local systems failed
- Key theme: ordinary people had rights and asserted them, through courts and custom

**Chapter 4: "The world is changed and overthrown"**
- Black Death (1348-49): killed 40-50% of population, the greatest catastrophe in English history
- Paradoxical long-term effect: improved living standards for survivors (higher wages, more land per person)
- Peasants' Revolt (1381): Wat Tyler's march on London — demanding end to serfdom, poll tax, corrupt government
- Significance: showed ordinary English people's political awareness and willingness to act collectively
- Hundred Years' War with France: Agincourt (1415), English conquest of Normandy, Joan of Arc, eventual loss of all Continental territory by 1453
- Wars of the Roses (1455-1485): dynastic civil war between York and Lancaster, ended with Henry Tudor's victory at Bosworth

### Part Three: The Great Divide (c.1500-1700)
**Chapter 5: Reformation**
- Henry VIII's "divorce" and break with Rome — personal obsession triggering the greatest revolution in English history
- Dissolution of the monasteries: largest land transfer since the Norman Conquest; cultural destruction incalculable
- Thomas More and John Fisher: executed for refusing to accept royal supremacy — principled resistance
- Thomas Cromwell: self-made genius, instrument of Henry's revolution, eventually executed himself
- Pilgrimage of Grace (1536): largest popular uprising of the century, crushed by Henry's vindictiveness
- Edward VI: genuine Protestant revolution under Cranmer; Book of Common Prayer (1549, revised 1552)
- Mary I: Catholic restoration, burning of ~300 Protestants including Cranmer — created "Bloody Mary" legacy
- Elizabeth I: religious settlement that was broadly Protestant but kept Catholic ceremonial elements
- Tyndale's Bible translation: revolutionary — "ere many years I will cause a boy that driveth the plough shall know more of the scripture than thou dost"

**Chapter 6: Revolution**
- Stuart era: James I united crowns of England and Scotland (1603)
- Charles I's conflicts with Parliament over taxation and religion led to Civil War (1642-1651)
- Oliver Cromwell: military genius, regicide, Lord Protector — established republican government
- The English Civil War as the last of Europe's wars of religion
- Restoration (1660): Charles II returned — but the old absolute monarchy did not
- Glorious Revolution (1688): William of Orange invited to replace James II — established constitutional monarchy
- Bill of Rights (1689): Parliament's supremacy confirmed, religious toleration (limited)
- Historiographical essay: the Civil War and the creation of "Whig history" — the narrative of England as a story of progressive liberty

### Part Four: Making a New World (c.1660-1815)
**Chapter 7: And All Was Light**
- The Enlightenment: England and Holland as intellectual centres
- Francis Bacon: father of scientific method
- Isaac Newton: Principia Mathematica (1687) — one of the greatest achievements of the human mind
- John Locke: Essay Concerning Human Understanding and Two Treatises of Government — foundational texts of liberal democracy
- Key insight: English Enlightenment was empirical and cautious, not dogmatic — Bacon, Newton, Locke all stressed observation over theory

**Chapter 8: A Free Country?**
- Party politics: Whigs vs Tories — first modern party system
- Robert Walpole: first de facto Prime Minister (1721-1742)
- Press freedom, public debate, coffee-house culture
- Yet England was also a place of inequality, patronage, corruption and violent punishment

**Chapter 9: The Rise and Fall of the Atlantic Nation**
- Slave trade and plantation economy: England as major participant
- American colonies: founded by religious dissenters and profit-seekers
- Seven Years' War (1756-63): Britain becomes global power
- American Revolution (1775-83): ironically fought in the name of English rights and liberties
- Loss of America: psychologically devastating but economically manageable

**Chapter 10: The First Industrial Nation**
- Industrial Revolution began in England: why?
- Combination of: cheap energy (coal), skilled labour, capital markets, legal protections for property and innovation, consumer demand
- Not planned or directed by the state — emerged from individual enterprise within a favourable institutional framework
- Transformed population, urbanisation, class structure — but gradually, not overnight

**Chapter 11: Wars of Dreams**
- French Revolution (1789) and its impact on England: fear and reaction
- Napoleonic Wars (1793-1815): existential struggle
- Nelson at Trafalgar (1805), Wellington at Waterloo (1815)
- Britain emerged as the unchallenged global power — but at huge financial cost (national debt 250% of GDP)

### Part Five: The English Century (c.1815-1918)
**Chapter 12: Dickensian England**
- Dickens as the most "English" of writers — shaped how we see 19th-century England
- Post-Waterloo crisis: unemployment, food shortages, Peterloo (1819)
- Corn Laws debate: protection vs free trade
- Reform Act 1832: widened suffrage but still very limited
- Chartist movement: demanded universal male suffrage
- Poor Law Amendment Act 1834: created workhouses — Dickens's great target
- Key insight: Dickensian England was "a place of intense political and social debate" — not a settled society but one grappling with unprecedented change

**Chapter 13: Victorian England**
- Respectability, religiosity, self-improvement as defining values
- The Great Exhibition (1851): confident display of industrial supremacy
- Education: expanding public schools, new universities
- Women: gradual expansion of rights, suffragette movement
- Victorian morality: a conscious effort to tame a turbulent, frightening society

**Chapter 14: Imperial England**
- Empire at its height: a quarter of the world's surface
- "Soft power" as much as military force
- Scramble for Africa, Great Game with Russia
- Growing tensions with Germany: naval arms race from 1900
- Key argument: Victorian hegemony was always limited — "a third-rate power with a great empire"

### Part Six: The New Dark Age (1914-1945)
**Chapter 15: The War to End War**
- WW1 as the shattering event that began four decades of violence
- England entered as the world's creditor nation, left as a debtor
- 750,000 British lives lost
- Key argument against received memory: soldiers saw it as a struggle for freedom, not futility. "Were they deluded, or are we uncomprehending?"
- The "lions led by donkeys" myth: more complex than popular memory allows

**Chapter 16: The Twenty-Year Truce**
- Versailles settlement: problematic but not solely responsible for WW2
- Interwar period: economic struggles, General Strike (1926), Great Depression
- Appeasement: Chamberlain was not naive — he was buying time while rearming

**Chapter 17: The Edge of the Abyss**
- WW2: Britain's "finest hour"
- Dunkirk, Battle of Britain, Blitz — genuine heroism but also much myth-making
- Britain won both wars but lost its empire and wealth
- By 1945: "shabby and tired" but unoccupied and undefeated

### Part Seven: An Age of Decline? (1945-c.2014)
**Chapter 18: Postwar**
- Labour government 1945-51: Welfare State, NHS, nationalisation
- Attlee and Bevin: models of late-Victorian probity
- Marshall Plan, NATO, beginning of Cold War
- Decolonisation: India (1947), Africa (1960s)

**Chapter 19: England's Cultural Revolutions**
- 1960s-70s: social and cultural transformation
- Immigration, race relations, women's liberation
- Pop culture: Beatles, Rolling Stones — English soft power at its peak
- Decline of deference, rise of individualism

**Chapter 20: Storm and Stress**
- Economic crises of the 1970s: strikes, three-day week, "Winter of Discontent"
- Margaret Thatcher (1979-1990): radical transformation of economy and society
- Falklands War (1982): restored national confidence
- Miners' Strike (1984-85): defeat of trade union power

**Chapter 21: Things Can Only Get Better (1997-c.2014)**
- Tony Blair and New Labour
- Kosovo, Iraq, Afghanistan
- 2008 financial crisis
- Scottish independence debate
- Euroscepticism as distinctly English phenomenon

### Conclusion: The English and Their History
- Five key themes synthesised:
  1. **Connections and disconnections:** England has never been isolated — always attached to wider political/trade networks
  2. **Continuities and discontinuities:** Institutional survival since 1066 is the key distinguishing feature
  3. **Power and participation:** Unusually broad political participation from very early
  4. **Decline is an illusion:** Britain remains among the richest, most powerful nations. "Declinism" has been a corrosive national neurosis
  5. **England without a state:** Since devolution, England is the largest European nation without its own political institutions

## Key Principles

### 1. The Nation Preceded the State
- The idea of "the English" as a people emerged in the 8th century (Bede), a political kingdom in the 9th (Alfred) — making England among the oldest nations in existence
- Identity began as a religious concept (the gens Anglorum) before becoming political
- **Mechanism:** The Church, as an organisation transcending political boundaries, created the idea of a single English people before any king ruled them all
- **When it applies:** Understanding why English institutions are so old and resilient
- **Exception:** The identity was not continuous — it was reinvented and reshaped after the Norman Conquest

### 2. The Sea Explains Almost Everything
- Island geography made large-scale invasion difficult but not impossible
- The same geography made trade and overseas expansion natural
- Security from invasion since 1066 explains institutional continuity
- Connection to the wider world explains economic dynamism and empire
- **Mechanism:** The Channel is narrow enough for constant contact but wide enough to deter mass invasion. This combination is unique among major nations
- **Best evidence:** Napoleon and Hitler both failed to invade; no successful conquest since 1066

### 3. The Norman Conquest Was the Most Important Single Event
- Rewired language, law, land, class, and religion
- Created the class system that persisted for centuries
- Yet English institutions survived because they were useful to the conquerors
- **Mechanism:** A small Norman elite (perhaps 8,000-10,000) took over but could not replace 2 million English peasants. They needed existing systems to govern
- **When it applies:** Understanding why English law and administration are so distinctive
- **When it doesn't:** The Conquest's effects faded over centuries — by 1400, the ruling class was thoroughly English-speaking again

### 4. Parliament Was Born from the King's Need for Money
- Not created as a democratic institution — emerged as a mechanism for raising taxes
- Kings needed consent to tax; barons and later commons demanded concessions in return
- This transactional relationship is the foundation of English representative government
- **Mechanism:** Unlike Continental monarchs who could raise revenue independently (through crown lands, feudal dues, or coercion), English kings were unusually dependent on parliamentary taxation
- **Best evidence:** Every major constitutional crisis — Magna Carta, Civil War, Glorious Revolution — was triggered by disputes over money

### 5. The Reformation Was Driven by Personal Obsession, Not Theology
- Henry VIII's break with Rome was caused by his desire for a male heir, not Protestant conviction
- He remained doctrinally conservative (burning Lutherans while defying the Pope)
- Yet his personal obsession opened the door to genuine religious revolution under Edward VI and Elizabeth
- **Mechanism:** Once papal authority was rejected, the door could not be closed — evangelicals used the opening to push genuine reform
- **Exception:** The Reformation also had deep social and intellectual roots (Lollardy, humanism, printing) that would have caused upheaval regardless

### 6. The Industrial Revolution Was Not Planned
- Emerged from individual enterprise within a favourable institutional framework
- Key preconditions: cheap coal, skilled labour, capital markets, legal protections, consumer demand
- NOT driven by the state, unlike later industrialisations in Germany, Japan, or Russia
- **Mechanism:** England's unique combination of secure property rights, accessible capital, and abundant coal allowed bottom-up innovation
- **When it doesn't apply:** Later industrialisations (Germany, Japan, China) were all state-directed — the English model was unrepeatable

### 7. "Decline" Is Largely an Illusion
- Britain remains among the richest nations in the world (in 2008, second only to the US among large nations in per capita income)
- The empire's loss was mutual liberation, not collapse
- "Declinism" has been a corrosive national neurosis that distorted politics and damaged cultural confidence
- **Mechanism:** Other countries catching up with the pioneer is normal — it is not the same as the pioneer declining
- **Best evidence:** Per capita income comparisons; military strength in the 1950s was far greater than at the height of the Victorian empire

### 8. England Is Not Britain
- England has a distinct history that is not subsumed into "British history"
- The union with Scotland (1707), conquest of Ireland, and empire all complicated but did not erase English identity
- Since devolution, England is the largest European nation without its own political institutions
- **When it applies:** Always — but especially when discussing the post-1707 period, when "England" and "Britain" are often used interchangeably
- **Exception:** For international and military history, "Britain" is the relevant unit

### 9. English Political Culture Values Compromise Over Purity
- Every major constitutional crisis was resolved by compromise, not revolution
- Magna Carta, the Glorious Revolution, the Reform Acts — all were negotiated settlements
- Even the Civil War ended with a restoration that preserved parliamentary gains
- **Mechanism:** The small ruling class (interconnected by marriage, education, landholding) had more to gain from compromise than from winner-take-all conflict
- **When it doesn't apply:** Ireland — where compromise consistently failed, with devastating consequences

### 10. Memory Shapes History as Much as Events Do
- How the English remembered their past shaped their present at every stage
- The Arthur myth, the Magna Carta myth, the "Whig history" of progressive liberty, the "finest hour" myth
- These memories are not simply false — they select and emphasise certain truths while obscuring others
- **Mechanism:** Collective memory creates the framework within which political action makes sense. If you believe England has always been moving toward liberty, you act accordingly
- **Best evidence:** The American Revolution was fought in the name of English rights described by Locke; the "decline" narrative drove both joining the EU and (arguably) leaving it

## Stories Worth Preserving

### 1. Alfred and the Cakes (c.878)
- Alfred, king of Wessex, forced to flee into the marshes of Athelney by Danish invaders
- Takes refuge with a peasant family, unrecognised. Left to watch baking bread, he burns it — preoccupied with planning his comeback
- Summons his men to "Ecgberht's Stone east of Selwood" — all of Somerset and Wiltshire rally to him
- Defeats the Danes at Edington (879), forces them to accept baptism
- **Illustrates:** The founding myth of the English kingdom — a vulnerable king who reinvents himself as a nation-builder

### 2. Thomas More in the Tower (1534-35)
- More refuses to swear the oath accepting Henry VIII's supremacy over the Church
- Offers silence rather than outright refusal: "I do nobody harm, I say none harm, I think none harm"
- The Solicitor-General Richard Rich is sent to entrap him — More denies saying anything treasonous
- Convicted anyway, beheaded in 1535
- **Illustrates:** The collision between individual conscience and royal power at the heart of the Reformation

### 3. Henry VIII and His Six Wives
- Catherine of Aragon: divorced (couldn't produce male heir) — triggered the Reformation
- Anne Boleyn: beheaded on trumped-up charges of adultery, probably because gossip about her flirtations and jokes about Henry's impotence reached the king — the unplanned fall of a queen
- Jane Seymour: produced the heir (Edward) and died in childbirth
- Anne of Cleves: marriage unconsummated ("the Flanders mare"), annulled — she wisely accepted generous compensation
- Catherine Howard: actually committed adultery (unlike Anne Boleyn), executed
- Catherine Parr: survived
- **Illustrates:** How one man's personal obsessions shaped the greatest revolution in English history

### 4. The Peasants' Revolt (1381)
- Wat Tyler marches on London demanding end to serfdom and poll tax
- Rebels carried lists of enemies they wanted killed
- Young Richard II meets the rebels at Smithfield; Tyler is killed in a scuffle
- Richard rides toward the rebels: "I am your captain, follow me!" — disperses them
- Aftermath: promises broken, leaders executed, but serfdom gradually disappeared anyway
- **Illustrates:** The political awareness and collective action of ordinary English people — and the limits of royal promises

### 5. Newton and the Apple
- Isaac Newton: introverted farmer's son from Lincolnshire, began experimenting with optics by pushing metal into his own eye sockets
- Principia Mathematica (1687): proved that experiment and calculation could explain the universe
- Also practised alchemy, studied biblical chronology, thought the Apocalypse might come around the year 2000
- First mathematician to be knighted — "honours that impressed Europeans with the unique prestige of science in England"
- **Illustrates:** The English Enlightenment as empirical and cautious, not dogmatic

### 6. The "Finest Hour" (1940)
- After Dunkirk, Britain stood alone against Nazi Germany
- Battle of Britain, the Blitz — genuine heroism
- But also much myth-making: many were terrified, some looted, morale varied
- Tombs argues: the real achievement was not heroism but the institutional resilience of a democratic society under extreme pressure
- **Illustrates:** The complex relationship between what happened and how it was remembered

### 7. Peterloo (1819)
- Peaceful crowd of 60,000+ in St. Peter's Fields, Manchester, gathered to hear political speaker Henry "Orator" Hunt
- Local magistrates panicked and ordered yeomanry to charge — 11 killed, 400+ injured
- Named "Peterloo" in mocking reference to Waterloo
- Notorious in English memory because of its rarity — state violence against peaceful protest was exceptional
- **Illustrates:** Both the violence of the early 19th century and the English political tradition of peaceful protest

## Critique & Cross-References

### Strengths
- Genuinely comparative: constantly measures England against France, Germany, and other nations
- The dual history/memory framework is intellectually original and illuminating
- Demolishes "declinism" with detailed evidence and comparative data
- Engagingly written — Tombs has real narrative skill despite the book's vast scope

### Weaknesses
- The argument that England is fundamentally distinct from Britain can feel strained, especially for the post-1707 period
- Tombs's anti-declinist thesis sometimes tips into complacency — there are genuine problems with British governance and economy that he downplays
- Coverage of the 20th century is inevitably more compressed than earlier periods
- The book is written from a broadly conservative perspective — sympathetic to institutions, sceptical of revolution, relatively uncritical of empire

### Cross-References to Vault
- **[[Sapiens - Yuval Noah Harari]]:** Broader framework for agricultural revolution and empire — Harari and Tombs agree that the nation-state is a relatively modern construct, but Tombs argues England is an exception
- **[[The Changing World Order - Ray Dalio]]:** Dalio's rise-and-fall framework maps onto Tombs's story, but Tombs would push back hard against Dalio's "decline" framing for Britain
- **[[The Dictator's Handbook - Bruce Bueno de Mesquita]]:** Selectorate theory explains why English kings needed Parliament (small winning coalition requiring taxation to maintain) — Tombs's account of Parliament's origins is essentially this argument in historical form
- **[[Strategy A History - Lawrence Freedman]]:** Freedman's account of strategy connects with Tombs on military history — especially the World Wars chapters

## Summarisation Notes

### Recommended Structure
Follow the book's own chronological flow — it is already well-structured. The seven-part structure works well:
1. Origins (Prelude + Part One): The birth of England as idea and kingdom
2. Medieval (Part Two): The English unleashed — society, law, wars
3. Reformation and Revolution (Part Three): The great divide
4. Enlightenment and Empire (Part Four): Making a new world
5. The Victorian Age (Part Five): The English century
6. The World Wars (Part Six): The new dark age
7. Modern England (Part Seven): The age of "decline"
8. Conclusion: Tombs's verdict

### Key Content That MUST Be Preserved
1. The argument that England is one of the world's oldest nations (not just kingdoms)
2. The history/memory duality — how the English remembered their past shaped their present
3. The Norman Conquest as the most transformative event
4. The Reformation driven by Henry VIII's personal obsessions
5. The anti-declinist thesis — this is Tombs's most distinctive and provocative argument

**British History** — this book is the backbone of the entire British History section. It is the first book on the reading list and the reference spine for all subsequent books.
