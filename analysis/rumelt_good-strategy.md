# Analysis: Good Strategy Bad Strategy — Richard Rumelt

## Central Thesis

Strategy is not ambition, goals, vision, or motivation. It is a coherent response to a challenge. Good strategy contains a **kernel** with three elements: (1) a **diagnosis** that simplifies the overwhelming complexity of reality by identifying critical aspects, (2) a **guiding policy** that channels effort without specifying every action, and (3) **coherent actions** that are coordinated and focused to carry out the policy. Bad strategy is not simply the absence of good strategy — it is an identifiable pathology with four hallmarks: fluff, failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, and bad strategic objectives. Most organizations do not have good strategy because leaders are unwilling to make the hard choices that focus requires, because template-style planning substitutes ritual for thinking, and because the New Thought doctrine of positive mental attitude makes real analysis seem unnecessary. The book argues that good strategy is rare, surprisingly powerful when it appears, and accessible to anyone willing to do the hard intellectual work of diagnosing problems and designing focused responses.

## Structural Map

The book is organized in three parts plus an introduction:

**Introduction: Overwhelming Obstacles** — Nelson at Trafalgar as exemplar of focused strategy; four examples of bad strategy syndrome; definition of the kernel

**Part I: Good and Bad Strategy (Chapters 1-5)** — Establishes the distinction
**Part II: Sources of Power (Chapters 6-15)** — Ten building blocks of good strategy
**Part III: Thinking Like a Strategist (Chapters 16-18)** — How to develop strategic thinking skills

## Key Frameworks

1. **The Kernel of Good Strategy** — Diagnosis + Guiding Policy + Coherent Actions. The irreducible core of any strategy. Not vision, not goals, not mission statements.

2. **Four Hallmarks of Bad Strategy** — (1) Fluff: buzzwords masquerading as insight; (2) Failure to face the challenge: no diagnosis of the actual problem; (3) Mistaking goals for strategy: stating desires without plans; (4) Bad strategic objectives: blue-sky or dog's-dinner lists.

3. **Sources of Power** — Leverage (anticipation + pivot points + concentration), proximate objectives, chain-link systems, design, focus, growth, advantage, dynamics, inertia and entropy.

4. **The Design-Type Strategy** — Strategy as engineering fit among parts, not choosing from a menu. Tighter integration needed when resources are scarce or challenges are high. Resources and integration are partial substitutes.

5. **The Arc of Enterprise** — Powerful resource positions create profits without effort → success breeds laxity → laxity leads to decline → upstarts with tight design-type strategies exploit the opening → cycle repeats.

6. **Three Pathways to Bad Strategy** — (1) Unwillingness/inability to choose; (2) Template-style strategy (vision → mission → values → goals → strategies → initiatives); (3) New Thought / positive mental attitude doctrine.

7. **Wave-Riding Strategy** — Exploit exogenous waves of change by understanding the second-order effects. Look for shifts in technology, cost structures, buyer behavior that create new high ground.

8. **Attractor States** — In transitions, envision the industry's future stable state and position toward it, rather than trying to predict the exact path.

## Key Principles

1. **Good strategy is unexpected** — because most organizations don't have one; coherent, focused action is inherently surprising
2. **Strategy is about choice, and choice means saying no** — to actions, interests, goals
3. **A diagnosis is a judgment, not a proven fact** — it frames reality to enable action
4. **The guiding policy is not the strategy** — without diagnosis you can't evaluate it; without action it has no punch
5. **Coordination is the most basic source of strategic advantage** — unnatural, imposed by design, not emergent
6. **Proximate objectives absorb complexity** — leaders must pass solvable problems to the organization
7. **Chain-link systems get stuck** — incremental improvement in one link doesn't help; need centralized campaign
8. **Tight integration substitutes for resources** — the upstart's advantage over the incumbent
9. **Focus creates bargaining power** — Crown Cork & Seal's short-run strategy reversed buyer-supplier dynamics
10. **Growth is not a strategy** — healthy growth is a reward for creating value, not a goal to engineer through acquisitions
11. **Advantage is rooted in asymmetry** — press where you're strong, sidestep where you're not
12. **Software's advantage comes from the rapidity of the development cycle** — the cost of fixing mistakes, not the cost of engineers
13. **Inertia has three forms** — routine (doing things the old way), cultural (values and beliefs resist change), proxy (existing profit streams mask decline)
14. **Entropy is organizational** — without active management, organizations drift toward disorder
15. **Being strategic means being less myopic than others** — overcoming your own cognitive limitations

## Best Stories & Case Studies

1. **Steve Jobs at Apple (1997)** — Ch.1. Returned to bankrupt Apple, cut 15 desktop models to one, cut portables to one laptop, eliminated printers/peripherals, moved manufacturing to Asia, cut 80% of inventory. "Business 101" that was nonetheless unexpected. When asked about long-term strategy: "I am going to wait for the next big thing."

2. **Schwarzkopf's Left Hook (Desert Storm)** — Ch.1. The "Plan A" envelopment from the U.S. Army's own field manual was a surprise because no one expected coherent, focused execution from a complex organization. 250,000 troops secretly shifted west; 100-hour ground war.

3. **Wal-Mart's Network Insight** — Ch.2. Sam Walton didn't break the rule that a discount store needs 100K population. He redefined "store" — the unit wasn't the individual store but a network of 150 stores sharing logistics, data, and management. This shift from store to network was Wal-Mart's hidden power.

4. **David and Goliath** — Ch.2. Traditional strength/weakness analysis would favor Goliath. The story's power is the discovery of hidden strength — David's sling delivering force with precision at distance, neutering size advantages.

5. **Marshall and Roche's Competitive Strategy vs. the Soviets (1976)** — Ch.2. Instead of matching Soviet strengths, use U.S. technology advantage to impose asymmetric costs on the USSR. Invest in technologies expensive to counter but that don't increase Soviet offensive capability.

6. **Chad Logan's 20/20 Plan** — Ch.3. CEO with "key strategies" that were just goals (grow 20%, profit margin 20%). Referenced Jack Welch quotes about reaching for the impossible. Refused real strategic work. Hired a "Visioning" consultant who had them "think twice as big." Rumelt contrasts with WWI's Passchendaele — motivation without competent strategy.

7. **International Harvester's Elephant** — Ch.3. 1979 strategic plan ignored the fundamental problem: grossly inefficient work organization and worst labor relations in American industry. Classic failure to face the challenge. Lost $3B, closed 35 of 42 plants.

8. **DEC's Inability to Choose** — Ch.4. Three executives proposed three directions (Boxes, Solutions, Chips). CEO Ken Olsen couldn't choose. The "strategy" was a compromise that tried to do all three. Company subsequently collapsed.

9. **Kennedy's Moon Landing as Proximate Objective** — Ch.7. Not a visionary leap — it was carefully judged as feasible based on von Braun's analysis. The moon required a 10x jump in rockets, which leveled the playing field with the Soviets. The most audacious-seeming goal was actually the most proximate.

10. **Phyllis Buwalda's Lunar Surface Spec** — Ch.7. NASA engineers couldn't design a lander without knowing the moon's surface. Buwalda wrote a spec describing it like the Southwestern desert. "If it turns out to be a lot more difficult than this, we aren't going to be spending much time on the moon anyway." Brilliant absorption of ambiguity.

11. **Hannibal at Cannae (216 B.C.)** — Ch.9. The archetype of design-type strategy. Mock retreat drew Romans into compression trap. 50,000+ Romans killed in one day — premeditation, anticipation, and coordinated action in their purest forms.

12. **Marco Tinelli's Chain-Link Turnaround** — Ch.8. Lombardy machine company stuck in low-quality equilibrium. Three sequential campaigns (quality → sales → cost) with central direction, shutting down normal measurement/reward systems during each campaign.

13. **Crown Cork & Seal's Focus** — Ch.10. Not about "hard-to-hold" cans. Real strategy: focus on short runs. Reversed buyer-supplier bargaining power — instead of one customer with competing suppliers, Crown had multiple customers per plant. 50-60% more profitable than majors.

14. **Crown's Disastrous Growth Under Avery** — Ch.11. After Connelly died, new CEO doubled size through acquisitions, chasing growth. Stock fell from $55 to $5. Abandoned competitive advantage without replacing it.

15. **Nvidia's Rise (1993-2007)** — Ch.15. Failed first product, then redesigned strategy around 3-D graphics pipeline. Three overlapping dev teams produced new chips every 6 months vs. industry's 18-month cycle. Invested in emulation, unified driver architecture, bypassed board makers. Forbes "Company of the Year" 2007.

16. **Cisco's Software Insight** — Ch.13. The heart of Cisco's router was 100,000 lines of very skillfully written code by a tiny team. Rumelt's epiphany: software's advantage comes from rapid development cycles — the cost of fixing mistakes, not engineer salaries. This shifted competitive advantage from scale (IBM, AT&T) to small-team skill.

17. **Morgan Stanley's "Economies of Mass"** — Ch.11. Investment banker justified Telecom Italia/Cable & Wireless merger with invented concept. When pressed, admitted real rationale: "With more cash flow you can do a bigger deal." Fee-driven advice masquerading as strategy.

18. **The Helicopter Pilot's Hierarchy of Skills** — Ch.7. PJ explains: you must make flying automatic before you can fly at night; fly at night before flying in formation; master formation before landing on a rolling ship. What is proximate for one organization depends on which lower rungs it has mastered.

## Critique

**Strengths:**
- Extraordinary clarity in distinguishing genuine strategy from the noise of goals, visions, and buzzwords
- The "kernel" framework is elegantly simple and immediately applicable
- Case studies span military history, national security, tech startups, and consumer goods — demonstrating the framework's universality
- Rumelt's willingness to name bad strategy directly (including from clients and governments) gives the book unusual intellectual honesty
- The chapter on thinking (Ch.17) is rare — most strategy books discuss what to think about, not how to think

**Weaknesses:**
- The book is heavily weighted toward diagnosis and criticism of bad strategy; less guidance on the creative act of designing good strategy
- Some case studies are dated (Cisco's 1990s rise, DEC's collapse) though the principles remain valid
- The "Sources of Power" section (Part II) reads as a collection of loosely related chapters rather than a tightly integrated framework
- Limited treatment of strategy in team/organizational contexts — Rumelt writes primarily from the perspective of a lone strategist or leader
- No systematic process offered — Rumelt explicitly rejects templates, but offers little structured alternative beyond "think harder"

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Introduction: Overwhelming Obstacles
- Nelson at Trafalgar (1805): outnumbered 33-27, broke fleet into two columns, hit Franco-Spanish line perpendicularly. Lost zero ships; enemy lost 22.
- Four examples of bad strategy: corporate retreat with balloons but no strategy; Lehman Brothers' "risk appetite" as strategy; Iraq insurgency without strategy; Web 2.0 CEO saying "Strategy is never quitting until you win"
- Defines the kernel: diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent action
- Strategy is not vision, not goals, not ambition — it is a coherent response to a challenge

### Chapter 1: Good Strategy Is Unexpected
- Apple 1997: Jobs cut to core, simplified product line, moved manufacturing offshore. Unexpected because coherent focus is rare.
- Electronics industry study: 26 executives could describe competitor strategies (seize windows of opportunity) but their own strategies were "doorknob polishing"
- Jobs: "I am going to wait for the next big thing"
- Desert Storm: Schwarzkopf's left hook was the Army's "Plan A" (envelopment from FM 100-5) — surprising because complex organizations rarely focus
- Key insight: good strategy is unexpected not because it's secret, but because focused coherent action from complex organizations is rare

### Chapter 2: Discovering Power
- David and Goliath: discovering hidden asymmetry. Traditional analysis would favor Goliath.
- Wal-Mart: network replaced the store as unit of management. Walton didn't break the conventional wisdom; he redefined "store." Network of 150 stores serving millions.
- Kmart's failure: decentralized each store → couldn't benefit from integrated network. Breaking with doctrine (decentralization) is harder than adopting new technique.
- Marshall & Roche (1976): competitive strategy against USSR. Use U.S. strengths to impose asymmetric costs. Technology investments expensive to counter. This contributed to Soviet overextension and collapse.

### Chapter 3: Bad Strategy
- Four hallmarks defined: fluff, failure to face the challenge, mistaking goals for strategy, bad strategic objectives
- Fluff: "customer-centric intermediation" = "we are a bank." Arthur Andersen's Enron bandwidth trading presentation.
- Failure to face the challenge: International Harvester's 1979 plan ignored grossly inefficient work organization. DARPA as counterexample — diagnoses the challenge directly.
- Mistaking goals for strategy: Chad Logan's 20/20 plan. Goals without action plan. Passchendaele analogy — motivation without competent strategic leadership.
- Bad strategic objectives: "dog's dinner" lists (47 strategies, 178 action items); blue-sky objectives (LAUSD wanting "transformational leadership" in broken bureaucracy)

### Chapter 4: Why So Much Bad Strategy?
- Three pathways: (1) unwillingness to choose (DEC's Boxes/Solutions/Chips paralysis), (2) template-style strategy (vision → mission → values → goals → strategies → initiatives), (3) New Thought / positive thinking doctrine
- DEC: three executives, three directions, unable to choose. "Strategy" was a compromise accommodating all three.
- Template strategy arose from charismatic leadership literature → transformational leadership → fill-in-the-blanks
- New Thought: from Emerson through Norman Vincent Peale to modern motivational speakers. "You only have to want it badly enough." Makes strategy seem superfluous.

### Chapter 5: The Kernel of Good Strategy
- Diagnosis: "What's going on here?" Simplifies reality. Starbucks example: is it overexpansion, eroding advantage, or a search for new platforms? Each diagnosis opens different action domains.
- Kennan's "long telegram" as diagnosis: Soviet Union not an ordinary nation-state, antagonism foundational to regime. Led to containment policy.
- Gerstner at IBM: changed diagnosis from "too integrated" to "uniquely integrated." Shifted from hardware platforms to IT consulting.
- Guiding policy: not a goal. Wells Fargo vision vs. cross-selling policy. Stephanie's grocery store: "serve the busy professional with little time to cook."
- Coherent action: the punch in strategy. European Business Group Pan-European initiative: nine months to decide to actually reorganize. "Only if it were really important."
- Ford's incoherence: bought Jaguar and Volvo for brand value, then put both on common platform (destroying brand value). Two policies in conflict.
- Coordination as centralized direction: FDR's WWII — focus Europe first, use production to support USSR. Not emergent from decentralized decisions.

### Chapter 6: Using Leverage
- Strategic leverage = anticipation + pivot points + concentration
- Anticipation: Toyota invested $1B in hybrids anticipating fuel economy pressure. Iraqi insurgents anticipated media coverage of U.S. casualties (had Arabic translation of Black Hawk Down).
- Pivot points: Pierre Wack at Shell anticipated oil crisis. 7-Eleven Japan identified micro-location as pivot. Brandenburg Gate as pivot for German reunification.
- Concentration: Harold Williams at the Getty — focused all resources on becoming world presence in art rather than spreading across many activities.

### Chapter 7: Proximate Objectives
- Kennedy's moon landing: judged feasible by von Braun. Soviet lead in heavy rockets neutralized because moon required much larger rockets neither nation had.
- Phyllis Buwalda's lunar surface spec: resolved ambiguity so engineers could work. "If it turns out to be much more difficult, we aren't going to be spending much time on the moon anyway."
- Business school example: forced to pick one feasible objective → "get students into better jobs" → select 10 target firms → create programs to meet their needs
- Helicopter pilot hierarchy: skills layer — what's proximate depends on which rungs you've mastered. Start-ups can't focus on Europe before mastering basic operations.
- More dynamic = more proximate objectives needed. Chess: "improve my position" not "checkmate in 12."

### Chapter 8: Chain-Link Systems
- Challenger O-ring: no point strengthening other links if weakest link fails
- Quality matching: if you manage one link, no point improving if other links don't. GM 1980-2008: better transmission meaningless if knobs fall off.
- Getting stuck: incremental improvement doesn't pay. Marco Tinelli's three sequential campaigns. Shut down normal measurement during each campaign. Cost reduction last because quality and sales competence needed first.
- IKEA as flip side: excellence in chain-link system is hard to replicate because competitor must match all links simultaneously.

### Chapter 9: Using Design
- Hannibal at Cannae: premeditation, anticipation, coordinated action. 50,000+ Romans killed. Father of strategy.
- BMW analogy: forty or fifty parameters, finding the "sweet spot." Design involves mutual adjustment, not choosing from a menu.
- Voyager spacecraft at JPL: 1,200-pound weight constraint forced tight integration. Systems engineering = holding all interactions in your mind.
- Trade-off principle: resources and tight integration are partial substitutes. More resources → less integration needed. Less resources → need clever, tight design.
- Arc of Enterprise: powerful resources → profits without effort → laxity → decline → upstarts with tight design exploit opening.
- Paccar trucks: quality leadership through focus on owner-driver, custom builds, smaller plants with multiple customers. 16% ROE vs. 12% industry average. Not magic — classic "hold the high ground" positioning.

### Chapter 10: Focus
- Crown Cork & Seal: conventional explanation (hard-to-hold applications) is incomplete. Real strategy: focus on short runs.
- Working backward from policies to target: technical assistance → smaller businesses; rapid response → rush orders; smaller plants with multiple customers → shorter runs → reversed bargaining power.
- Crown's price per can ~40-50% higher than majors. But captured larger fraction of value created.
- Focus has two meanings: coordination of policies for extra power, and application of that power to right target.

### Chapter 11: Growth
- Crown under Avery: doubled size through acquisitions. Stock $55 → $5. Left competitive advantage behind without replacing it.
- Growth in commodity industry: profits are illusion — plowed back into capacity. When growth slows, profits vanish for firms without advantages.
- Morgan Stanley banker: merger rationale = "with more cash flow you can do a bigger deal."
- Healthy growth is reward for capability, not goal to engineer. Shows up as market share gain simultaneous with superior profit.

### Chapter 12: Using Advantage
- Wrestling the gorilla: textile start-up team wanted to build clothing company. Venture capitalist: "You've won gold in the 1,500m run. Now you want to switch to wrestling gorillas."
- Afghanistan: U.S. military designed for crushing high-intensity force spends $1M/year per soldier. Taliban has advantage of patience and insensitivity to casualties.
- Competitive advantage = lower cost OR more perceived value OR mix. Sustainability requires isolating mechanisms (patents, reputation, network effects, scale, tacit knowledge).
- Stewart Resnick: serial entrepreneur. "Interesting" businesses = ones where you can see ways to increase value. Nut farming became interesting at scale — large enough to invest in demand development.
- "Silver machine" puzzle: advantage alone doesn't make you richer. Must deepen advantage (bricklaying metaphor) or broaden it (Disney brand extension).
- Pomegranate juice: Resnicks built POM Wonderful brand around health research. Created demand for a product they dominated.

### Chapter 13: Using Dynamics
- Riding waves of change: exogenous shifts in technology, cost, competition create new high ground. Leader's job is to harness the wave.
- Historical perspective: 1875-1925 changes (electricity, automobile, airplane, telephone) were more transformative than recent decades.
- Jean-Bernard Lévy at Matra: Cisco's advantage was 100,000 lines of skillfully written code by a tiny team. Software became sharp source of advantage.
- Microprocessor as cause of computer industry deconstruction: smart components meant systems integration became trivially simple. Specialist firms could make and sell individual components.
- Cisco rode three waves: enterprise networking, Internet infrastructure, voice-over-IP. Each leveraged position gained from previous wave.
- Guideposts for transitions: rising fixed costs, deregulation, predictable biases in incumbent response, attractor states.
- Attractor state: envision where the industry must end up. New York Times → must become authoritative digital news source with subscription revenue.

### Chapter 14: Inertia and Entropy
- Inertia of routine: Continental Airlines' outdated routines. Gordon Bethune's turnaround: burn the policy manual, focus on on-time performance.
- Inertia of culture: AT&T couldn't transition from regulated utility culture to competitive market. Decades of selection and promotion for "regulated" personality.
- Inertia by proxy: PSFS savings bank kept paying dividends from profitable business while core eroded. DSL business delayed by telcos' profitable voice revenue.
- Entropy: without management attention, organizations drift toward disorder. Denton's restaurant chain: "hump chart" showing each outlet declining over time. GM's entropy across divisions and bureaucracy.
- Renewal requires breaking old routines, which is painful and politically costly.

### Chapter 15: Putting It Together — Nvidia
- Nvidia's rise illustrates nearly every source of power: diagnosis (3-D graphics = key PC performance frontier), guiding policy (push graphics pipeline integration faster than Moore's law, six-month release cycle), coherent action (three overlapping dev teams, emulation investment, unified driver architecture, bypass board makers via Dell).
- 3dfx tried proprietary standard (Glide). Nvidia bet on Microsoft DirectX.
- Three overlapping dev teams: each on 18-month cycle but staggered → new product every 6 months.
- Emulation investment: chip logic verification + electrical simulation + driver development before chips existed.
- Unified driver architecture: all Nvidia chips use same driver. Simplified user experience, took control from board makers.
- Dell as powerful buyer: helped Nvidia bypass Diamond Multimedia's retail channels.
- Competition failed: Intel tried twice (i740, Larrabee) but chip design culture favored cost reduction over performance. 3dfx went proprietary, couldn't keep pace.

### Chapter 16: The Science of Strategy
- Strategy as hypothesis and test (scientific method analogy). Howard Schultz at Starbucks as "scientist" — hypothesis about Italian espresso bar culture in America, tested with first store.
- Deduction insufficient when you don't know everything worth knowing. Must also use induction.
- Anomalies drive insight: why do Americans drink weak coffee? Anomaly led to Schultz's insight.
- Learning and vertical integration: sometimes you must integrate to learn what you don't know you don't know.

### Chapter 17: Using Your Head
- Fred Fletcher's baffling comment: "most useful conversation I've had all year" — Rumelt had only asked basic questions. Forcing reflection on purpose and priorities is itself valuable.
- Frederick Taylor and Carnegie: "Make a list of the ten most important things you can do. Then start doing number one." Value was in constructing the list, not the list itself.
- TiVo exercise: 17 executives each grabbed first solution that popped into their heads. Quick closure on first insight. Fear of going back to "choppy waters."
- Being strategic = being less myopic than your undeliberative self.
- Mind tools: the kernel (diagnosis/policy/action), problem-solution framework, create-destroy (systematically challenge your own ideas), panel of experts (imagine how different experts would approach the problem).

### Chapter 18: Keeping Your Head
- Independent judgment without eccentricity. Doubting without being curmudgeon.
- Global Crossing: built transatlantic fiber optic cable. "Build for $1.5B, sell for $8B." But worst industry structure imaginable — zero marginal cost of capacity means prices collapse.
- 2008 financial crisis: not a black swan. The pattern (extend credit in boom, crash when music stops) has repeated in 1819, 1837, 1873, 1893. Inside view and social herding blinded people.
- Gödel and stock prices: mathematical proof that some true things cannot be proven. Future stock prices are inherently unpredictable — yet the industry pretends otherwise.
- Key lesson: be skeptical of consensus, especially when everyone is making money. Look for the engineering margin of safety that is being consumed.

## Summarisation Notes

- **Target length:** 1600+ lines — this is a substantial argument book with rich case studies across military, business, and government domains
- **Structure:** Three-part argument naturally maps to: (1) What is good/bad strategy, (2) Sources of strategic power, (3) How to think strategically
- **Tone:** Rumelt writes with unusual directness and intellectual honesty. He names names (Chad Logan, Arthur Andersen, LAUSD). The summary should preserve this directness.
- **Case studies:** The book's power comes from its stories. Every concept is illustrated with at least one vivid case study. These must be preserved in detail.
- **Key diagrams:** The kernel (diagnosis → guiding policy → coherent actions), four hallmarks of bad strategy, sources of power cascade, arc of enterprise, chain-link logic, design trade-off
- **Formatting notes:** Many principles come as contrasts (good vs. bad, focus vs. diffusion, diagnosis vs. goals). Tables and mermaid diagrams work well for these.
- **Wikilinks:** Rich cross-reference potential with other strategy books, decision-making books, and cognitive bias treatments in the vault
