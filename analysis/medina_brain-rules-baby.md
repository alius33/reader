# Brain Rules for Baby — John Medina
## Classification & Context
Science translation / evidence-based parenting guide. Medina is a developmental molecular biologist and affiliate professor of bioengineering at the University of Washington. He is the author of the bestselling Brain Rules series. This book applies peer-reviewed neuroscience research to parenting from pregnancy through age five. Medina is rigorous about evidence standards — he only includes findings that have been replicated in peer-reviewed journals, published in refereed literature, and ideally confirmed by independent research groups. Written with humour and real parent anecdotes.
## Central Thesis
Parents dramatically overestimate how much they know about brain development and dramatically underestimate their influence. There are no commercial "brain-boosting" products proven to work. What actually builds smart, happy brains is four things during pregnancy (weight management, balanced nutrition, exercise, stress reduction) and a handful of evidence-based principles after birth centred on safety, empathy, talking/reading, and disciplined sleep. The four seeds of intelligence are: breastfeeding, talking to your child, guided play, and praising effort (not intelligence). The recipe for emotional health is: a demanding but warm parenting style ("authoritative"), empathy training, and making friends with the child's emotions rather than dismissing them.
## Key Frameworks
- Seed and Soil: nature (genetics, ~50%) provides the seed; nurture (environment) provides the soil. Parents control the soil.
- Four Things That Help In Utero: healthy weight gain, balanced nutrition (folic acid, omega-3s), moderate exercise, stress management
- Four Things That Hurt In Utero: excessive stress (toxic cortisol damages baby's brain), alcohol (there is no known safe amount), smoking, excessive weight gain
- Smart Baby: intelligence is about executive function (impulse control, working memory, cognitive flexibility), not IQ score. Best predictors of academic success: self-control and desire to explore.
- Happy Baby: emotional regulation is the key to happiness. Parents who acknowledge and label children's emotions (emotion coaching) raise more emotionally intelligent children. Dismissing or punishing emotions backfires.
- The Empathy Reflex: describe the emotional state, make a guess at its source, and then pause. This is the most powerful tool for emotional development.
- Authoritative Parenting: demanding + warm. Clear rules + emotional responsiveness. Consistently produces the best outcomes across every measure.
- Praise Effort Not Intelligence: Carol Dweck's research — children praised for being "smart" develop fragility; children praised for effort develop resilience.
- Talking and Reading: the quantity and quality of words directed at a child is the strongest predictor of vocabulary, IQ, and academic performance.
- TV/Screen Time: zero benefit before age 2. Every hour of TV per day before age 2 increases the risk of attention problems by 10%.
