# The Second American Civil War

## Core Question

Is a second American Civil War likely, what form would it take, and what would be the outcome?

Prof. Jiang argues that three structural forces make civil war virtually inevitable: (1) the over-militarisation of American society, where weapons saturation and uncontrollable special forces mean anyone can start a war; (2) the breakdown of the national narratives that bound America together — the American Dream, American goodness, and liberalism are all dead; and (3) the collapse of every trusted institution — government, media, science, military, universities, and courts. Combined with America's deep cultural addiction to violence and a 250-year history of resolving conflicts through war rather than diplomacy, these forces make a violent fracture unavoidable. The civil war will not resemble the 1861 conflict but will be a chaotic series of overlapping violent episodes — riots, civil conflicts, state secessions, insurgencies, and special forces coups — unfolding over 10-50 years. The most likely outcome, Prof. Jiang argues, is that America becomes a Christian, isolationist theocracy, because the people most willing to fight and die are Christian nationalists embedded in law enforcement, the military, and special forces.

## Lecture Type

Geopolitical-structural analysis of American domestic fragmentation. This lecture returns to the domestic American arc from Lectures 3, 5, and 6, synthesising their themes (empire, culture wars, institutional decay) into a prediction of civil war. It also connects the domestic crisis to the Iran war arc — Trump's likely war with Iran would both feed and accelerate the civil war.

## Central Thesis

America's second civil war is structurally inevitable because three forces — the over-militarisation of society, the death of binding national narratives, and the collapse of every trusted institution — combine with a 250-year cultural addiction to violence and a pattern of resolving conflicts through war rather than diplomacy. The civil war will not be a single event but a chaotic, overlapping series of violent episodes (riots, civil conflicts, state secessions, insurgencies, special forces coups) unfolding over 10-50 years. The trigger will be Donald Trump's return to power and his likely war with Iran — connecting the domestic fracture to the international conflict arc of the entire Geo-Strategy series. The most probable outcome is that America becomes a Christian, isolationist theocracy, because the people most willing to fight and die are Christian nationalists embedded in the military and special forces. When this civil war consumes America, it will retreat from the world, creating the multipolar future that Lecture 12 will explore.

The chain of reasoning:
1. America is culturally addicted to violence — guns, football, war worship
2. America has been continuously at war since 1776 — revolution, Manifest Destiny, Civil War, and onwards
3. Even the first Civil War was unnecessary — they chose violence over compromise
4. Today's divisions (inequality, culture wars, empire vs. democracy) are deeper than 1861
5. Three structural forces make violent fracture inevitable: over-militarisation, dead narratives, collapsed institutions
6. Special Forces are the most dangerous variable — uncontrollable, extremely powerful, ideologically extreme
7. Trump embodies and accelerates every division — his return guarantees escalation
8. Trump will start a war with Iran (Nikki Haley, pro-Israel billionaires, deep state accommodation)
9. The Iran war dragging on creates the conditions for a 2028 constitutional crisis
10. Special Forces and deep state actors intervene to keep Trump in power — the civil war explodes
11. The most willing fighters are Christian nationalists → America becomes a Christian isolationist theocracy
12. America retreats from the world → multipolar future (Lecture 12)

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: America Is Culturally Addicted to Violence

**The theory:** America's relationship with violence is not incidental but constitutional — embedded in its founding documents, its culture, and its history. This means violence, not diplomacy, is the default conflict resolution mechanism.

**Evidence for:**
- 434 million guns — 1.3 per person — many semi-automatic

- America worships its war history as noble and sacred
- America has been continuously at war since 1776

**Evidence against:**
- [INFERENCE] America has had many peaceful periods and successful diplomatic achievements
- [INFERENCE] Gun ownership doesn't necessarily translate to willingness to use guns in civil conflict

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang treats this as axiomatic — the starting premise from which the entire argument flows. He doesn't entertain counterarguments.

### Argument 2: The First Civil War as Template

**The theory:** The first Civil War (1861-1865) demonstrates that Americans choose violence over compromise even when alternatives exist. The standard narrative (noble fight against slavery) is wrong — the real causes were economic and political, and the war was avoidable.

- The South seceded; Lincoln invaded. Both chose war over negotiation
- 800,000-1,000,000 dead — more than WWI and WWII combined for America

**Evidence against:**
- [INFERENCE] Some historians argue the war was morally necessary to end slavery
- [INFERENCE] The South's secession made peaceful resolution extremely difficult

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang uses this as proof that America's pattern is violence over compromise, and that official narratives (fighting to end evil) mask the true structural causes.

### Argument 3: Today's Divisions Are Deeper Than 1861

**The theory:** The fault lines dividing America today are more numerous and more irreconcilable than the single North-South divide of 1861.

- [INFERENCE] America survived deep polarisation before (1960s civil rights, Vietnam era)

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang treats the multiplicity of divisions as the key difference from 1861 — the first civil war had two sides; the second will have many factions, making it more chaotic and prolonged.
