# The Psychology of Money — Morgan Housel

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Argument with 20 short essay-chapters, each exploring one facet of how emotions and biases shape financial decisions.
**Author:** Morgan Housel is a partner at the Collaborative Fund and former columnist for the Motley Fool and Wall Street Journal. Known for blending financial history with behavioural psychology.

## Central Thesis
Doing well with money has little to do with intelligence and a lot to do with behaviour. Financial success is not a hard science — it's a soft skill where how you behave matters more than what you know. People make financial decisions at the dinner table based on their unique personal histories, worldview, ego, and emotions — not spreadsheets. The book's 20 chapters are 20 variations on this theme, each supported by historical anecdotes and behavioural science.

## Key Frameworks
- No One's Crazy (everyone's financial behaviour makes sense to them given their experience)
- Luck & Risk (they are siblings; can't have one without the other)
- Never Enough (Rajat Gupta / Bernie Madoff — insatiable greed destroys)
- Confounding Compounding (Warren Buffett's wealth is 99.7% after age 50)
- Getting Wealthy vs Staying Wealthy (offense vs defense; survival mindset)
- Tails, You Win (a small number of events drive the majority of outcomes)
- Freedom (controlling your time is the highest dividend money pays)
- The Man in the Car Paradox (no one cares about the car's owner; they imagine themselves in the car)
- Wealth Is What You Don't See (spending is the enemy of wealth)
- Save Money (the only factor you can control)
- Room for Error (margin of safety is the most important concept in finance)
- You'll Change (long-term plans are hard because future-you is a stranger)
- Nothing's Free (volatility is the price of admission, not a fine)
- You & Me (beware taking financial cues from people playing a different game)
- The Seduction of Pessimism (pessimism sounds smart; optimism sounds naive)
- When You'll Believe Anything (narratives beat data)

## Best Stories
1. Ronald Read (janitor who died with $8M) vs Richard Fuscone (Merrill Lynch exec who went bankrupt)
2. Rajat Gupta (had $100M, committed insider trading for more)
3. Warren Buffett (99.7% of net worth earned after age 50 — compounding)
4. Bill Gates and the Lakeside computer (1 in a million luck)
5. The Man in the Car Paradox (Housel as a valet seeing people admire cars, never drivers)
6. Post-WWII American expectations reset (everyone was poor together, then everyone got rich together)
