# How Evil Triumphs

## Core Question

Why does evil triumph in the world? Why do the powerful commit horrific acts — ritual sacrifice, mass violence, taboo-breaking — and not only get away with it, but use it to consolidate and maintain power? And what is the deeper metaphysical framework that explains how transgression generates cohesion and "divine energy"?

## Lecture Type

Theory-building / Multi-layered argument: combines contemporary geopolitical example → thought experiment → historical analogies → game theory → metaphysical philosophy (Kant, Hegel, Plato, Dante, Gnosticism)

## Structural Map

1. **Opening provocation (~0:00-9:00):** Prof. Jiang begins with what is happening in Gaza, framing it not merely as atrocity but as deliberate ritual sacrifice — drawing parallels with Aztecs, Phoenicians, and Romans. Argues the deliberate public nature of it is intentional: designed to unite the perpetrators by making them irreversible outcasts (the "river behind your back" military stratagem from Chinese history). The taboo becomes the point of no return.

2. **Monkey Island thought experiment (~9:00-20:00):** A thought experiment about 100 men from different backgrounds stranded on an island with flesh-eating monkeys. Demonstrates how extreme adversity produces: common language → founding myth/religion → rituals (including sexual bonding) → leadership selection (the one who sacrifices most, not the wisest) → cohesion → synchronicity → telepathy/hive mind → self-sacrifice instinct. Then: what happens when they return to the real world — they become a secret elite controlling society.

3. **Historical analogies — Sparta, Thebes, Macedonia (~20:00-30:00):** Three military societies that demonstrate the same principle:
   - Sparta: boys taken at 5-6, hazed by older boys → solidarity; mentored by older soldiers (sexual bonding) → hive mind; graduation mission of killing helots → ritual sacrifice builds ultimate cohesion
   - Thebes: copied Spartan system but made it voluntary — Sacred Band of 300 lovers became dominant military force
   - Macedonia: Philip II learned from Thebes, built greatest army; Battle of Chaeronea (338 BCE) — Sacred Band's last stand blocking Macedonian charge

4. **Game theory of coordination (~30:00-33:00):** In a world of a million competing individuals, the best strategy is secret coordination (cheating). Obvious coordination methods (family, religion, ethnicity, language) are visible and force counter-coordination. The secret strategy that enables invisible coordination is transgression.

5. **Transgression as power mechanism (~33:00-40:00):** The theory: greater transgression → greater cohesion → greater synchronicity. Escalation ladder: pranks → petty theft → worse acts → ultimate taboos (killing, incest). Each step feels empowering and liberating, is addictive, and forces secrecy which strengthens group bonds. Secret societies practice these rituals to maintain cohesion and access "divine energy." The visible leaders of society are "puppets" — the real power lies with secret groups bonded by transgression.

6. **Kant's epistemology (~40:00-44:00):** Introduces Kant's framework: no objective reality that we passively absorb; our brains are active filters adding space and time; the noumena (things-in-themselves) vs. phenomena (what we perceive). Three questions Kant creates: (1) What is the noumena? (2) Who gave us our filters? (3) How do we know we're seeing the same world?

7. **Hegel's Geist (~44:00-47:00):** Hegel's solution to Kant's three problems: the noumena is the Geist (spirit). The Geist = ghost (parallel spiritual reality) + geyser (expanding/becoming force) + gist (essence of things). The Geist gives us our filters and because we all access the same Geist, we see the same world.

8. **Plato, Dante, and Gnosticism — the monad (~47:00-55:00):** Where does the Geist come from? Plato and Gnosticism provide the answer: the Monad (the One/Nous) — a supreme spiritual sun. The Monad creates through emanation (breathing/vibrating), producing dyads which create layered realities. Earth is the outermost shell. Purpose of life: return to the Monad. Plato says through knowledge (specifically geometry). Dante says through love (accessible to everyone, not just elites). Evil = moving away from the Monad, embracing the material world. The powerful invert the model — they claim their god is good and the Monad is evil, using material world dominance as their framework.

9. **How transgression works in the metaphysical model (~55:00-58:00):** Transgression allows the powerful to "tune in" to the same frequency — like locking onto the same website on the internet. Shared evil acts align participants to a specific layer of the Geist, enabling synchronicity and coordinated thinking/action.

10. **Q&A (~54:00-end):** Student questions about pensioners from last class (power of elderly in Chinese society), the nature of evil in the monad framework (evil = denial of the system, moving away from the monad), free will (the monad gives free will because love requires trust), why different religions arrive at the same conception (because ideas come from the Geist, not the brain), Plato as elitist vs. Dante as egalitarian.

## Central Thesis

Evil triumphs because **transgression — the deliberate breaking of taboos — is the most powerful mechanism for creating group cohesion and synchronicity.** Those who transgress together become bound by secrets, energised by the feeling of breaking rules, and aligned in a way that makes them more coordinated than any group bonded by conventional means (family, religion, ethnicity). This is why secret societies practising extreme transgression end up controlling society — not because they are smarter, but because their bond is stronger.

The deeper metaphysical explanation (via Kant → Hegel → Plato/Dante/Gnosticism) is that reality is a multi-layered spiritual structure emanating from a supreme source (the Monad). Transgression allows practitioners to "tune in" to specific (lower) layers of this structure, achieving synchronicity. The powerful maintain their control by denying the spiritual reality and reinforcing the material world as the only reality.

Prof. Jiang presents two paths back to the Monad: Plato's path of knowledge (elitist — requires geometry/philosophy) and Dante's path of love (egalitarian — accessible to all). Evil, in this framework, is movement away from the Monad toward the material — and the powerful deliberately accelerate this because their power depends on the material world being the only perceived reality.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### Ritual Sacrifice
- Definition: The deliberate, public killing of enemies or innocents as a religious/political act
- How it works: Creates irreversibility (no going back), unites perpetrators through shared transgression, serves as public demonstration of power
- Examples: Aztec temples with thousands of skulls, Phoenician/Carthaginian child sacrifice, Roman triumph (parading and strangling enemies at Temple of Jupiter), Gaza
- First introduced: This lecture

### The "River Behind Your Back" Stratagem (背水一战)
- Definition: Chinese military strategy where an army retreats to a river, eliminating escape, forcing soldiers to fight to the death
- How it works: Removes the option of retreat, galvanises soldiers with survival instinct
- Application: Israel's crossing of the ultimate taboo (publicly killing children) as a point of no return that unites the population
- First introduced: This lecture

### Cohesion → Synchronicity → Hive Mind
- Definition: A progression from group unity to coordinated action to telepathic-like shared consciousness
- How it works: Shared extreme experiences → deep mutual understanding → ability to act in unison without verbal communication → instinctive self-sacrifice for the group
- Examples: Sports teams, mother's intuition about endangered child, soldiers jumping on grenades, Spartan warriors
- First introduced: This lecture (builds on concepts from earlier lectures)

### Transgression
- Definition: The deliberate breaking of taboos, social norms, and laws as a strategy for generating cohesion and power
- How it works: Shared guilt forces secrecy → secrecy forces trust → breaking rules feels empowering and liberating → empowerment is addictive → escalation to greater taboos increases bonding
- Escalation ladder: Pranks → petty theft → ... → ultimate taboos (killing, incest)
- Key insight: Greater transgression = greater cohesion = greater synchronicity
- First introduced: This lecture

### Kant's Epistemological Framework
- Noumena: things-in-themselves (objective reality we can never directly access)
- Phenomena: things as we perceive them (filtered through our minds)
- Filters: space and time, added by our brains — do not exist outside us
- Three unsolved problems: (1) What is the noumena? (2) Who made our filters? (3) Why do we see the same world?
- First introduced: Referenced from "first class" (Lecture 1), expanded here

### Hegel's Geist
- Definition: Spirit — the spiritual reality underlying the material world
- Etymology: Ghost (parallel reality) + Geyser (expanding force) + Gist (essence)
- Solves Kant's three problems: (1) Noumena = Geist, (2) Geist gives us filters, (3) We share the same Geist
- First introduced: This lecture

### The Monad / Nous / The One
- Definition: The supreme spiritual source — like a divine sun — from which all reality emanates
- How it works: The Monad breathes/vibrates → creates dyads (pairs of forces) → dyads create layered realities → Earth is the outermost shell
- Origin: Plato, Gnosticism
- Purpose of life: Return to the Monad
- First introduced: This lecture

### Plato vs. Dante — Two Paths to the Monad
- Plato: Return through knowledge, specifically sacred geometry — elitist (only the educated can access)
- Dante: Return through love — egalitarian (everyone can love, therefore everyone has access)
- The Monad gives free will because love requires trust; free will enables evil, but the Monad cannot interfere without destroying love
- First introduced: This lecture

### Evil in the Monad Framework
- Definition: Movement away from the Monad toward the material world; denial of the spiritual reality
- How the powerful use it: They invert the model — claim their god is the good one and the Monad is evil; maintain power by keeping everyone focused on material reality
- Science as tool of evil: Science validates material world and denies spiritual world, serving the interests of the powerful
- First introduced: This lecture

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### 1. Roman Triumph
- Who: Roman generals and captured enemy leaders
- What: After major wars, Romans paraded captured enemies through the streets of Rome, then strangled them at the Temple of Jupiter
- When: Throughout Roman Republic and Empire
- Where: Rome, Temple of Jupiter
- Why: Human sacrifice disguised as military ritual — religious offering to Jupiter
- Outcome: Maintained Roman cohesion and demonstrated absolute power
- Supports: Argument that ritual sacrifice is a recurring feature of powerful civilisations

### 2. Aztec Temple Skulls
- Who: Aztec priests and warriors
- What: Mass human sacrifice before wars — thousands of skulls found in temples
- When: Pre-Columbian era
- Where: Mesoamerica
- Why: Religious ritual sacrifice to empower the warriors and please the gods
- Supports: Same pattern as Romans and others — ritual sacrifice as power mechanism

### 3. Spartan Agoge System
- Who: Spartan boys aged 5-6 through 18
- What: Three-phase education: (1) Hazing phase — older boys (10-11) brutalise younger boys, building solidarity; (2) Mentorship phase — older soldiers (28-30) take pubescent boys as lovers, building hive mind; (3) Graduation — boys hunt helots (slaves) at night and slit their throats
- When: Ancient Greece (c. 7th-4th century BCE)
- Where: Sparta, Laconia
- Why: Creates the ultimate military cohesion through shared suffering, intimate bonding, and transgressive violence
- Outcome: Made Sparta the dominant military power in Greece despite being universally hated by other city-states
- Supports: Both the cohesion argument and the transgression argument — Sparta was the purest example

### 4. Sacred Band of Thebes
- Who: 300 soldiers, all lovers (paired in couples), voluntary enlistment
- What: Thebes copied the Spartan system but opened it to all citizens, not just elites; the Sacred Band served as the vanguard in battle
- When: 4th century BCE
- Where: Thebes, Boeotia
- Why: Lovers fight harder because they fight for each other — not for abstract patriotism but for the person next to them
- Outcome: Became dominant military power after Sparta's decline; at Battle of Chaeronea (338 BCE), made a last stand to cover the retreat of Theban and Athenian forces, fighting to the last man against Philip II of Macedon
- Supports: The cohesion/synchronicity argument — voluntary bonding through love produces the same military effectiveness as Sparta's coercive system

### 5. Battle of Thermopylae (480 BCE)
- Who: 300 Spartans led by King Leonidas, backed by other Greek soldiers
- What: Held the pass at Thermopylae against the massive Persian invasion; fought to the death; Leonidas's head cut off and placed on a pike
- When: 480 BCE
- Where: Thermopylae, Greece
- Why: Demonstrates ultimate sacrifice and how it inspires broader resistance
- Outcome: All 300 killed, but their sacrifice inspired Greece to repel the Persian invasion
- Supports: Leadership through sacrifice — the willingness to die is what makes a leader legitimate

### 6. Battle of Chaeronea (338 BCE)
- Who: Macedonia (Philip II) vs. combined force of Thebes and Athens
- What: Macedonia destroyed the Theban-Athenian alliance; the Sacred Band made a last stand in the centre of the field to cover the retreat
- When: 338 BCE
- Where: Chaeronea, Boeotia
- Why: Macedonia had copied and improved the Spartan/Theban system of military bonding
- Outcome: End of Greek independence; beginning of Macedonian dominance
- Supports: The system of cohesion through bonding and transgression can be iterated and improved upon

## Student Q&A Insights

### Q: What is the "river" for Israel?
- Prof. Jiang's answer: The taboo itself is the river — killing children in public is the ultimate taboo in modern society. Once crossed, there is no return. You either go all the way or the world comes and destroys you.

### Q: Why do governments benefit the elderly? (from previous lecture)
- Prof. Jiang's answer: In Chinese society, respect for elders is foundational — the elderly control culture and government, so policies naturally serve them. This is the structure of Chinese society itself.

### Q: How does sacrifice relate to the Monad model?
- Prof. Jiang's answer: Sacrifice is not personal — it is the consummation of a religion. The practitioners worship a specific god and send sacrificed people to that god. This accesses a lower part of the Geist structure. There are evil beings in the model too.

### Q: If evil creates love/cohesion among perpetrators, doesn't that bring them closer to the Monad?
- Student Amber's sophisticated question. Prof. Jiang clarifies: our current society is fundamentally "anti-love" — it does not allow normal human relationships to flourish (school forces parents to care about grades rather than happiness, denying children's free will). The denial of free will is anti-love, and therefore evil.

### Q: Why does the Monad have two meanings?
- Prof. Jiang's correction: The Monad has ONE meaning — it is the source of everything, the divine sun. It has different NAMES (Monad, Nous, the One). Plato and Dante debate HOW to return to it, not WHAT it is. Plato = knowledge/geometry (elitist). Dante = love (egalitarian — "the most ridiculous" to deny everyone access).

### Q: Why do different religions arrive at the same conception?
- Prof. Jiang's answer: Because ideas come from the Geist, not from the brain. Neuroscience cannot explain where thoughts come from — the Geist can. Different people in different places access the same spiritual reality through inspiration.
