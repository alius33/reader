# The Prince — Niccolo Machiavelli

## Classification & Context

- **Genre:** Political treatise / argument-polemic
- **Written:** 1513, published posthumously 1532
- **Context:** Machiavelli wrote this after losing his elevated government position when the Florentine Republic collapsed in 1512 under Medici attack. He was tortured, accused of conspiracy, released, and retired to his farm. The Prince was his bid to regain political relevance—dedicated to Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici (grandson of Lorenzo the Magnificent).
- **Historical backdrop:** Italy was fragmented into city-states, ravaged by French, Spanish, and Swiss invasions. Mercenary armies dominated warfare. The papacy was a major temporal power.
- **Category:** Power & Influence (primary), Wisdom & Big Ideas (secondary)
- **Tone:** Practical, unapologetic, empirical. Rejects idealism in favour of observed reality.
- **Translation:** Jonathan Bennett (Early Modern Texts, 2017) — modernised English, editorial clarifications in brackets.

## Central Thesis

Political power must be understood and exercised as it actually works, not as moralists wish it worked. A prince who wants to survive must learn when to act against conventional morality—when to be cruel, deceptive, and ruthless—because the world is populated by people who will not keep their promises and who respond more reliably to fear than to love. The successful prince combines *virtù* (ability, skill, decisive action) with an understanding of *fortuna* (circumstances beyond one's control), adapting his methods to the times while building foundations that can withstand misfortune.

## Structural Map

The book divides into two parts (division not Machiavelli's own):

### Part I: Kinds of Principality (Chapters 1–11)
- Ch 1: Taxonomy — hereditary vs. new, acquired by arms/fortuna/virtù
- Ch 2: Hereditary principalities — easy to hold (just don't offend)
- Ch 3: Mixed principalities — the real difficulties; Louis XII's errors; Romans as model
- Ch 4: Two types of government — Turkey (centralised) vs. France (feudal barons)
- Ch 5: Governing formerly free cities — destroy, occupy, or proxy-rule
- Ch 6: New principalities by one's own virtù — Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus
- Ch 7: Principalities by others' fortuna — Cesare Borgia as model (despite failure)
- Ch 8: Principality through wickedness — Agathocles and Oliverotto; well-used vs. badly-used cruelty
- Ch 9: Civil principality — nobles vs. people as power base; always choose the people
- Ch 10: Strength of principalities — fortification and self-sufficiency
- Ch 11: Ecclesiastical principalities — held by divine authority; the rise of papal temporal power

### Part II: Other Aspects of Political Power (Chapters 12–26)
- Ch 12-13: Military — mercenaries useless, auxiliaries dangerous, citizen armies essential
- Ch 14: Prince's military duties — constant study of war, terrain, history
- Ch 15: Praised/blamed qualities — reject idealism, learn when to act "immorally"
- Ch 16: Spending — better to be called a miser than to bankrupt the state
- Ch 17: Cruelty/mercy — better feared than loved; Hannibal vs. Scipio
- Ch 18: Keeping word — fox and lion; appear virtuous while prepared to be otherwise
- Ch 19: Avoiding hatred and contempt — the longest chapter; Roman emperors as case studies
- Ch 20: Fortresses and devices — six princely tools; "not being hated" beats any fortress
- Ch 21: Prestige — great enterprises, decisive alliances; Ferdinand of Spain
- Ch 22: Ministers — judge a prince by his advisers; three kinds of intellect
- Ch 23: Flatterers — controlled truth-telling; cabinet of wise men
- Ch 24: Why Italian princes lost states — indolence in peacetime
- Ch 25: Fortuna — river metaphor; match your temperament to the times; boldness wins
- Ch 26: Plea to liberate Italy — passionate call to the Medici to unite Italy

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Chapter 1 — Taxonomy
- All states are republics or principalities
- Principalities: hereditary or new; new ones are entirely new or grafted onto existing states
- Acquisition variables: prior freedom, means (own arms vs. others'), cause (fortuna vs. virtù)

### Chapter 2 — Hereditary Principalities
- Easy to maintain: just follow ancestral customs
- Duke of Ferrara survived Venetian and papal attacks because long-established
- Hereditary prince loved by default unless extraordinarily vicious

### Chapter 3 — Mixed Principalities (longest chapter in Part I)
- Annexed territories: same language = easy; different language = hard
- Key strategies: relocate personally, establish colonies (cheap), avoid garrisons (expensive and enraging)
- Become head of weaker elements, weaken the stronger
- Romans as model: colonies, friendly with weak, subdued strong, blocked foreign powers
- Foresee problems early — "hard to spot but easy to cure" vs. "visible to everyone but incurable" (tuberculosis metaphor)
- Louis XII's 6 errors: eliminated minor powers, strengthened the Church, brought in Spain, didn't relocate, didn't colonise, pulled down Venice
- General rule: whoever causes another to become powerful brings about his own ruin

### Chapter 4 — Government Types
- Centralised (Turkey): hard to conquer (no disloyal barons) but easy to hold (destroy ruling family, done)
- Feudal (France): easy to invade (always a disloyal baron) but hard to hold (barons lead revolts)
- Alexander's conquest of Persia was Turkey-type → successors held easily
- Roman provinces were France-type → required long occupation to erase local loyalties

### Chapter 5 — Governing Free Cities
- Three options: destroy, occupy personally, puppet government
- Republics remember freedom and will always rebel — safest to destroy them
- People used to a prince are easier to rule — they're habituated to obedience
- "In rebellions the rebels will always rally to the cry of Freedom!"

### Chapter 6 — New Principalities by Virtù
- Greatest examples: Moses, Cyrus, Romulus, Theseus
- Each found his opportunity in crisis (enslaved Israelites, oppressed Persians, etc.)
- Hard to acquire (resistance to innovation) but easy to keep
- "Nothing is more difficult to set up, more likely to fail, and more dangerous to conduct, than a new system of government"
- Armed prophets conquer; unarmed ones are destroyed (Savonarola)
- Hiero of Syracuse as lesser example — abolished old army, made new alliances

### Chapter 7 — Principalities by Others' Fortuna (Cesare Borgia)
- Those who float up on fortuna find it hard to stay up
- Cesare Borgia: model prince despite ultimate failure
- His method: broke the Orsini and Colonna factions, won over their supporters, created own army
- Ramiro d'Orco episode: installed a ruthless governor → restored order → then publicly executed him to shift blame and win popular love
- Four-part plan for long-term security: exterminate dispossessed lords, win Roman gentlemen, control college of cardinals, conquer more territory
- Failed only because father died while he was mortally ill — "extraordinary hostility of fortuna"
- Fatal error: allowed Julius II (a personal enemy) to become pope

### Chapter 8 — Principality Through Wickedness
- Agathocles: potter's son → army commander → killed senators → became king of Syracuse
- Not virtù (because wicked) and not fortuna (because self-made) — a third category
- Oliverotto da Fermo: murdered his uncle and leading citizens at a banquet → became prince → lasted one year before Borgia killed him
- Key distinction: cruelty well-used (all at once, then stopped) vs. badly-used (escalating over time)
- "Men should be treated well or utterly crushed"

### Chapter 9 — Civil Principality
- Prince chosen by nobles vs. chosen by people
- Nobles want to oppress; people want not to be oppressed — people's desires are "more honourable"
- Always better to have the people as your base — they're too numerous to fight, and they ask less
- Nobles can be made and unmade; the people are always there
- "He who builds on the people builds on mud" — true only for private citizens, not for real princes
- Prince must make citizens need the government at all times

### Chapter 10 — Measuring Principality Strength
- Can you field your own army, or must you hide behind walls?
- German cities as model: fortified, self-sufficient, year's supplies, military training
- People will support a besieged prince if they've already lost property in his defence — binding through shared sacrifice

### Chapter 11 — Ecclesiastical Principalities
- Acquired by virtù or fortuna, held by divine authority alone
- Unique: undefended states, ungoverned subjects — yet secure
- Rise of papal temporal power: Alexander VI → Julius II → Leo X
- Orsini/Colonna factions kept popes weak until Alexander destroyed them

## Key Frameworks

### 1. Virtù vs. Fortuna
- The dominant tension of the entire work
- Virtù: ability, skill, decisive action, adaptability — what you control
- Fortuna: luck, circumstances, the uncontrollable — the raging river
- Fortuna decides ~50% of outcomes; virtù handles the rest
- Prepare dykes and dams in calm times; match your temperament to the times

### 2. Fox and Lion
- Two modes of conflict: law (human) and force (beast)
- Fox: cunning, detecting traps, deception
- Lion: strength, scaring off wolves, intimidation
- Must master both; relying on one alone fails

### 3. The Cruelty Calculus
- Cruelty well-used: all at once, at the start, then diminishing → people recover
- Cruelty badly-used: starts small, escalates → creates permanent enemies
- "Men should be treated well or utterly crushed"

### 4. Appearance vs. Reality
- Prince must appear merciful, trustworthy, friendly, straightforward, devout
- Must actually be prepared to switch any of these off when necessary
- "Men judge by the eye rather than by the hand" — appearances > reality
- "Everyone sees what you appear to be, but few feel what you are"

### 5. People vs. Nobles as Power Base
- People want not to be oppressed (modest goal)
- Nobles want to oppress (ambitious goal)
- Always choose the people — too many to fight, ask less, more reliable
- The worst people will do is abandon you; nobles will attack you

### 6. Military Self-Sufficiency
- Own army > mixed army > mercenaries > auxiliaries
- Mercenaries: disloyal, cowardly when it counts, only care about pay
- Auxiliaries: obey someone else; their virtù is their danger
- "A principality that doesn't have its own army isn't safe"

### 7. The Tuberculosis Metaphor (Foresight)
- Problems are like tuberculosis: easy to cure when hard to diagnose, impossible to cure when obvious
- Wise rulers prepare in peacetime for wartime
- Romans fought wars early to prevent worse ones later
- "Never let yourself be driven off-course by your desire to avoid a war"

## Key Principles (15)

1. **Reality over idealism** — Study how men actually behave, not how they should behave
2. **Fear over love** — Men break bonds of love readily; fear of punishment is constant
3. **People as foundation** — The people's goodwill is the best fortress
4. **Cruelty front-loaded** — Do all necessary harsh acts at once, then shift to generosity
5. **Appearance management** — Appear virtuous while being prepared to act otherwise
6. **Military self-reliance** — Only your own army is reliable; mercenaries and auxiliaries will ruin you
7. **Foresight as survival** — Spot and treat problems when they're small; don't wait for crisis
8. **Adaptability** — Match your methods to the times; rigidity kills
9. **Decisive action** — Armed prophets succeed; unarmed ones perish
10. **Never empower rivals** — Whoever causes another to become powerful brings about his own ruin
11. **Avoid hatred and contempt** — These are the two things that actually destroy princes
12. **Fox and lion** — Master both cunning and force; neither alone suffices
13. **Ministers reveal the prince** — Judge a ruler by the quality of his advisers
14. **Control the narrative** — Never let flattery or uncontrolled truth-telling dominate
15. **Fortuna favours boldness** — Impetuous action beats cautious inaction more often than not

## Stories Worth Preserving

1. **Cesare Borgia and Ramiro d'Orco** — Installed a brutal enforcer to pacify Romagna, then had him publicly executed (cut in two, left on the piazza with a bloody knife) to win the people's love. Cruelty used, then theatre used to disown it.

2. **Oliverotto's Banquet** — Returned to his hometown claiming a ceremonial visit, threw a grand banquet for his uncle and civic leaders, then led them to a private room where soldiers emerged and slaughtered them all. Became prince of Fermo — for exactly one year.

3. **Agathocles the Potter's Son** — Rose from the lowest class to King of Syracuse through military talent and one morning of mass murder (assembled the people and senate, had soldiers kill all senators and the richest citizens). Held power for decades.

4. **Louis XII's Cascade of Errors** — Came to Italy, made six strategic mistakes in sequence (strengthened Church, brought in Spain, didn't relocate, didn't colonise, eliminated minor powers, pulled down Venice). Each error compounded the last.

5. **Machiavelli and the Cardinal of Rouen** — When the Cardinal said "Italians don't understand war," Machiavelli shot back that "the French don't understand politics" — predicting France's loss of Italy because they empowered the Church.

6. **David and Saul's Armour** — David rejected Saul's armour to fight Goliath with his own sling and knife. Metaphor: another's weapons won't fit you; fight with your own tools.

7. **The Roman Treatment of Greece** — Kept friendly with the weak, humbled the strong, and blocked foreign powers — the model of intelligent empire management.

8. **Septimius Severus** — A master of fox-and-lion: used the pretext of avenging Pertinax to march on Rome, tricked Albinus with fake co-emperor title, then destroyed him. Held power 18 years.

9. **Pope Julius II at Bologna** — Moved so fast that Spain, Venice, and France couldn't coordinate responses. Impetuous action where caution would have produced paralysis through negotiation.

10. **The Fortuna-as-River Metaphor** — Fortune is a flooding river; you can't stop it in flood, but in calm weather you can build dykes. Italy was "open countryside with no dams."
