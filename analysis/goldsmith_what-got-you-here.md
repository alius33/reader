# Analysis: What Got You Here Won't Get You There — Marshall Goldsmith

## Structure
Four sections: (1) The trouble with success, (2) The twenty habits that hold you back, (3) How we can change for the better, (4) Pulling out the stops. The core is the twenty habits taxonomy — each one gets a short chapter with coaching anecdotes.

## Core Arguments
- The habits that made you successful become liabilities at the next level
- The twenty habits are all **interpersonal** — not about competence, intelligence, or technical skills
- Successful people suffer from a "superstition" that their success came FROM their behaviour, when often it came DESPITE their behaviour
- The fix is not doing MORE but STOPPING — eliminating behaviours rather than adding new ones
- Feedback, apologising, following up, and feedforward are the change mechanics

## The Twenty Habits (Grouped)
**Need to Win:**
1. Winning too much
2. Adding too much value
3. Telling the world how smart we are

**Need to Be Right:**
4. Passing judgment
5. Making destructive comments
6. Starting with "No," "But," or "However"
7. Negativity ("Let me explain why that won't work")

**Need for Control:**
8. Withholding information
9. Failing to give proper recognition
10. Claiming credit we don't deserve

**Need to Please:**
11. Making excuses
12. Clinging to the past
13. Playing favourites

**Need for Status:**
14. Refusing to express regret
15. Not listening
16. Failing to express gratitude
17. Punishing the messenger

**Need to Be Me:**
18. An excessive need to be "me"
19. Goal obsession (pursuing goals at the expense of the larger mission)
20. Speaking when angry

## Strengths
- "Stop doing" framing is genuinely counterintuitive for high achievers
- Based on 150+ real CEO coaching engagements, not theory
- The "adding too much value" insight alone is worth the book — it explains why leaders demotivate their best people
- Feedforward concept (asking for suggestions about future behaviour) is practical and non-threatening

## Weaknesses
- The twenty habits overlap significantly — several are variations of "need to be right"
- Heavy anecdote padding — many stories make the same point
- Lacks structural depth — no model for WHY these habits emerge at specific career stages
- Light on implementation beyond "get feedback and apologise"

## Key Frameworks
1. **Success Delusion**: "I behave this way. I am successful. Therefore I am successful BECAUSE I behave this way."
2. **Adding Too Much Value**: A 5% improvement in an idea reduces the owner's commitment by 50%
3. **Feedforward**: Instead of feedback about the past, ask for suggestions about the future
4. **The Apology + Follow-up Loop**: Change requires public commitment and consistent follow-through

## Connections
- Peter Drucker's "stop doing" list maps directly to Goldsmith's thesis
- Jeffrey Pfeffer's power dynamics explain WHY some of these habits persist (they signal dominance)
- Kerry Patterson's Crucial Conversations provides the communication skills Goldsmith's clients lack
- Carol Dweck's growth mindset is the internal shift that enables Goldsmith's behavioural change
