# Analysis: Post-Traumatic Parenting — Dr. Robyn Koslowitz

## Classification

**Book type:** Practical manual for parents who carry childhood trauma. Blends personal memoir (Koslowitz's own trauma: father's death, chronic illness), clinical case studies, neuroscience (DMN, ECN, polyvagal), and actionable exercises.

**Central thesis:** Your trauma created a "trauma app" in your brain — an algorithmic response that fires automatically when triggered. This app hijacks your parenting, making your wounded inner child respond instead of your adult self. But your trauma also gave you a superpower — heightened empathy, pattern recognition, emotional depth. The path forward is not eliminating the trauma but integrating it: Accept what happened, Integrate it into your identity, and Make Meaning from it to become the parent you want to be.

**Key differentiator:** This is the ONLY book in the vault that directly addresses how trauma affects PARENTING. Other books explain the wound (Walker, van der Kolk) or break the cycle generally (Wolynn, Forward). Koslowitz provides the specific mechanics: what triggers look like during parenting, why you react the way you do to your child, and how to parent THROUGH the trauma without passing it on.

## Structural Map

### Part I: It's Not You; It's Your Trauma (Ch 1-3)

**Ch 1: Understanding Trauma** — Four types: trauma of absence (what didn't happen), trauma of commission (what was done), complex trauma, intergenerational trauma. Koslowitz's own story: father's chronic heart condition, his death when she was 17. "Trauma isn't only what happened to you. It's also what didn't happen for you."

**Ch 2: Your Inner Child Can't Raise a Child** — The "trauma app" concept: your brain created an algorithm based on past trauma that fires automatically. When triggered, your wounded inner child takes over parenting. The "self-parenting" exercise: parent your inner child WHILE parenting the real child. Inner child envy: "I wish I was my kid."

**Ch 3: How Trauma Shows Up in Parenting** — Triggers are automatic and CANNOT be controlled. The Default Mode Network (DMN) vs. Executive Control Network (ECN). DMN = automatic trauma response. ECN = present-moment focus. You can retrain the DMN by activating the ECN. Trigger tables by child's age (infant → high school). The straw experiment for understanding panic.

### Part II: Post-Traumatic Parenting Types (Ch 4-8)

**Ch 4: The Entangled Parent** — Over-identified with the child. Boundary-less. Child's emotions become parent's emotions. The parent who can't let the child be sad because it triggers THEIR childhood sadness.

**Ch 5: The Perfectionist Parent** — Must do everything right. Terrified of being a "bad parent." Rigid rules and expectations. Driven by shame of their own "imperfect" childhood.

**Ch 6: The Disengaged Parent** — Emotionally absent. Checks out during parenting. May be physically present but psychologically gone. Often dissociative.

**Ch 7: The Paralyzed Parent** — Frozen by fear. Can't make decisions. Terrified that any choice will harm the child. Analysis paralysis rooted in trauma.

**Ch 8: The Survivor Parent** — Still living in active trauma. Most resources going to basic survival, not growth. Often the parent dealing with poverty, domestic violence, or ongoing abuse.

### Part III: From Cycle Breaking to Cycle Making (Ch 9-12)

**Ch 9: Becoming the Parent You Want to Be** — The AIM Model: Accept → Integrate → Make Meaning. Accepting means naming the trauma without shame. Integration means incorporating trauma into a coherent self-narrative. Making Meaning means transforming trauma into parenting values.

**Ch 10: R2 Parenting** — Responsive (emotionally attuned to child) AND Responsible (maintaining structure and boundaries). PTPs tend to be strong in one but weak in the other.

**Ch 11: Post-Traumatic Parenting Hacks** — Practical, in-the-moment strategies.

**Ch 12: Bringing Our Whole Selves to Parenting** — Integration of all parts. The AIM model in practice. Mission statements. Mr. Rogers as the ultimate post-traumatic parent.

## Key Principles

### 1. The "Trauma App" runs your parenting
- Your brain created an algorithm based on past trauma: "if this, do that"
- When triggered (child's behaviour, a tone of voice, a time of day), the app fires
- Your wounded inner child takes over — reacting to THEIR childhood, not your child's current situation
- Example: Sarah's daughter Penny is late for school → Sarah's inner child (humiliated for dyslexia as a child) panics → Sarah screams at Penny instead of helping her

### 2. Five Post-Traumatic Parenting Types
- **Entangled:** boundaries dissolved, child's pain = your pain
- **Perfectionist:** rigid, shame-driven, "good enough" feels impossible
- **Disengaged:** checked out, physically present but emotionally absent
- **Paralyzed:** frozen, can't make decisions, terrified of harm
- **Survivor:** still in crisis, triage mode, survival over growth
- Most parents are HYBRIDS — different types activate with different triggers and different children

### 3. The AIM Model: Accept → Integrate → Make Meaning
- **Accept:** Name the trauma without shame. It happened. It wasn't your fault. It changed you.
- **Integrate:** Weave the trauma into a coherent self-narrative. "This happened AND I am a good parent."
- **Make Meaning:** Transform the trauma into parenting values. "My trauma taught me X, and that's what I'll give my child."
- Integration means rewriting your story from fairy tale (victim) to hero's journey (agent)

### 4. Your inner child's envy is a roadmap to your values
- It's normal to be envious of your own child having what you didn't
- The envy points to what you VALUE — if you're envious of it, you value it
- "If you value it, you can have it, even if you have it only by giving it"
- "As you give it, your inner child gets to experience it"

### 5. Triggers cannot be eliminated — but the DMN can be retrained
- DMN = automatic response ("loud = danger")
- ECN = present-moment focus (turns OFF the DMN)
- You retrain by activating the ECN when triggered: grounding, naming objects, focusing attention
- "You cannot blame yourself for your reactions to your triggers. It's like blaming yourself for gravity."

### 6. R2 Parenting: Responsive AND Responsible
- Responsive = emotionally attuned, validating, empathetic
- Responsible = maintaining structure, setting limits, saying no
- PTPs tend to over-index on one at the expense of the other
- Perfectionist/Entangled parents are over-responsive, under-responsible
- Disengaged/Paralyzed parents are under-responsive

### 7. Mr. Rogers as the model
- Bullied, fat-shamed, invalidated as a child
- Accepted his emotions, integrated his trauma, found meaning
- Created a world where children's emotions are normalized and validated
- "He hacked his trauma into his mission"

## Best Stories

1. **Koslowitz's Father** — Chronic heart condition. First scary episode when she was 5 — she believed it was her fault because he was swinging her to the top of the refrigerator. Neighbour's gardener cut down his rosebushes → father died → mother's grief was triggered years later by the same gardener cutting new roses.

2. **Sarah and Penny** — Sarah was humiliated for dyslexia as a child. When daughter Penny is late for school, Sarah's "trauma app" fires: she screams, calls names. Her inner child is responding to the childhood teacher, not to Penny.

3. **The Toy Aisle at Target** — Aaron demands toys. Parent's inner child gets triggered: "I never got any toys." Envy masquerades as discipline: "He needs to learn." The parent who can recognize the envy can parent from values instead.

4. **Sandy's Son Turns Six** — Sandy was 6 when his parents divorced. Profound sadness when his son reaches the same age. Sadness points to his value: family closeness. Creates mission statement and concrete plan.

5. **Koslowitz's Own Integration** — "I'm raising capable future adults, who are both independent humans and interconnected beings who support and value one another."

6. **Fred Rogers** — Bullied kid → became Mr. Rogers. Bodybuilding phase (trying to fix himself) → acceptance → integration → mission: "a world where children's emotions are talked about."
