## Main Arguments

1. **Career alignment with manager is non-negotiable** — Most engineers passively wait for year-end reviews instead of explicitly aligning on promotion goals, timeline, and expected ratings throughout the year.

2. **The surprise factor should be zero** — A good manager ensures no engineer is surprised at performance review time. Laurent used a paper-matching exercise to verify alignment.

3. **Calibration has local, unwritten rules** — Auto-meets for role changes, minimum tenure before promotion, and other rules are developed locally within orgs and are rarely communicated to ICs. Managers should be transparent and challenge rules they disagree with.

4. **Management style must be flexible** — One-size-fits-all coaching fails. Laurent explicitly references situational leadership (directing → selling/coaching → supporting → delegating) as the unlock.

5. **The senior-to-staff jump is a mindset shift** — Seniors solve problems others identify; staff engineers independently discover, pitch, and solve problems end-to-end. The full cycle of problem discovery is the distinguishing factor.

6. **Lines of code is a terrible productivity metric** — Instead, measure the journey of a code change (branch → merge) and identify where bottlenecks appear across the flow.

7. **Boredom is a career signal** — When you feel bored, something needs to change. Laurent used boredom as a trigger for his IC→manager→IC transitions.

8. **Leadership at scale means asking questions, not giving answers** — When leading 70–140 engineers, you know less than every individual about their domain. Influence through questions, not directives.

9. **Sustainability first** — At every assignment, Laurent designs for his own departure: documentation, automation, no single points of failure.

10. **Saying "I don't know" protects your credibility** — Claiming something wrong with confidence destroys trust permanently in tech culture. Admitting ignorance is far safer.

## Key Stories (8)

### 1. The Manager Test (Airbnb)
Laurent becomes manager of his peers. A direct report tells him: "I have a scenario. Based on your answer, I'll know if you're a good manager." The test: your report missed a scheduled interview — what do you do? Laurent passes by asking for the facts first instead of punishing.

### 2. The Stand-Up Nobody Wanted (Airbnb)
Laurent sends a 25-minute Google Form surveying every team process. Result: everyone thought the stand-up was useful for OTHER people but not themselves. Nobody actually found it useful. They changed the format and team health scores rose.

### 3. The Wedding Quarter (Airbnb)
Laurent leaves for 3 weeks for his wedding. Comes back to find the team planned the entire next quarter using his planning algorithm, assigned all projects, and didn't need him. He realised: "What am I doing here? I'm bored."

### 4. The Role-Change Surprise (Airbnb)
Laurent expects a promotion to staff as an IC but transitions to management mid-cycle. Gets an automatic "meets expectations" rating because of the untold rule: change roles with <2.5 months in the new role = automatic meet. He was blindsided because he hadn't had explicit conversations with his manager.

### 5. 4th of July On-Call at Instagram
Laurent is on-call for Instagram during July 4th at a new house. Keeps getting paged, running across the street to his laptop. Decides to pitch a project to bring Facebook's reliability tools to Instagram because Instagram engineers don't use them.

### 6. The Arianne Mentorship (Instagram/Meta)
Laurent identifies Arianne, a tech lead on the Facebook side, as someone who challenges him and changes his mind. He explicitly pitches her for mentorship. She helps him build the Instagram reliability project and becomes one of the most influential mentors of his career.

### 7. The Boot Camp Mentee (Facebook, 2014)
Laurent onboards 4 people at Facebook boot camp. Three do well, one makes no progress. The programme lead suggests situational leadership. Laurent discovers the struggling mentee had already read all the documentation — they were in the "low motivation, medium skill" bucket, not the "high motivation, low skill" bucket. Switching from directing to supporting unlocked their performance, and they became the most effective of the four.

### 8. The Friction Log Habit (Stripe)
At Stripe, Laurent uses friction logs systematically: go through any developer flow, take pictures of every step, document every friction point (50–60 per flow), categorise into families, rank, and solve. This becomes his primary method for identifying staff-level problems.

## Frameworks & Models

### 1. The Surprise Factor
- Before delivering performance review, both manager and employee write expected rating on paper
- Papers revealed simultaneously
- Match = pass, mismatch = fail
- Goal: drive to 100% match rate
- Positive surprises are also failures (means you under-coached)

### 2. Situational Leadership (Hersey & Blanchard, referenced)
Four phases mapped to learner state:
1. **Directing** — High motivation, low skill → Tell them exactly what to do
2. **Coaching/Selling** — Motivation dipping, skill growing → Encourage + make high-level decisions
3. **Supporting** — Moderate motivation, good skill → Step back, give less advice
4. **Delegating** — High skill, high motivation → Stay out of the way

### 3. The Code Change Journey (Dev Productivity Metric)
- Don't count lines of code or PRs
- Instead: map the full journey from branch creation to merge
- Measure duration at each phase (thinking, coding, CI, code review, iteration)
- Compare across orgs to find bottlenecks
- Reframes "is this person productive?" → "why is this person's flow slower?"

### 4. The Problem Discovery Cycle (Senior vs Staff)
- Senior: Solve problems others identify → Design solution → Execute
- Staff: Discover problem → Pitch problem → Solve problem → Document outcome
- The full cycle (discovery + pitch + solve + document) is what distinguishes staff

### 5. Friction Log Method
- Use your own product end-to-end
- Screenshot every step
- Annotate every friction point ("why a new window? why a loading screen?")
- Imagine doing it exhausted — where would you fail?
- Categorise frictions into families, rank by impact, solve top ones
