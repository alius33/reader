# Pre-Suasion — Robert B. Cialdini

## Classification & Context

**Book type:** Argument — a single thesis (the moment before the message matters more than the message itself) supported by research across 14 chapters.
**Audience:** Practitioners of influence (marketers, negotiators, leaders, salespeople) and general readers who read Influence and want the sequel.
**Author background:** Same as Influence — Cialdini is Regents' Professor Emeritus at ASU. This book draws on his continued research plus the explosion of behavioral science since 1984.

## Central Thesis

The best persuaders become the best through pre-suasion — arranging for recipients to be receptive to a message before they encounter it. What we present first changes how people experience what we present next. The key insight: it's not necessary to alter a person's beliefs or attitudes to change their behavior — you only need to change what's prominent in their mind at the moment of decision. This is accomplished through channeled attention, strategic associations, and privileged moments.

## Key Frameworks

- **Privileged Moments** — time-limited windows of heightened receptivity after a pre-suasive opener
- **Focusing Illusion** — nothing is as important as you think it is while you are thinking about it (Kahneman)
- **Channeled Attention** — what receives attention gains perceived importance AND causality
- **Associative Coherence** — mental elements fire when readied, not when ready; openers activate related concepts
- **Unity Principle** — the 7th weapon of influence: shared identity (We relationships) produces compliance
- **If/When-Then Plans** — self-manufactured pre-suasion for personal goals
- **Single-Chute Questions** — "Are you unhappy?" forces confirmatory search in one direction only

## Key Principles (12)

1. What you present first changes how people experience what comes next
2. The focusing illusion: attention = importance
3. What's focal is presumed causal (false confessions, lottery choices)
4. Single-chute questions bias self-assessment ("Are you helpful?" → 97% say yes → 77% then help)
5. Background cues shape decisions without awareness (clouds → comfort preference; pennies → price preference)
6. Metaphors transfer associations (crime as beast → catch/cage; crime as virus → treat causes)
7. Physical environments pre-suade (Cialdini wrote better at home desk facing pedestrians than at university desk facing academic buildings)
8. If/when-then plans manufacture pre-suasive moments for ourselves (80% of opiate addicts in withdrawal completed a resume vs 0% without the plan)
9. The six Influence principles work best as pre-suasive openers, not just direct appeals
10. Unity (7th principle): shared identity drives compliance more powerfully than liking
11. Acting together (singing, marching, cooking) creates unity even among strangers
12. Unethical pre-suasion backfires: it attracts dishonest employees who cheat the organization

## Best Stories

1. Jim the fire alarm salesman — pretended to forget materials so family would give him a key, associating him with trust
2. The $75K consultant — joked "I'm not going to charge you a million dollars" → eliminated all price resistance
3. Embedded reporters in Iraq — 93% of stories from soldiers' perspective, burying the question of whether the war was justified
4. Crime as beast vs virus — changing ONE word shifted policy preferences by 22%, more than gender (9%) or party (8%)
5. Cialdini's two desks — wrote poorly for general audience at university desk (saw academic buildings); wrote well at home desk (saw ordinary pedestrians)
6. Asian American women math test — recording gender reduced scores; recording ethnicity improved them
7. Opiate addicts and if/when-then plans — 80% completed a resume during withdrawal vs 0% without the plan
8. South Korean hostage negotiation — replacing translator with Pashtun-speaking negotiator created "strong intimacy" and won hostages' swift release
9. Coca-Cola "Share a Coke" — replacing branding with 150 common first names produced first sales increase in a decade
