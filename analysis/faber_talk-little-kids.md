# How to Talk So Little Kids Will Listen — Joanna Faber & Julie King
## Classification & Context
Toolkit / practical communication guide for parents of children ages 2-7. Joanna Faber is the daughter of Adele Faber (co-author of the classic *How to Talk So Kids Will Listen*) and grew up as a living test subject for these communication tools. Julie King is a parent educator and counselor. Together they adapted the original Faber & Mazlish approach for the specific challenges of toddlers and preschoolers — ages where language is limited, emotions are enormous, and "use your words" is sometimes asking the impossible.
## Central Thesis
When children don't feel right, they can't behave right. Most "behavior problems" in young children are actually communication problems — the child is experiencing big emotions they can't manage or express. The solution is not to suppress the behavior but to address the underlying feeling. Five core toolkits — for handling emotions, engaging cooperation, resolving conflict, offering praise, and adapting for neurodivergent children — give parents a practical, script-ready approach that replaces punishment with connection and cooperation.
## Key Frameworks
- Tool 1: Acknowledge Feelings — name the emotion, give it in fantasy, use art/writing/play; feelings that are denied get bigger; feelings that are acknowledged diminish
- Tool 2: Engage Cooperation — give information (not commands), offer choices, describe what you see, say it with a word or gesture, be playful, use "I" messages
- Tool 3: Resolve Conflict Without Punishment — express your feelings strongly, state your expectations, show how to make amends, offer a choice, take action without insult, problem-solve together
- Tool 4: Praise Descriptively — describe what you see instead of evaluating; let the child draw their own conclusion about their worth
- Tool 5: Adaptations for Differently Wired Kids — modifications for autism spectrum, sensory processing, ADHD; same principles, different implementation
- The Basics: sleep, food, exercise, and screens — conditions under which no communication tool will work
## Best Stories
1. The broccoli muffin — a child who refused all vegetables ate "broccoli muffins" after being given a choice and involved in the cooking; playfulness and autonomy changed the dynamic completely
2. The monster spray — a child terrified of bedtime monsters was given a spray bottle labeled "Monster Spray"; acknowledging the feeling (fear) and giving it in fantasy (monster spray works!) solved the problem
3. The shoe rebellion — a toddler who refused shoes every morning cooperated when the shoes were given a "voice" and asked to be put on ("I'm so cold! Please put me on your foot!")
4. The hitting brother — instead of time-out, the parent acknowledged both children's feelings, described the problem, and problem-solved: "It sounds like you were really frustrated. Hitting hurts. What else could you do when you're that angry?"
