# Analysis: Black and British — David Olusoga

## Classification & Context

- **Book type:** Argument-driven chronological narrative — traces the presence and role of Black people in Britain from Roman times to the present, arguing this history is central (not marginal) to British identity
- **Audience:** General readers; accessible to newcomers but substantial enough for scholars. Written alongside a BBC television series
- **Olusoga's approach:** Deeply personal (he opens with his own experience of racial violence in 1980s Newcastle), then transitions to a rigorously sourced narrative drawing on archaeology, parish records, legal cases, newspapers, paintings, personal accounts, and parliamentary debates. Combines individual stories with structural analysis
- **Scope:** Roman Britain (3rd century AD) to 2012 Olympics. Britain, West Africa, the Caribbean, North America, Sierra Leone. Fourteen chapters plus introduction and conclusion
- **Key sources discussed:** Peter Fryer's *Staying Power*, James Walvin's *Black and White*, Folarin Shyllon's *Black People in Britain*, Richard Hakluyt's *Principal Navigations*, radioisotope analysis of Roman-era remains, parish registers, newspaper advertisements, legal transcripts (Somerset case), parliamentary records, Mass Observation archives, Windrush-era oral histories
- **Plan note:** The counterbalance to Ferguson. Cross-ref extensively with Empire. Olusoga argues Black British history is not a footnote but central to British identity. Present his evidence and argument fully

---

## Central Thesis

Olusoga argues that Black people have been present in Britain for nearly two thousand years — since the Roman era — and that their history is not a marginal footnote but central to the story of Britain itself. The Black British story is inseparable from the histories of empire, slavery, abolition, industrialisation, two world wars, and post-war immigration. Britain's national wealth, its cities, its legal traditions, its moral self-image, and its cultural identity were all shaped by encounters with Africa and Africans.

The book demolishes the myth that Black British history begins with the Empire Windrush in 1948. It traces an unbroken presence from Afro-Romans on Hadrian's Wall through Black Tudors, Georgian enslaved servants, abolitionist campaigners, World War soldiers, and Windrush-era immigrants. At every stage, Black people were active participants — not passive victims — in British history.

The overarching arc: Britain's relationship with Black people moved through phases of encounter and trade (16th century), enslavement and enrichment (17th–18th centuries), abolition and moral self-congratulation (19th century), imperial racism dressed as science (mid-19th century), wartime service and post-war exclusion (20th century), and ongoing tension between inclusion and rejection (present day).

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Introduction: Bunce Island and the triangular history
- **Main argument:** Black British history is global — rooted in Britain, Africa, and the Americas simultaneously. Bunce Island slave fortress as physical emblem of the trade
- **Key stories:** The "production line" at Bunce Island — sorting yard, branding, holding yards, the "rape house", the two-hole golf course; the Gullah homecomings
- **Connection:** Sets up the triangular framework that structures the entire book

### Ch 1: 'Sons of Ham' — Romans to Tudor voyages
- **Main argument:** Africans have been in Britain since the 3rd century; medieval myths shaped how Europeans perceived Africa; English traders broke into Portuguese trade routes in the 1550s
- **Key evidence:** Ivory Bangle Lady (York, 3rd century) — radioisotope analysis proving North African ancestry; Beachy Head Lady — sub-Saharan African woman in Roman Sussex; Hereford Mappa Mundi and Mandeville's Travels; Prester John legend
- **Key stories:** Wyndham's 1553 voyage to Benin; John Lok and the five men from Shama (1554); the return of three men to Shama and the emotional reunion; John Hawkins' slave voyages 1562–68; the Curse of Ham as biblical justification for slavery

### Ch 2: 'Blackamoors' — Black Tudors and the sugar revolution
- **Main argument:** Black Tudors were present in significant numbers; Shakespeare engaged with blackness in complex ways; sugar drove the transition to full-scale slavery
- **Key stories:** John Blanke the trumpeter at Henry VIII's court; Jacques Francis the salvage diver testifying in court 1547; Barbados Slave Code 1661; Royal African Company
- **Key concepts:** The Barbados Slave Code creating the legal architecture of racial slavery; "whiteness" as a category invented to unite all Europeans against enslaved Africans

### Ch 3: 'For Blacks or Dogs' — Georgian London
- **Main argument:** Thousands of Black people lived in Georgian Britain as enslaved servants, free workers, sailors, beggars; the fashion for Black pageboys; slave collars; a complex picture between slavery and service
- **Key stories:** Black Harriott the courtesan; newspaper advertisements for the sale of enslaved children; Scipio Africanus's grave in Henbury; Ignatius Sancho's literary career; Julius Soubise's spectacular rise and fall; Francis Barber and Samuel Johnson
- **Key concepts:** The "ship not the whip" as the enforcement mechanism of slavery in Britain; the blurred boundary between slave and servant

### Ch 4: 'Too Pure an Air' — The legal battle
- **Main argument:** The legal status of slaves in Britain was a centuries-long muddle; Granville Sharp forced the issue through a series of cases culminating in the Somerset judgement (1772)
- **Key stories:** Jonathan Strong beaten nearly to death by David Lisle (1765); Thomas Lewis abducted and rescued from the Thames; James Somerset's case — the courtroom drama, Hargrave's speeches, Mansfield's reluctant judgement; Dido Elizabeth Belle at Kenwood House
- **Key concepts:** Yorke–Talbot opinion (1729); Mansfield's narrow ruling vs. popular interpretation; "England was too pure an Air for Slaves to breathe in" (1569 Cartwright case)

### Ch 5: 'Province of Freedom' — Sierra Leone
- **Main argument:** The founding of Sierra Leone was an attempt to create a free Black settlement; it went through catastrophic early phases before Freetown was established by Nova Scotian Black loyalists
- **Key stories:** The Black Poor of London; the Province of Freedom disaster; Thomas Peters' Atlantic journeys; Harry Washington (George Washington's former slave) settling in Freetown; the 1800 Maroon settlers from Jamaica

### Ch 6: 'The Monster is Dead' — Abolition of the trade
- **Main argument:** Abolition was a mass movement — unprecedented petitions, boycotts, public meetings — driven by religious groups but requiring popular support; the slave trade was abolished in 1807 but slavery continued until 1833/1838
- **Key stories:** The Zong massacre (1781); Equiano's and Cugoano's autobiographies; Clarkson's model of the slave ship Brooks; Wilberforce's annual parliamentary defeats; the Haitian Revolution; George Hibbert as the "anti-Wilberforce"
- **Key concepts:** The distinction between abolishing the trade vs. slavery; "gradualism"; the sugar boycott; the role of women in abolitionism

### Ch 7–8: Moral Mission / Liberated Africans
- **Main argument:** After 1807, Britain positioned itself as a moral crusader against slavery worldwide; the West Africa Squadron patrolled for slavers; compensation went to slave owners, not the enslaved; "liberated Africans" were resettled in Sierra Leone
- **Key stories:** Sara Forbes Bonetta (Yoruba girl rescued from Dahomey, adopted by Queen Victoria); the 1840 Anti-Slavery Convention painting; the £20 million compensation to slave owners (equivalent to ~£17 billion today)

### Ch 9: 'Cotton is King' — Uncle Tom's Cabin and American slavery
- **Main argument:** Uncle Tom's Cabin was the best-selling book in 19th-century Britain after the Bible; it shaped British views of slavery and race; Frederick Douglass toured Britain to enormous acclaim; the Lancashire cotton famine during the Civil War
- **Key stories:** Douglass' 1845–47 tour; Henry "Box" Brown; the Staffordshire Uncle Tom merchandise; the Welsh mining village audience weeping

### Ch 10: 'Mercy in a Massacre' — Scientific racism and Morant Bay
- **Main argument:** Mid-Victorian Britain saw the rise of "scientific" racism (polygenism, phrenology, Social Darwinism); the Morant Bay Rebellion (1865) and Governor Eyre's brutal repression split Victorian intellectuals into two hostile camps
- **Key stories:** Carlyle's "Occasional Discourse on the Nigger Question" (1849); Dr Hunt's racist paper at the Newcastle conference and William Craft's devastating rebuttal; the Morant Bay massacre (439 killed, 600 flogged, 1000+ homes burned); George William Gordon's judicial murder; the Eyre controversy — Mill, Darwin, Spencer vs. Carlyle, Dickens, Tennyson, Ruskin

### Ch 11: 'Darkest Africa' — The Scramble and human zoos
- **Main argument:** The Scramble for Africa was justified by racial theories; Africans were exhibited in "human zoos"; blackface minstrelsy became a mass entertainment; yet Black British individuals like Walter Tull achieved remarkable things
- **Key stories:** The three kings of Bechuanaland who came to London to save their kingdom from Cecil Rhodes (1895); human exhibitions at Earl's Court; Walter Tull — first Black outfield football player, WWI officer who died at the Somme

### Ch 12: 'We are a Coloured Empire' — World War I
- **Main argument:** Black West Indians and Africans served in WWI with enormous enthusiasm but were largely confined to labour roles; the 1919 race riots in British port cities were a violent rejection of their presence
- **Key stories:** The nine Trinidad stowaways at West Ham court (1915); Lance Corporal Alhaji Grunshi firing the first British shot; the BWIR in France and the Middle East; the Taranto mutiny (1918); 1919 riots in Liverpool, Cardiff, Glasgow, London — Charles Wootton murdered

### Ch 13: 'We Prefer their Company' — World War II
- **Main argument:** The arrival of Black American GIs in Britain exposed the gulf between American segregation and British racial attitudes; the British public overwhelmingly sided with Black GIs against white American racism; yet official policy was cowardly compromise
- **Key stories:** "Only blacks served here" pub sign in Bristol; the vicar's wife's six-point code at Worle; the Welcome to Britain propaganda film; Learie Constantine turned away from a London hotel; the Battle of Bamber Bridge (1943) — Black GIs vs. white American MPs

### Ch 14: 'Swamped' — Windrush to present
- **Main argument:** Post-war immigration was shaped by labour needs and imperial obligations; Black immigrants faced the colour bar, discrimination, and violence; Powell's "Rivers of Blood" speech; the 1981 riots; the Stephen Lawrence murder; the long struggle for recognition
- **Key stories:** Empire Windrush (1948); the 1958 Notting Hill attacks; Peter Griffiths' "If you want a nigger neighbour" campaign (1964); Powell's speech (1968); the New Cross fire (1981); Brixton riots; Stephen Lawrence murder (1993); the 2012 Olympics Windrush tribute

## Key Frameworks

1. **The Triangular History** — Black British history is planted in three continents: Britain, Africa, and the Americas. It cannot be understood from any single vantage point
2. **The Forgetting** — At every stage, Black contributions and presence were written out of the mainstream narrative, requiring repeated acts of historical recovery
3. **The Ship, Not the Whip** — Slavery in Britain was enforced not by the violence of the plantation but by the threat of deportation to the Caribbean
4. **The Hypocrisy Paradox** — Britain simultaneously practised slavery abroad while maintaining it was illegal at home; opposed slavery morally while profiting from it economically; welcomed Black people in wartime while rejecting them in peace
5. **Active Participants, Not Passive Victims** — Black Britons at every stage fought for their freedom (Somerset, the Nova Scotians, the Sons of Africa, the BWIR, the 1981 rioters)

---

## Key Principles

1. Black British history is not a separate history — it is everyone's history
2. The presence of Black people in Britain predates the English language itself
3. Racial categories ("white," "negro") were invented to serve economic interests, particularly the sugar economy
4. Abolition was not a gift from white Britain — it was fought for by the enslaved themselves and by Black activists alongside white allies
5. Each generation of immigrants faced the same cycle: enthusiasm, exploitation, exclusion, and eventual (partial) acceptance
6. The popular understanding of legal decisions (Somerset) often mattered more than the actual rulings
7. Britain's self-image as an anti-slavery nation has persistently obscured its role as the dominant slave-trading power

## Stories Worth Preserving (12)

1. **Ivory Bangle Lady** (Ch 1) — Mixed-race woman of North African descent found in a high-status Roman sarcophagus in York; radioisotope analysis proved her origins in 2009
2. **The five men from Shama** (Ch 1) — John Lok brought five Africans to London as translators in 1554; the emotional reunion when three were returned to their families
3. **John Blanke** (Ch 2) — Black trumpeter at Henry VIII's court; appears in the Westminster Tournament Roll (1511); petitioned the king for higher wages; received a royal wedding gift
4. **Jonathan Strong** (Ch 4) — Beaten nearly to death by David Lisle; rescued by Granville Sharp; recaptured and imprisoned; freed by the Lord Mayor; died at 25 from his injuries
5. **James Somerset** (Ch 4) — The landmark legal case; the courtroom drama; Mansfield's reluctant judgement; Somerset delivering Sharp's own documents to Lord Mansfield
6. **Harry Washington** (Ch 5) — George Washington's former slave; escaped during the Revolutionary War; evacuated to Nova Scotia; migrated to Freetown; exiled after the 1800 rebellion
7. **The Zong massacre** (Ch 6) — 133 slaves thrown overboard over three days; the insurance claim; Mansfield admitting it "shocks one very much"
8. **Uncle Tom's Cabin in Wales** (Ch 9) — Mrs Whalley sobbing in the audience: "Oh, oh" at each lash; escorted out, "still sobbing and crying and would not be comforted"
9. **William Craft vs Dr Hunt** (Ch 10) — Fugitive slave demolishes a polygenist scientist at the 1863 Newcastle conference; "thick skulls" quip; Hunt's furious attempt to deny Craft's Blackness
10. **The West Ham stowaways** (Ch 12) — Nine Trinidadians stow away to fight in WWI; magistrate jokes "Do they want to enlist in the Black Guards?"; they refuse to go home
11. **"We ain't no slaves, this is England"** (Ch 13) — Black GIs confronted by white American Military Police in Cosham; British civilians intervene on their behalf
12. **Operation Swamp 81** (Ch 14) — The Metropolitan Police name their stop-and-search operation after Thatcher's "swamped" language; the Brixton riots follow

## Critique & Cross-Reference

### Strengths
- Enormous chronological range (1800 years) sustained with consistent narrative energy
- Deeply personal opening transforms what could be dry history into urgent moral argument
- Exceptional use of individual lives to illuminate structural forces
- Does not romanticise Black agency — shows how constrained choices were at every stage
- Skilful use of legal history to show how racism was institutionalised gradually

### Weaknesses
- Coverage of the 20th century is thinner than earlier periods — post-1945 chapters feel rushed
- Women's stories are acknowledged as under-represented but this is not fully remedied
- The Caribbean plantation system is deliberately kept brief — readers need other sources for that
- The transition from chapter-to-chapter can feel abrupt in places

### Cross-References
- **[[Empire - Niall Ferguson]]** — Ferguson's positive framing of empire is the direct counterpart to Olusoga's focus on exploitation. Ferguson emphasises institutions and economic growth; Olusoga emphasises human cost. The £20 million compensation to slave owners is a key intersection
- **[[A History of Britain Vol 3 - Simon Schama]]** — Schama covers abolition and the Victorian period; Olusoga expands the Black perspective that Schama touches on
- **[[The English and Their History - Robert Tombs]]** — Tombs offers the mainstream English identity narrative; Olusoga challenges its completeness
- **[[The Victorians - A.N. Wilson]]** — Wilson's portrait of Victorian society can be read alongside Olusoga's account of scientific racism and the Morant Bay controversy
- **[[A History of Modern Britain - Andrew Marr]]** — Marr covers post-war immigration; Olusoga provides the deeper context of what those immigrants were arriving into
