# Thinking in Bets — Annie Duke

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Argument — a single thesis (decisions are bets on uncertain futures) supported by poker analogies and behavioural science.
**Audience:** Decision-makers in business, leadership, and daily life.
**Author:** Annie Duke is a former professional poker player who won a WSOP gold bracelet and over $4M in tournament prizes. She holds an NSF fellowship and completed doctoral coursework in cognitive psychology at UPenn before becoming a poker professional.

## Central Thesis
There are exactly two things that determine how our lives turn out: the quality of our decisions and luck. Most people confuse the two. They judge decisions by their outcomes ("resulting"), take credit for lucky wins, blame bad luck for poor decisions, and fail to learn from experience. The fix: treat every decision as a bet on an uncertain future. This forces you to separate decision quality from outcome quality, embrace uncertainty, calibrate your confidence, and build truthseeking groups.

## Key Frameworks
- **Resulting:** Judging decision quality by outcome quality
- **Life is poker, not chess:** Hidden information + luck make life fundamentally uncertain
- **Outcome fielding:** Sorting outcomes into "luck" vs "skill" buckets — and doing it wrong (self-serving bias)
- **Belief calibration:** Expressing confidence as percentages, not certainties
- **Truthseeking groups (CUDOS):** Communism, Universalism, Disinterestedness, Organized Skepticism
- **Mental time travel:** Backcasting, premortems, Ulysses contracts, 10-10-10
- **If/when-then plans:** Pre-committing to future decisions

## Best Stories
1. Pete Carroll's Super Bowl pass play — brilliant decision, terrible result, universal condemnation
2. Nick the Greek — played worst hand in poker (7-2) because "they never expect it," never learned
3. Phil Ivey's victory dinner — spent the celebration analyzing his mistakes
4. The CEO who fired his president — good process, bad result, concluded he'd made a mistake
5. Steve Bartman — Cubs fan blamed for losing the World Series for touching a foul ball
6. The SnackWell's Phenomenon — wrong causal attribution for weight gain
