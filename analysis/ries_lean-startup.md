# The Lean Startup — Eric Ries
## Central Thesis
Most startups fail not because they can't build a product, but because they build a product nobody wants. The antidote is validated learning — running rapid experiments to test fundamental business hypotheses before committing resources. The Build-Measure-Learn feedback loop replaces the traditional "build it and they will come" approach with a scientific method for entrepreneurship. Start with a Minimum Viable Product (MVP), measure real customer behaviour (not vanity metrics), and learn whether to pivot (change strategy) or persevere (double down). Innovation accounting provides a disciplined framework for measuring progress when traditional accounting metrics (revenue, profit) are not yet meaningful.
## Key Frameworks: Build-Measure-Learn loop (core engine), Validated Learning (progress = learning what customers actually want), MVP (smallest experiment that tests a hypothesis), Pivot or Persevere (structured decision point), Innovation Accounting (measuring startup progress), Leap-of-Faith Assumptions (value hypothesis and growth hypothesis), Three Engines of Growth (sticky, viral, paid), Five Whys (root cause analysis for defects), Small Batches (reduce cycle time, find problems faster)
## Best Stories: IMVU avatar chat product (Ries' own startup — built 3D instant messaging nobody wanted, then pivoted), Zappos MVP (Nick Swinmurn photographed shoes in stores to test demand before buying inventory), Dropbox video MVP (Drew Houston made a demo video to test demand before building the product), Food on the Table (concierge MVP — CEO personally did grocery shopping for one customer), Intuit's SnapTax (internal startup using lean methods inside a large company), Groupon's WordPress MVP (started as a simple WordPress blog with manually emailed PDF coupons)
## Cross-References: Zero to One - Thiel (Thiel disagrees — argues lean/iterative is for cowards; advocates bold conviction), The Effective Executive - Drucker (systematic abandonment, focus on contribution), The Phoenix Project - Kim (continuous deployment, small batches, feedback loops in IT operations)
