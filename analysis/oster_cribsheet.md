# Cribsheet — Emily Oster
## Classification & Context
Reference / data-driven decision guide. Oster is a professor of economics at Brown University specializing in health economics. Her first parenting book, *Expecting Better*, applied the same approach to pregnancy. Cribsheet extends it to the first three years. Unlike most parenting books that argue for a philosophy, Oster reviews the actual research behind each major parenting decision and presents the data as neutrally as possible — then gives parents a framework for making their own choices based on their own values and circumstances.
## Central Thesis
Most parenting debates are driven by ideology, guilt, and bad interpretation of research rather than by actual data. When you look at the evidence carefully — controlling for confounding variables, distinguishing correlation from causation, and assessing effect sizes — many of the fiercest parenting controversies turn out to be much less clear-cut than advocates on either side claim. The "right" answer depends on your family's specific values, constraints, and preferences. Oster provides the data and a cost-benefit framework; you make the decision.
## Key Frameworks
- The Decision Framework: (1) What does the data actually say? (2) What are your family's specific constraints and preferences? (3) What is the right choice for you? — separating facts from values
- Correlation vs Causation: the single biggest error in parenting research — breastfed babies do better on many measures, but mothers who breastfeed are also wealthier, more educated, and more health-conscious; controlling for these factors dramatically shrinks the apparent benefits
- Randomized trials vs observational studies: the gold standard (randomized trials) vs the common reality (observational studies that can't prove causation)
- Effect sizes: even when effects are "statistically significant," they may be too small to matter practically
- The "Bottom Line" approach: each chapter ends with a clear summary of what the data does and doesn't support
## Key Topics Covered
- Breastfeeding: benefits are real but much smaller than commonly claimed once you control for confounders; the biggest proven benefit is reduced gastrointestinal infections in the first year
- Sleep training: cry-it-out and other methods are safe and effective; no evidence of harm to attachment or long-term development
- Sleep position: back sleeping reduces SIDS risk; bed-sharing increases it; room-sharing is recommended but evidence is weaker than presented
- Vaccination: overwhelming evidence of safety and efficacy; no link to autism
- Maternal employment: no negative effects on children's development from working mothers
- Child care: quality matters more than type; center-based care may have slight academic advantages
- Screen time: limited evidence of harm for moderate use; context matters more than total hours
- Discipline: no single approach works for all children; physical punishment is harmful; time-outs are supported by evidence
- Potty training: later is easier; no evidence that early training produces better outcomes
## Best Stories
1. The Miracle Blanket crisis — a power outage forces Oster to unswaddle her baby; he sleeps fine; illustrating that parents have less control than they think
2. Jesse and the meal kits — Oster and her economist husband apply cost-benefit analysis to dinner planning, illustrating the decision framework in action
3. The breast-is-best debacle — Oster's systematic takedown of the breastfeeding research, showing how most claimed benefits evaporate when you control for socioeconomic factors
4. The mom who quit her job — a woman quits working to breastfeed, then feels resentful; Oster argues the decision matrix should include maternal well-being, not just infant outcomes
