# How to Win Friends and Influence People — Dale Carnegie

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Manual/how-to — 30 principles organised into 4 parts, each illustrated with historical anecdotes.
**Audience:** Anyone who interacts with other humans. Originally written for Carnegie's public speaking and human relations courses.
**Author:** Dale Carnegie (1888-1955) was a lecturer, author, and developer of courses in self-improvement, salesmanship, and interpersonal skills. This book has sold over 30 million copies since 1936.

## Central Thesis
You can get more of what you want by becoming genuinely interested in what other people want. The book's operating principle: every person you meet is fighting a battle for significance, approval, and feeling important. If you address that need — sincerely, not manipulatively — they will like you, trust you, and cooperate with you. Criticism, condemnation, and complaint achieve the opposite. The 30 principles are variations on this theme, each with historical stories demonstrating the principle in action.

## Structural Map
- **Part 1: Fundamental Techniques in Handling People** (3 principles)
  1. Don't criticise, condemn, or complain
  2. Give honest and sincere appreciation
  3. Arouse in the other person an eager want
- **Part 2: Six Ways to Make People Like You** (6 principles)
  1. Become genuinely interested in other people
  2. Smile
  3. Remember that a person's name is the sweetest sound
  4. Be a good listener — encourage others to talk about themselves
  5. Talk in terms of the other person's interests
  6. Make the other person feel important — and do it sincerely
- **Part 3: How to Win People to Your Way of Thinking** (12 principles)
  1. Avoid arguments — you can't win them
  2. Show respect for the other person's opinions; never say "you're wrong"
  3. If you are wrong, admit it quickly and emphatically
  4. Begin in a friendly way
  5. Get the other person saying "yes" immediately (Socratic method)
  6. Let the other person do most of the talking
  7. Let the other person feel the idea is theirs
  8. Try honestly to see things from the other person's point of view
  9. Be sympathetic to the other person's ideas and desires
  10. Appeal to the nobler motives
  11. Dramatise your ideas
  12. Throw down a challenge
- **Part 4: Be a Leader — How to Change People Without Giving Offence** (9 principles)
  1. Begin with praise and honest appreciation
  2. Call attention to mistakes indirectly
  3. Talk about your own mistakes before criticising
  4. Ask questions instead of giving direct orders
  5. Let the other person save face
  6. Praise every improvement, however slight
  7. Give the other person a fine reputation to live up to
  8. Use encouragement — make the fault seem easy to correct
  9. Make the other person happy about doing what you suggest

## Best Stories
1. Lincoln's unsent letter to General Meade — criticism withheld
2. Charles Schwab's $1M salary — because he knew how to deal with people
3. Al Capone seeing himself as a public benefactor — everyone justifies their actions
4. Teddy Roosevelt visiting Oyster Bay — remembered every servant's name
5. The $50 steak dinner story — listening beats talking every time
6. Andrew Carnegie naming a steel mill after J. Edgar Thomson to win a railroad contract
7. Stevenson praising Lincoln's "honesty" to the jury
8. The Rockefeller telegram to striking miners — opened with praise
