# The Religious Imagination

## Core Question

If we cannot travel back in time, how do we know what early humans believed — and how did the religious imagination make their world fundamentally different from ours?

Secondary question: What makes a religion powerful enough to function as truth — more real than physical reality itself?

## Lecture Type

Single-topic deep dive — uses two contemporary indigenous cultures (Barasana of the Amazon, Pygmies of Central Africa) as ethnographic windows into the pre-modern religious mind. Not a theory evaluation lecture but an immersive reconstruction of a lost worldview, with analytical frameworks woven in.

## Structural Map

1. **Review of Lectures 1-2** (0:00–9:25): Prof. Jiang summarises the series so far — for 200,000 years, humans were peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic. He recaps the evidence: no organised warfare, no hierarchy (except shamans with higher burial status), art as religious expression. Introduces the Yamnaya as next week's topic (a new people with a religion celebrating warfare, patriarchy, and wealth). Reviews animism: mother goddess, permanent soul, spirit world as the true reality, cosmic order requiring harmony. References Göbekli Tepe's T-pillars and the 40,000-year-old Lion Man figurine and flute from Germany as evidence that religion existed from the dawn of humanity. Introduces anthropology as the method: if the theory is correct, living indigenous cultures should still practise this religion.

2. **The Barasana of the Amazon — Creation Myth** (9:25–13:51): Reads an extended passage from Wade Davis's *The Wayfinders* about the Barasana creation myth (Romi Kumu, the woman shaman/mother goddess). Uses the myth to teach two things: (a) creation myths encode a society's legal system (rules against incest, killing the young), and (b) introduces the framework of **grandness, completeness, and unity** — the three properties a religion needs to achieve the authority of science.

3. **The Barasana — Cosmology and the Spirit World** (13:51–23:48): Continues reading from Davis. Demonstrates the sophistication of indigenous religion — the Barasana cosmology is vast, complete, and unified. Introduces the **shadow dimension** (the invisible spirit world behind every physical form, accessible only to shamans). Dismantles the prejudice that indigenous people are "stupid" because they lack technology. Introduces **ritual** — the expression of religion in everyday practice — by comparing it to the ritualised structure of a school day.

4. **The Barasana — Hunting Ritual and Reciprocity** (23:48–36:13): The shaman's role as "cosmic engineer" who enters the spirit world to restore balance. Reads about the hunting ritual: hunting requires permission from spirit guardians, meat is a gift not a right, killing without permission risks vengeance from the animal's spirit. The concept of **reciprocity** (give and take). Links back to Göbekli Tepe's animal carvings and cave paintings as the same religion expressed through art.

5. **The Pygmies of Central Africa — Trust vs. Control** (36:13–48:12): Shifts to Colin Turnbull's ethnography of the Pygmies. Reads a passage about Pygmies walking silently through the forest at night without weapons. The key analytical moment: Turnbull is shocked because he (and we) fear nature — we need to **control** it. The Pygmies **trust** nature because they are part of it. First cognitive divide: modern mind = control; pre-modern mind = trust. Second cognitive divide: **materialism vs. spirituality** — the modern belief that only the measurable is real vs. the pre-modern belief that the invisible is more real. Prof. Jiang argues that materialism is "obviously wrong" — imagination clearly exists even though it cannot be measured.

6. **The Pygmies — The Molimo and Collective Belief** (48:12–52:45): Reads about the molimo (a trumpet-like instrument that communicates with the forest/spirit world). The ultimate crime among the Pygmies is sleeping during the molimo singing — not murder. Prof. Jiang's analysis: sleeping during the ritual means rejecting the community's shared belief, which threatens the religion's existence, because **collective belief only works if everyone participates**. The key insight: a collectively shared imagination, when believed by everyone, becomes more real than physical reality. Introduces the word "pretending" as the chasm between the modern and pre-modern mind — Turnbull sees pretending; the Pygmies experience truth.

7. **The Pygmies — Waking the Forest** (52:45–end): Final passage: when bad things happen, the Pygmies believe the forest is "sleeping" and must be awakened through song. When good things happen, they sing to share their happiness. The religion is the same as the Barasana's despite being on a different continent: interconnection, spirit world as the real world, harmony maintenance. Closes by posing the question for next week: if humanity was peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic for 200,000 years, what changed? Answer: the Yamnaya and their religion of warfare, patriarchy, and wealth.

## Central Thesis

For most of human history — roughly 200,000 years — humans lived inside a **religious imagination** that was fundamentally different from the modern mind. This imagination treated the spirit world as more real than physical reality, treated all living things as equal manifestations of the same spiritual essence (the mother goddess), and structured every moment of daily life through ritual. Two contemporary indigenous cultures — the Barasana of the Amazon and the Pygmies of Central Africa — still practise this religion today, providing ethnographic proof that the animism found in archaeological evidence (cave paintings, Göbekli Tepe, Çatalhöyük) is real and coherent.

The lecture's deepest claim is about the nature of shared belief: **a collectively imagined world, when believed by everyone, becomes more real and more powerful than the physical world**. This is why sleeping during a religious ceremony is a capital crime — not because it insults a person, but because it threatens the collective imagination that holds the community together. The religion only exists if everyone believes it is true.

Prof. Jiang introduces two cognitive divides that separate the modern mind from the pre-modern mind: (1) **control vs. trust** — we fear nature because we need to control it; they trust nature because they are part of it; and (2) **materialism vs. spirituality** — we believe only the measurable is real; they believe the invisible spirit world is more real than the visible one.

The lecture sets up the central tragedy of the series: this peaceful, egalitarian, artistic worldview, sustained for 200,000 years, was destroyed by a new religion — the Yamnaya religion of warfare, patriarchy, and wealth — which is the subject of the next lectures.

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: Anthropology as Time Travel
- **Claim:** If animism was the universal religion of early humans, then living indigenous cultures that still practise it can serve as windows into the prehistoric mind
- **Evidence for:** The Barasana of the Amazon and the Pygmies of Central Africa, on different continents with no contact, practise essentially the same religion — animism with mother goddess worship, spirit world primacy, and harmony maintenance
- **Evidence against:** None presented (Prof. Jiang does not address the critique that modern indigenous cultures may have evolved their own distinct practices)
- **Verdict:** Presented as established methodology — anthropology is the discipline that makes this reconstruction possible
- **Source discipline:** Anthropology

### Argument 2: Creation Myths as Legal Systems
- **Claim:** Creation myths are not just explanatory stories about the origin of the world — they encode a society's fundamental laws and moral structure
- **Evidence for:** The Barasana creation myth explicitly shows chaos (spirits preying on kindred, incest, devouring young) being destroyed by the mother goddess, who then creates an ordered world — the myth explains WHY certain behaviours are forbidden (they belong to the pre-creation chaos of demons)
- **Verdict:** ✅ Presented as a key insight — creation myths function as constitutions, making rules feel cosmic rather than arbitrary

### Argument 3: The Three Properties of Authoritative Religion
- **Claim:** For a religion to function with the authority of science (i.e., to be treated as genuinely true by its practitioners), it must achieve three things: **grandness** (vast scope), **completeness** (explains everything visible in the world), and **unity** (a beginning and an end, a coherent whole)
- **Evidence for:** The Barasana cosmology demonstrates all three — it is vast, explains the origin of every natural feature (rivers, mountains, animals), and has a unified narrative arc
- **Future application:** Prof. Jiang flags this as a recurring framework that will be applied to Judaism, Christianity, and other religions throughout the series
- **Verdict:** ✅ Presented as a key analytical tool for the course

### Argument 4: Indigenous Sophistication
- **Claim:** Indigenous people are not "stupid" because they lack technology — their religious and cosmological thinking is extremely sophisticated and imaginative
- **Evidence for:** The Barasana creation myth and cosmological system is described as "very complicated" and "extremely elaborate"; the sophistication of shamanic dress and ritual organisation; the complexity of the hunting permission system
- **Verdict:** ✅ Presented as a corrective to modern prejudice — technological advancement is not the measure of intellectual sophistication

### Argument 5: The Pre-Modern Mind vs. the Modern Mind
- **Claim:** Two fundamental cognitive divides separate us from early humans:
  - **Control vs. Trust:** Modern humans fear nature because they need to control it (we like animals in zoos, not forests). Pre-modern humans trust nature because they are part of it.
  - **Materialism vs. Spirituality:** Modern humans believe that if you cannot see/measure something, it is not real. Pre-modern humans believed the spirit world was MORE real than the physical world.
- **Evidence for:**
  - Control vs. Trust: Turnbull's observation that the Pygmies walk through the forest at night without weapons, unafraid of leopards — "When we are children of the forest, what need have we to be afraid of it?"
  - Materialism vs. Spirituality: Prof. Jiang argues that materialism is "obviously wrong" because imagination clearly exists despite being unmeasurable. Science classes teach that only the measurable is real, but for most of human history, people believed otherwise.
- **Verdict:** ✅ These are presented as fundamental analytical categories for understanding the entire series

### Argument 6: Collective Belief as Reality
- **Claim:** A collectively shared imagination, when believed by everyone, becomes more real and more powerful than the physical world. This is the defining power of the religious imagination.
- **Evidence for:**
  - The Pygmy molimo ceremony: the worst crime is sleeping during the singing — because sleeping means you don't believe, and if one person doesn't believe, it threatens the reality of the shared imagination for everyone
  - Prof. Jiang's classroom analogy: a classroom only exists when everyone participates; if someone sleeps, they are saying "this is not real," which destroys the collective experience
  - The word "pretending" in Turnbull's text: Turnbull sees the Pygmies as "pretending"; the Pygmies experience it as truth. This single word captures the chasm between the modern and pre-modern mind.
- **Verdict:** ✅ This is the lecture's most important claim — the central thesis about the nature of religious imagination

### Argument 7: Animism Implies Peace, Equality, and Art
- **Claim:** If you truly believe all living things share the same spiritual essence, certain social consequences follow necessarily: no warfare (it causes chaos in the spirit world), egalitarianism (we all come from the same mother goddess), and art (as the means of worshipping and communicating with the spirit world)
- **Evidence for:** Both the Barasana and the Pygmies are peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic — consistent with the worldview
- **Verdict:** ✅ This connects back to the series' framing from the opening review: for 200,000 years humans were peaceful, egalitarian, and artistic BECAUSE of their religion

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

- **The Religious Imagination:** The collective human capacity to imagine a world that, when believed by everyone, becomes more real than physical reality. The defining feature of humanity for 200,000 years. Introduced formally in this lecture but building on Lectures 1-2.
- **Animism (reviewed):** The religion of early humans everywhere — all living beings share one spiritual essence from the mother goddess; body and soul are separate; the spirit world is the true reality; death is transition, not ending; humans must maintain harmony with all life. Reviewed from Lectures 1-2.
- **Shamanism (reviewed):** The shaman communicates with the spirit world on behalf of the community. Among the Barasana, the shaman is compared to "a modern engineer who enters the depths of a nuclear reactor to renew the entire cosmic order." His role: diagnose why the spirits are unhappy and fix it.
- **Creation Myths as Law:** Creation myths encode the legal and moral rules of a society — they explain not just where the world came from but why certain behaviours are forbidden (they belong to the chaos that preceded creation).
- **Grandness, Completeness, Unity:** Three properties a religion needs to function with the authority of science. Grandness = vast scope. Completeness = explains everything you can observe. Unity = coherent narrative with beginning and end. Flagged as a recurring framework for evaluating religions throughout the series (Judaism, Christianity, etc.).
- **The Shadow Dimension:** The invisible spirit world that exists behind every physical form — "more real than our world." Plants, animals, rocks, and rivers all have shadow counterparts. Accessible only to shamans.
- **Reciprocity:** The moral principle underlying the relationship between humans and animals — hunting is a negotiation, not a right. You must ask permission, give thanks, and ask forgiveness, or risk vengeance from the animal's spirit guardian.
- **Ritual:** The expression of religion in everyday practice. Every action in the pre-modern world has religious significance. Prof. Jiang compares it to the ritualised structure of a school day (attendance, bells, raising hands) — both are rituals backed by a belief system.
- **Control vs. Trust:** The first cognitive divide between modern and pre-modern minds. We fear nature because we want to control it (animals in zoos, not forests). Pre-modern humans trust nature because they are part of it.
- **Materialism vs. Spirituality:** The second cognitive divide. Modern materialists believe only the measurable is real. Pre-modern humans believed the spirit world was more real. Prof. Jiang argues materialism is "obviously wrong" — imagination exists but cannot be measured.
- **Collective Belief as Reality:** The lecture's capstone concept — a shared imagination, when maintained by universal participation, becomes more real than physical reality. Sleeping during the molimo ritual is a capital crime because it breaks the collective belief that sustains the community.
- **The Molimo:** A trumpet-like instrument used by the Pygmies to communicate with the forest/spirit world. Central to their religious practice. When things go wrong, they use it to "wake the forest" through song.

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### The Barasana Creation Myth (Romi Kumu)
- Source: Wade Davis, *The Wayfinders*
- The Barasana of the Amazon believe that before creation, the universe was chaos — spirits and demons preyed on their own kindred, bred without thought, committed incest, devoured their own young
- Romi Kumu (the woman shaman / mother goddess) responded by destroying the world with fire and floods
- She then turned the charred world upside down "just as a mother turns over a warm slab of manioc bread on the griddle"
- Created a new world: land, water, forest, and animals
- The myth simultaneously explains the origin of the world AND encodes the society's fundamental laws: incest is forbidden, killing the young is forbidden — because these belong to the chaos of demons
- The accompanying cosmological visualisation is described as "extremely complicated" — demonstrating intellectual sophistication

### The Barasana Cosmology and the Shadow Dimension
- Source: Wade Davis, *The Wayfinders*
- The Barasana see the entire natural world as "saturated with meaning and cosmological significance"
- Every rock and waterfall embodies a story; plants and animals are "distinct physical manifestations of the same essential spiritual essence"
- Behind every tangible form is the shadow dimension — "invisible to ordinary people, but visible to the shaman"
- This is "the realm of the whole spirit" where deified ancestors reside, rocks and rivers are alive, and plants and animals are human beings
- Sap and blood are "the bodily fluids of the primordial river of the Anaconda"
- At the centre are the "great malaccas" where everything is beautiful — shining feathers, coca, tobacco powder that is itself "the skull and brain of the sun"

### The Barasana Shaman as Cosmic Engineer
- Source: Wade Davis, *The Wayfinders*
- The shaman never uses or manipulates medicinal plants — his duty is to "move in the timeless realm... embrace the primordial powers and harness and restore the energy of all creation"
- Compared to "a modern engineer who enters the depths of a nuclear reactor to renew the entire cosmic order"
- When plants or animals are dying, the cause is spiritual: the spirits are unhappy. The shaman must enter the spirit world, diagnose the problem, and fix it
- Renewal is "a fundamental obligation of the living"
- Without people maintaining spiritual order, the natural world would have "no order or meaning — all would be chaos"

### The Barasana Hunting Ritual
- Source: Wade Davis, *The Wayfinders*
- When men go to hunt or fish, "it is never a trivial passage" — permission is required first
- The shaman travels in trance to "negotiate with the masters of the animals, forging a mystical contract with the spirit guardians" based on **reciprocity**
- The Barasana compare hunting to marriage/courtship — seeking the blessing of a greater authority to take "a precious being" into one's family
- "Meat is not the right of a hunter, but a gift from the spirit world"
- Killing without permission risks death by a spirit guardian — jaguar, anaconda, tapir, or harpy eagle
- The ritual before hunting involves the shaman transforming — "from fish to animal, to human being and back again, transcending every form, becoming pure energy"
- The ceremony involves two series of dances separated by the liminal moments of dawn, dusk, and midnight
- The shaman calls "by name every point of geography met on the ancestral journey of the Anaconda"
- Links to Göbekli Tepe animal carvings and cave paintings: they celebrate the animals, not the hunters, because animals give life and nourishment

### The Pygmies and the Forest at Night
- Source: Colin Turnbull (unnamed book, likely *The Forest People*)
- Turnbull follows a group of Pygmies through the forest at night — they are completely silent, which is unusual (normally they are deliberately noisy)
- It is the time leopards would be prowling — but the Pygmies carry no spears or bows
- They are "a part of the silence and the darkness of the forest itself" — afraid only of "that which was not of the forest"
- A Pygmy explains: "When we are the children of the forest, what need have we to be afraid of it? We are only afraid of that which is outside the forest"
- Turnbull records this with "sudden shock" — the word "shock" reveals his inability to understand their worldview
- Prof. Jiang's analysis: the difference is control vs. trust. We fear nature because we want to control it. The Pygmies trust nature because they ARE nature.

### The Molimo Ceremony and the Capital Crime of Sleeping
- Source: Colin Turnbull
- The Pygmies have a communal meal, then sing and play the molimo (trumpet-like instrument)
- "One of the greatest crimes the pygmy can commit, if not the greatest, is to be found asleep when the molimo is singing"
- The punishment: they would search for the sleeping man with spears, kill him "completely and forever," bury him under the fire pit, and forbid anyone from ever mentioning his death or absence
- Prof. Jiang's analysis: sleeping means you don't believe. If one person doesn't believe, it threatens the shared reality for everyone. You are not committing violence against a person — you are committing violence against the community, the religion, and the forest itself.
- The classroom analogy: a classroom only exists when everyone participates. If someone sleeps, they are saying "this is not real" — destroying the collective experience.

### The Pygmies Waking the Forest
- Source: Colin Turnbull
- A Pygmy elder explains: when things go wrong (bad hunting, illness, death), "it must be because the forest is sleeping and not looking after its children"
- The solution: "We wake it up. We wake it up by singing to it... we want it to awaken happy, then everything will be well and good again"
- When things are going well, they also sing — "we want it to share our happiness"
- Same religion as the Barasana despite being on a different continent: interconnection, spirit world as the real world, harmony maintenance through communication

### Lion Man Figurine and Bone Flute (40,000 years ago, Germany)
- A small figurine found in a cave in Germany, dating to 40,000 years ago
- Evidence that religion existed from the very dawn of humanity's spread out of Africa (50,000 years ago)
- A bone flute from the same period — religion was celebrated through music and art from the beginning
- These figurines were believed to have "religious qualities" — like the cave paintings and the shaman-dressed-as-animal figures

## Student Q&A Insights

1. **What stands out about the shamanic dress photograph?** → Students initially focus on the small number of people and elaborate clothing. Prof. Jiang guides them to see: (a) the sophistication of the dress (care and thought, not random), (b) the organisation and symmetry (central elder, deliberate body positioning), and (c) that everything has religious significance — if this were just about hunting and eating, "none of this makes any sense." Their first priority is religion, not survival.

2. **Why is their cosmology so complicated?** → Because for religion to function as truth (with the authority of science), it must achieve three things: **grandness** (vast scope), **completeness** (explains everything), and **unity** (beginning and end). This is flagged as a recurring analytical framework for the course.

3. **Implicit Q&A through passage analysis:** Prof. Jiang leads students through close reading of the Turnbull passage, asking them to identify the word "shock" (revealing Turnbull's incomprehension) and later the word "pretending" (the chasm between the modern and pre-modern mind). These are presented as analytical exercises, not traditional Q&A, but they surface the lecture's deepest insights.

4. **Why would sleeping be worse than murder?** → Prof. Jiang walks through the logic step by step: a classroom exists only when everyone participates → sleeping means "this is not real" → you are not insulting a person, you are rejecting the community → the religion only exists if everyone believes → one non-believer threatens to kill the religion itself → therefore sleeping is violence against the community, the religion, and God.

5. **Why do we fear nature?** → Students eventually arrive at "the unknown." Prof. Jiang pushes further: we fear the unknown because we are "obsessed with control." We like animals in zoos (controllable) and fear animals in forests (uncontrollable). The Pygmies don't fear nature because they **trust** it — they believe the leopards know them and they know the leopards.
