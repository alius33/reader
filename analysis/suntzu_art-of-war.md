# The Art of War — Sun Tzu

## Classification & Context

- **Genre:** Classical military treatise / strategic manual
- **Original language:** Classical Chinese
- **Period:** Late Spring and Autumn period, approximately 5th century BC
- **Translation used:** Lionel Giles (1910) — the most scholarly English translation with extensive commentaries from 11 Chinese commentators spanning 1,800 years (Ts'ao Ts'ao through Chang Yu)
- **Structure:** 13 chapters of aphorisms and principles, each addressing a different aspect of warfare and strategy
- **Historical context:** Written during the Warring States era of China when dozens of feudal principalities competed for dominance. Sun Tzu served the Wu state under King Ho Lu (514-496 BC), who used his strategies to defeat the much larger Ch'u state
- **Influence:** Studied by every major Chinese military commander for 2,500 years (Han Hsin, Ts'ao Ts'ao, Li Ching, Yo Fei). Now a foundational text in business strategy, geopolitics, and competitive theory worldwide
- **Key biographical incident:** Sun Tzu demonstrated his principles by drilling the King's concubines; when they laughed at orders, he beheaded the two leaders despite the King's protests, after which discipline was absolute. This established his core principle: authority must be backed by consequences

## Central Thesis

**War is won before it is fought.** The supreme art of war is to subdue the enemy without fighting. Victory comes through superior information, strategic positioning, deception, and the exploitation of the enemy's weaknesses — not through brute force. The general who makes the most calculations before battle wins; the one who makes few calculations loses. Every engagement should be decided in advance through intelligence, planning, and psychological advantage.

Secondary thesis: **Adaptability is supreme.** No fixed rules can govern warfare. Like water, the successful strategist shapes his approach to the terrain and the opponent. The general who rigidly follows doctrine will be defeated by the one who reads circumstances and adapts.

## Structural Map

The 13 chapters follow a deliberate progression:

1. **I. Laying Plans** — Strategic assessment before action (the five constant factors)
2. **II. Waging War** — Economics of conflict; the imperative of speed
3. **III. Attack by Stratagem** — Winning without fighting; the hierarchy of strategic options
4. **IV. Tactical Dispositions** — Defense and offense; making defeat impossible before seeking victory
5. **V. Energy** — Direct vs. indirect methods (cheng/ch'i); combined force
6. **VI. Weak Points and Strong** — Initiative, concentration vs. dispersion, fluidity
7. **VII. Maneuvering** — Converting disadvantage to advantage; army management
8. **VIII. Variation in Tactics** — Flexibility; the five dangerous faults of generals
9. **IX. The Army on the March** — Terrain, observation, reading signs of the enemy
10. **X. Terrain** — Six types of ground; six calamities; the general's responsibilities
11. **XI. The Nine Situations** — Nine varieties of ground and psychological warfare
12. **XII. The Attack by Fire** — Using environmental weapons; restraint in war
13. **XIII. The Use of Spies** — Intelligence as the foundation of all strategy

**Logic flow:** Assessment → Economics → Strategy → Positioning → Tactics → Adaptation → Terrain → Psychology → Intelligence

## Chapter-Level Detail

### I. Laying Plans
- War is a matter of life and death for the State — never to be neglected
- Five constant factors: (1) Moral Law (alignment between ruler and people), (2) Heaven (timing, weather, seasons), (3) Earth (terrain, distances, danger), (4) The Commander (wisdom, sincerity, benevolence, courage, strictness), (5) Method and Discipline (organization, logistics, expenditure)
- Seven considerations for forecasting victory (which sovereign has Moral Law, which general is more able, advantages of Heaven/Earth, discipline, army strength, training, consistency of rewards/punishments)
- "All warfare is based on deception" — the foundational axiom
- Attack where unprepared, appear where unexpected
- Many calculations lead to victory; few calculations to defeat

### II. Waging War
- War is enormously expensive — 1,000 ounces of silver per day for 100,000 men
- Prolonged warfare dulls weapons, dampens ardor, exhausts resources, invites opportunistic enemies
- "There is no instance of a country having benefited from prolonged warfare"
- Speed over perfection: "stupid haste" is better than "clever dilatoriness"
- Forage on the enemy; one captured cartload equals twenty of your own
- Use captured soldiers; augment your strength from the conquered
- The leader of armies is the arbiter of the people's fate

### III. Attack by Stratagem
- **The hierarchy:** (1) Balk the enemy's plans, (2) Prevent junction of enemy's forces, (3) Attack the army in the field, (4) Besiege walled cities (worst option)
- "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting"
- Force ratios: 10:1 surround, 5:1 attack, 2:1 divide, equal offer battle, inferior evade, vastly inferior flee
- Three ways a ruler brings misfortune: (1) Hobbling the army by ignorant orders, (2) Governing army like a kingdom, (3) Employing officers without discrimination
- Five essentials for victory: knowing when to fight, handling superior/inferior forces, unity of spirit, preparedness, military capacity without sovereign interference
- **"Know the enemy and know yourself; in a hundred battles you will never be in peril"**

### IV. Tactical Dispositions
- First make yourself invincible, then wait for the enemy to become vulnerable
- Security lies in your own hands; the opportunity to defeat the enemy is provided by the enemy
- "One may KNOW how to conquer without being able to DO it"
- True excellence: winning without the world noticing — victories that bring neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage
- "He wins his battles by making no mistakes"
- The victorious strategist seeks battle after the victory has been won; the loser fights first and looks for victory after
- Five-step chain: Measurement → Estimation → Calculation → Balancing of chances → Victory

### V. Energy
- Control of large forces = same principle as control of few (subdivision and signals)
- Direct methods (cheng) join battle; indirect methods (ch'i) secure victory
- Cheng and ch'i are infinitely interchangeable, like a circle with no end
- Five notes, five colors, five tastes — finite elements, infinite combinations
- Energy = bending a crossbow; decision = releasing the trigger
- Simulated disorder requires perfect discipline; simulated weakness requires great strength
- Combined energy over individual talent; rolling stones analogy (round stones on steep slopes)

### VI. Weak Points and Strong
- Whoever arrives first is fresh; whoever arrives second is exhausted
- "The clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him"
- Attack undefended places; defend positions that cannot be attacked
- "O divine art of subtlety and secrecy! Through you we learn to be invisible"
- Force the enemy to disperse while remaining concentrated yourself
- "Do not repeat the tactics which have gained you one victory"
- Water analogy: avoid what is strong, strike at what is weak; shape your course according to the terrain
- "There are no constant conditions" in warfare

### VII. Maneuvering
- "Turning the devious into the direct, and misfortune into gain"
- The artifice of deviation: take circuitous routes while making the enemy take direct ones
- Practical limits: forced marches lose leaders and soldiers (100 li = 1/10 survive, 50 li = lose first division leader, 30 li = 2/3 arrive)
- "Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest"
- In raiding: like fire. In immovability: like a mountain. Plans: dark as night. Movement: like a thunderbolt
- Robbing an army of its spirit; robbing a commander of his presence of mind
- Soldier's spirit: keenest in morning, flagging by noon, gone by evening
- Do not pursue enemies simulating flight; do not swallow bait; leave an outlet when surrounding; do not press desperate foes

### VIII. Variation in Tactics
- Five roads not to follow, armies not to attack, towns not to besiege, positions not to contest, sovereign commands not to obey
- Blend considerations of advantage and disadvantage in every plan
- Rely not on the enemy not coming, but on your readiness
- **Five dangerous faults of a general:** (1) Recklessness → destruction, (2) Cowardice → capture, (3) Hasty temper → provoked by insults, (4) Delicacy of honor → sensitive to shame, (5) Over-solicitude for men → worry and trouble

### IX. The Army on the March
- Terrain management: mountains (keep near valleys), rivers (don't fight mid-stream), salt-marshes (cross quickly), flat country (accessible position with rising ground on right/rear)
- Army prefers high ground, sunny places, hard ground
- Reading enemy signs: quiet enemy = strong position; aloof enemy provoking battle = wants you to advance; easy-access camp = bait
- Dust signs: high column = chariots; low and wide = infantry; branching = firewood gathering; small clouds moving = encamping
- Humble words + increased preparations = about to advance
- Peace proposals without sworn covenant = a plot
- Balance of humanity and discipline: "soldiers must be treated with humanity, but kept under control by means of iron discipline"

### X. Terrain
- Six kinds: accessible, entangling, temporizing, narrow passes, precipitous heights, distant positions
- Six calamities (from general's faults): flight, insubordination, collapse, ruin, disorganization, rout
- "If fighting is sure to result in victory, then you must fight, even though the ruler forbid it"
- The ideal general: advances without coveting fame, retreats without fearing disgrace
- "Regard your soldiers as your children" but avoid being indulgent without authority (spoilt children = useless)
- Complete knowledge: know enemy + know yourself + know Heaven + know Earth = complete victory

### XI. The Nine Situations
- Nine grounds: dispersive, facile, contentious, open, intersecting highways, serious, difficult, hemmed-in, desperate
- On desperate ground, fight. On hemmed-in ground, use stratagem. On dispersive ground, unify purpose
- Deep penetration into enemy territory creates cohesion; shallow penetration creates dispersion
- "Rapidity is the essence of war"
- The shuai-jan snake: strike its head, the tail attacks; strike its tail, the head attacks; strike its middle, both attack — the army should operate like this
- Throw soldiers into positions with no escape and they will fight to the death
- The general must mystify his own officers and men; burn boats and break cooking pots
- "Exhibit the coyness of a maiden, then emulate the rapidity of a running hare"

### XII. The Attack by Fire
- Five targets: soldiers in camp, stores, baggage trains, arsenals, dropping fire
- Five developments when fire breaks out; timing with wind and stellar constellations
- Fire shows intelligence; water gains strength — but water cannot rob the enemy of all possessions
- "No ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen"
- "Anger may change to gladness; vexation may be succeeded by content. But a kingdom destroyed can never come again"
- The chapter pivots from tactical fire to the philosophy of restraint in warfare

### XIII. The Use of Spies
- Intelligence is the foundation of all strategy
- Five types: (1) Local spies (inhabitants), (2) Inward spies (enemy officials), (3) Converted spies (enemy spies turned), (4) Doomed spies (fed false info, sent to enemy, sacrificed), (5) Surviving spies (bring back news)
- "Divine manipulation of the threads" — all five types working simultaneously
- The converted spy is the most important: enables use of all other types
- Spies must be treated with utmost liberality and secrecy
- Historical examples: I Chih (Yin dynasty rise), Lu Ya (Chou dynasty rise)
- "Spies are a most important element in war, because on them depends an army's ability to move"

2. **The Hierarchy of Strategic Options** — Balk plans > Prevent junction > Attack in field > Besiege cities
3. **Cheng/Ch'i (Direct/Indirect)** — Infinite combinations from two basic methods
4. **The Water Analogy** — Avoid strength, strike weakness, adapt to terrain
5. **Five Types of Spies** — Local, Inward, Converted, Doomed, Surviving

## Key Principles (15)

### 1. Win Before You Fight
- **Principle:** Victory is determined by preparation and calculation, not by combat itself
- **Mechanism:** More calculations = more scenarios anticipated = more contingencies covered = higher probability of the right response in every situation
- **Applies when:** Any competitive engagement with significant stakes
- **Doesn't apply when:** The situation is truly novel with no analogues to calculate from
- **Best evidence:** "The victorious strategist only seeks battle after the victory has been won, whereas he who is destined to defeat first fights and afterwards looks for victory" (Ch. IV)

### 2. All Warfare Is Based on Deception
- **Principle:** Appear weak when strong, strong when weak; appear far when near, near when far
- **Mechanism:** Deception creates asymmetric information. The side with better information makes better decisions; the deceived side wastes resources responding to false signals
- **Applies when:** Any adversarial situation where the opponent's choices depend on their perception of your state
- **Doesn't apply when:** Allies need trust; deception among friends destroys cohesion
- **Best evidence:** Sun Pin's decreasing campfires (100k → 50k → 20k) that lured P'ang Chuan into a fatal ambush (Ch. V)

### 3. Supreme Excellence: Win Without Fighting
- **Principle:** The highest form of victory is to subdue the enemy without battle
- **Mechanism:** Fighting is expensive, unpredictable, and destructive. Diplomatic, economic, and psychological pressure preserve your resources while achieving your objectives
- **Applies when:** You have leverage (economic, diplomatic, informational) that makes fighting unnecessary
- **Doesn't apply when:** The enemy is irrational, ideological, or when non-military leverage is insufficient
- **Best evidence:** "Supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting" (Ch. III)

### 4. Know Your Enemy, Know Yourself
- **Principle:** Complete knowledge of both sides eliminates uncertainty and ensures victory
- **Mechanism:** Self-knowledge prevents overreach; enemy knowledge reveals vulnerabilities. The intersection identifies the optimal strategy
- **Applies when:** Always — this is the meta-principle that governs all others
- **Doesn't apply when:** Never — ignorance is never strategically advantageous
- **Best evidence:** Fu Chien's disastrous overconfidence (383 AD) — knowing his own strength but not the enemy's, he boasted his million troops could dam the Yangtsze with their whips, then was routed at the Fei River (Ch. III)

### 5. Speed Over Perfection
- **Principle:** Rapid action, even if imperfect, beats prolonged operations that drain resources
- **Mechanism:** Prolonged warfare exhausts treasury, dulls morale, and invites opportunistic enemies. Speed denies the enemy time to prepare, recover, or seek allies
- **Applies when:** You have initiative and the enemy is not yet consolidated
- **Doesn't apply when:** You are the defender with strong positions; rushing wastes defensive advantage
- **Best evidence:** Ssu-ma I's eight-day forced march to crush Meng Ta's rebellion before he could prepare (227 AD) (Ch. XI)

### 6. Impose Your Will; Never Accept the Enemy's
- **Principle:** Arrive first, choose the ground, dictate the terms of engagement
- **Mechanism:** The first mover is fresh, positioned, and forces the enemy to react. Reactive forces are exhausted, poorly positioned, and psychologically inferior
- **Applies when:** You have mobility and intelligence advantages
- **Doesn't apply when:** The enemy has already secured strong ground; then lure them out
- **Best evidence:** "Whoever is first in the field and awaits the coming of the enemy, will be fresh for the fight" (Ch. VI)

### 7. Concentrate Against Dispersion
- **Principle:** Keep your forces concentrated while forcing the enemy to spread thin
- **Mechanism:** By concealing your intentions, you force the enemy to guard everywhere; being everywhere weak, he is everywhere vulnerable
- **Applies when:** You have intelligence superiority and can identify the enemy's dispositions
- **Doesn't apply when:** You face a concentrated force that knows your position — then divide
- **Best evidence:** "Should he strengthen his van, he will weaken his rear... If he sends reinforcements everywhere, he will everywhere be weak" (Ch. VI)

### 8. Adapt Like Water
- **Principle:** No fixed tactics; shape your strategy to the enemy and the terrain
- **Mechanism:** Rigid doctrine is predictable. The enemy who understands your system can exploit it. Constant adaptation denies them a pattern to exploit
- **Applies when:** Always — but especially against sophisticated opponents who study your methods
- **Doesn't apply when:** Basic discipline and doctrine are still being established in your forces
- **Best evidence:** "Water shapes its course according to the nature of the ground... just as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions" (Ch. VI)

### 9. Exploit Psychological Momentum
- **Principle:** Attack morale and spirit, not just bodies and positions
- **Mechanism:** A demoralized army with superior numbers loses to a spirited inferior force. Spirit peaks in the morning and flags by evening; a retreating army fights desperately; a surrounded army with an exit will not fight to the death
- **Applies when:** The enemy has brittle morale, internal divisions, or tired troops
- **Doesn't apply when:** The enemy is desperate with nothing to lose — then leave an outlet
- **Best evidence:** Ts'ao Kuei waiting for the third drum-roll before attacking Ch'i (the first drums create spirit, the second diminishes it, the third exhausts it) (Ch. VII)

### 10. The Indirect Method (Ch'i) Wins
- **Principle:** Direct engagement (cheng) holds the enemy; indirect maneuver (ch'i) delivers victory
- **Mechanism:** The enemy focuses on what he expects (cheng); the surprise from an unexpected quarter (ch'i) finds him unprepared. What the enemy prepares for becomes cheng; what surprises him is ch'i
- **Applies when:** Any competitive situation — the direct/indirect principle is universal
- **Doesn't apply when:** When overwhelming force makes subtlety unnecessary (but even then, indirect methods reduce cost)
- **Best evidence:** Han Hsin's crossing of the Yellow River in wooden tubs while ostensibly marching toward Lin-chin (Ch. V)

### 11. Terrain Shapes Strategy
- **Principle:** The physical environment dictates what tactics are possible and which side has advantage
- **Mechanism:** High ground, narrow passes, rivers, and marshes create asymmetries that can multiply or divide force. The side that understands and exploits terrain fights with nature as an ally
- **Applies when:** Always in physical conflict; metaphorically in any competition where "ground" (market position, institutional advantage) matters
- **Doesn't apply when:** In purely informational or diplomatic contests without physical dimension
- **Best evidence:** P'ei Hsing-chien's decision to relocate camp to higher ground the night before a catastrophic flood (Ch. X)

### 12. Intelligence Is the Foundation
- **Principle:** All strategy depends on knowledge obtained through spies
- **Mechanism:** Without knowledge of the enemy's dispositions, plans, and vulnerabilities, strategy is guesswork. The cost of espionage is trivial compared to the cost of ignorance
- **Applies when:** Always — information asymmetry is the root of strategic advantage
- **Doesn't apply when:** Never — but the type of intelligence needed varies
- **Best evidence:** Fan Chu's use of converted spies to replace the capable Lien P'o with the bookish Chao Kua, leading to the massacre of 400,000 Chao soldiers (260 BC) (Ch. XIII)

### 13. Discipline Precedes Compassion
- **Principle:** Build discipline first, then show compassion — never the reverse
- **Mechanism:** An army that respects authority can then be inspired by kindness. An army shown kindness without discipline becomes "spoilt children; useless for any practical purpose"
- **Applies when:** Building any team or organization from scratch
- **Doesn't apply when:** The team already has strong cultural discipline; then compassion builds loyalty
- **Best evidence:** Sun Tzu's beheading of the king's concubines who disobeyed during the drill demonstration — after which, the remaining women performed with "perfect accuracy and precision" (Introduction)

### 14. Leave an Outlet for the Desperate
- **Principle:** Never push an enemy to absolute desperation; always leave a perceived escape route
- **Mechanism:** A truly cornered enemy fights with suicidal ferocity. A perceived exit drains the will to fight to the death, making the enemy easier to defeat in flight
- **Applies when:** Surrounding or besieging an enemy force
- **Doesn't apply when:** You want YOUR OWN troops to fight desperately — then eliminate their escape routes
- **Best evidence:** Kao Huan blocking his own army's escape routes to inspire desperate courage against Erh-chu Chao's vastly superior force (532 AD) (Ch. XI)

### 15. The Sovereign Must Not Interfere
- **Principle:** The general in the field must have autonomous authority; commands from home are often fatal
- **Mechanism:** The ruler lacks real-time information about battlefield conditions. Orders based on court politics or outdated intelligence force the general into positions the enemy exploits
- **Applies when:** The sovereign (CEO, president, board) is distant from the operational reality
- **Doesn't apply when:** The general has lost strategic vision and is making local decisions that undermine the larger campaign
- **Best evidence:** Napoleon's success attributed to not being "hampered by central authority" (Ch. III commentary)

## Stories Worth Preserving

1. **The Concubines Drill** (Introduction) — Sun Tzu drilling the king's concubines, beheading the two leaders when they laughed, establishing that clear orders + disobedience = officers' fault; discipline is non-negotiable
2. **Sun Pin's Decreasing Campfires** (Ch. V) — 100,000 → 50,000 → 20,000 fires over three nights to feign desertions, luring P'ang Chuan into an ambush where he was killed under a tree inscribed with his name
3. **T'ien Tan's Oxen** (Ch. IX) — Strapped blades to horns, torches to tails, launched 1,000 flaming oxen into enemy camp at night with 5,000 soldiers behind them; recovered 70 cities
4. **Han Hsin at the Wei River** (Ch. IX) — Built a dam of sandbags, lured enemy across the river, then broke the dam, cutting the enemy army in half
5. **Ts'ao Ts'ao Cutting His Hair** (Ch. I) — After his horse trampled crops (violating his own law), he condemned himself to death, then substituted cutting his hair as penalty — demonstrating that leaders are not above the law
6. **Pan Ch'ao's Night Raid** (Ch. XII) — With only 36 men against Hsiung-nu envoys, set fire to their camp in a gale, used drums to simulate a large force, killed the envoy and 30 attendants, and won the allegiance of an entire kingdom
7. **The Empty City Stratagem** (Ch. VI) — Chu-ko Liang opened city gates and had men sweep streets when Ssu-ma I approached with a large army; Ssu-ma I, suspecting an ambush, retreated
8. **Han Hsin's Backs-to-the-River Battle** (Ch. XI) — Placed troops with backs to river (violating all doctrine), then used a separate force to capture enemy's undefended camp; trapped enemy between two forces. When questioned, quoted Sun Tzu: "Plunge your army into desperate straits, and it will come off in safety"

## Summarisation Notes

### Recommended Category
**Strategy & Systems Thinking** — While the book touches on power and influence, its primary contribution is as the foundational text of strategic thought. It belongs alongside [[Strategy A History - Lawrence Freedman]], [[Thinking Strategically - Avinash K. Dixit & Barry J. Nalebuff]], and [[Predatory Thinking - Dave Trott]] rather than the influence/persuasion books. The principles are about competitive strategy, systems of advantage, and decision-making under adversarial conditions.

### Summary Approach
- Organize by **thematic clusters** rather than strict chapter order, since chapters overlap significantly (terrain is treated in IX, X, and XI; the cheng/ch'i concept spans V-VII)
- Lead with the five most transformative strategic principles, then deep-dive each
- Use the rich historical anecdotes from the Giles commentaries as illustration — these are a unique asset of this translation
- Emphasize the **meta-principles** (deception, adaptation, intelligence) over the purely military-tactical content
- Draw explicit parallels to modern business/career strategy to make the 2,500-year-old text feel contemporary
- Include the key frameworks as visual diagrams (Five Factors, Hierarchy of Strategy, Nine Grounds)
- The book's brevity belies its depth — the summary must unpack what Sun Tzu compresses

### Tone
- Authoritative but accessible
- Story-driven — let the anecdotes carry the principles
- No academic commentary on translation issues
- Present tense for principles; past tense for stories
