# How Societies Collapse

## Core Question

Why do societies rise and why do they fall? What are the mechanisms and patterns behind societal decline and collapse?

## Lecture Type

Theory evaluation + Synthesis — Prof. Jiang presents three theories of societal decline, then synthesises them into a comprehensive structural model of how power works and how societies move through rise, decline, and collapse phases.

5. **Theory 3: Civilizational Life Cycle (Spengler)** (~5 min): Oswald Spengler's organic model — village → town → city → mega city. Each stage brings greater abstraction, individualism, and disconnection. Mega cities represent death of civilization (no trust, no children, no collective identity)

## Central Thesis

Societies rise and fall through predictable structural dynamics. Three academic theories — Piketty's financialization, Turchin's elite overproduction, and Spengler's civilizational life cycle — each capture a piece of the puzzle but are incomplete alone. Prof. Jiang synthesises them into a structural model: society is controlled by a small number of founding families who exercise power through finance, religion, and intelligence. These families operate through a managerial middle class that administers society on their behalf. When society is young and rising, this system operates through consent, openness, and meritocracy. But elite overproduction (too many elite children competing for positions) forces the managerial class into rent-seeking and exploitation of the people. Society transitions from consent to deception to coercion. The rise is steep, the decline is slow, and the collapse — triggered by a perfect storm of simultaneous crises — is sudden.

## Argument Structure

### Theory 1: Financialization (Thomas Piketty)

- **The theory:** Capitalism naturally transitions through three phases — consumer capitalism (creating goods people want), financial capitalism (investing money for returns), and monopoly capitalism (a few companies dominate). In late-stage capitalism, the financial economy grows at ~5% while the real economy grows at ~2%, so rational actors invest rather than create real wealth
- **Evidence for:** Piketty's statistical analysis of income tax data across centuries. The incentive structure is clear — $1M in restaurant yields $20K/year, $1M in stock market yields $50K/year. This explains unemployment, debt, and declining work ethic
- **Evidence against:** Not explicitly discussed — Prof. Jiang presents this as one contributing theory, not the complete answer
- **Verdict:** Endorsed as a valid partial explanation. Piketty sees it as a natural byproduct of capitalism — a cycle, not a fixable bug
- **Source discipline:** Economics, statistical analysis of historical tax records

### Theory 2: Elite Overproduction (Peter Turchin)

- **The theory:** Societies collapse because too many powerful people (specifically, children of the elite) compete for limited positions of power. This competition is a zero-sum game that inevitably leads to war or revolution
- **Evidence for:** Turchin's decades of historical research across multiple civilizations (Roman Empire, French Revolution, etc.). Illustrated through James B. Calhoun's Rat Utopia experiments (1950s-70s): sealed colonies with abundant resources always resulted in rats killing each other — they competed for status, not food, and had nowhere else to go
- **Evidence against:** Student question about whether limiting family size could prevent it — Prof. Jiang argues elite families are incentivized to have many children to pass on power, and everyone wants to marry into elite families
- **Verdict:** Strongly endorsed as a key mechanism of collapse. The dynamic is structural and unavoidable within the system
- **Source discipline:** History, sociology, animal behaviour experiments

### Theory 3: Civilizational Life Cycle (Oswald Spengler)

- **The theory:** Civilizations follow the same life cycle as organisms — birth, growth, maturity, death. The trajectory moves from village → town → city → mega city, with increasing abstraction (removal from reality) at each stage
- **Evidence for:** Observable patterns: village life features hard work, unity, collective mentality, high birth rates. Mega cities feature individualism, money-based relationships (replacing trust/tradition/emotion), unwillingness to work, reliance on immigrants, refusal to have children. Modern mega cities (Beijing, Shanghai, NYC, London, Paris) exhibit all decline characteristics
- **Evidence against:** Student asks if external threats could extend the cycle — Prof. Jiang says no, because mega city populations are too selfish and fragmented to unite; factions would align with external enemies against internal rivals
- **Verdict:** Endorsed as the overarching framework — the life cycle is natural and inevitable. No external force can reverse it once the mega city phase is reached
- **Source discipline:** Philosophy of history, cultural morphology

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### Financialization
- **Definition:** The transition from consumer capitalism (creating goods) to financial capitalism (generating money through investment) to monopoly capitalism (few companies dominate)
- **How it works:** When financial returns exceed real economy returns (5% vs 2%), rational actors stop creating real wealth and speculate instead
- **What it explains:** Unemployment, debt, declining work ethic, concentration of wealth
- **Introduced in:** This lecture (Lecture 2)

### Elite Overproduction
- **Definition:** Too many children of the elite competing for limited positions of power
- **How it works:** Status is zero-sum — only one person can hold each position. When elite children cannot find positions, conflict escalates to war or revolution
- **What it explains:** Factionalism, civil war, revolution, societal collapse
- **Introduced in:** This lecture (also covered in Civilization series Lecture 6)

### Rat Utopia
- **Definition:** James B. Calhoun's sealed-colony experiments (post-WWII) demonstrating that abundance leads to lethal status competition
- **How it works:** Rats given unlimited food, water, and housing in a sealed environment always ended up killing each other. They competed for status (mating rights/alpha male position), not resources. In nature, losing rats would leave; in the sealed colony, there was nowhere to go
- **What it explains:** Why abundance and security do not prevent societal collapse — status competition is the driver
- **Introduced in:** This lecture (also referenced in Civilization Lecture 8)

### Civilizational Life Cycle (Village → Town → City → Mega City)
- **Definition:** Spengler's organic model of civilizational development and death
- **How it works:** Each stage brings greater abstraction (removal from reality). Village = hard work, unity, high birth rates, trust-based relationships. Mega city = individualism, money-based relationships, no children, reliance on immigrants, disconnection from production
- **What it explains:** Why modern mega cities exhibit all symptoms of decline — they represent the natural death phase of civilization
- **Introduced in:** This lecture

### Three Pillars of Power
- **Definition:** Finance, religion, and intelligence (spies) — the mechanisms through which founding families control society
- **How it works:** These three pillars operate as a nexus that controls all institutions (schools, military, government, media, corporations, even organized crime). The people generate wealth; the middle class manages; the elite families own
- **What it explains:** The structural mechanics of how a small number of families (~100-200 in the Roman Empire) can control entire civilizations
- **Introduced in:** This lecture (will be expanded in future lectures)

### Rent-Seeking Behaviour
- **Definition:** Extracting wealth from others through positional power rather than productive work
- **How it works:** The middle class (lawyers, landlords, managers) hold positions that allow them to collect "rent" — payment for access to their gatekeeping function. When the system is under stress, they exploit this power harder
- **What it explains:** Why the middle class aligns with elite interests against the people during decline — they are protecting their rent-seeking positions
- **Introduced in:** This lecture

### Three Phases of Society: Rise, Decline, Collapse
- **Rise characteristics:** Openness, consent, democracy, meritocracy, innovation, unity, empathy, welcoming criticism
- **Decline characteristics:** Bureaucracy, deception, rent-seeking, stability-focused, managers exploit workers
- **Collapse characteristics:** Authoritarianism, coercion, factionalism, survival mentality, suppression of critics, civil war/revolution
- **Key insight:** 1950s China (communist) and 1950s America (democratic) were BOTH open societies — political system is irrelevant, only the phase of social development matters

### Timeline Dynamics
- Rise is steep, decline is slow, collapse is sudden
- Collapse is triggered by a "perfect storm" — multiple crises hitting simultaneously (plague + drought + war + revolution)
- Society cannot handle simultaneous shocks because the authoritarian phase suppresses the critics who would have warned about and prepared for these threats

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### Rat Utopia Experiments (James B. Calhoun, post-WWII era)
- **Who:** American scientist James B. Calhoun
- **Context:** Post-WWII Cold War, era of increasing abundance due to technology and peace
- **What happened:** Created sealed rooms with abundant food, water, and housing for rat colonies (~10 rats initially, growing to 400-500). No matter how the experiment was configured, rats always ended up killing each other. Ran experiments for 20 years, never achieved peaceful coexistence
- **Key insight:** Rats competed for status (mating rights, alpha male position), not food. In nature, losing males would leave and start new colonies. In the sealed environment, there was nowhere to go — status became zero-sum
- **Supports:** Elite overproduction theory and the broader argument that abundance does not prevent collapse

### Roman Empire — Rule by ~200 Families
- **Who:** The Roman elite
- **What:** The Roman Empire, which controlled most of Europe and Anatolia and Egypt, was ultimately governed by approximately 200 families
- **Supports:** The structural model — a tiny elite controls the entire system

### 1950s China and America — Both Open Societies
- **Who:** Post-revolution China and post-war America
- **What:** Despite radically different political systems (communist vs. democratic), both societies in the 1950s were open — criticism of leaders was encouraged, meritocracy prevailed, innovation was welcomed
- **Supports:** Political system is irrelevant to the rise/decline dynamic — what matters is the phase of social development

### McDonald's vs. Pizza Hut Analogy (Consent → Deception → Coercion)
- **What:** Prof. Jiang illustrates the three phases through a lunch decision:
  - Rise/Consent: Group discusses, votes, majority wins
  - Decline/Deception: Teacher lies ("Jack Ma will be there" or "free hamburgers") to manipulate the group
  - Collapse/Coercion: Teacher threatens physical force
- **Supports:** The transition from consent to deception to coercion across social phases

## Student Q&A Insights

### Q1: Could an external threat unite a declining society?
- **Student's idea:** If there's a universal threat (like aliens), wouldn't everyone put aside problems and work together?
- **Prof. Jiang's response:** No. Once society reaches the mega city phase: (1) people are too self-absorbed to even recognize external threats, (2) people no longer trust each other enough to cooperate, (3) factions would actually align with the external enemy to destroy rival factions
- **Added nuance:** This is one of the lecture's most counterintuitive claims — even an alien invasion would not unite a declining society

### Q2: What if elite families don't have many children?
- **Student's idea:** Could the elite overproduction problem be avoided if elite families limited their children?
- **Prof. Jiang's response:** Elite families are specifically incentivized to have many children because children are the mechanism for passing on power and privilege. Marriage into other elite families is a power-maintenance strategy. Everyone wants to marry into elite families
- **Added nuance:** The dynamic is structural, not voluntary — the elite cannot choose to limit reproduction without surrendering their power

### Q3: Do governments use wars to distract from collapse?
- **Student's idea:** Do governments deliberately start wars to hide societal problems?
- **Prof. Jiang's response:** Yes — that's exactly what wars are for. The elite's choice is stark: send the discontented to war, or face revolution at home. War is preferable to revolution from the elite's perspective
- **Prof. Jiang's caveat:** "There's no right and wrong here" — he's explaining how power works, not making moral judgments. The course is about understanding power mechanisms, not ethics
