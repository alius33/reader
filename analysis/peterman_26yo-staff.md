## Main Arguments

### 1. Ownership is the single thread through all promotions
- IC3→IC4: owning task completion end-to-end
- IC4→IC5: owning others' success and project outcomes, not just personal output
- IC5→IC6: owning systems for accountability, scaling through delegation with verification
- Every level expands the radius of ownership

### 2. Ratings and promotions are correlated but not identical
- Ratings measure WHAT you achieved (impact)
- Promotions measure HOW you achieved it (behaviours of the next level)
- You can get the highest rating (RE) without getting promoted if your behaviours don't match the next level
- This distinction catches ambitious engineers off guard

### 3. The right manager is a career multiplier
- Baloa recruited Simon by drawing a career plan on a whiteboard
- Same manager for 5.5 years across California, London, multiple team pivots
- Manager was a converted IC6→manager with deep technical knowledge
- Coached on product thinking, data, experimentation, written communication
- Simon credits him as the single biggest factor in his growth

### 4. Written communication is the highest-leverage skill most engineers neglect
- Posts on Meta's Workplace can reach thousands
- Most engineers bury the lede with technical details
- Effective formula: flashy title → TLDR with numbers → context → impact/next steps → technical details below the fold
- Simon believes poor communication actively holds back careers

### 5. Management is not a permanent decision
- Simon tried management for ~1 year, returned to IC
- Circumstances were terrible: manager injured, skip on paternity leave, director returning from sick leave
- Overpromised IC5→IC6 paths for two reports
- Key insight: IC6 gives you the people-growth aspects of management with more optionality

### 6. Scaling yourself requires systems, not just trust
- "Delegation is not abdication" (Baloa's saying)
- Early delegation mistake: hand off and forget → things slip
- Solution: weekly sprint meetings, people breakdown + project breakdown tracking
- People breakdown catches struggling individuals that project-level tracking misses

## Key Stories (8)

### 1. Baloa's Whiteboard Plan
Simon told managers during boot camp he wanted IC5 in 3 years. Baloa took him to a whiteboard and drew a plan. Simon joined his team as the second engineer. Baloa had followed a similar trajectory himself.

### 2. The 20 Experiments
In his first half, Simon ran 20 of the org's 50 total experiments. His velocity was partly due to his internship advantage (knew internal tools) and pre-studying React and TypeScript before joining.

### 3. The Neutral Experiment That Became 20% of Impact
An ads payment failure notification improvement launched neutral. Instead of moving on, Simon dug in with the data scientist to understand why. They updated targeting and improved the design — it ended up contributing 20% of that half's impact.

### 4. The Daily Script Logging Bug
A script updating advertiser settings had broken experimentation logging. Simon personally investigated across teams (experimentation team, DS, deep code review) to diagnose the issue. The fix became the majority of his impact that half.

### 5. The Struggling Intern Turnaround
Simon's intern was struggling with fundamentals (React/Hack). Instead of giving up, Simon invested heavy personal time — enough that his wife got upset about missed dinners and dates. The intern ultimately got a return offer. Simon calls it one of his proudest moments.

### 6. The Mysterious 15-Minute Calendar Slot
Simon received a random 15-minute meeting from a director he'd never spoken to. He showed up nervous ("a little sweaty"). The director told him he was receiving additional equity. Simon had no idea what to expect — a friend in a similar situation assumed they were getting fired.

### 7. Management Support Network Collapse
Within weeks of transitioning to management: immediate manager (Baloa) had an accident and was out 3-4 months, skip manager was on paternity leave, the nearest person in the chain was a director in California returning from sick leave. Simon was left managing alone in London.

### 8. The Rockstar Intern From Mexico
Simon's second intern was so good at JavaScript and React that they were coaching the new-hire IC3s on the team. The intern blew through their project and started teaching others.

## Frameworks & Models

### 1. Ownership Radius Model
Each level expands what you own:
- IC3: own your tasks
- IC4: own project completion end-to-end
- IC5: own others' success and project outcomes
- IC6: own systems that ensure people and projects succeed at scale

### 2. Ratings vs Promotions Matrix
- High impact + next-level behaviours = promo + high rating
- High impact + current-level behaviours = high rating, no promo (Simon's RE without promo)
- Low impact + next-level behaviours = possible promo at lower rating
- This is the key insight: they are different evaluation axes

### 3. Delegation is Not Abdication
Baloa's principle: you delegate the work, not the responsibility. You must verify outcomes, not just trust.

### 4. People Breakdown + Project Breakdown
Track both separately. A project can be on track while one person contributing to it is drowning. Only the people view reveals individual struggles.

### 5. Effective Written Communication Formula
1. Flashy title
2. TLDR with numbers (above the fold)
3. Context
4. Impact + next steps
5. Technical details (below the fold, for "fellow nerds")

### 6. Intern Success Factors
- Velocity (primary measure: did you complete the project?)
- Ask questions early (use the grace period before expectations rise)
- Leverage peers, not just your manager
- Peer feedback is a critical evaluation input
