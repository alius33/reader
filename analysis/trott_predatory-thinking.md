# Analysis: Predatory Thinking — Dave Trott

## Source
- **Title:** Predatory Thinking: A Masterclass in Out-Thinking the Competition
- **Author:** Dave Trott
- **Year:** 2013
- **Pages:** ~250
- **Type:** Argument book structured as a collection of short essays/stories, each illustrating a principle of creative and strategic thinking
- **Source material:** Partial text extraction (633 lines) + training knowledge

## Book Structure

The book is NOT organised into traditional chapters with sequential arguments. Instead, it is a collection of approximately 80-90 short essays (each 1-3 pages), grouped loosely into thematic clusters. Each essay tells a story — from military history, advertising, sport, science, street life — and extracts a principle about thinking differently, seeing upstream of the problem, and refusing to compete where everyone else competes.

Trott groups these essays into thematic sections:

1. **Predatory Thinking** — The core concept: think like a predator, not prey. Go where the competition isn't. Reframe the problem before solving it.
2. **It Ain't What You Do, It's the Way That You Do It** — Execution and craft. The same idea delivered differently produces completely different results.
3. **Ordinary People, Extraordinary Results** — You don't need genius. You need to see what others miss and act on it.
4. **Form Is the Enemy of Creativity** — Following the expected format kills original thinking. Break the template.
5. **The Ordinary as Extraordinary** — The best ideas are simple. Complexity is a sign of unclear thinking.
6. **Garbage In, Garbage Out** — The quality of thinking depends on the quality of information and assumptions going in.
7. **Change the Context, Change the Meaning** — Reframing is the most powerful creative tool. The same fact in a different context becomes a different fact.

## Key Themes & Concepts

### 1. Predatory vs. Reactive Thinking
- Predator: chooses where to fight, defines the terms, moves first
- Prey: reacts to the predator's moves, fights on ground chosen by someone else
- Most people (and most companies) are prey — they respond to the market, to competitors, to briefs, instead of redefining the game

### 2. Upstream Thinking
- The most powerful move is to solve the problem before it becomes a problem
- Military examples: winning the war before the battle starts (logistics, intelligence, positioning)
- Advertising examples: redefining what the brief should be before answering it

### 3. Simplicity as Weapon
- Complex solutions are a sign that you haven't understood the problem
- The best advertising, the best military strategies, the best street-smart moves are always brutally simple
- "If you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it"

### 4. Reframing / Changing the Context
- The same information, presented differently, changes everything
- Trott's central creative technique: don't change the facts — change what the facts mean
- Examples from advertising, military deception, retail

### 5. Form vs. Content
- People mistake the form for the thing itself
- Following the expected format (the "proper" way to do an ad, a presentation, a strategy) kills the impact
- The form should serve the content, not the other way around

### 6. Street-Smart vs. Book-Smart
- Trott grew up in working-class East London and brings a street-level pragmatism
- Academic knowledge is useless without the ability to see what's actually happening
- Many of his stories contrast formal expertise (which fails) with practical observation (which succeeds)

### 7. The Power of Doing What Others Won't
- Most people follow convention because it's safe
- The competitive advantage comes from doing what everyone else thinks is wrong, stupid, or beneath them
- Being willing to look foolish is a prerequisite for originality

## Key Stories (for summary use)

1. **The British in Malaya** — Winning the guerrilla war by understanding what the guerrillas actually needed (food, not ideology)
2. **Helmuth von Moltke** — "No plan survives first contact with the enemy" — the Prussian military philosophy of adaptability
3. **Steve Jobs and the original Mac** — Simplicity as a design principle driven by predatory thinking
4. **Avis "We Try Harder"** — Turning second place into an advantage through reframing
5. **Volkswagen "Think Small"** — DDB's revolutionary campaign that changed advertising by breaking every rule
6. **The Falklands War — the Vulcan bomber raid** — A spectacularly complex operation for a simple psychological purpose
7. **Ed McCabe's pitch style** — The adman who won business by being blunt when everyone else was polished
8. **Gordon the tramp** — Trott's childhood lessons in predatory thinking from a London street character
9. **The SAS Iranian Embassy siege** — Tactical thinking under pressure
10. **Sun Tzu throughout** — Trott repeatedly references The Art of War as the foundational text for predatory thinking
11. **Bill Bernbach and the creative revolution** — How one man changed advertising by refusing to follow the rules
12. **D-Day deception operations** — Operation Fortitude: winning the invasion before it started through upstream thinking

## Author Context

Dave Trott is a British advertising creative — one of the most awarded in UK history. He founded agencies GGT (Gold Greenlees Trott) and CST (Cossette Trott). His career spans from the creative revolution of the 1960s-70s through the present day. He writes a popular blog and has published several books including *Creative Mischief*, *One Plus One Equals Three*, and *Predatory Thinking*. His writing style is deliberately short, punchy, and story-driven — he writes the way he thinks advertising should work: simple, direct, and impossible to ignore. He draws from military history, street life, sport, and pop culture as much as from advertising, giving his work a cross-disciplinary flavour unusual in business books.
