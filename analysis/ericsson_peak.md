# Peak — Anders Ericsson
## Classification & Context
Argument / research synthesis. Ericsson is the psychologist whose research on deliberate practice was the basis for Malcolm Gladwell's "10,000 hours" rule in Outliers. This book is Ericsson's own account — correcting Gladwell's oversimplification and presenting the actual science of how experts are made. Co-written with science writer Robert Pool.
## Central Thesis
Expertise is not born — it is built through a specific type of practice called deliberate practice. Naive practice (just putting in hours) produces a plateau, not mastery. Purposeful practice adds goals and feedback. Deliberate practice goes further: it requires a well-developed field with established training methods, a coach who can design exercises targeting weaknesses, practice outside the comfort zone, and full concentration. The central cognitive mechanism is the development of increasingly sophisticated mental representations — internal models that allow experts to see patterns, anticipate outcomes, and make decisions that novices cannot.
## Key Frameworks
- The Practice Spectrum: Naive Practice → Purposeful Practice → Deliberate Practice
- Mental Representations: internal cognitive structures that encode patterns, rules, and relationships in a domain
- The Comfort Zone Model: learning only happens at the edge of current ability — too easy = no growth, too hard = breakdown
- Homeostasis Principle: the body and brain resist change; sustained challenge is required to push past equilibrium
- The "OK Plateau": the point where performance becomes automatic and improvement stops — broken only by deliberate challenge
- The "10,000 hours" correction: hours alone mean nothing without the right type of practice, feedback, and progressive difficulty
## Best Stories
1. Steve Faloon — ordinary college student trained to memorise 82 random digits (normal memory span is 7) through 200+ hours of deliberate practice, proving memory is trainable
2. The Polgár sisters — three Hungarian girls raised to be chess prodigies by their father using deliberate practice; Judit became the strongest female chess player in history
3. Benjamin Franklin's self-taught writing method: finding essays he admired, making notes on each sentence, waiting days, reconstructing from notes, comparing to originals, and repeating
4. London taxi drivers' hippocampi physically growing larger from years of navigating complex routes — the brain adapts structurally to sustained challenge
5. The "prodigy" myth debunked: Mozart's early compositions were heavily edited by his father; his first truly original masterwork came after 10+ years of intense training
