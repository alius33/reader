# Analysis: The Dictator's Handbook — Bruce Bueno de Mesquita & Alastair Smith

## Book Metadata
- **Title:** The Dictator's Handbook: Why Bad Behavior is Almost Always Good Politics
- **Authors:** Bruce Bueno de Mesquita & Alastair Smith
- **Year:** 2011
- **Type:** Argument / Political Theory (non-taxonomy)
- **Pages:** ~319
- **Chapters:** 10 + Introduction

## Core Thesis

All political behaviour — in dictatorships, democracies, corporations, and any organisation — can be explained by leaders' need to acquire and retain power. Leaders are not good or evil; they respond to structural incentives created by three groups: interchangeables (nominal selectorate), influentials (real selectorate), and essentials (winning coalition). The size of the winning coalition relative to the selectorate determines whether a regime produces public goods or private rewards, prosperity or poverty, freedom or oppression.

## Three Political Dimensions

1. **Interchangeables (Nominal Selectorate)** — everyone with a nominal say (voters, shareholders)
2. **Influentials (Real Selectorate)** — those whose support truly matters (swing voters, institutional shareholders)
3. **Essentials (Winning Coalition)** — the minimum group a leader must keep happy to stay in power

## Five Rules of Political Survival

1. Keep the winning coalition as small as possible
2. Keep the nominal selectorate as large as possible (supply of replacements)
3. Control the flow of revenue
4. Pay key supporters just enough to keep them loyal
5. Don't take money from supporters' pockets to improve people's lives

## Chapter Structure

### Introduction: Rules to Rule By
- Bell, California as microcosm of all politics
- Robert Rizzo's $787K salary in a poor town
- Small coalition + large selectorate = maximum discretion
- Politics is about individuals, not national interest

### Chapter 1: The Rules of Politics
- Three dimensions framework introduced
- Five rules explained with historical examples
- Lenin's rigged-election universal suffrage as ideal selectorate expansion
- Rules apply to democracies too (gerrymandering, tax code, earmarks)

### Chapter 2: Coming to Power
- Three requirements: remove incumbent, seize apparatus, form coalition
- Samuel Doe's Liberia as case study
- Speed is essential — no prize for second place
- Mortality as opportunity (Khomeini, Corazon Aquino, Kabila vs dying Mobutu)
- Ottoman fratricide law and succession dynamics
- Pope Damasus I's coalition building
- Russian Revolution as bankruptcy problem (vodka tax)
- Silence is golden (Ben Bella's fatal announcement)
- Democratic inheritance (Tafts, Kennedys, Bushes)
- Lincoln's divide-and-conquer strategy in 1860

### Chapter 3: Staying in Power
- Carly Fiorina at HP — corporate coalition dynamics
- Sack, shuffle, shrink the coalition
- Perils of meritocracy — loyalty > competence (Saddam's Ba'ath Party purge)
- Chemical Ali as ideal supporter (loyal, incompetent)
- Eunuchs as solution to competent-but-threatening advisers
- Keeping essentials off-balance — rigged elections as warning
- Democrats aren't angels — gerrymandering, bloc voting
- Leader survival statistics: autocrats survive early risk, then outlast democrats

### Chapter 4: Steal from the Poor, Give to the Rich
- Taxation as dual tool: generates revenue, impoverishes non-coalition
- Tax rates: autocrats maximise revenue; democrats constrained by coalition
- Mexico's democratisation = declining tax rates
- Tax collectors and privatised collection (Caliphate tax farmers)
- Resource curse — oil wealth correlates with poverty, autocracy, civil war
- Nigeria: $350B in oil revenue, per capita income fell
- Borrowing incentives — smaller coalition = greater profligacy
- Debt forgiveness enables autocrats to re-borrow (counterproductive)

### Chapter 5: Getting and Spending
- Public goods serve different purposes: autocrats provide minimum for productivity; democrats provide for welfare
- Education: autocrats fund primary (workers need literacy), suppress higher education
- Cuba's infant mortality — good under both Batista and Castro, but declining relative position
- Clean drinking water: Honduras (democratic, poor) vs Equatorial Guinea (autocratic, rich)
- Infrastructure: Mobutu never built roads (prevent people reaching him)
- Airport road straightness correlates with autocracy
- Earthquakes: Iran (Bam, 26,271 dead) vs Chile (Iquique, 11 dead) — same magnitude range
- Freedom as cheapest and most valuable public good

### Chapter 6: If Corruption Empowers
- Corruption is a feature, not a bug — private goods for coalition loyalty
- Genghis Khan's strategic brutality
- Private goods in democracies: left/right policies as coalition rewards
- Iran vs Turkey comparison (coalition size, taxation, corruption)
- IOC and FIFA as small-coalition organisations riddled with bribery
- Wall Street bonuses as coalition payments
- Julius Caesar killed for helping people at coalition's expense
- "Big" Paul Castellano killed for enriching self at coalition's expense
- Kleptocracy hall of fame: Suharto ($35B), Mobutu, Marcos, Bashir
- Hall of fame: Deng Xiaoping, Lee Kuan Yew (discretionary spending done well)
- Hall of shame: Khrushchev's agricultural disaster

### Chapter 7: Foreign Aid
- Aid is policy purchase, not poverty relief
- Democrats act angelic at home, devilish abroad
- Haile Selassie's customs fees on famine relief
- Aid-for-policy model: autocrats sell cheap policy concessions for cash
- US-Egypt Camp David deal as aid-for-peace purchase
- US-Pakistan relationship: pay or don't play
- Marshall Plan worked because recipients were democracies
- UNSC membership: more aid = worse outcomes
- Aid fungibility: NGOs substitute for government spending, freeing money for corruption
- Fixing aid: escrow payments, reward results not promises
- Nation building hypocrisy: democracies prefer compliant dictators

### Chapter 8: The People in Revolt
- Revolution requires tipping point where expected future misery outweighs risk
- Middle-of-the-road dictators most vulnerable (not the worst or best)
- Nipping movements in bud: Than Shwe's Burma, suppressing monks
- Natural disasters as revolutionary catalysts (Mexico City 1985, Nicaragua 1972)
- Myanmar cyclone: dispersed survivors, blocked aid, sold food on black market
- Katrina vs Nargis: democrats punished for deaths, autocrats rewarded
- Ghana's Rawlings: reluctant democrat forced by bankruptcy
- Washington, Mandela, Nehru: started with large coalitions
- Resource wealth prevents democratisation (Burma, Russia under Putin)
- Financial crises as windows for democratic reform

### Chapter 9: War, Peace, and World Order
- Sun Tzu vs Weinberger Doctrine: autocratic vs democratic war-fighting
- Six Day War: Israel (democratic, high spend per soldier) vs Egypt (autocratic, private rewards)
- Democrats try harder to win; autocrats preserve coalition payments
- WWI spending patterns: democracies escalate, autocracies plateau
- Democrats pick fights they expect to win (93% win rate when initiating)
- Autocrats survive defeat; democrats almost never do
- Leave no soldier behind (US in Mogadishu) vs leave no tank behind (Ethiopia at Afabet)
- Democratic peace: democracies rarely fight each other
- Democracies prefer compliant dictators abroad to unpredictable democrats

### Chapter 10: What Is To Be Done?
- Coalition members' incentive to expand shifts at a threshold point
- Green Bay Packers as model of large-coalition corporate governance
- Fixing corporations: shareholder networking, not government regulation
- Sarbanes-Oxley failed because government regulators serve their own coalition
- US states with larger coalitions developed faster (Jeff Jensen study)
- Gerrymandering as primary democratic corruption
- Free and fair elections are necessary but insufficient
- Financial crises are the best windows for reform — don't rescue dictators
