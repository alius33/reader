# Discourses — Epictetus
## Classification & Context
Compilation of philosophy lectures. Epictetus (c. 50–135 CE) was born a slave in Hierapolis (modern Turkey), studied Stoic philosophy under Musonius Rufus while still enslaved, was eventually freed, and became one of the most influential teachers in Rome — then in Nicopolis after Domitian expelled philosophers from the city. He wrote nothing himself; his student Arrian recorded his lectures and conversations in eight books, of which four survive as the Discourses, with the Enchiridion (Handbook) as a condensed summary.
## Central Thesis
The dichotomy of control is the foundation of all Stoic practice: some things are "up to us" (eph' hēmin) — our judgments, desires, aversions, and impulses — and everything else is "not up to us." Freedom, happiness, and virtue all come from wanting only what is within your power and being indifferent to what is not. This is more systematic and actionable than Marcus Aurelius's Meditations, which are private reflections. Epictetus is teaching — he wants you to practise, not just understand.
## Key Frameworks
- The Dichotomy of Control (prohairetic vs aprohairetic) — the master framework
- Prohairesis (Moral Choice / Ruling Faculty): the uniquely human capacity to choose how to interpret and respond to events
- Prosoche (Attention): constant self-monitoring of impressions before assenting to them
- Three Disciplines: Desire (want only what is in your power), Action (act with reservation — pursue goals while accepting outcomes), Assent (test every impression — "is this up to me?")
- Role Ethics: you occupy multiple roles (citizen, parent, friend, professional) — each has duties; fulfil them without complaint
- The Discipline of Impressions: events are neutral; your judgment makes them good or bad — "It is not things that disturb us, but our judgments about things"
## Best Stories
1. Epictetus's own slavery — his master Epaphroditus once twisted his leg; Epictetus calmly said "You will break it." When it broke, he said "I told you so." His lameness became his teaching example of indifference to the body.
2. The analogy of the actor: you didn't choose your role (beggar, king, cripple) — your job is to play the assigned role well
3. Socrates in prison — choosing to stay and die rather than escape, because he had lived by Athens's laws and would die by them
4. The metaphor of the banquet: life passes dishes before you — take a moderate portion, don't grab, and when the plate passes, don't reach after it
5. Diogenes's radical freedom — owning nothing, fearing nothing, and therefore free from everything
