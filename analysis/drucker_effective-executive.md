# The Effective Executive — Peter F. Drucker
## Central Thesis
Effectiveness can be learned. Intelligence, imagination, and knowledge are essential resources — but only effectiveness converts them into results. The effective executive focuses on contribution, manages time ruthlessly, builds on strengths (not weaknesses), concentrates on the few major areas where superior performance will produce outstanding results, and makes effective decisions. Effectiveness is a habit — a complex of practices that can be learned.
## Key Frameworks: Five Habits of Effectiveness (know thy time, focus on contribution, make strength productive, first things first, effective decisions), Time management (record-analyse-consolidate), Contribution focus ("What can I contribute?"), Strength-based deployment, Concentration (do first things first, second things not at all)
## Best Stories: Alfred Sloan at GM, Harry Truman's decision-making, the executive who scheduled "unscheduled time"
