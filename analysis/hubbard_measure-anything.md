# How to Measure Anything — Douglas W. Hubbard

## Classification & Context
**Book type:** Argument-driven methodology book. Hubbard builds a systematic case that anything can be measured — including "intangible" things like employee morale, brand value, or cybersecurity risk — and provides a step-by-step framework (Applied Information Economics) for doing so.
**Author:** Douglas Hubbard is a management consultant specialising in quantitative decision analysis. He developed the Applied Information Economics (AIE) framework and has consulted for organisations ranging from the US Department of Defense to Fortune 500 companies.

## Central Thesis
Everything is measurable if you define measurement correctly. Measurement does not mean perfect precision — it means an observation that reduces uncertainty. Most organisations make critical decisions based on gut feeling when even a small amount of measurement would dramatically improve outcomes. The problem is not that things are immeasurable — it's that people don't know what measurement actually is, and they over-invest in measuring the wrong things while ignoring the variables that actually matter.

## Key Frameworks
- Measurement redefined: an observation that quantitatively reduces uncertainty (not "exact number")
- Applied Information Economics (AIE): (1) Define the decision, (2) Determine what you already know, (3) Compute the value of additional information, (4) Measure where it matters most, (5) Make a decision
- Calibrated estimation: training humans to give honest confidence intervals (90% CI)
- The Rule of Five: a random sample of 5 from any population has a 93.75% chance of containing the median
- Expected Value of Information (EVI): measure only what reduces uncertainty enough to change your decision
- Monte Carlo simulation: model uncertainty by running thousands of scenarios with probability distributions

## Best Stories
1. The "immeasurable" IT security risk that turned out to be easily modelled with basic probability
2. EPA measuring the value of migratory birds ($1 billion per year)
3. The lens-crafting monks who measured refractive error centuries before formal optics
4. Military examples where calibrated estimation beat expensive sensor systems
5. Organisations spending millions on metrics that have zero impact on decisions
