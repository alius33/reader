# The Road to Character -- David Brooks

## Classification and Context

**Book type:** Argument with biographical portraits -- a central thesis (modern culture overvalues external achievement and undervalues inner character) illustrated through deep biographical studies of historical figures who cultivated what Brooks calls "eulogy virtues."

**Audience:** Readers interested in moral philosophy, character development, biography, and the tension between worldly success and inner depth.

**Author:** David Brooks is a Canadian-American conservative political and cultural commentator. Op-ed columnist for the New York Times since 2003, commentator on PBS NewsHour, frequent contributor to NPR and NBC. Previously at the Weekly Standard and Wall Street Journal. Known for bridging political commentary with moral philosophy and social science. Author of Bobos in Paradise (2000) and The Social Animal (2011).

## Central Thesis

Modern culture has shifted from a "culture of humility" (epitomised by the World War II generation) to a "culture of the Big Me" -- a society that celebrates self-promotion, external achievement, and personal branding while neglecting the harder, slower work of building inner character. Brooks distinguishes between "résumé virtues" (skills that help you succeed in the marketplace) and "eulogy virtues" (qualities people praise at your funeral -- courage, honesty, faithfulness, depth of relationships). The book argues that the road to character runs through confrontation with your own weaknesses, submission to a moral tradition or calling larger than yourself, and the hard discipline of self-conquest.

- The Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire (1911)
- Perkins' transformation from socialite to labour reformer

- Eisenhower's volcanic temper as a young man
- His mother Ida's influence and discipline
- The long years of suppressing ego under MacArthur

- Day's dissolute youth -- drinking, affairs, bohemian life
- The birth of her daughter Tamar as a turning point
- Conversion to Catholicism and founding the Catholic Worker movement

### Chapter 5: Self-Mastery -- George Marshall
- Marshall's self-effacing discipline
- Turning down the D-Day command
- The Marshall Plan

### Chapter 6: Dignity -- A. Philip Randolph and Bayard Rustin
- Randolph's fight for the Brotherhood of Sleeping Car Porters
- Rustin's behind-the-scenes brilliance organising the March on Washington

- Eliot's physical unattractiveness and intellectual brilliance
- Her scandalous relationship with George Henry Lewes

### Chapter 8: Ordered Love -- Augustine
- Augustine's early life of ambition and sensuality

- Johnson's struggles with depression, compulsion, and sloth

### Chapter 10: The Big Me
- Return to the cultural shift thesis
- The Humility Code (15 propositions)
- The road to character as a counterweight to the achievement culture
- Call for moral realism

## Key Frameworks

- **Résumé Virtues vs. Eulogy Virtues:** External marketplace skills vs. internal moral qualities
- **Adam I vs. Adam II:** The career-oriented self vs. the moral/spiritual self (from Rabbi Joseph Soloveitchik)
- **The Summoned Self vs. The Big Me:** Being called by external circumstances vs. cultivating self-brand
- **The U-shaped curve of character:** Weakness → struggle → confrontation → self-conquest → depth
- **Ordered Love (Augustine):** Loving the right things in the right order -- the hierarchy of attachments
- **The Humility Code:** 15 propositions for building character (Chapter 10)
- **The Crooked Timber tradition:** Kant's insight that "out of the crooked timber of humanity, no straight thing was ever made"

## Best Stories

1. Frances Perkins witnessing the Triangle Shirtwaist Factory fire (1911) -- 146 workers dying as she watched, transforming her life's direction
2. Eisenhower's mother Ida forcing him to bandage his own infected hand after she forbade him from going to a friend's house and he beat his fists against a tree
3. Dorothy Day giving birth to Tamar and feeling gratitude so overwhelming it demanded a response -- conversion
4. George Marshall being passed over for the D-Day command by FDR, responding with total loyalty and zero self-pity
5. Bayard Rustin organising the March on Washington from behind the scenes while A. Philip Randolph took the public credit, because Rustin's homosexuality made him a political liability
6. George Eliot being so plain that Henry James called her "magnificently ugly" -- yet producing the most morally profound novels in English
7. Augustine hearing the child's voice saying "Take up and read" in the garden in Milan (386 AD)
8. Samuel Johnson's prayers of self-accusation -- begging God for the strength to stop being lazy, written every New Year's Day for decades
9. The 1945 Bing Crosby radio broadcast vs. modern self-help culture -- Brooks' opening contrast
