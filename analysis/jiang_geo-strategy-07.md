# Who Killed Iranian President Ebrahim Raisi

## Core Question

How and why did Iranian President Ebrahim Raisi die on May 19, 2024 — and who benefits from his death?

Prof. Jiang frames this as an exercise in **game theory analysis under information constraints**. He identifies three epistemological problems that anyone analysing current events faces: (1) very limited information available, (2) the information that exists may be false (misinformation), and (3) there is always an official narrative that may not be true but is widely accepted. The lecture's method is to evaluate the three possibilities — accident, foreign adversary, internal enemy — by examining opportunity and motive for each.

The deeper question beneath the surface: is the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) engineering a power consolidation that will accelerate Iran toward war with the United States?

## Lecture Type

Geopolitical analysis — game theory applied to a current event. The lecture uses the death of Raisi as a case study to examine Iranian internal power dynamics, the IRGC's institutional interests, and the implications for the Iran-US confrontation arc that has been building since Lecture 1.

## Structural Map

1. **Opening framework — three problems of analysis** (0:00-1:30): Prof. Jiang establishes the epistemological constraints: limited information, misinformation, and official narratives. He frames the lecture as a game theory exercise rather than a factual investigation.

2. **The established facts** (1:30-3:00): On May 19, Raisi was in Azerbaijan for a dam opening ceremony. His helicopter crashed while flying back, killing all nine on board: the President, Foreign Minister, three crew members, a provincial governor, a local religious leader, the President's bodyguard, and the head of his security detail.

3. **Possibility 1: Accident** (3:00-4:30): The most probable explanation. Evidence: (a) helicopter crashes happen frequently — Kobe Bryant's 2020 death as a precedent; (b) the helicopter was an old American model from the 1970s that Iran could never properly maintain after the revolution due to US sanctions and non-cooperation. Bad weather (fog) caused the pilot to crash into a mountain. Prof. Jiang acknowledges this is probably the explanation that will go down in history.

4. **Possibility 2: Foreign adversary** (4:30-7:30): The US, Israel, or Azerbaijan. Problems: (a) opportunity — extremely difficult for a foreign government to plan an assassination of an Iranian leader inside Iranian territory; (b) motive — unclear what strategic benefit comes from killing the president specifically. Prof. Jiang reviews three precedents of foreign assassinations of Iranians: the January 2020 Soleimani assassination by Trump (clear motive: Soleimani ran Middle East foreign policy), Israeli assassinations of nuclear scientists (clear motive: stopping nuclear weapons), and the April 1 Damascus embassy airstrike killing two Iranian generals (clear motive: generals working with Hamas). In each case the motive was clear and specific. For Raisi, the motive is unclear and the risk is high. Prof. Jiang does not entirely discount this possibility but finds it the weakest.

5. **Possibility 3: Internal enemy** (7:30-10:00): Certain factions within Iran wanted Raisi dead. Opportunity: no problem — internal enemies have the resources and access to track and kill the president. Key evidence: three helicopters were flying together; only Raisi's crashed; the other two (likely military escort) were fine. The escorts were there to protect the president — but if they wanted to kill him, they could. The motive question is then examined in detail.

6. **The succession stakes — Raisi vs. Mojtaba Khamenei** (10:00-12:00): Raisi, age 63, was widely expected to succeed Ayatollah Khamenei (age 85) as Supreme Leader within five years. Prof. Jiang explains the Iranian political system: it is an Islamic Republic where ultimate authority rests with the Ayatollah (who represents God and the Quran), while the President functions as the CEO. With Raisi gone, the second contender for next Ayatollah is Mojtaba Khamenei — the current Supreme Leader's son. This creates a legitimacy crisis: Iranians overthrew a hereditary monarchy in 1979, and hereditary succession to the supreme leadership would undermine the Islamic Republic's founding principle.

7. **The IRGC — origins, power, and corruption** (12:00-20:00): The lecture's central exposition. Prof. Jiang traces the IRGC from its founding to its current dominance:
   - **Founding (1979):** Ayatollah Khomeini returned from exile but feared the army (which had sworn allegiance to the deposed Shah). He established the IRGC as a parallel armed force loyal only to him, separate from the regular military.
   - **Dual mandate:** (a) protect the Ayatollah and the Islamic Revolution domestically; (b) export the revolution across the Middle East (Syria, Iraq, etc.).
   - **Forged in the Iran-Iraq War (1980-1988):** When Saddam Hussein invaded (supported by the US, Soviets, and Saudi Arabia), the IRGC successfully defended the revolution, becoming the dominant military force with virtually unlimited funding.
   - **Economic corruption:** Over time, the IRGC came to control an estimated 10-50% of the entire Iranian economy. This monopoly of power stagnated the economy.
   - **Waves of protest:** 1999 (50,000 students protesting lack of democracy — really protesting IRGC corruption); 2009-2010 Green Movement (reform candidate lost a suspected rigged election — IRGC suppressed violently); 2023 Mahsa Amini protests (young woman died in police custody for failing to wear head covering — massive nationwide protests). Each protest wave was crushed by the IRGC.
   - **Paradox of protest:** The more protests there are, the more powerful the IRGC becomes, because the regime depends on them as its most loyal enforcer.

8. **Why the IRGC benefits from Raisi's death** (19:00-22:00): The central game theory argument. Any new Ayatollah would need to establish his own power base, which means reducing IRGC power — they are loyal to the current Ayatollah, not to the institution of the Ayatollah. If Raisi became Supreme Leader, he would threaten their authority. But if Mojtaba Khamenei becomes Ayatollah, things stay the same because: (a) he is deeply unpopular, (b) he would only be in power because the IRGC put him there, (c) he would therefore be dependent on and obedient to the IRGC. Even better: if his accession creates a legitimacy crisis, the regime becomes even more dependent on the IRGC to maintain order.

9. **Student Q&A — the military vs. IRGC** (36:31-37:30): Student (Celine) asks if the regular military still exists. Prof. Jiang confirms yes, but the IRGC dominates because it controls: (a) the Navy (critical for the Strait of Hormuz, also enables smuggling revenue); (b) the missile programme (where most military spending goes); (c) foreign policy (the Axis of Resistance — Soleimani's domain).

10. **Student Q&A — why IRGC can control Mojtaba** (37:30-40:00): Student (Jack) asks why the IRGC believes it can control Mojtaba. Prof. Jiang explains: when Khamenei succeeded Khomeini in 1989, he deliberately cultivated personal relationships with every IRGC member — reportedly memorising 20,000-50,000 names including their children's names. This personal bond transfers to Mojtaba's credibility. More importantly, Mojtaba is extremely unpopular — he has no independent power base. If he becomes Ayatollah, it will only be because the IRGC installed him, so he must remain loyal to them.

11. **Student Q&A — what changes now** (40:11-44:00): Student (Celine) asks what changes with Raisi dead. Prof. Jiang distinguishes two scenarios:
    - **If accident:** Nothing fundamental changes — the Ayatollah still controls policy.
    - **If IRGC assassination:** Everything changes — the political class is sidelined and the military class takes over. Predictions: (a) Mohammed Mokhber (current VP, from the IRGC) wins the late June election; (b) rhetoric becomes more extremist, preparing the population for total war; (c) harsher crackdown on political dissent.

12. **The IRGC's revolutionary DNA — the Embassy Hostage Crisis** (22:00-26:00): Prof. Jiang traces the ideological formation of current IRGC leadership back to 1979. When the Shah fled to America, Iranians feared (a) the Shah would escape justice for human rights crimes, and (b) the US would invade and reinstall the Shah (as it did in 1953). In November 1979, 300-500 students stormed the US Embassy and took 53 Americans hostage. The students methodically pieced together shredded embassy documents over months, discovering that the real centre of power in Iran was not the palace but the US Embassy — it directed policy, enabled brutality, and staged the 1953 coup. Three of those students became top military leaders: Hossein Dehghan (Minister of Defence 2013-2017), Mohammed Ali Jafari (head of IRGC 2007-2019), and Mohammed Bagheri (current Chief of Staff of the Iranian Army).

13. **The Basij — Iran's volunteer army** (26:00-31:00): When Saddam invaded in 1980, Iran had no functioning army (the IRGC had arrested, exiled, or executed officers loyal to the Shah). The IRGC created the Basij — a volunteer army of poor, religious villagers, aged 16-18, given a rifle and a key (symbolising entry to heaven upon death in battle). The Basij would run ahead across Iraqi minefields and into tank fire, absorbing so much ammunition that the Iraqi army became exhausted. Despite lacking armour or equipment, they forced Iraq to withdraw. This demonstrates that Iran's military power rests on fanaticism and willingness to absorb casualties. After the Iran-Iraq War, the IRGC concluded: if enemies attack, Iranians will die — so take the fight to them. This is why the IRGC funds the Axis of Resistance.

14. **Domestic opposition to IRGC policy** (31:00-33:00): Many Iranians question the IRGC's Middle East interventionism. Their argument: if Iran withdrew from the Middle East and focused on its economy, Israel, Saudi Arabia, and the US would have no reason to attack. The problem might not be Israel or America but the IRGC itself — their fanaticism creates enemies and cripples the economy.

15. **Evidence of political restraint vs. IRGC hawkishness** (32:47-36:00): Two examples: (a) After Soleimani's assassination in January 2020, the IRGC demanded vengeance and war, but politicians like Raisi counselled patience and restraint; (b) After the April 1 Damascus embassy strike, the IRGC again wanted full war, but politicians argued they could not beat the US and Israel and should be strategic and patient. Prof. Jiang suggests the IRGC viewed these politicians as cowards and non-believers, and that the May 19 fog presented an opportunity.

16. **Escalation predictions — four provocations** (42:00-46:00): If the IRGC is behind Raisi's death, Prof. Jiang predicts four escalation moves designed to provoke the US into invading: (a) accelerate the nuclear programme (an existential red line for Israel); (b) encourage proxies — Hezbollah attacks on Israel, Shia militia attacks on US bases in Iraq, Houthi attacks on Saudi oil fields; (c) disrupt shipping lanes; (d) launch terror attacks against the US and allies through proxies. Prof. Jiang adds a critical caveat: even if these things happen, it does not prove the IRGC killed Raisi — but if the IRGC did kill Raisi, these escalations should follow.

17. **The war strategy — luring America in** (46:38-48:00): Student (Celine) asks how the IRGC would fight. Prof. Jiang: they can only win one way — by luring America into invading Iran. An open fight against American shock and awe (satellites, air supremacy, special forces) is suicide. Iran must provoke America into sending an invasion force into Iranian territory, where the terrain and population will ensure American defeat. This is the topic of Lecture 8.

18. **Closing — critical caveats** (47:40-end): Prof. Jiang reiterates this is speculation with no evidence. He has never said Iran will win the war — only that the US will lose it. The war will be brutal for Iran: tens of millions will die. Politicians like Raisi understood this and counselled restraint. He promises Lecture 8 will explore what the actual war would look like.

## Central Thesis

The death of Iranian President Ebrahim Raisi was most likely an accident — but game theory analysis reveals that the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC) had both the opportunity and the strongest motive to orchestrate it. Raisi was expected to succeed Ayatollah Khamenei as Supreme Leader, and any new Supreme Leader would need to establish his own power base by reducing IRGC power. By eliminating Raisi, the IRGC clears the path for Mojtaba Khamenei — an unpopular figure who would be entirely dependent on the IRGC for his authority, thus preserving their monopoly on power and policy. If this analysis is correct, the consequence is a fundamental shift in Iranian governance: the political class is sidelined, the military class takes over, and Iran accelerates toward confrontation with the United States.

The chain of reasoning:
1. Raisi died in a helicopter crash — probably an accident, but game theory demands examining alternatives
2. Foreign adversary theory fails on both opportunity (extremely difficult inside Iran) and motive (no clear benefit from killing the president specifically)
3. Internal enemy theory succeeds on opportunity (military escorts were present; only Raisi's helicopter crashed) and reveals strong motive
4. Raisi was the presumptive next Supreme Leader — any new leader would reduce IRGC power to build his own base
5. With Raisi gone, Mojtaba Khamenei becomes the most likely successor — he is unpopular and would depend entirely on the IRGC
6. Even a legitimacy crisis from hereditary succession benefits the IRGC: more instability = more dependence on them
7. If the IRGC has consolidated control, policy shifts toward provocation: nuclear acceleration, proxy escalation, shipping disruption, and terror campaigns — all designed to lure the US into invading Iran
8. The IRGC wants this invasion because it believes it can win on home territory (Lecture 8 will explore why)

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: The Three Possibilities Framework

**The theory:** When analysing current events with limited information, you must evaluate all possibilities by examining opportunity and motive for each actor — not just accept the official narrative.

**Evidence for the accident theory:**
- Helicopter crashes are common — Kobe Bryant's 2020 crash as precedent
- The helicopter was an old American model from the 1970s, never properly maintained due to US sanctions post-revolution
- Bad weather (fog) obscured the pilot's vision, causing a mountain collision
- This is the explanation most widely accepted and likely to go down in history

**Evidence for the foreign adversary theory:**
- Precedent exists: US killed Soleimani (Jan 2020), Israel killed nuclear scientists, Israel struck Damascus embassy (April 2024)
- But: opportunity is extremely difficult for a foreign government operating inside Iranian territory
- But: motive is unclear — the president is not a military threat like Soleimani or nuclear scientists
- But: the risk of getting caught is enormous compared to the benefit

**Evidence for the internal enemy theory:**
- Opportunity: strong — three helicopters flew together, only Raisi's crashed; the military escort could have caused it
- Motive: strongest of all three — Raisi's death directly benefits the IRGC's power preservation (see Argument 2)

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang favours accident as most probable but argues the internal enemy theory deserves serious examination because it explains subsequent events best. He explicitly acknowledges this is speculation.

### Argument 2: The IRGC's Institutional Motive

**The theory:** The IRGC had the strongest motive to kill Raisi because his succession to Supreme Leader would directly threaten their monopoly on power, money, and policy.

**Evidence for:**
- Raisi (age 63) was widely expected to succeed Khamenei (age 85) within five years
- Any new Supreme Leader must build his own power base by reducing the incumbent power-holders
- The IRGC's power derives from personal loyalty to the current Ayatollah — transferable to his son but not to an outsider like Raisi
- With Raisi gone, Mojtaba Khamenei becomes the likely successor — he is deeply unpopular, has no independent power base, and would only become Ayatollah through IRGC support, making him entirely dependent on them
- Even if hereditary succession causes a legitimacy crisis, this benefits the IRGC: more instability = more dependence on the IRGC as enforcers
- There is historical evidence of the IRGC's willingness to crack down brutally (1999 student protests, 2009 Green Movement, 2023 Mahsa Amini protests)

**Evidence against:**
- Prof. Jiang acknowledges he has no direct evidence — this is entirely game theory analysis
- The IRGC might not need to kill Raisi — Khamenei could simply nominate his son
- Killing the president creates its own risks (exposure, backlash, foreign scrutiny)

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang presents this as the most analytically satisfying explanation — the motive is structural and institutional, not personal.

### Argument 3: The IRGC as Revolutionary Fanatics

**The theory:** The IRGC is not merely a corrupt military-industrial complex — it is run by ideological fanatics whose worldview was formed in the 1979 Embassy Crisis and the Iran-Iraq War, and who genuinely believe in exporting Islamic revolution and confronting America.

**Evidence for:**
- Three current top military leaders (Dehghan, Jafari, Bagheri) were among the students who stormed the US Embassy in 1979
- These students personally pieced together shredded embassy documents, discovering the extent of US control over Iran (the 1953 coup, the Shah's brutality directed by the Embassy)
- The Basij volunteer army — poor religious villagers given rifles and keys to heaven — ran across minefields and absorbed Iraqi fire through sheer fanatical willingness to die
- The IRGC's entire post-war strategy (funding the Axis of Resistance) is premised on taking the fight to enemies before they come to Iran
- When politicians counselled restraint after Soleimani's assassination and the Damascus strike, the IRGC saw them as cowards and non-believers

**Evidence against:**
- Many Iranians — possibly including political leaders — see the IRGC as the problem, not the solution
- The IRGC's interventionism has crippled the Iranian economy and given enemies legitimate reasons to attack
- Rational self-interest might argue against provoking a war that will kill tens of millions of Iranians

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang argues the IRGC's revolutionary fanaticism makes them genuinely willing to pursue war — they are not cynical power-brokers but true believers whose worldview was formed by direct experience of American interference.

### Argument 4: The Political Class vs. Military Class Dynamic

**The theory:** Iran's internal politics are defined by a tension between a political class (pragmatists who counsel restraint) and a military class (the IRGC and its allies who push for confrontation). Raisi represented the political class; his death shifts the balance decisively toward the military class.

**Evidence for:**
- Raisi came from the judiciary — a different power base from the IRGC
- After Soleimani's assassination, politicians urged restraint while the IRGC demanded vengeance
- After the Damascus strike, the same dynamic repeated — politicians argued for patience, IRGC wanted war
- If Mohammed Mokhber (current VP, from the IRGC) becomes president, military interests directly control the presidency
- Prof. Jiang predicts: more extremist rhetoric, harsh crackdowns, preparation for total war

**Evidence against:**
- The Ayatollah still holds ultimate authority regardless of who is president
- The political class may not be as unified or as dovish as presented
- [INFERENCE] Even IRGC members may have internal divisions on the wisdom of provoking the US

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang treats this as a binary: if the IRGC killed Raisi, the political class is finished and the military class dictates policy. If it was an accident, the dynamic continues but the balance has still shifted.

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### Game Theory Analysis Under Information Constraints
- **Definition:** Evaluating possibilities by examining opportunity and motive for each actor when direct evidence is limited or unreliable
- **How it works:** You accept the most probable explanation (accident) but systematically explore alternatives by asking "who benefits?" (cui bono) and "who had the means?"
- **What it explains:** Why the IRGC is the most analytically interesting suspect — they had both opportunity and the strongest structural motive
- **Introduced:** This lecture (though game theory was referenced in prior lectures)

### The Three Problems of Analysis
- **Definition:** Three epistemological challenges in analysing current events: (1) very limited information; (2) available information may be false; (3) official narratives are widely accepted but may not be true
- **How it works:** Forces the analyst to be humble about certainty while still pursuing analytical rigor
- **What it explains:** Why Prof. Jiang repeatedly caveats his analysis as speculation
- **Introduced:** This lecture

### The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC)
- **Definition:** A parallel armed force established by Ayatollah Khomeini in 1979, separate from and more powerful than the regular Iranian military
- **How it works:** Dual mandate: (a) protect the Ayatollah and the Islamic Revolution domestically; (b) export the revolution across the Middle East. Controls the Navy (Strait of Hormuz), the missile programme, and foreign policy (Axis of Resistance). Estimated to control 10-50% of the Iranian economy.
- **What it explains:** Why Iran's foreign policy is so aggressive — it is driven by revolutionary fanatics, not pragmatic politicians. Why Iran's economy stagnates — monopoly of power crowds out private enterprise.
- **First introduced in detail:** This lecture (referenced in passing in Lectures 1 and 4 through Soleimani and the Axis of Resistance)

### The Basij (Volunteer Army)
- **Definition:** A volunteer militia of poor, religious Iranians organized by the IRGC, originally formed during the Iran-Iraq War
- **How it works:** Religious motivation substitutes for military equipment — volunteers given rifles and keys (symbolising heaven) run into enemy fire, absorbing ammunition and exhausting the opposing force. Tens of millions available for conscription.
- **What it explains:** Why Iran is not afraid of a ground war despite military inferiority — it has an effectively unlimited supply of religiously motivated fighters willing to die
- **Introduced:** This lecture (complements the asymmetric warfare concept from Lecture 1)

### The Succession Dilemma
- **Definition:** The structural problem that any new Supreme Leader must reduce IRGC power to establish his own authority, creating an inherent conflict of interest between the IRGC and any non-IRGC successor
- **How it works:** The IRGC's power derives from personal loyalty to the current Ayatollah. A new Ayatollah will bring his own people, reducing IRGC influence. The exception: if the successor is so weak and unpopular that he depends on the IRGC for legitimacy.
- **What it explains:** Why the IRGC would prefer Mojtaba Khamenei (weak, dependent) over Raisi (independent, judiciary-based)
- **Introduced:** This lecture

### The Provocation Strategy
- **Definition:** The IRGC's four-pronged approach to provoking the US into invading Iran: nuclear acceleration, proxy escalation, shipping disruption, and terror campaigns
- **How it works:** The IRGC cannot defeat America in open combat (shock and awe = suicide). The only path to victory is luring America into invading Iran. To do this, they must provoke America beyond its tolerance threshold.
- **What it explains:** Why seemingly irrational Iranian escalation is actually strategic — every provocation is designed to bring the US closer to invasion, which is the only scenario where Iran can win
- **Connected to:** Lecture 1 (Iran Strategy Matrix — all four goals must be served simultaneously), Lecture 6 (shock and awe fails in mountains — the strategic basis for wanting invasion on home turf)
- **Introduced:** This lecture (previewing Lecture 8)

### The Political Class vs. Military Class Divide
- **Definition:** Iran's internal power struggle between pragmatic politicians (who counsel strategic patience) and the IRGC (who push for confrontation and revolution export)
- **How it works:** Politicians like Raisi understood that war with the US would kill tens of millions and urged caution. The IRGC viewed this as cowardice and betrayal of revolutionary ideals.
- **What it explains:** Why Raisi's death is consequential even if it was an accident — it shifts the balance of power toward the military class regardless of the cause
- **Introduced:** This lecture

### Q2: Why does the IRGC believe it can control Mojtaba? (Jack)

**Added insight:** This is the key strategic logic — the IRGC doesn't need a loyal ally, they need a weak one. Weakness = dependency = control.
