# A History of Britain Vol 3: The Fate of Empire -- Simon Schama

## Classification & Context

- **Book type:** Narrative history -- interpretive, essay-driven, story-centred. Not a textbook but a deliberately selective, frankly personal reading of modern British history
- **Audience:** General educated reader; companion to the BBC2 television series (2000-2002)
- **Schama's approach:** Concentrates on a smaller number of stories and arguments, treated in detail, rather than giving cursory attention to everything. The last half of the 20th century is treated with "essay-like breadth and looseness" because Schama has trouble treating any period contemporary with his own life as history
- **Relation to Vols 1 & 2:** This is the final volume of a trilogy. Vol 1 covered 3000 BC to 1603, Vol 2 covered 1603-1776. This volume picks up at the American Revolution and carries through to 2000
- **Distinctive feature:** Schama explicitly tries to bring together imperial and domestic history, looking at the importance of India in particular for Britain's prosperity and power, and the responsibility the Raj had for India's and Ireland's plight. The epigraphs from H.G. Wells (Tono-Bungay) and George Orwell (England Your England) frame the entire book -- Wells's vision of an England passing away, Orwell's of an England that changes beyond recognition yet remains the same
- **Narrative voice:** Literary, evocative, with long character studies. Schama uses biographical portraits (Thomas Pennant, Thomas Bewick, Mary Wollstonecraft, William Wordsworth, Victoria, Curzon, Churchill, Orwell) as windows into larger historical forces

## Central Thesis

Schama argues that modern Britain has been shaped by a persistent, irresolvable tension between two impulses: the romantic desire to recover an authentic, "natural" Britain (rooted in landscape, community, and ancient liberties) and the relentless drive of industrial empire that transformed and often destroyed the very communities the romantics cherished. The "fate of empire" in the title refers not just to the overseas empire but to the empire of industrial modernity itself -- the conviction that progress, commerce, and rational administration would automatically produce prosperity and freedom for all.

The book traces how this faith was repeatedly tested and found wanting -- in the enclosures that destroyed rural communities, in the Irish famine that killed a million while the treasury preached self-reliance, in Indian famines that killed tens of millions while the Raj boasted of its civilizing mission, in the industrial wastelands of 1930s Lancashire. Yet Schama also argues that Britain's distinctive achievement was a tradition of self-correction: moral crusades (abolition, suffrage, welfare reform) that arose from within the system to challenge its worst excesses, driven by what 18th-century writers called "social affection" -- the assumption that human beings are bound by natural sympathy.

The book culminates in a double portrait of Churchill and Orwell as complementary embodiments of two visions of British history -- Churchill's pageant of parliamentary liberty and imperial grandeur, Orwell's tradition of popular resistance and common decency -- arguing that both are needed for Britain to face its post-imperial future honestly.

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Preface

- Schama warns this is "a" history, not "the" history -- the indefinite article is truly warranted
- He opts for a smaller number of stories treated in depth rather than cursory comprehensiveness
- His distinctive goal: to unite imperial and domestic history, especially the role of India in Britain's prosperity and the Raj's responsibility for India's and Ireland's suffering
- The 20th century is treated with essay-like looseness; he struggles to treat his own lifetime as "history"
- Dedicated to the memory of historian Roy Porter (1946-2002)

### Chapter 1: Forces of Nature: The Road to Revolution

**Main argument:** While Britain was losing an empire (America), it was finding itself -- through a Romantic rediscovery of its own landscape, people, and "natural" identity. This recovery of national authenticity fuelled both conservative patriotism and radical reform.

**Key stories:**
1. **Thomas Pennant's travels (1770s):** A Flintshire gentleman-naturalist who toured Wales and the Hebrides documenting "ancient, outlandish, unpolished Britain" -- druids' circles, the formidable 90-year-old Margaret Uch Evans (huntress, fiddler, wrestler, blacksmith). Pennant and Thomas West's Lake District guide told Britons: "come home." The message was that the British had wandered too much, lost touch with their own landscape. The domestic tour became a "tutorial in the recovery of national virtue."

2. **Thomas Day's experiment (1769):** A Rousseau disciple who hand-picked two orphan girls (Sabrina and Lucretia) to raise as candidates for a perfect wife based on Rousseau's principles. He poured hot wax on Sabrina's arm and fired guns near her head to test her fortitude. The experiment was a spectacular failure. Day himself died in 1789 when an unbroken horse he was trying to tame with gentleness threw him on his head.

3. **Thomas Bewick's childhood in Northumberland:** Born 1753, son of a farmer-collier. Escaped school by playing truant, drawing on every surface (gravestones, church floors). Two formative moments of animal compassion: catching a hare being coursed and hearing its child-like screams; knocking a bullfinch from its perch and seeing it look at him "piteously." Became Britain's greatest naturalist-illustrator. His wood-engraved vignettes smuggled in portraits of rural poverty between pictures of birds.

4. **Rousseau's disastrous stay in England (1766):** Invited by David Hume, Rousseau arrived seasick and paranoid. Believed Hume was conspiring against him. In Derbyshire locals remembered him as "owd Ross Hall" in his strange Armenian costume gathering herbs. Fled back to France within a year. But his cult of sensibility put down deep roots in provincial England -- the "Lichfield circle" around Brooke Boothby, Erasmus Darwin, and Anna Seward.

5. **Oliver Goldsmith's "The Deserted Village" (1769):** The great verse lament for rural community destroyed by enclosure and "engrossment" (consolidation of small farms into large estates). "One only master grasps the whole domain / And half a tillage stints thy smiling plain." Hugely influential on public opinion.

**Key concepts:**
- The equation of walking with democracy and moral virtue (Karl Moritz, "Walking" Stewart, Wordsworth)
- Breast-feeding as a political statement (Rousseau's influence on parenting)
- "Social affection" -- the assumption expressed in Sterne's sermon that we are naturally bound to "take part in every accident to which man is subject"
- The evangelical revival of the 1770s-80s: Unitarians like Joseph Priestley and Richard Price reading Jesus as the first social reformer
- The anti-slavery campaign: Wedgwood's "Am I not a Man and a Brother?" medallion; the Zong scandal
- The beginning of parliamentary reform agitation

**Connection to next chapter:** The chapter ends with the French Revolution electrifying British politics, dividing the country between Paine-ite radicals and Burke-ite patriots -- a division that Chapter 2 will trace through the lives of Wordsworth and Coleridge.

### Chapter 2: Forces of Nature: The Road Home

**Main argument:** The generation that had been electrified by the French Revolution gradually "came home" -- literally and philosophically -- as the revolution devoured its own, Napoleon threatened invasion, and nature replaced politics as the source of moral regeneration. But this retreat from radicalism did not eliminate the demand for social justice; it merely channelled it differently.

**Key stories:**
1. **Mary Wollstonecraft's life and death:** Her attack on Rousseau in A Vindication of the Rights of Woman (1792); her disastrous infatuation with Fuseli; her journey to revolutionary Paris where she witnessed Louis XVI's trial; her affair with the American Gilbert Imlay who abandoned her; her two suicide attempts (laudanum overdose, then jumping from Putney Bridge, rescued by the Royal Humane Society's boatmen); her redemption through marriage to William Godwin; her death from septicaemia after childbirth at 38.

2. **Wordsworth's journey in and out of revolution:** His walking tour of France in 1790 (landing at Calais on the eve of the Bastille anniversary); his love affair with Annette Vallon and their illegitimate daughter Caroline; his friendship with the French officer Beaupuis who showed him a "hunger-bitten girl" leading a heifer; his painful return to England; his gradual conversion from Paine-ite revolutionary to Burkean conservative patriot.

3. **The treason trials of 1794:** Thomas Hardy, John Thelwall, and Horne Tooke arrested and charged with "compassing the death of the king." Thomas Erskine's heroic seven-hour defence speech. Hardy acquitted; the jury applauded. The crowd untethered horses from carriages and pulled them through the streets.

4. **Nelson's apotheosis (1805-6):** His funeral was on a scale that "completely overshadowed William Pitt's" the following month. The body preserved in alcohol, borne from Greenwich to St Paul's in black barges "like Arthur to Avalon." Nelson's genius for self-promotion made him the first military man treated as a Romantic genius.

5. **Peterloo (16 August 1819):** 50,000-60,000 gathered peacefully in St Peter's Fields, Manchester, beneath "Universal Suffrage" banners, some singing Methodist hymns. The Manchester and Salford Yeomanry charged in to arrest the speakers; a small girl trampled to death; hussars sent in with unsheathed sabres. 11 killed, 421 seriously wounded, 162 with sabre cuts. Lord Sidmouth congratulated the magistrates; Wordsworth seems to have agreed.

6. **William Cobbett's conversion (c.1803-4):** The ex-government journalist visited Horton Heath and saw its unenclosed common supporting 100 beehives, 60 pigs, 15 cows, 800 poultry -- proof that commons were not uneconomic waste. Then he calculated: 1 million paupers in England and Wales, one-eighth of the population. His Weekly Political Register sold 60,000 copies a week at its height.

**Key concepts:**
- Coleridge's three 1798 poems ("Fears in Solitude," "France: An Ode," "Frost at Midnight") as a manifesto of disillusionment with France and embrace of patriotic nature
- "National defence patriotism" during the Napoleonic invasion scare: 400,000 volunteers in 1804; 800,000 in total national defence out of 15 million population
- The mythology of Merrie England born during the Napoleonic wars: Shakespeare revivals, Madame Tussaud's, historical costume books
- The Lyrical Ballads (1798) as a revolutionary act: plain speech, the broken and the poor, nature as physical reality not political slogan
- Hazlitt's savage portrait of the "Modern Tory" (1816) and his contempt for Wordsworth's apostasy
- The Reform Act of 1832: 140 boroughs lost members; new industrial towns enfranchised; the vote extended to half a million new voters; "Old Corruption" swept away
- Slavery abolished in all British colonies in 1833 -- "destroyed, overwhelmingly, by the force of moral argument"

**Connection to next chapter:** Chapter 2 ends with the paradox that industrial Britain was also the most attached to its village memories -- allotments beside railway tracks, public parks like Birkenhead. Chapter 3 picks up with the Great Exhibition as an attempt to reconcile these contradictions.

### Chapter 3: The Queen and the Hive

**Main argument:** The Great Exhibition of 1851 was the high-water mark of Victorian optimism -- the belief that industry, nature, and social harmony could all be reconciled. But this complacency was already being challenged by critics like Carlyle and by the Chartist movement, which represented the most serious working-class challenge to the political order since the Civil War.

**Key stories:**
1. **The Great Exhibition (1 May 1851):** Victoria's rapturous diary entry; Joseph Paxton's Crystal Palace built in 17 weeks; the two ancient 90-foot elms preserved inside by bending the transept ribs into a semicircular roof; 6 million visitors, three-quarters by railway; not a single act of vandalism reported. Wellington wanted 15,000 troops; Paxton insisted on cheap shilling admission days. "The People have now become the Exhibition."

2. **Thomas Carlyle's critique of the Machine Age:** Past and Present (1843) evoked the chronicle of Jocelin of Brakelond at Bury St Edmunds abbey to contrast medieval spiritual community with modern soulless materialism. "Nothing is now done directly or by hand; all is by rule and calculated contrivance." Yet the Victorians were not complacent -- "the more Carlyle berated them, the more they lapped up the punishment."

3. **Augustus Welby Northmore Pugin and Gothic Revival:** The son of a French immigrant designer, a prodigy summoned by George IV at 15 to design furniture. His Contrasts (1836) lined up devastating visual comparisons between medieval and modern towns. Converted to Catholicism. His masterpiece: St Giles at Cheadle, "a glowing vault of intense, radiant colour." Died at 40.

4. **Elizabeth Gaskell's Mary Barton (1848):** The first novel to give polite middle-class readers the voice of working-class Manchester, including dialect songs. Her tragic hero John Barton goes to London with the Chartist petition and is told by a policeman to stop "molesting the ladies and gentlemen." Gaskell was cold-shouldered by the Manchester cotton barons but stuck to her guns: "evils once recognized are half way towards their remedy."

5. **The Chartist crisis of 10 April 1848:** Feargus O'Connor's monster petition with five million names (allegedly), brought to Kennington Common on a farm wagon pulled by four dray horses. Wellington mobilized 85,000 special constables; the Stock Exchange volunteered 300 employees. O'Connor, looking at the logistical odds, chose retreat over confrontation. Subsequent myth dismissed 1848 as farce, but the London Illustrated News admonished parliament not to ridicule a petition signed by even a fraction of the claimed number.

6. **The Chartist Land Company at Great Dodford:** O'Connor's scheme to resettle redundant factory workers on smallholdings. Ann Wood, an Edinburgh charlady who had saved 150 pounds, got the pick of the lots and lived to 86. The conspicuous presence of women in the settlement.

**Key concepts:**
- Prince Albert's vision of the Exhibition as a template for a peaceful future: war between states replaced by peaceful competition of commerce
- The semantic somersault of "industry" -- conveying henceforth the saving, not the expenditure, of physical labour
- "Manchester, the entrance to hell realised" (Sir Charles Napier): average life expectancy for mechanics and labourers was 17 years vs. 38 for professionals
- The New Poor Law of 1834: workhouses as deliberate deterrents; families separated; inmates shorn and uniformed
- The Anti-Corn Law campaign as a model for modern pressure-group politics
- The Newport rising (3 November 1839): 15 dead, 50+ wounded -- "the largest loss of life inflicted by a British government on its own people at any time in the 19th or 20th century"

### Chapter 4: Wives, Daughters, Widows

**Main argument:** Victoria's reign was shaped by the paradox of a woman ruling an empire whose ideology confined women to the domestic sphere. Yet from within that ideology emerged the instruments of its own subversion -- photography, education, professional ambition, and ultimately political militancy.

**Key stories:**
1. **Victoria and Albert's marriage:** Victoria proposed; Albert was told his function was "decorative, supportive and generative, possibly in that order." Albert's elaborate job description: "the natural head of her family, superintendent of the royal household, manager of her private affairs, sole confidential adviser in politics." The Swiss Cottage at Osborne with child-sized furniture. Victoria's startling disenchantment: "the poor woman is bodily and morally the husband's slave."

2. **Albert's death (14 December 1861):** Typhoid misdiagnosed by Dr Clark. Victoria's howl of grief; her 40 years of compulsive mourning -- fresh clothes laid out daily, hot water and shaving soap, his nightshirt taken to bed with a plaster cast of his hand. "These commanding premises to be let or sold in consequence of the late occupant's declining business."

3. **Julia Margaret Cameron's photography:** Given a camera at 48; converted her coalhouse into a darkroom, her henhouse into a studio. Bullied Herschel, Carlyle, and Tennyson into ten-minute exposures. Denounced by the Photographic Society for "out of focus portraits." Her family was going bankrupt; photography was meant to make money as well as art. Died in Ceylon in 1879 at 64.

4. **Elizabeth Garrett Anderson's medical career:** Placed first in her teaching hospital's exams in 1861 when examiners didn't realize "E. Garrett" was a woman. Dissected body parts in her bedroom. The Society of Apothecaries, after she passed their exam, retroactively excluded women. At Edinburgh, a flock of sheep was pushed in after women students attempting to take the medical examination.

5. **The match girls' strike (1888):** Annie Besant exposed conditions at Bryant and May's Fairfield Works in The Link: wages of 4-12 shillings a week; "phossy jaw" from phosphorus fumes; heads bald at 15 from carrying boxes. 1,400 girls came out. "You had spoken up. We weren't going back on you." The company settled; Annie was hailed as champion of London working women.

**Key concepts:**
- The carte-de-visite revolution: hundreds of thousands of royal photographs circulated, placing the royal family on middle-class drawing-room tables for the first time
- The three holy texts of Victorian domesticity: Patmore's "The Angel in the House," Ruskin's "Of Queen's Gardens," Mrs Beeton's Book of Household Management (2 million copies before 1870)
- Ruskin's paradox: his marriage unconsummated, his obsession with adolescent Rose La Touche; yet Sesame and Lilies sold 160,000 copies
- Octavia Hill's Charity Organisation Society: tenants had to report weekly conduct; "persons of drunken, immoral or idle habits" evicted
- Emily Davies and Girton College (1873): Helena Swanwick's ecstasy at having "my own fire, my own desk, my own easy chair" -- and her mother's tears
- Darwin's Descent of Man (1871) shattering the "great sheltering dome of faith" and giving women new gospels of Education and Work
- Constance Lytton carving "V" for Votes on her breast in prison with a broken hatpin

### Chapter 5: The Empire of Good Intentions: Investments

**Main argument:** The liberal imperial project -- the belief that British law, education, free trade, and honest administration would transform India and raise it to self-governing prosperity -- was sincere but fatally flawed. It destroyed indigenous institutions and economies while failing to deliver the promised benefits, and its non-interventionist economic orthodoxy produced catastrophic results in both Ireland and India.

**Key stories:**
1. **Curzon as viceroy (1899-1905):** Arrived at his "home from home" (Government House, Calcutta, modelled on Kedleston Hall, Derbyshire). Commissioned the Victoria Memorial Monument as a "British Taj Mahal." But 19 million excess deaths from famine and disease in the previous decade; 6.5 million lives lost in the 1899-1900 famine alone. The memorial's statuary shows grateful natives "being succoured at the breast of the Mother Raj."

2. **Macaulay's Minute on Education (1835):** "A single shelf of a good European library was worth the whole native literature of India and Arabia." Versus the orientalist H.H. Wilson: "Macaulay knew nothing of the people." The creation of English-educated intermediaries -- "Indian in blood and colour but English in taste, opinions, morals and in intellect." The abolition of government grants to Islamic Madrassas and Hindu Sanskrit Colleges.

3. **The Scottish soldier-scholars (1800s-1830s):** Munro, Malcolm, Elphinstone, Thomason -- the first true imperial soldier-scholars, fluent in Indian languages, deeply versed in local customs. "What got created was a non-English British Indian government; perhaps the best the British ever made in Asia." They introduced the ryotwari system (direct peasant-to-government tax assessment) to replace extortionate middlemen.

4. **The Irish Famine (1845-1850):** Trevelyan at the treasury: "the judgement of God on an indolent and unself-reliant people." Peel's secret purchase of American corn saved lives in 1846, but Russell's Whig government stopped intervention. Gregory's clause forced peasants to give up holdings larger than a quarter-acre to qualify for workhouse relief. The rock-breaking work gangs on the High Burren: a brass ring used to judge whether rocks were small enough to warrant threepence an hour. Small girls selling their hair; mothers carrying dead babies to cliff-edge graves in Connemara. One million dead; one million emigrated.

5. **Emily Eden's journey through famine-stricken India (1837-38):** The Governor-General's sister travelling in a fleet of golden barges and 850 camels. "Perfect skeletons in many cases, their bones through their skin." She took a starving baby under her wing. When her pet flying squirrel died she became "really upset."

**Key concepts:**
- The utilitarian framework: "happiness before freedom" or "firm but impartial despotism"
- The East India Company college at Haileybury, where Reverend Thomas Malthus taught Charles Trevelyan
- Sati abolition (1829) as test case for "civilized values" -- but sati was actually opposed by Hindu reformer Ram Mohan Roy, and unauthorized by Hindu scriptures
- "Thugophobia" -- the hysteria about ritual strangling that expanded police power while Queen Victoria demanded galley proofs of The Confessions of a Thug
- The "bungalow" world: the segregation of British and Indian life; gardens laid out "as if they were in Hampshire"; kitchens isolated because of "unavoidable smells"
- The "Great Hedge" -- 1,500 miles of thorns to prevent Orissa salt competing with Cheshire salt imports
- John Mitchel's verdict from American exile: "The Almighty indeed sent the potato blight, but the English created the famine"
- Trevelyan's legacy: knighted for his famine relief work; went on to reform the Civil Service through competitive examination

### Chapter 6: The Empire of Good Intentions: The Dividend

**Main argument:** The Indian Mutiny/Rebellion of 1857 shattered the illusion of benevolent imperial partnership. The post-Mutiny Raj became more overtly authoritarian, and the economic exploitation of India intensified. Gladstone's attempt to create a genuinely moral liberalism -- responsive to the suffering of Ireland and India -- was the noblest, and ultimately most tragic, of Victorian political crusades.

**Key stories:**
1. **The Indian Mutiny (1857):** Mangal Pande's pot-shot at Barrackpore; the shackling of 85 sepoys at Meerut; Harriet Tytler's escape from Delhi eight months pregnant ("Mama, will these naughty sepoys kill my Papa?"); the siege of Lucknow (1700 defenders vs. 8,000-10,000 sepoys); Katherine Bartrum's journey through hostile territory carrying her baby. The jihadi quality of the revolt -- Ahmadullah Shah the "Maulvi with a drum" preaching holy war; the date fixed for uprising falling on the 10th day of Muharram, which in the Western calendar was 11 September.

2. **Lytton's Imperial Assemblage at Delhi (1877):** 80 feet up on a dais, 68,000 troops paraded, while 100,000 people that same week died of starvation in Mysore and Madras. The Anti-Charitable Contributions Act was passed to prevent aid reaching India. Temple's relief camps: 1.2 lb of rice per day, reduced to 1 lb with no lentils. "One official described a roadworks in the Bombay Deccan as resembling a battlefield."

3. **Gladstone's Midlothian campaign (1879):** 85,000 people at 15 venues in two weeks. "The most American campaign Britain had ever seen." Gladstone, speaking from train windows and hotel balconies, denounced Beaconsfield's extravagance and championed "the sanctity of life in the hill villages of Afghanistan."

4. **The Irish Land War and Parnell:** Michael Davitt's Land League; Captain Boycott's exemplary ostracism; Parnell's imprisonment in Kilmainham; the secret negotiations through Katherine O'Shea (Parnell's mistress); the Phoenix Park murders (1882) -- Lord Frederick Cavendish and Thomas Burke killed with long surgical knives. Gladstone refused Parnell's offer to resign and pressed ahead with the arrears bill a week after the murders.

5. **Gandhi's encounter with the empire:** His politicization in South Africa; his arrival in London; the early development of satyagraha (non-violent resistance). Briefly treated but positioned as the empire's ultimate nemesis.

**Key concepts:**
- The Begum of Awadh's 1858 proclamation listing British offences: defiled food, destroyed temples, compulsory Western doctors, burning of sacred books
- Dalhousie's "doctrine of lapse" -- annexation of states without male heirs, in violation of the ancient practice of adoption
- The Rani of Jhansi: dismissed by Dalhousie at 18, became a formidable guerrilla leader
- The post-Mutiny Raj: 35% of revenues spent on army/police, 4% on public works
- Dadabhai Naoroji's Poverty and Un-British Rule in India (1901): the first Asian MP (Finsbury Central, 1892)
- The Home Rule crisis: three bills, three failures; the marriage of Irish national and social grievances
- George Campbell's insight: "those Englishmen who know something of India are even now those who understand Ireland best"

### Chapter 7: The Last of Bladesover?

**Main argument:** The Edwardian era saw the last serious attempt to remake Britain as a progressive, socially just society within the framework of liberal capitalism -- the "New Liberalism" of Lloyd George and Churchill. But the forces pulling against it (suffragette militancy, Irish crisis, industrial unrest, German rearmament) proved overwhelming, and the First World War destroyed the old order.

**Key stories:**
1. **Churchill's early career:** His switch from Tory to Liberal in 1904; his partnership with Lloyd George on the People's Budget (1909-10); the Tonypandy crisis (1910) -- he sent police, then troops, to striking Welsh miners; "Bloody Friday" (18 November 1910) when police beat suffragettes for six hours in Parliament Square.

2. **The People's Budget (1909):** Lloyd George's revolutionary taxation of land values, higher income taxes, and new supertax on the wealthy to fund old-age pensions, labour exchanges, and unemployment insurance. The House of Lords rejected it -- precipitating the constitutional crisis that ended in the Parliament Act of 1911, permanently curbing the Lords' power.

3. **Emily Wilding Davison's martyrdom (1913):** After organizing arson attacks on Lloyd George's house and letter-box bombings, she threw herself under the king's horse at the Epsom Derby. "V for Votes" carved on Lady Constance Lytton's breast.

4. **Churchill's switch to oil (1914):** As First Lord of the Admiralty, his decision to fuel the Royal Navy with oil rather than coal. The acquisition of 51% of the Anglo-Persian Oil Company. Schama traces the consequences: British mandate in Palestine, Iraq, Jordan; Suez; Islamic fundamentalism. "And all the while the coal mines of Britain were relegated to terminal redundancy."

5. **Churchill's letter to Clementine (28 July 1914):** "Everything tends towards catastrophe & collapse. I am interested, geared-up & happy. Is it not horrible to be built like that?" Followed by: "The two black swans on St James's Park Lake have a darling cygnet."

**Key concepts:**
- H.G. Wells's Tono-Bungay and the concept of "Domdaniel" -- the vast, parasitic country house system feeding off the nation
- The "New Liberalism" vs. classical laissez-faire: the state as guarantor of minimum standards, not just referee of the market
- The Parliament Act 1911: the constitutional revolution that ended aristocratic legislative power
- The Agadir incident (1911) and the Anglo-German naval arms race
- The impossibility of Irish Home Rule: Carson's Ulster Volunteers vs. Redmond's Nationalists, both armed
- The Gallipoli disaster and Churchill's near-destruction

### Chapter 8: Endurance

**Main argument:** The 20th century is the story of Britain's double transformation -- from imperial power to welfare state, and from welfare state to something still being defined. Schama tells this story through two parallel lives: Winston Churchill (the champion of imperial parliamentary liberty) and George Orwell/Eric Blair (the champion of common decency and popular resistance). Both were patriots; both believed in British history; their visions were complementary, not contradictory.

**Key stories:**
1. **Eric Blair's transformation into George Orwell:** Five years as an imperial policeman in Burma, returning in 1927 to sell his clothes for a shilling and embrace destitution. Two years in doss-houses and spikes; hop-picking with itinerant labourers. Down and Out in Paris and London (1933) published under his new name. Wigan Pier (1936): the coal miners crawling through 4-foot passages for miles before work began; Orwell fainting at the coalface.

2. **The General Strike (May 1926):** 1.5 million workers out for nine days. Churchill ran the British Gazette as government propaganda; food deliveries escorted by tanks. The TUC capitulated; the miners fought on alone and lost. The 1927 Trade Disputes Act made sympathy strikes illegal.

3. **Munich (September 1938):** Chamberlain's three flights to Germany; the Czech sellout; "Peace for our time" waved from the Downing Street window. Churchill's devastating response in the Commons: "One pound was demanded at the pistol's point. When it was given, two pounds were demanded at the pistol's point." His prophecy: "This is only the first sip, the first foretaste of a bitter cup."

4. **Dunkirk and the Battle of Britain (1940):** 330,000 soldiers rescued in 800+ boats; the RAF's 1,032 fighters vs. the Luftwaffe's 1,011; the radar advantage; Spitfire Funds averaging 1 million pounds a month. Churchill on the roof of Number 10 watching the Blitz. Orwell in the Home Guard, training for street fighting.

5. **The 1945 election:** Churchill's catastrophic broadcast comparing Labour's welfare plans to "some form of Gestapo." Attlee's devastating reply. Labour won 393 seats to 213. Clementine said it might be a blessing in disguise. "If this is a blessing, it is certainly very well disguised."

6. **The welfare state and its fate:** The Beveridge Report (1942) selling 635,000 copies; the NHS created by Bevan in 1948; council housing; nationalization. But the American loan of $3.5 billion came with strings -- Cold War obligations, Middle East commitments. Bevin's imperialism; Suez (1956) as the empire's final humiliation. Thatcher's revolution dismantling the post-war settlement.

7. **Orwell's death and legacy (21 January 1950):** His review of Churchill's Their Finest Hour as his last published piece, praising Churchill's "certain largeness and geniality." Nineteen Eighty-Four: Winston Smith's toast "To the past" as a revolutionary act; the glass paperweight as "the trigger of memory, the talisman of imaginative freedom." The "Golden Country" -- "an old, rabbit-bitten pasture, with a foot-track wandering across it."

**Key concepts:**
- H.V. Morton's In Search of England (1927) vs. Orwell's Road to Wigan Pier (1936): the windscreen vs. the boot
- J.B. Priestley's three Englands: clapped-out industrial, antique-rural, electro-modern
- The evacuations of 1939 as the first great exercise in national reacquaintance across classes
- The Battle of Britain's real numbers: not the "few" but 1,032 RAF fighters vs. 1,011 Luftwaffe
- Churchill and Orwell as complementary visions: parliamentary pageant vs. popular insurrection; both rooted in British history
- The colouring of post-imperial Britain: West Indian immigration, Notting Hill carnival, the murder of Stephen Lawrence, interracial marriage rates
- Schama's personal conclusion: "our make room for that peculiarity or we should not bother showing up for dinner"

## Key Frameworks & Mental Models

1. **The "social affection" tradition:** From Sterne's Good Samaritan sermon through Bewick's compassion, Wollstonecraft's feminism, Gaskell's social realism, to Orwell's "common decency" -- a continuous British tradition of moral sympathy crossing class lines

2. **The Nature-Industry dialectic:** Every chapter turns on the tension between romantic attachment to landscape/community and the transformative power of industrial capitalism. Resolved (temporarily) in the Crystal Palace; ruptured again in the slums of Manchester and the famines of Ireland and India

3. **The "empire of good intentions" critique:** Liberal imperialism's promise of shared betterment was sincere but structurally doomed: the conditions in which "progress" was introduced were the conditions that guaranteed its failure. Military costs consumed resources meant for development; free-trade ideology prevented intervention in famines

4. **The "coming home" pattern:** Repeated through the book -- from America to the Lake District, from France to patriotic England, from Burma to Wigan, from empire to welfare state. Each homecoming involves a painful reckoning with what home has become

5. **The Churchill-Orwell duality:** Two visions of British history that between them encompass the full range of the nation's self-understanding: liberty from above (parliamentary tradition) and liberty from below (popular resistance). Both are needed; neither alone is sufficient

## Key Principles (15)

1. **The "cautionary indefinite article":** History is always "a" history, never "the" history -- interpretive, subjective, necessarily selective. (Preface)

2. **Landscape as moral teacher:** The Romantic discovery that Britain's own unpolished landscape, not Italian ruins, could recover national virtue. (Ch. 1)

3. **Reform through moral crusade:** The pattern of British social progress: evangelical fervour + mass petition + parliamentary pressure = incremental reform. Applied to slavery, suffrage, working conditions. (Chs. 1-4)

4. **The apostasy of comfort:** Radicals (Wordsworth, Coleridge, Cobbett) who retreat into conservatism once they achieve material security. Hazlitt never forgives them. (Ch. 2)

5. **The Crystal Palace illusion:** The belief that technology, free trade, and good intentions would harmonize all social contradictions -- nature and industry, rich and poor, domestic and imperial. (Ch. 3)

6. **The "angel in the house" paradox:** The ideology confining women to domesticity actually gave them a platform of moral authority from which to challenge the public world. (Ch. 4)

7. **Knowledge vs. action in empire:** The orientalists who truly understood India were replaced by Westernizers who didn't. "Too much knowledge" was thought to bewitch rational minds. (Ch. 5)

8. **Providence as policy:** Trevelyan's doctrine that famine was God's instrument of social improvement -- allowing non-intervention to be dressed up as cosmic wisdom. (Ch. 5)

9. **The Indian-Irish mirror:** The same economic doctrines, the same officials (Trevelyan), the same catastrophic results applied in both countries. Campbell: "those who know India best understand Ireland best." (Chs. 5-6)

10. **Imperial cost displacement:** The military costs of imperial defence were borne by the colonized populations, not the home country. 35% of Indian revenue went to army/police. (Ch. 6)

11. **The liberal paradox:** Gladstone's discovery that genuine liberalism might require state intervention to protect the weak, not just free markets. (Ch. 6)

12. **Oil as imperial destiny:** Churchill's 1914 switch from coal to oil as naval fuel created the chain of dependencies that shaped the entire subsequent history of the Middle East. (Ch. 7)

13. **Endurance as national virtue:** The capacity to absorb punishment (the Blitz, rationing, austerity) and emerge with social solidarity rather than revolutionary fury. (Ch. 8)

14. **History as freedom:** Orwell's argument that memory is the enemy of tyranny; that to have a free future presupposes keeping faith with the past. Winston Smith's toast "To the past." (Ch. 8)

15. **Mongrel glory:** Post-imperial Britain's identity lies not in racial purity but in "the roving, unstable, complicated, migratory character" of its history. (Ch. 8)

## Stories Worth Preserving

1. **Thomas Day's orphan wife experiment (1769):** The Rousseau disciple who selected two orphan girls, Sabrina and Lucretia, to raise as candidates for a perfect wife. Hot wax on arms, guns fired near heads. Sabrina was eventually packed off to boarding school; Day died when an "unbroken colt" he was taming with gentleness threw him on his head. (Ch. 1, 4-8 sentences)

2. **Rousseau's paranoid sojourn in England (1766):** Arrived seasick, cold, tearful. Hume locked his dog Sultan inside to get him out to the theatre. Locals remembered him in his "Armenian" costume gathering herbs. Believed servants were putting cinders in his food. Fled within a year. (Ch. 1, 4-6 sentences)

3. **Thomas Bewick and the hare (c.1760s):** The boy who caught a coursed hare and heard it scream "like a child," then watched the farmer break its leg for the dogs' further sport. "From that day forward, I have ever wished that this poor, persecuted, innocent creature might escape with its life." The bullfinch knocked from its perch that "looked me piteously in the face." (Ch. 1, 4-6 sentences)

4. **Mary Wollstonecraft's suicide attempts and rescue (1795):** Walked up and down Putney Bridge for half an hour to saturate her dress, climbed the railing, jumped. Rescued by the Royal Humane Society's boatmen, who were paid to fish suicides from the Thames. Taken to the Duke's Head tavern in Fulham. Then proposed to Imlay that they live in a menage a trois. (Ch. 2, 6-8 sentences)

5. **The treason trials and Erskine's defence (1794):** Hardy, Thelwall, Horne Tooke charged with "compassing the death of the king." The Attorney-General laboured for nine hours; the fat ex-Lord Chancellor shouted "Then there is no treason, by God!" Erskine responded for seven hours, then collapsed: "I am sinking under fatigue." The jury applauded. Crowds pulled the acquitted men's carriages through the streets. (Ch. 2, 6-8 sentences)

6. **The Peterloo Massacre (1819):** Bamford's description: "chopped limbs, and wound-gaping skulls were seen; and groans and cries were mingled with the din of that horrid confusion." A small girl trampled by yeomanry mounts. 11 killed, 421 wounded, at least 100 women and children among the hurt. Lord Sidmouth congratulated the magistrates. (Ch. 2, 5-7 sentences)

7. **Victoria's first night of marriage (1840):** "When day dawned, for we did not sleep much and I beheld that beautiful angelic face by my side, it was more than I can express!" "My dearest Albert put on my stockings for me. I went in and saw him shave; a great delight for me." (Ch. 4, 4-6 sentences)

8. **The famine graves of Connemara:** Fathers carrying dead babies to the edge of the Atlantic, digging little graves marked by rough stones cut from the cliffs. "Circles of 30 and 40 of the wind-scoured, lichen-flecked stones, their jagged grey edges pointing this way and that, stand by the roaring surf, the saddest little mausoleum in all Irish history." (Ch. 5, 4-6 sentences)

9. **Harriet Tytler's escape from Delhi (1857):** Eight months pregnant, her maid announcing "Madame, this is a revolution." Her four-year-old asking "Mama, will these naughty sepoys kill my Papa, and will they kill me too?" She pricked holes in her own feet to let her two-year-old "play nursey." Kept two bottles of laudanum to kill her children and herself. Baby Stanley Delhi-Force born in a bullock cart. (Ch. 6, 6-8 sentences)

10. **Churchill's Munich speech (5 October 1938):** "One pound was demanded at the pistol's point. When it was given, two pounds were demanded." Quoting the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle on Ethelred the Unready buying off the Danes. "Thou art weighed in the balance and found wanting." "This is only the first sip, the first foretaste of a bitter cup." (Ch. 8, 6-8 sentences)

11. **Orwell's descent into the underclass (1927):** Sold his clothes for a shilling, received "a patina of antique filth." "Thanks, mate -- No one had called me mate before in my life." Spent the night in a doss-house reeking of "paregoric and foul linen." The bathroom where "all the indecent secrets of our underwear were exposed." (Ch. 8, 6-8 sentences)

12. **The 1945 election result:** Churchill's "Gestapo" broadcast. Attlee's quiet devastation: "He wanted the electors to understand how great was the difference between Winston Churchill, the great leader in war of a united nation, and Mr Churchill, the party leader." Clementine: "Perhaps it's a blessing in disguise." Winston: "If this is a blessing, it is certainly very well disguised." (Ch. 8, 4-6 sentences)

## Critique & Cross-Reference

**Evidence strength:**
- Schama is strongest on the 18th and 19th centuries, where his narrative gifts illuminate complex social and political transformations through vivid biographical portraits
- The Irish Famine and Indian Mutiny chapters are among the most powerful in the book, drawing on extensive primary sources (Emily Eden's journals, Katherine Bartrum's memoir, Harriet Tytler's account)
- The 20th century is deliberately treated more loosely; the Churchill-Orwell parallel, while brilliantly executed, means other major themes (the two world wars' actual military history, the inter-war economy, the Cold War, decolonization in Africa) receive thin treatment
- Schama's personal perspective as a transatlantic Briton (Jewish, raised in London, working at Columbia) colours the conclusion -- the argument for Britain's "mongrel glory" is sincere but under-argued

**Limitations:**
- Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland are given supporting rather than starring roles; the "British" in the title sometimes defaults to "English"
- The industrial revolution itself receives surprisingly little direct attention; Schama approaches it through its critics (Carlyle, Ruskin, Gaskell) rather than its engineers
- Women's history is concentrated in Chapter 4 rather than woven throughout
- The post-1945 period is compressed into roughly 30 pages, with Thatcher, the Falklands, and New Labour receiving only glancing treatment
- Economic analysis is subordinated to cultural and political narrative

**Cross-references:**
- [[The English and Their History - Robert Tombs]] -- Tombs provides the comprehensive narrative treatment that Schama explicitly avoids; they disagree on the degree to which Britain's story is one of continuity (Tombs) vs. rupture (Schama)
- [[A History of Britain Vol 1 - Simon Schama]] -- Foundations of the British state; the Norman Conquest; the Civil War
- [[A History of Britain Vol 2 - Simon Schama]] -- The 17th-18th century: Civil War to American Revolution
- [[Empire - Niall Ferguson]] -- Ferguson makes a stronger case for the economic benefits of empire; Schama is more sympathetic to the "wrecked" liberal vision but clearer-eyed about its catastrophic costs
- [[Black and British - David Olusoga]] -- Schama's brief treatment of slavery and race in Britain would be complemented by Olusoga's deeper focus
- [[The Victorians - A.N. Wilson]] -- Wilson provides the cultural depth on the Victorian period that Schama's selective approach omits
- [[A History of Modern Britain - Andrew Marr]] -- Marr picks up where Schama becomes most sketchy, covering 1945-2000 in much greater detail

## Summarisation Notes

**Recommended structure:** Follow Schama's chronological arc but group thematically:
1. The Romantic awakening and reform tradition (Chs. 1-2)
2. Victorian confidence and its critics (Chs. 3-4)
3. The empire of good intentions and its catastrophes (Chs. 5-6)
4. The Edwardian crisis and World War I (Ch. 7)
5. The 20th century: endurance and reinvention (Ch. 8)

**Key content to preserve:**
- The biographical portraits are the book's greatest strength -- Bewick, Wollstonecraft, Wordsworth, Victoria, Curzon, Gladstone, Churchill, Orwell must all be given full treatment
- The Irish Famine section is perhaps the book's most powerful sustained argument and deserves extensive coverage
- The Indian chapters (5-6) contain Schama's most original thesis about the connection between imperial ideology and famine
- The Churchill-Orwell parallel in Ch. 8 is the book's emotional and intellectual climax
- Schama's prose is exceptionally quotable; preserve key formulations

**Category:** British History
**Estimated length:** 1600+ lines (substantial narrative history with many stories and characters)
**Difficulty:** Moderate -- accessible prose but dense with names, dates, and interconnections
