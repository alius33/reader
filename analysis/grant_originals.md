# Originals — Adam Grant

## Classification & Context
Argument/research hybrid. Author is organisational psychologist at Wharton (youngest tenured professor). Challenges myths about originality — argues creative success comes from volume not genius, strategic risk management not fearlessness, and speaking up at the right time in the right way. Draws heavily on social science research, historical examples, and business case studies.

## Central Thesis
Originals are people who take the initiative to make their visions a reality. But they are far less special than we imagine. They doubt themselves constantly, procrastinate strategically, feel fear like everyone else, and generate far more bad ideas than good ones. What distinguishes them is not innate brilliance but learned behaviours: generating lots of ideas and selecting ruthlessly, managing risk by hedging bets, building coalitions through strategic framing, and knowing when to act versus when to wait.

## Structure
Eight chapters plus introduction:
1. Creative Destruction (challenging the default; entrepreneurs who keep day jobs)
2. Blind Inventors and One-Eyed Investors (idea generation and selection; why creators are bad judges of their own work)
3. Out on a Limb (speaking up; when and how to voice original ideas)
4. Fools Rush In (timing; why procrastination can help originality, first-mover disadvantage)
5. Goldilocks and the Trojan Horse (coalition building; how to get support for radical ideas)
6. Rebel with a Cause (origins of originality; birth order, parenting, and values-based rule-breaking)
7. Rethinking Groupthink (managing group dynamics; bridgewater vs polaroid; dissent culture)
8. Rocking the Boat and Keeping It Steady (emotions of change; managing anxiety and turning it into excitement)

## Key Frameworks
- The Default vs. Originality (vuja dé — looking at familiar things with fresh eyes)
- Risk Portfolio Model (originals hedge their bets — keep day jobs, diversify commitments)
- Quantity breeds quality (Simonton's research — creative output follows a distribution; more attempts = more hits)
- Idea Selection problem (creators are overconfident about pet ideas; peer evaluation beats self-evaluation)
- Status and Idiosyncrasy Credits (high status gives licence to deviate)
- Strategic Timing (procrastination as incubation; first-mover disadvantage)
- Coalition Building via Tempered Radicalism (moderate framing to recruit unlikely allies)
- The Sarick Effect (leading with weaknesses disarms critics)
- Birth Order and originality (laterborns more likely to champion radical ideas)
- Groupthink prevention (strong cultures need dissent mechanisms)
- Emotional Reappraisal (reframe anxiety as excitement rather than trying to calm down)

## Best Stories
1. Warby Parker founders — kept day jobs/grad school while launching; Grant almost didn't invest because they seemed "not committed enough" — they succeeded precisely because of risk management
2. Carmen Medina at CIA — first attempt to push for digital intelligence sharing failed; she retreated, built status, then succeeded years later
3. Martin Luther King Jr. — the "I Have a Dream" refrain was improvised, not in the written speech; example of procrastination enabling spontaneous brilliance
4. Ray Dalio at Bridgewater — culture of radical transparency and "obligation to dissent"
5. Polaroid and Edwin Land — groupthink failure; Land was so dominant that nobody challenged the Polavision instant movie camera
6. Seinfeld — rated 100th out of 100 new shows by NBC; survived only because one champion believed in it
7. Galileo — chose to write in Italian not Latin to build a broader coalition; framed heliocentrism within religious values
8. Donna Dubinsky at Apple — risked her career to challenge Steve Jobs's distribution plan; spoke up but chose her timing carefully
9. The Babble Hypothesis — people who talk more in groups are perceived as leaders regardless of idea quality
10. The Segway — overhyped as world-changing; example of inventor overconfidence

## Key Research
- Dean Simonton: creative genius is a function of volume, not quality of individual ideas. Shakespeare, Beethoven, Edison all produced enormous quantities of mediocre and bad work alongside masterpieces
- Justin Berg: circus acts study — creators are worse than managers at predicting which of their ideas will succeed
- Sahlman study: entrepreneurs who keep their day jobs have 33% lower failure rates
- Birth order research: laterborns are 2x more likely to support radical scientific theories
- Nemeth research: minority dissent improves group decision-making even when the dissenter is wrong
