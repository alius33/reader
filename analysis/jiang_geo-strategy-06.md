# America's Imperial Hubris

## Core Question

Trump wants war with Iran — but will the US military agree? Why would professional soldiers sign up for a war they should know is unwinnable?

Prof. Jiang's answer: the military will go along because of what happened in 2003. The Iraq War's spectacular success created a doctrine — shock and awe — that became a theory of empire. This theory made the military overcommitted, strategically unfocused, and hubristic. The same institution that should know better has been psychologically captured by one anomalous victory.

## Lecture Type

Geopolitical analysis — tracing a single military doctrine (shock and awe) from its origins in Vietnam through its apparent vindication in Iraq to its current role as the engine of imperial hubris.

## Central Thesis

The US military will agree to war with Iran because it has been psychologically captured by shock and awe — a doctrine born from one anomalous victory in Iraq that confirmed every institutional bias the military developed after Vietnam. Shock and awe is not really a theory of war; it is a theory of empire that allows America to project power without democratic accountability. But it has produced three fatal weaknesses: overcommitment (trying to be everywhere at once), lack of strategic focus (no plan beyond destruction), and hubris (believing no one can challenge American supremacy). The current military leadership has never experienced real war — they inherited an empire and want to enjoy it, not manage it responsibly.

The chain of reasoning:
1. Vietnam traumatised the military — they concluded democracy was the enemy of empire
2. The 1991 Gulf War produced a responsible theory of empire (limited goals, coalition, UN umbrella)
3. The 2003 Iraq War's success destroyed the responsible approach — a new generation took over who saw war as a video game
4. Shock and awe became the operating system of American empire — invisible, unaccountable, addictive
5. This produced structural weakness: shrinking navy, shrinking army, no manufacturing capacity, no strategic plan
6. When Trump pushes for Iran, the military — overcommitted, unfocused, and arrogant — will go along
7. But Iran is not Iraq (mountains, not desert; air defences; no surprise) — shock and awe will fail there (next lecture)

## Argument Structure

### Argument 1: Shock and Awe as Military Doctrine

**The theory:** War has been revolutionised by three capabilities — air supremacy, technological omniscience (satellite surveillance + electronic eavesdropping), and special forces (embedded operators directing air power). These three capabilities allow a small force to decapitate enemy command-and-control instantly. The body (army) collapses once the head is severed.

**Evidence for:**
- Iraq 2003: 130,000 US troops destroyed 370,000-strong Iraqi army in three weeks
- US casualties: approximately 200 (mostly friendly fire)
- Iraqi casualties: tens of thousands
- Thunder runs through Baghdad — pure intimidation, demonstrating total dominance
- Cluster bombs: single bomb carries ~440 GPS-guided submunitions, each finds and destroys a tank — one bomb eliminates an entire tank division
- Special forces operating independently, calling in pinpoint airstrikes on military installations

**Evidence against (Prof. Jiang's three unique conditions):**
1. Saddam had no air defence — he learned from 1991 that he could never beat America, and concluded America wouldn't overthrow him (it would destabilise the region), so he focused resources on suppressing internal dissent instead. Even when invasion was imminent, he had nothing to counter US air supremacy.
2. Iraq is a desert — ideal terrain for air power (full visibility), satellite surveillance (no cover), and special forces mobility (can drive anywhere). [INFERENCE: This contrasts with Iran's mountainous terrain, which Prof. Jiang promises to address in Lecture 7]
3. Surprise — no one had ever fought a war this way. Saddam was so confused by special forces activity in western Iraq (destroying missile bases threatening Israel) that he redeployed forces west, leaving Baghdad exposed from the east.

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang rejects shock and awe as a genuine revolution in military affairs. It worked in Iraq due to three unrepeatable conditions. But America interpreted it as proof of the doctrine's universal validity, and restructured the entire military around it.

### Argument 2: Shock and Awe as Theory of Empire

**The theory:** Shock and awe is not merely a way to fight wars — it is a way to run an empire. It allows America to project power globally through invisible special forces operations, without democratic accountability, congressional oversight, or public sacrifice.

**Evidence for:**
- Libya 2011: Gaddafi overthrown by special forces directing air power in the background — the public barely knew
- Special forces operate outside normal military supervision — black ops with budgets hidden from public accounting
- Official budget $13.7B; actual budget estimated 10-100x more because of black ops classification
- Growth from 30,000 to 73,000 special forces personnel post-2001

**The Vietnam origin:**
- Vietnam proved that democracy is the enemy of empire — public protest, media coverage (My Lai Massacre), and the draft made war politically unsustainable
- Pentagon Papers (1971, leaked by Daniel Ellsberg) revealed three truths: (1) the government deceived the public about the war's expansion, (2) American leaders knew the war was unwinnable, (3) the only reason to continue was credibility ("we don't want to be laughed at")
- Vietnamese peasants were unbeatable: tunnel warfare made them invisible, they acquired weapons from three sources (abandoned US ammunition, unexploded US bombs converted to landmines, corrupt South Vietnamese army selling US weapons to rebels)
- More bombs dropped on Vietnam than in all of WWII combined; ~20% didn't explode and became enemy weapons
- 58,000 US dead, 300,000+ wounded, 3 million+ Vietnamese dead (2 million civilians)
- The military felt betrayed by democracy — politicians prioritised elections over victory, media undermined morale, public refused necessary sacrifices
- Conclusion within the military: empire and democracy are incompatible — to maintain the empire, you must escape democracy's shackles
- Shock and awe is the solution: quick (no time for protest), cheap (no draft needed), invisible (special forces, not conscripts), no oversight needed

**Verdict:** Prof. Jiang endorses this reading — shock and awe was designed specifically to divorce American empire from American democracy. It succeeded: America can now wage war without public knowledge or consent.

### Argument 3: Two Theories of Empire

**First theory (1991 Gulf War model):**
- Limited strategic objectives (remove Saddam from Kuwait — nothing more)
- Coalition warfare (dozens of countries participated; US deferred to partners like Saudi Arabia who opposed regime change)
- UN authority (US made the case to the UN; military action was a UN action, not a US action)
- Core principle: with great power comes great responsibility — America must show humility, discipline, restraint
- Rules-based international order as the priority — military force as last resort only
- Proponents: the generation that fought WWII and the Cold War, who understood war's horror

**Second theory (2003 shock and awe model):**
- Unlimited strategic objectives (bring democracy to Iraq and the Middle East)
- Unilateral military action (America acts alone, doesn't need partners)
- America makes the rules — no need for UN authority
- Core principle: we have the greatest military in the world, so we should use it
- Proponents: a new generation who never experienced real war — Iraq 2003 looked like a video game

**Why the transition happened — the billionaire father analogy:**
- Father (WWII/Cold War generation) built the empire through intelligence, hard work, and some corruption
- Father has 100 experienced advisors who manage the empire responsibly, generating 10% returns
- Father's dying wish: son (post-2003 generation) should listen to the advisors
- Son immediately fires all advisors, hires his friends, and makes reckless investments
- The point: inherited power always destroys discipline. The people who built the empire understood restraint; the people who inherited it only want to enjoy it

**Evidence for the transition being catastrophic:**
- Iraq 2003 strategic objectives were unachievable ("bring democracy and peace to Iraq and the Middle East")
- Immediate post-invasion actions contradicted stated goals: (1) too few troops to maintain order (looting), (2) destroyed all civilian infrastructure (water, electricity), (3) de-Ba'athification disbanded the entire governing apparatus and military
- If the goal was democracy, why destroy every institution needed for governance?
- Prof. Jiang's conclusion: the real intention was never democracy — it was destruction, to maintain supremacy and teach a lesson ("don't mess with us")

### Argument 4: Three Fatal Weaknesses of Shock-and-Awe Empire

**Overcommitment:**
- America believes shock and awe allows it to be everywhere at once, fighting all wars simultaneously
- Special forces deployed globally to maintain the empire
- 73,000 special forces "running around the world" — psychologically atypical individuals addicted to risk and violence

**Lack of strategic focus:**
- No actual plan for the empire beyond maintaining it
- Destroy countries but don't rebuild them — Iraq, Libya, Syria all left as failed states
- No answer to: "what comes after destruction?"

**Hubris:**
- Refuse to believe anyone can challenge American supremacy
- Not spending necessary resources to maintain the empire:
  - US Navy: 7,600 ships in 1945 → 475 today (must patrol all global shipping lanes)
  - US Army: 2 million soldiers in 1991 → 1.3 million today
  - Manufacturing: for every 1 ship America builds, China builds 300
  - No wartime manufacturing capacity — cannot sustain a prolonged war
- The current generation has no experience of real war — they think war is a video game

## Key Concepts & Frameworks

### Shock and Awe (expanded from Lecture 4)
- **Definition:** Military doctrine claiming three capabilities (air supremacy, technological omniscience, special forces) can replace traditional mass-force warfare
- **How it works:** Decapitate enemy command and control; the body collapses without the head
- **Promise:** Quick, cheap, decisive war
- **Reality (per Prof. Jiang):** Only worked in Iraq due to three unrepeatable conditions; has become a theory of empire rather than a theory of war
- **Introduced:** Referenced in Lecture 1 (Millennium Challenge), Lecture 4 (Yemen failure); fully developed here in Lecture 6

### Three Principles of Conventional Warfare
- **Mass forces:** Overwhelm the enemy; 3:1 advantage rule for invasion (need to advance, hold territory, resupply, and maintain reserves)
- **Avoid encirclement:** Prevent enemy from surrounding your forces
- **Protect supply lines:** Most of war is logistics — getting fuel, weapons, supplies from point A to point B
- Prof. Jiang positions these as "1,000 years of experience" versus shock and awe's untested theory

### Technological Omniscience
- **Definition:** The ability to see and hear everything — satellite surveillance of all ground activity + electronic eavesdropping on all communications
- **How it works:** Gives the military "the power of God" — complete information dominance
- **Limitation (implied):** Only works in open terrain (desert), not in mountains or tunnels

### Thunder Runs
- **Definition:** Armoured vehicles driving through enemy capital city unchallenged — purely as intimidation and demonstration of dominance
- **Context:** Three thunder runs through Baghdad during the 2003 invasion
- **Significance:** Prof. Jiang uses this as the clearest symbol of hubris — "you're in a fight and you do a backflip because you're bored"

### De-Ba'athification (transcript: "de bifurcation")
- **Definition:** Policy of removing all former Ba'ath Party members from government positions and disbanding the Iraqi military entirely
- **Significance:** Prof. Jiang uses this as evidence that the true goal was destruction, not democracy — why destroy every institution needed for governance if you intend to build a democratic state?

### The Pentagon Papers (1971)
- **Definition:** Secret internal history of the Vietnam War, compiled by military analysts, leaked by Daniel Ellsberg to the Washington Post and New York Times
- **Three revelations:** (1) Government deceived the public — multiple presidents expanded the war without public or congressional approval; (2) Leaders knew the war was unwinnable — Vietnamese tunnel warfare, self-supplying weapons cycle, civilian radicalisation; (3) The only reason to continue was credibility — avoiding being laughed at by the Soviets and Chinese
- **Significance:** Triggered the public anger that forced withdrawal, and convinced the military that democracy is the enemy of empire

### Empire-Democracy Incompatibility Thesis
- **Definition:** Prof. Jiang's framing of the post-Vietnam military mindset — if you want to maintain the empire, you must divorce it from democratic oversight
- **How shock and awe solves this:** Quick (no time for protests), cheap (no draft), invisible (special forces, not conscripts), no Congressional approval needed
- **Implication:** America now wages war without public knowledge or consent

### The Two Theories of Empire
- **First theory (1991):** Limited goals, coalition, UN authority, humility, discipline, restraint — rules-based international order
- **Second theory (2003):** Unlimited goals, unilateral action, American rules, hubris — "what's the point of an empire if you can't blow things up?"
- **Why the transition:** Generational shift — builders (WWII/Cold War veterans who knew war's horror) replaced by inheritors (post-2003 generation who saw war as a video game)

### The Billionaire Father Analogy
- **Setup:** Father built a $10B empire through smarts, hard work, and corruption. Has 100 experienced advisors generating 10% annual returns. Offers dying son $100M/year to just listen to the advisors.
- **Punchline:** Son immediately fires advisors, hires friends, invests in reckless speculation, loses everything
- **Application:** The WWII generation built and maintained the empire responsibly; the post-2003 generation inherited it and wants to have fun, not manage it

### Three Fatal Weaknesses
1. **Overcommitment:** Believe you can be everywhere at once, fighting all wars
2. **Lack of strategic focus:** No plan beyond maintaining the empire; destroy countries without rebuilding
3. **Hubris:** Refuse to believe anyone can challenge you; not investing in the resources needed to sustain the empire

## Historical Narratives & Examples

### Story 1: The Pentagon vs. Rumsfeld/Wolfowitz — Planning the Iraq Invasion
- **Who:** Pentagon generals vs. Secretary of Defence Donald Rumsfeld and Deputy Secretary Paul Wolfowitz, under President George W. Bush and VP Dick Cheney
- **When:** Pre-2003 invasion planning
- **What happened:**
  - Pentagon applied standard doctrine: Iraq has 370,000 soldiers; 3:1 rule means ~1 million US troops needed
  - Rumsfeld and Wolfowitz (who had never fought a war) rejected this — demanded fewer troops
  - Pentagon: "Okay, 900,000 minimum." Wolfowitz: "Lower." Back and forth until Rumsfeld/Wolfowitz insisted on 130,000
  - Pentagon called it insane — outnumbered 3:1, can't mass forces, can't avoid encirclement, can't protect supply lines
  - Rumsfeld/Wolfowitz presented their theory: shock and awe — air supremacy + technological omniscience + special forces = quick, cheap, decisive
  - Pentagon: "This is just a theory. Our principles come from thousands of years of experience."
  - Rumsfeld/Wolfowitz overruled the generals
- **Outcome:** The plan worked 100% as predicted — vindicating the civilians over the professionals
- **Supports argument:** This anomalous success convinced the military that shock and awe was universally valid

### Story 2: The Three-Week War — Iraq 2003
- **When:** 2003
- **What happened:**
  - 130,000 US troops destroyed 370,000-strong Iraqi army in exactly three weeks
  - US casualties: approximately 200 (most from friendly fire)
  - Iraqi casualties: tens of thousands
  - Cluster bombs: one bomb carries ~440 GPS-guided submunitions, each targeting individual tanks — single bomb destroys entire tank division
  - Special forces drove around independently locating military installations, calling in airstrikes
  - Three "thunder runs" through Baghdad — armoured vehicles circling the capital unchallenged, purely as intimidation
- **Supports argument:** The spectacular nature of the victory made it impossible for the military to question the doctrine

### Story 3: Saddam's Miscalculation from 1991
- **Who:** Saddam Hussein
- **When:** 1991-2003
- **What happened:**
  - In the 1991 Gulf War (Operation Desert Storm), Saddam's army was destroyed by American air power
  - America could have invaded and overthrown him but chose not to
  - Saddam drew two conclusions: (1) he can never beat America militarily, (2) America would never overthrow him because it would destabilise the country, the region, empower terrorists, and strengthen Iran
  - Based on these conclusions, he focused all resources on suppressing internal dissent and built no air defences
  - Even when the 2003 invasion was clearly imminent, he had nothing to counter US air supremacy
- **Supports argument:** Iraq's lack of air defence was a unique condition, not a universal feature of future wars

### Story 4: Saddam's Western Iraq Misdirection
- **When:** 2003 invasion
- **What happened:**
  - US special forces operated in western Iraq, destroying missile bases (to prevent Scud attacks on Israel)
  - So much damage was inflicted in the west that Saddam became convinced the main US force was there
  - He redeployed his forces westward, leaving Baghdad completely exposed from the east
  - The main US force attacked from the east into an undefended capital
- **Supports argument:** The element of surprise was a one-time advantage — "you can only do this once"

### Story 5: The Growth of Special Forces
- **When:** 2001-present
- **Key data:**
  - Pre-2001: ~30,000 special forces; today: 73,000 (out of 1.3 million total army)
  - Official budget: ~$2B in 2001 → $13.7B today (~7x increase)
  - Actual budget estimated at 10-100x the official figure (black ops classification)
  - Special forces operate outside normal military supervision
- **Supports argument:** The military has structurally committed to shock and awe — this is not just a theory but a massive institutional investment

### Story 6: SAS Selection — The Psychology of Special Forces
- **Who:** British Special Air Service (SAS) — one of the two top special forces in the world (the other being Delta Force)
- **What the selection process involves:**
  - First test: run six marathons in five days, carrying a backpack of bricks, on a mountain
  - Some candidates go insane during this first test alone — hospitalised
  - Final test: endure torture (to prove resistance to interrogation when captured)
  - French special services test: wear a bulletproof vest while a comrade shoots at you holding a clay disc
- **The interview:** A reporter asked a special forces member why he chose this career. Answer: "It's either special forces or I rob banks — one of the two."
- **Supports argument:** Special forces personnel are psychologically atypical — "addicted to risk and violence." Having 73,000 such individuals operating globally with minimal oversight is the operational reality of the American empire.

### Story 7: Libya 2011 — The Shadow War
- **When:** 2011
- **What happened:**
  - Gaddafi was overthrown in the Libyan war
  - Special forces were operating in the background, directing air power against Gaddafi
  - The public barely knew special forces were involved
- **Supports argument:** Shock and awe as theory of empire — America maintains its empire invisibly, without democratic accountability

### Story 8: The Vietnam War
- **When:** 1965-1973
- **Scale:**
  - 3 million US soldiers deployed over the war; 500,000 in-country at peak (1969)
  - 58,000 US soldiers killed; 300,000+ wounded
  - 3 million+ Vietnamese killed; 2 million were civilians
  - More bombs dropped on Vietnam than in all of WWII combined
- **Why America couldn't win:**
  - Vietnamese peasants dug tunnels — could "swim underground," appearing and disappearing at will
  - Self-supplying weapons cycle: (1) US soldiers dropped ammunition fleeing in jungle, (2) ~20% of bombs didn't explode and were converted to landmines, (3) corrupt South Vietnamese army sold US weapons to rebels
  - Civilians radicalised as America killed their families — each year produced more enemies
- **Why America stayed anyway:** Credibility — "we don't want to be laughed at" by the Soviet Union and China
- **Domestic impact:**
  - The draft forced young Americans into a war they didn't understand
  - Journalists reported atrocities — My Lai Massacre (American soldiers killed 300-400 civilians in a village) was just one publicised example; it "was happening actually quite a lot"
  - Widespread anger and protests forced withdrawal in 1973
- **Military's conclusion:** Democracy betrayed the empire — politicians, media, and public refused to make necessary sacrifices
- **Supports argument:** Vietnam is the origin of shock and awe — the military designed a doctrine specifically to escape democratic oversight

### Story 9: The Pentagon Papers (1971)
- **Who:** Group of military analysts who secretly compiled a classified history of the Vietnam War; Daniel Ellsberg leaked it to the Washington Post and New York Times
- **Three revelations:**
  1. The government deceived the public — Eisenhower, Kennedy, and Lyndon Johnson expanded the war without public or congressional approval
  2. American leaders knew the war was unwinnable
  3. The only reason to continue was credibility
- **Public reaction:** Widespread anger that ultimately forced withdrawal
- **Military reaction:** Felt betrayed by democracy — the leak itself was seen as proof that democratic institutions undermine imperial power
- **Supports argument:** The Pentagon Papers crystallised the military's belief that empire and democracy are incompatible

### Story 10: The 1991 Gulf War as Responsible Empire
- **When:** 1991
- **What happened:**
  - Saddam Hussein invaded Kuwait
  - America (now sole superpower after Soviet collapse) led a response
  - Three constraints: (1) limited objective — remove Saddam from Kuwait only, don't invade Iraq, (2) coalition of dozens of countries, with America deferring to partners (Saudi Arabia insisted: no regime change), (3) UN authority — presented as a UN action, not a US action
  - Principle: humility, discipline, restraint — "with great power comes great responsibility"
- **Why it was abandoned:** Prof. Jiang's billionaire father analogy — the generation that built the empire understood restraint; the generation that inherited it only wanted to enjoy it
- **Supports argument:** America had a responsible theory of empire and chose to abandon it

### Story 11: De-Ba'athification as Evidence of True Intent
- **When:** Immediately after 2003 invasion
- **What happened:**
  - Officially, the goal was to bring democracy to Iraq
  - First actions: (1) too few troops to prevent looting — country broke down, (2) air campaign destroyed water supply and electricity grid, (3) de-Ba'athification fired all former government workers, (4) disbanded the entire Iraqi military
  - Prof. Jiang: if the goal was democracy, why destroy every institution needed for governance?
- **Supports argument:** The real intention was destruction — to maintain supremacy and teach a lesson, not to build democracy

## Student Q&A Insights

### Q1: Celine — Why did special forces grow? Supply or demand?
- **Question:** Are there more special forces because more people want to join, or because America needs them?
- **Prof. Jiang's answer:** Both. After the Soviet Union fell, America shifted doctrine to fighting "rogue regimes" (Iraq, Iran, North Korea — the "axis of evil") rather than peer competitors. This required "shadow wars" — quick, invisible regime changes. Resources shifted to special forces, which in turn allowed more recruits.
- **Added value:** Clarifies the strategic logic behind special forces expansion — it's not just institutional drift but a deliberate doctrinal choice linked to the post-Cold War unipolar moment.

### Q2: Student (unnamed) — Shock and awe only works in deserts?
- **Question:** If shock and awe only worked in Iraq, what happens in other environments?
- **Prof. Jiang's answer:** America has mainly fought in Middle Eastern deserts for 20 years — Iraq, Libya, Syria. Shock and awe has had "tremendous success" there because deserts are ideal for air power, satellites, and special forces mobility. But Iran is mountains, not desert. Next lecture will show why shock and awe will fail there.
- **Added value:** Sets up Lecture 7 explicitly — the Iran-specific analysis of terrain as the critical variable.

### Q3: Jack — Didn't America lose Iraq because of the insurgency?
- **Question:** Most people say America lost the Iraq War because of the insurgency. Doesn't that prove shock and awe failed?
- **Prof. Jiang's reframing:** Shock and awe was never designed to stabilise countries — it was designed to topple and destroy them. The real question is: what is the empire's intention? Is it to replace regimes or to destroy countries? Answer: shock and awe is designed to destroy countries. Iraq is not a functional state. Libya is not a functional state. Syria is in civil war. Mission accomplished.
- **Added value:** This is a critical analytical move — redefining "success" changes the entire evaluation. If the goal is destruction (to prevent any regional power from challenging American supremacy), then shock and awe hasn't failed at all. It has succeeded perfectly. This reframing also explains de-Ba'athification: it makes no sense as democracy-building but perfect sense as state destruction.

### Q4: Celine — What's the difference between first-theory and second-theory people?
- **Question:** Who believed in the responsible 1991 theory vs. the reckless 2003 theory?
- **Prof. Jiang's answer:** Generational divide. First theory: the generation that fought WWII and the Cold War — they knew what war looks like ("really bloody and terrible"), believed military force must be last resort. Second theory: individuals who "don't know what war is" — Iraq 2003 "looked like a video game," only ~200 died, you couldn't even see people dying. They've "inherited a lot of money and want to enjoy it."
- **Added value:** This is the emotional core of the hubris argument — it's not about strategy but about psychology. People who have never suffered don't know how to be cautious. The video-game quality of 2003 made war seem costless, which made recklessness seem rational.

- **Overcommitment and decline:** Navy from 7,600 ships to 475; army from 2 million to 1.3 million; manufacturing collapse. The empire is shrinking while its ambitions grow.
