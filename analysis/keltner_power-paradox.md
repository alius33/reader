# Analysis: The Power Paradox — Dacher Keltner (2016)

## Book Structure

- Introduction (20 Power Principles listed)
- Chapter 1: Power Is About Making a Difference in the World (Principles 1-4)
- Chapter 2: Power Is Given, Not Grabbed (Principles 5-8)
- Chapter 3: Enduring Power Comes from a Focus on Others (Principles 9-12)
- Chapter 4: The Abuses of Power (Principles 13-16)
- Chapter 5: The Price of Powerlessness (Principles 17-20)
- Epilogue: A Fivefold Path to Power

## Core Thesis

The power paradox: we rise in power through what is best about human nature (empathy, generosity, focus on others), but we fall from power through what is worst (impulsivity, selfishness, incivility). The very experience of having power erodes the skills that earned us that power.

## 20 Power Principles (Grouped)

### What Power Is (Ch 1)
1. Power is about altering the states of others
2. Power is part of every relationship and interaction
3. Power is found in everyday actions
4. Power comes from empowering others in social networks

### How Power Is Given (Ch 2)
5. Groups give power to those who advance the greater good
6. Groups construct reputations that determine the capacity to influence
7. Groups reward those who advance the greater good with status and esteem
8. Groups punish those who undermine the greater good with gossip

### Enduring Power (Ch 3)
9. Enduring power comes from empathy
10. Enduring power comes from giving
11. Enduring power comes from expressing gratitude
12. Enduring power comes from telling stories that unite

### Abuses of Power (Ch 4)
13. Power leads to empathy deficits and diminished moral sentiments
14. Power leads to self-serving impulsivity
15. Power leads to incivility and disrespect
16. Power leads to narratives of exceptionalism

### Price of Powerlessness (Ch 5)
17. Powerlessness involves facing environments of continual threat
18. Stress defines the experience of powerlessness
19. Powerlessness undermines the ability to contribute to society
20. Powerlessness causes poor health

## Key Frameworks

1. **The Power Paradox** — rise through best traits, fall through worst
2. **The Big Five** — enthusiasm, kindness, focus, calmness, openness
3. **Greater Good Score** — degree to which actions benefit others vs harm
4. **Four Practices of Enduring Power** — empathy, giving, gratitude, storytelling
5. **Four Abuses of Power** — empathy deficits, impulsivity, incivility, exceptionalism
6. **Four Prices of Powerlessness** — threat, stress, undermined contribution, poor health
7. **Fivefold Path to Power** — awareness, humility, giving, respect, change context

## Key Definitions
- **Power**: capacity to make a difference by altering the states of others
- **Status**: the respect/esteem others direct to you
- **Control**: capacity to determine your own life outcomes (≠ power)
- **Social class**: wealth + education + occupational prestige
- **Greater good**: collective well-being of a social network
