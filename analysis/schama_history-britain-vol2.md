# A History of Britain Vol 2: The British Wars — Simon Schama

## Classification & Context

- **Book type:** Narrative history — sweeping, interpretive, storytelling-driven
- **Audience:** General educated reader; companion volume to BBC television series (2000–2001)
- **Scope:** 1603 (accession of James VI/I) to approximately 1776 (loss of American colonies and Cornwallis in India)
- **Schama's approach:** Emphatically anti-revisionist. Schama openly declares himself a "born-again Whig" in the preface, insisting that the civil wars were a genuine battle of beliefs and principles, not merely an imbroglio of interests. He argues that the "eventual, admittedly partial, success of the party of liberty represented a genuine turning point in the political history of the world." He is a master storyteller who uses vivid characters and set-piece scenes to carry sweeping arguments, weaving together political narrative, cultural observation, and personal drama.
- **Distinctive features:** Schama consistently treats "Britain" — not just England — as the unit of analysis. The book's central thesis is that the obsessive attempts to forge "Great Britain" through uniformity produced the very fractures they were meant to heal. Scotland, Ireland and Wales are not peripheral — they are causal.
- **Series position:** Volume 2 of 3. Vol 1 covers prehistory to 1603; Vol 3 covers 1776–2000.

## Central Thesis

Schama's overarching argument is that the idea of "Great Britain" — the compulsive drive to forge a unified, harmonized realm from the archipelago's diverse and hostile kingdoms — was both the engine and the destroyer of the Stuart monarchy. James VI/I invented the fantasy of a united Britain; his sons Charles I and Charles II inherited it; and the obsession with imposing religious and political uniformity across Scotland, Ireland and England guaranteed catastrophe rather than harmony. "The more strenuously that governments, both royal and republican, laboured to pull the pieces of Britain together, the more abysmally they fell apart."

The British wars were not an English event accidentally complicated by Celtic peripheries. They were caused by Scotland and Ireland. The Scottish Calvinist rebellion of 1637 triggered the crisis; the Irish Catholic rebellion of 1641 made it irreversible; and only Scottish military intervention decided the outcome of the English civil war. "Britain killed England." Schama insists that the revisionist historians who portray pre-civil-war England as a docile, consensus-bound polity have been "asking the right questions about the wrong country."

The second half of the book traces how the sovereignty vacuum left by the executed king was eventually filled — first by Cromwell's military republic (which reproduced many of the flaws it claimed to have overthrown), then by the restored but chastened monarchy, and finally by the revolutionary settlement of 1688, which created a constitutional monarchy constrained by parliamentary sovereignty. But this settlement itself generated a new problem: the creation of a vast military-commercial empire that betrayed its own founding principles of liberty. The book ends with the bitter irony that the "empire of liberty" championed by Pitt and the American colonists dissolved into exactly the kind of coercive imperialism — in India, in the slave trade, in the subjugation of the Highlands — that its founders had sworn to reject.

## Chapter-Level Detail

### Chapter 1: Re-inventing Britain (1603–1637)

**Main argument:** James VI/I arrived in England with a grandiose vision of "Great Britain" — a fantasy of harmonious union embodied in new flags, coinage, and allegorical masques. But the project of unification was auto-destructive: the more it was imposed, the more it provoked resistance. The chapter traces four interlocking zones of tension: (1) the constitutional quarrel over royal prerogative vs. parliamentary rights; (2) the religious divide between Puritans demanding further reformation and a king content with the Anglican status quo; (3) the massive colonial plantation of Ireland; and (4) the cultural distance between Lowland and Highland Scotland.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **John Speed's atlas (1611):** The tailor-turned-cartographer who produced the first popular British atlas, stitching together pilfered maps from Saxton, Norden and Mercator. Speed described the vale of the Red Horse in Warwickshire as another Eden — the same ridge where, in October 1642, Charles I would watch 3,000 men die at Edgehill. Schama uses this as his framing device: pastoral paradise to Golgotha.

- **Thomas Dekker and the plague (1603):** Dekker and Jonson were hired to stage the king's entry pageant, but plague killed 30,000–40,000 Londoners that summer, forcing postponement. Dekker pivoted to writing "The Wonderfull Yeare," a vivid plague pamphlet. When the pageant finally happened in 1604, Stephen Harrison built 90-foot triumphal arches featuring Britannia herself.

- **The Gunpowder Plot (1605):** Robert Catesby, Francis Tresham, Guy Fawkes and others planned to blow up Parliament, kill the king, and place Princess Elizabeth on the throne. Fawkes was found with 36 barrels of gunpowder beneath the House of Lords. The conspirators came to gruesome ends — Catesby shot dead, Fawkes hanged and eviscerated alive. The plot paradoxically made James hugely popular and 5 November became the Protestant holy day par excellence.

- **Frances Howard, Robert Carr and the Overbury murder (1612–1616):** The most sensational court scandal of the Jacobean age. Frances Howard divorced the Earl of Essex on grounds of non-consummation (with a disputed virginity inspection), married the king's favourite Robert Carr (Earl of Somerset), and arranged the murder of Carr's friend Sir Thomas Overbury by poisoned enema in the Tower. The crook-backed apothecary, the fashion-queen Anne Turner, and the poisoned tarts formed a cast that "made the most lurid productions of John Webster seem understated." The Howards were convicted; minions were executed; the nobles were merely imprisoned.

- **The Bohemian crisis and the Spanish match (1618–1623):** Frederick, the Elector Palatine (married to James's daughter Elizabeth), accepted the throne of Bohemia and was crushed at the Battle of the White Mountain (1620). James's desperate attempt to negotiate Frederick's restoration through a Spanish marriage for Prince Charles led to the farcical Madrid adventure of 1623, where Charles and Buckingham (as "Tom and Jack Smith" in false whiskers) went to woo the Infanta — only to be held as virtual hostages and humiliated with probationary terms.

- **Dorchester's Puritan reformation (1613–1640):** After a devastating fire, the Puritan rector John White and his allies transformed the small Dorset town into a moral police state: fornication prosecuted, theatre banned, swearing fined (Henry Gollop: 40 curses in a row), a "Frenchwoman" without hands doing tricks with her feet turned away. Schools, almshouses and a municipal brewhouse were established. Dorchester became a fount of charity and a laboratory of godly governance.

- **The forced loan crisis (1626–1628):** Charles I's extra-parliamentary levy provoked resistance from powerful nobles and gentry. The Earl of Lincoln mobilized 70 gentry resisters and was sent to the Tower. In Yorkshire, Sir John Jackson threatened to hang any tenant who paid. The Petition of Right followed in 1628.

- **The 1629 parliamentary crisis:** Speaker Finch was physically held in his chair by Denzil Holles while Sir Miles Hobart locked the door and Sir John Eliot read resolutions declaring anyone who supported illegal taxation "a capital enemy of the kingdom." Schama calls this shift "from speaking to shoving and shouting" a genuinely ominous collapse of deference.

- **Viscount Scudamore and Abbey Dore (1635):** The Herefordshire royalist and Laudian gentleman who restored the ruined Cistercian abbey, commissioning John Abel to carve a spectacular Palladian chancel screen. The desecrated altar slab had been used to salt meat. Scudamore's restoration represents the Laudian vision of beauty in worship — which the Puritans viewed as a Catholic abomination.

**Key concepts:**
- The "British problem" — troubles in Scotland and Ireland caused the English civil war, not the other way around
- The political birth of English public opinion through newsletters, "separates," and the news business (John Pory charging £20/year for subscribers)
- James I's "divine right" theory vs. Sir Edward Coke's "ancient constitution"
- The personal rule (1629–1640) — not an era of "halcyon days" but of suppressed tensions
- Ship money as the extension of a legitimate naval levy into an unconstitutional inland tax
- The gunpowder/saltpetre project — Charles I seriously demanding citizens preserve their urine for national defence

**Connection to next chapter:** The chapter ends with Charles's fateful decision to impose the Laudian Prayer Book on Scotland in 1634–37, setting the stage for the explosion that begins Chapter 2.

### Chapter 2: Give Caesar His Due? (1637–1649)

**Main argument:** The British wars began not in England but in Scotland, when the attempt to impose a Laudian prayer book triggered a revolutionary uprising. The Scottish Covenanter rebellion of 1637–38 broke the Stuart monarchy's capacity to govern without parliament, forced Charles I to summon the Long Parliament, and set in motion the chain of events leading to civil war and regicide. The chapter follows the war from the Prayer Book riots through Edgehill, Marston Moor, Naseby and finally the trial and execution of Charles I in January 1649.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **The Prayer Book riots (23 July 1637):** Foot stools flew down the nave of St Giles's Cathedral in Edinburgh when the dean and bishop attempted to read from the new royally authorized Prayer Book. Women wailed "Woe, woe" and hands reached to strip the surplices from the clergy. The riots were pre-organized but their ferocity shocked even the organizers.

- **The National Covenant (28 February 1638):** Signed at Greyfriars Church, Edinburgh, in a four-hour ceremony. Archibald Johnston of Wariston, the "dyspeptic, self-mortifying zealot" who drafted much of it, called it "the glorious marriage day of the kingdom with God." On getting into bed with his teenage wife Jean, Johnston immediately assured God (out loud) that he preferred His face to hers.

- **Sir Edmund Verney and the standard at Nottingham (August 1642):** The Knight Marshal, a man who privately disagreed with the king's cause but was bound by duty, told Edward Hyde: "I do not like the quarrel, and do heartily wish that the King would yield ... But for my part ... I have eaten his bread, and served him near thirty years, and will not do so base a thing as to forsake him." Verney raised the 20-man standard in a storm; it blew down that night. He was killed at Edgehill holding the flag, his body never found — only (reportedly) his severed hand clutching a piece of the pole with the king's ring still on his finger.

- **The Verney family torn apart:** Sir Edmund (royalist) vs. his son Ralph (parliamentarian). Eleanor, Lady Sussex, tried to mediate: "lett me intrete you as a frende that loves you most hartily, not to right passynatly to your father, but ovour com him with kandnes." Ralph's wife Mary was sent back to England to recover sequestered estates, succeeded after exhausting lobbying, but their infant son and daughter both died. Mary herself died of lung disease in France; Ralph shipped her coffin back to Buckinghamshire alone.

- **The Battle of Edgehill (23 October 1642):** The first major engagement. Prince Rupert's cavalry charge swept through the parliamentary right but then galloped off to loot the baggage train 3 miles away, leaving the royalist infantry exposed. Sir William Balfour (a Scots Covenanter) rallied the parliamentary cavalry reserve. Hand-to-hand pike combat lasted hours. The nine-year-old Duke of York watched from behind the lines and marvelled his whole life that "neither side giving ground except to exhaustion." 3,000 dead.

- **Waller and Hopton's tragic friendship:** Parliamentary General Sir William Waller and royalist General Sir Ralph Hopton — friends, both professional soldiers, Hopton himself a Puritan who had voted for Strafford's attainder — found themselves enemies in the West Country. Waller's letter to Hopton: "Certainly my affections to you are so unchangeable, that hostility itself cannot violate my freindshipp to your person ... Wee are both upon the stage, and must act those parts that are assigned us in this Tragedy."

- **Lady Brilliana Harley's siege (1643):** With her husband Sir Robert away at Westminster, Brilliana defended Brampton Bryan castle in Herefordshire against 700 royalist troops for six and a half weeks with just 50 musketeers. The roof was smashed in; she was "most upset by the perpetual and noisy enemy cursing." She survived the siege but died of a lung affliction in October 1643.

- **Strafford's trial and execution (March–May 1641):** Thomas Wentworth, Earl of Strafford, conducted his own defence with "compelling logic," tearing apart the prosecution's case. When impeachment seemed likely to fail, Pym switched to an act of attainder — a legislative process needing only a "presumption of guilt." Strafford's letter to the king asking Charles to sign his death warrant is one of the most moving documents of the period. Charles signed "with teary eyes" and believed his own execution eight years later was God's judgement for this betrayal.

- **Charles I's attempted arrest of the Five Members (4 January 1642):** The king marched into the Commons with armed guards to arrest Pym, Hampden, Holles, Haselrig and Strode. They had already escaped. Speaker Lenthall: "I have neither eyes to see nor tongue to speak in this place but as the House is pleased to direct me." Charles: "the birds are flown." An unmitigated fiasco that destroyed the last possibility of moderate consensus.

- **The Battle of Marston Moor (2 July 1644):** 40,000 men; Cromwell wounded in the neck but returned to wheel his disciplined cavalry around the royalist rear. 6,000 dead. The Duke of Newcastle, who had emptied his own coffers for Charles's army, "would not remain to hear the laughter of the court" and went into exile with £90.

- **The Battle of Naseby (14 June 1645):** Cromwell and Fairfax with 14,000 vs. Charles with 7,000. Charles tried to charge Cromwell's troopers with his Life Guard; horrified aides led his horse away, misread as a command for tactical withdrawal. 4,500 royalist foot and 500 officers captured, plus the complete artillery train, the king's correspondence, and £100,000 in jewels. Welsh camp-followers called "Irish whores" were butchered or mutilated.

- **The trial and execution of Charles I (January 1649):** Charles refused to acknowledge the court's jurisdiction: "Remember I am your King, your lawful King." John Downes protested Charles should be heard; Cromwell snapped "What ails thee?" Only 59 signed the death warrant. On 30 January, wearing two shirts "lest shivering be mistaken for fear," Charles stepped through the Banqueting House — past Rubens' ceiling paintings celebrating Stuart power — and onto the scaffold. He declared "their Liberty and Freedom consist in having of Government, those Laws, by which their Life and their Goods may be most their own. It is not for having share in Government ... that is nothing pertaining to them." Brandon severed his head with a single blow.

**Key concepts:**
- The Solemn League and Covenant (1643) — the alliance with Scotland that turned the war, the first genuinely British union since James I
- The New Model Army — Fairfax and Cromwell's professionalised, ideologically motivated force
- The birth of constitutional royalism (Hyde, Falkland)
- Pym's mastery of parliamentary management and propaganda
- The Grand Remonstrance (November 1641) — parliament's complete rewriting of Charles I's reign
- The Eikon Basilike — Charles's posthumous propaganda masterpiece, 35 editions in 1649 alone

**Connection to next chapter:** The execution creates a sovereignty vacuum — "a something or a nothing" — that Chapter 3 explores through Hobbes, Cromwell, the Levellers and the sects.

### Chapter 3: Looking for Leviathan (1649–1658)

**Main argument:** The execution of Charles I created an unprecedented vacuum of sovereignty. The chapter tracks the competing visions that attempted to fill it: Milton's republican idealism, the Levellers' proto-democracy, the Fifth Monarchists' millenarian theocracy, the Quakers' inner light, and Hobbes's amoral Leviathan — with Cromwell stumbling between all of them, never quite becoming the king he increasingly resembled.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **Thomas Hobbes and the Torricellian vacuum:** Hobbes, living in Paris, debated with French philosophers about whether vacuums could exist in nature while writing Leviathan — his answer to the vacuum of sovereignty. Hobbes was a "plenist" (abhorring vacuums) in both physics and politics. Published 1651, the book shocked royalists because it counselled submission to whoever could provide protection.

- **Milton's Eikonoklastes (1649):** Milton, losing his sight, was mobilized to counter the Eikon Basilike. He compared the king to a "Vultur in the Mountains" feeding off the carcasses of the free. The tract was a commissioned job that Milton later confessed he'd been told to do.

- **John Lilburne and the Levellers:** The saga of "Freeborn John" — flogged through London streets, jailed repeatedly, barricading himself in his cell, sticking fingers in his ears. His wife Elizabeth and Katherine Chidley led mass women's petitions. Parliament told them to "go home and looke after your own businesse, and meddle with your huswifery." Lilburne published "An Impeachment of High Treason against Oliver Cromwell," was tried at the Guildhall, played brilliantly to the gallery, and was acquitted. He eventually became a Quaker.

- **The Putney Debates (1647):** Colonel Rainborough: "The poorest he that is in England hath a life to live as the greatest he." Ireton countered with the sovereignty of property. Cromwell: "A nobleman, a gentleman, a yeoman. That is a good interest of the nation."

- **The Drogheda massacre (September 1649):** Cromwell's own account is "startlingly unapologetic": "I forbade them to spare any that were in arms in the town." At least 3,000 royalist soldiers killed, most after they had surrendered or been disarmed. At St Peter's Church, pews were burned beneath the steeple to smoke out defenders; many fell to their deaths in flames. Schama calls Cromwell "a pig-headed, narrow-minded, Protestant bigot and English imperialist" but meticulously separates documented military atrocity from apocryphal civilian massacre stories.

- **George Fox and the Quakers (1643–1652):** Fox, a weaver's son, walked through a war-torn landscape in his grey leather coat, experiencing "openings" to illumination. He climbed Pendle Hill in 1652 and "beheld, if not the Promised Land, then the green Ribble valley stretching west to the Irish Sea." He was punched in York Minster, smacked with a Bible in Tickhill, repeatedly arrested. The Quakers "refused to doff their hats or to be quiet in church" and were "somehow deeply offensive."

- **James Nayler's blasphemy trial (1656):** Nayler rode through Bristol imitating Christ; his few disciples cried hosanna. He was pilloried for two hours, his forehead branded with a "B," his tongue bored through with a hot iron, flogged through London and then flogged again in Bristol. Cromwell was disturbed by the punitive overkill.

- **Cromwell's dissolution of the Rump (20 April 1653):** Cromwell crossed the line from bullying to despotism. "When he sent the Rump packing, Cromwell liked to think that he was striking a blow at 'ambition and avarice'. But what he really wounded, and fatally, was the Commonwealth itself." Schama calls it an act that "makes the presence of his statue outside the House of Commons a joke in questionable taste."

- **The readmission of the Jews (1655–1656):** Cromwell met Menasseh ben Israel, who was said to have "pressed his hands against Cromwell's body to make sure he was, after all, made of mortal stuff." Despite Council opposition, Cromwell used personal authority to protect London's existing Jewish community. Antonio Rodrigues Robles' 1656 petition — claiming he was "not a Spaniard but a Jew" — created the precedent for open Jewish life in England for the first time in 350 years.

- **William Petty and the anatomy of empire:** The prodigy who resuscitated Ann Greene (pronounced dead, packed in a coffin, rescued from dissection). He became Physician-General to the army in Ireland and chief cartographer of dispossession — "the anatomist of the mutilated body of Ireland."

- **Cromwell's death (3 September 1658):** Anniversary of Dunbar and Worcester. A tornado ripped through England. The botched embalming; the lying-in-state at Somerset House; the effigy "winched upright" for two months, crown on head. The state funeral was a fiasco: altercations about protocol delayed proceedings, it went dark inside the Abbey, no funeral orations, "just a few short sharp blasts on the trumpets."

**Key concepts:**
- The "vacuum of sovereignty" — Hobbes' Leviathan as the answer to political nihilism
- The Leveller programme: franchise for male householders over 21, annual parliaments, simplified law, abolished tithes — "genuinely revolutionary boldness"
- The Fifth Monarchists and millenarian politics
- The Protectorate as a quasi-monarchy that dared not speak its name
- The major-generals and the "morals police" — the failed attempt to impose Puritan virtue by military force (Christmas riots, Christmas banned)
- Cromwell's deepest conviction: tolerance and "a quiet life" — "the most deeply felt principle of the man who created the matrix of the modern English state was, in its essence, liberal"

**Connection to next chapter:** Cromwell's death leaves the Protectorate without a viable successor; the path to Restoration opens.

### Chapter 4: Unfinished Business (1660–1688)

**Main argument:** The Restoration of Charles II in 1660 closed but did not heal the wounds of the civil war. The chapter tracks the recurring crises — plague (1665), the Great Fire (1666), the Dutch humiliation at Chatham (1667), the Exclusion Crisis — that repeatedly shook confidence in the monarchy's capacity to protect the nation. Wren's St Paul's becomes a symbol of the struggle to build a reformed, Protestant, but architecturally magnificent Britain. The chapter climaxes with James II's self-destructive Catholic absolutism and the "Glorious Revolution" of 1688.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **The Plague of 1665:** Pepys's diary entries; Evelyn's horror at mass graves. The court fled London.

- **The Great Fire of London (2–5 September 1666):** Evelyn: "All the skie were of a fiery aspect, like the top of a burning Oven." Lead melted off St Paul's roof, flowing down streets. 13,200 houses, 87 churches, the Guildhall and the Exchange destroyed. Damage estimated at £10 million. Booksellers stored their stock in St Faith's Chapel beneath St Paul's — the choir crashed through the floor, incinerating everything.

- **Christopher Wren's vision for London:** Just two days after the rain stopped, Wren presented Charles II with a plan for a completely rebuilt London: an oval piazza, radiating streets, a domed basilica. Roger Pratt was "incensed at the temerity of this unknown." The merchants wanted to rebuild where things had been. Wren's grand plan was doomed — but over three decades he got to build dozens of churches and the greatest of all British sacred buildings.

- **Wren and Bernini:** Wren's 1665 visit to Paris, where he met Bernini and saw his drawings for the Louvre. The encounter shaped Wren's conviction that classical domed architecture could be transplanted into Protestant England.

- **The Dutch raid on the Medway (June 1667):** Admiral de Ruyter sailed up the Medway, broke the barrier chain, and captured or destroyed the English fleet including the flagship Royal Charles. "Astoundingly" — a humiliation that destroyed Clarendon's career.

- **The Exclusion Crisis (1679–1681):** The Earl of Shaftesbury's campaign to exclude the Catholic James, Duke of York, from the succession. The Duke of Monmouth as the Protestant alternative. Shaftesbury's ultimate failure and James's accession.

- **James II's self-destruction (1685–1688):** James's Declaration of Indulgence; the expulsion of Magdalen College fellows ("Get you gone! Know I am your King"); the trial of the Seven Bishops (acquitted to national celebration); the birth of James Francis Edward Stuart (immediately denounced as a fraud — "the warming-pan baby").

- **Schama's nuanced portrait of James II:** A "divine-right absolutist" but also a champion of toleration. "While in conventional histories the European 'enlightened despots' ... get credit for imposing toleration on their subjects, the same benefit is not extended to James II." In Ireland, James was "the first English king who actually made a strenuous and politically dangerous effort to reverse the brutal wars of colonization." Schama insists 1688 was "not a simple struggle between the forces of reaction and progress."

- **The invasion of 1688:** Not a "revolution" but an invasion — 600 vessels, 15,000 Dutch and German troops. The Battle of the Boyne (1690) was "fought mainly between non-English Europeans — the French under the Duc de Lauzun and on the other a force of 36,000, two-thirds of whom were Dutch, German and Danes." John Churchill's defection was crucial.

**Key concepts:**
- The Restoration closed but did not heal the "puncture wounds" of the civil war
- Wren as the architect of Protestant grandeur — the struggle to make classical architecture "at home in England"
- The Exclusion Crisis as a dress rehearsal for 1688
- The critical difference between 1680–85 and 1688: Princess Mary as legitimate alternative to James
- William III's Dutch imperatives — 1688 as a chapter in European, not just British, history
- James Thornhill's ceiling at Greenwich as "the first great visual manifesto of the post-1688 monarchy"

**Connection to next chapter:** The Revolution settlement creates the constitutional framework — but also the military-fiscal state — that makes "Britannia Incorporated" possible.

### Chapter 5: Britannia Incorporated (1689–1745)

**Main argument:** The post-1688 settlement created a paradox: a war-forged, heavily taxed, bureaucratic military state that was simultaneously the freest and most politically combative society in Europe. The Act of Union (1707) absorbed Scotland into a commercial empire. The Jacobite risings of 1715 and 1745 were the last convulsions of the old honour-culture against the new Britain of cash, commerce and constitutional monarchy.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **The Act of Union (1707):** Daniel Defoe, ex-bankrupt and spy, disguised as a fish merchant in Glasgow and wool manufacturer in Aberdeen, propagandized for union. Edinburgh riots: "a Terrible Multitude ... shouting and swearing and Cryeing Out all Scotland would stand together. No Union. No Union. English Dogs." The "Equivalent" — £398,085.10s. — precisely compensated Scotland's Darien losses. Lord Belhaven's Shakespearean lament: "Like Caesar sitting in the midst of our Senate attending the final blow." The Earl of Seafield, signing: "There's ane end of ane auld sang."

- **The Sacheverell Trial (1710):** The ultra-Tory preacher who "hung out the bloody banner of defiance," condemning the Glorious Revolution and accusing Whigs of endangering the Church. 100,000 copies of his sermon printed. His impeachment became a public-relations fiasco; Dissenter meeting-houses were burned; Huguenot chapels in Spitalfields ransacked.

- **The '45 rising:** Bonnie Prince Charlie's march to Derby and the fateful council debate at Exeter House (5 December 1745). Charles Edward wanted to press on to London; Lord George Murray insisted on retreat to Scotland. Murray won the argument. "Needless to say, they had barely begun their long tramp home when Louis XV ... was finally impressed enough ... to send the long-awaited invasion fleet!"

- **Culloden (16 April 1746):** 9,000 Hanoverians vs. 5,000 Jacobites. The Highlanders charged uphill into a northeaster and into cannon. Between 1,000 and 1,500 dead on the field, 700 prisoners. Perhaps 1,000 wounded were methodically slaughtered afterwards. The "orders to give no quarter" purportedly from the Prince were "a pure invention of Hanoverian propaganda."

- **Simon Fraser, Baron Lovat:** Found hiding in a hollowed-out tree trunk. Hogarth drew him. En route to trial, a woman shouted "You ugly old dog, don't you think you will have that frightful head cut off?" Lovat: "You damned ugly old bitch, I believe I shall." His execution stand collapsed, killing seven.

- **The Scottish Enlightenment:** Hume, Ferguson, Adam Smith — "the first theory of progress was systematically articulated." The Encyclopedia Britannica (1768–71), first published in Edinburgh. Robert Adam as "Britain's first invincible king of style" — Kedleston modelled on the Arch of Constantine.

- **Alexander Speirs and the Glasgow tobacco trade:** Speirs, Bowman and Co. sent Clydeside-built ships on sixteen voyages to Virginia. Scottish agents dealt directly with growers, cutting out middlemen. "Nearly half of those leaving fortunes worth more than £1,000 in Jamaica in the second half of the eighteenth century were Scots."

**Key concepts:**
- The military-fiscal state: by 1710, military spending consumed 10% of national income; Britons were taxed twice as heavily per capita as the French
- The paradox of the role reversal: Whigs (once champions of limited monarchy) running the war; Tories (once champions of unlimited monarchy) opposing it
- Partisan politics as "prototypically modern" — contested elections, newspapers, coffee-houses, mob violence
- The electorate: 250,000 adult males (15%), divided right down the middle, voting their party choice irrespective of the local squire
- The Scottish transformation: from defeated Jacobite nation to engine of empire, Enlightenment, and industrialization
- The "vindication of Darien" — union providing access to English markets

**Connection to next chapter:** The imperial Britain forged by union and war now faces the test of whether an "empire of liberty" can survive contact with slavery, conquest and extraction.

### Chapter 6: The Wrong Empire (1745–c.1800)

**Main argument:** The British Empire was founded on a contradiction: it proclaimed itself an "empire of liberty" (in conscious contrast to Roman, French and Spanish despotism) but became, in practice, an empire of coercion, slavery and extraction. The chapter traces this betrayal through the slave trade, the loss of the American colonies, and the conquest of India — showing how at each stage the empire violated the very principles its champions invoked.

**Key stories with names/dates/specifics:**

- **Olaudah Equiano:** Kidnapped in West Africa aged eleven, bought by a sea captain, served in the Royal Navy during the Seven Years' War. At the siege of Louisbourg he held "the scalp of an Indian king, killed in the engagement: the scalp was taken off by a Highlander." After the war, his master Pascal — who had promised him freedom — resold him to a captain bound for the West Indies. "Thus, at the moment I expected my toils to end, was I plunged ... into a new slavery."

- **Stowe and the "Patriot Boys":** Lord Cobham's garden at Stowe as the ideological nursery of the British Empire: the Temple of British Worthies (featuring Hampden, Milton, William III), the Temple of Ancient Virtue vs. the Temple of Modern Virtue (a ruin, satirizing Walpole). William Pitt was educated in this milieu and inherited its conviction that British empire must be uniquely free.

- **The fall of Quebec (13 September 1759):** Wolfe's suicidal night ascent of the cliffs along a ravine trail identified by the escaped prisoner Robert Stobo. 4,800 troops and two cannon atop the Plains of Abraham. Montcalm's militia ran at will towards the immobile British lines. Wolfe held fire until the French were 40 yards away. "An immense volley of fire, 'like cannon', ... tore huge holes in both the white-coated French regulars and the militia." Wolfe, shot in wrist, gut and chest, achieved his desired martyrdom. Montcalm was also mortally wounded.

- **Benjamin Franklin as "A Briton":** Franklin in London as Pennsylvania's commissioner, exhilarated by the conquest of Canada: "the foundations of the future grandeur and stability of the British Empire lie in America." He signed his petition to parliament "A Briton." Just seventeen years later he signed the Declaration of Independence.

- **The American Revolution:** Schama frames it as Britain's betrayal of its own principles. Jefferson modelled the Declaration of Independence on the Bill of Rights of 1689. The Stamp Act and Townshend Acts violated the Stowe-Pitt principle that Britons should not be taxed without representation. Chatham (Pitt), crippled and in extreme pain, spoke against the war in the Lords: "we are the aggressors. We have invaded them." He collapsed and died on 11 May 1778; "the empire of liberty, the right empire, died with him."

- **Robert Clive and Bengal:** Clive's grasping of the diwani (revenue collection rights) of Bengal in 1765 — the fateful step from commerce to governance. His famous defence: "a great prince was dependent on my pleasure, an opulent city lay at my mercy ... Mr Chairman, at this moment I stand astonished at my own moderation." He died of an opium overdose in 1774.

- **Warren Hastings and "orientalism":** Hastings as the first great patron of Indian scholarship — Halhed's Sanskrit law digest, Gilchrist's Hindustani dictionary, the first English translation of the Bhagavadgita. Hastings genuinely wanted to make the Company Indian. But he was impeached by Burke, serving as scapegoat for the loss of America. Schama: "Warren Hastings was subconsciously being impeached for losing America."

- **Burke's thunderbolt speech (December 1783):** "Our conquest there, after twenty years, is as crude as it was the first day ... Young men (boys almost) govern there, without society and without sympathy with the natives ... wave after wave; and there is nothing before the eyes of the natives but an endless, hopeless prospect of new flights of birds of prey and passage."

- **Cornwallis and the "Permanent Settlement" of Bengal (1789):** The attempt to create an English-style landed gentry from the zemindars — who were actually Mughal tax officials, not aristocrats. The system produced debt, dispossession and the enrichment of Calcutta money-men. "Was it really only seventy years since the inspirational inscription had been set in Stowe's Temple of Gothic Liberty, thanking the gods for not making the free British Romans?"

- **Richard Wellesley and the conquest of Mysore (1799):** The storming of Seringapatam; Tipu Sultan's body found beneath a mound of the dead. Among the plunder: Tipu's mechanical tiger that devoured a British soldier (now in the V&A). Wellesley bragged to his wife: "Behold me covered with glory."

**Key concepts:**
- The "empire of liberty" vs. the empire of coercion
- The slave trade — Equiano's autobiography as counter-narrative to imperial self-congratulation
- Pitt's American strategy: fight an American war the American way (reimburse colonial assemblies, equalize officer ranks)
- The opium triangle: Indian opium → China → tea → British workers
- Cornwallis' delusion that Indian society could be rebuilt on English county principles
- Wellesley's Napoleonic paramountcy — "India responded to unapologetic supremacy"
- The book's final line: "Most of those who came later to British India would resist the first craving. But as for the opiate of global mastery, nineteenth- and even twentieth-century Britain would remain helplessly hooked."

## Key Frameworks & Mental Models

1. **Britain killed England:** The civil war was caused by the multinational dynamics of the Stuart realms, not by English domestic politics alone. Scotland and Ireland were not peripheral — they were causal.

2. **The auto-destructive union:** Every attempt to impose uniformity across the British archipelago (religious, constitutional, cultural) produced the opposite — fragmentation, resistance, war.

3. **The sovereignty vacuum:** The execution of Charles I created an unprecedented crisis of legitimacy. Hobbes's Leviathan and Milton's republican theory represent the two poles of response: amoral realism vs. idealistic constitutionalism.

4. **Cromwell's paradox:** The destroyer of monarchy who increasingly resembled a king; the champion of liberty who dissolved parliament at sword-point; the tolerant Independent who massacred at Drogheda.

5. **The Whig-Tory role reversal:** After 1688, positions flipped — Whigs became the war party running big government; Tories became the opposition champions of overtaxed country gentlemen.

6. **The wrong empire:** The British Empire was founded on the principle that it would be uniquely free, commercial and non-coercive — the anti-Rome. It became, in practice, a coercive, slave-dependent, military-extractive empire that violated its own founding myth.

7. **The three-kingdom analysis:** Throughout the book, Schama insists on analysing events through the prism of all three (or four) kingdoms simultaneously, showing how actions in one always had consequences in the others.

## Stories Worth Preserving

1. **John Speed's Eden to Golgotha (1611/1642)** — The map-maker's bucolic epiphany at Edgehill, transformed into a charnel house thirty years later. Schama's framing device for the entire book.

2. **Sir Edmund Verney's doomed loyalty (1642)** — "I do not like the quarrel ... But for my part ... I have eaten his bread." His death at Edgehill holding the flag; the severed hand with the king's ring; the phantom army in the night sky.

3. **Waller and Hopton's tragic correspondence (1643)** — "Wee are both upon the stage, and must act those parts that are assigned us in this Tragedy." Two friends forced to kill each other's men.

4. **Lady Brilliana Harley's siege (1643)** — Six and a half weeks defending a castle with 50 musketeers; most upset by the enemy cursing; died replanting her garden.

5. **Strafford's letter to the king (May 1641)** — Asking Charles to sign his death warrant to save the kingdom: "To say Sir, that there hath not been strife in me, were to make me less man." One of the most moving political documents of the period.

6. **Charles I's execution (30 January 1649)** — Walking through the Banqueting House past Rubens' ceiling; two shirts against shivering; "I go from a corruptible to an incorruptible Crown." Brandon's single blow.

7. **Cromwell's dissolution of the Rump (1653)** — The crossing of the line from bullying to despotism; the undoing of everything the civil war was fought for.

8. **The Great Fire and Wren's vision (1666)** — Lead melting off St Paul's roof, books incinerating in St Faith's Chapel; Wren presenting his utopian plan just two days after the rain stopped; the merchants wanting everything put back where it was.

9. **Olaudah Equiano's betrayal (1763)** — Promised freedom after wartime naval service; resold instead to a West Indian captain. "Thus, at the moment I expected my toils to end, was I plunged ... into a new slavery."

10. **Wolfe at Quebec (1759)** — The suicidal night ascent of the cliffs; 4,800 men and two cannon atop the Plains of Abraham; the volley at 40 yards; Wolfe's triple wounding and hero's death.

11. **Franklin's transformation** — Signing a petition to parliament "A Briton" in 1759; signing the Declaration of Independence in 1776. "What happened?"

12. **Tipu Sultan's mechanical tiger** — The wind-up automaton that devoured a British soldier and produced satisfying plaintive cries. Now in the V&A. A perfect emblem of the clash of empires.

## Critique & Cross-Reference

**Evidence strength:**
- Schama is superb at narrative synthesis, drawing on a vast range of primary sources (diaries, letters, pamphlets, newsletters) to recreate the texture of lived experience
- His treatment of the "British" dimension (Scotland, Ireland, Wales as causal agents, not peripheral) is a genuine historiographical contribution
- He is honest about his interpretive stance ("if to tell the story again, and yet insist that that much is true, is to reveal oneself as that most hopeless anachronism, a born-again Whig, so be it")

**Limitations:**
- The companion-to-TV-series format sometimes produces bravura set-pieces at the expense of sustained analytical argument
- Economic and social history gets relatively short shrift — the transition to commercial capitalism, the agricultural revolution, enclosures, proto-industrialization are mostly background
- The treatment of the slave trade and abolition movement is compressed; Equiano is the only sustained African voice
- Ireland after Cromwell gets somewhat less attention than Scotland; the 18th-century Penal Laws are lightly handled
- Some characterizations verge on the novelistic — Schama's Cromwell is brilliantly drawn but may overplay the tortured-conscience angle
- The final India section is compressed and may leave readers wanting more on the Mughal context

**Cross-references to vault:**
- [[The English and Their History - Robert Tombs]] — Tombs takes a more England-centric view and is more sympathetic to the revisionist case that the civil war was "eminently avoidable"; Schama emphatically disagrees
- [[A History of Britain Vol 1 - Simon Schama]] — Volume 1 covers the background (Reformation, Elizabeth, Mary Queen of Scots) that Vol 2 presupposes
- [[Revolution - Peter Ackroyd]] — Ackroyd's volume covers the same period (1688–1815) from a more cultural-social angle
- [[Empire - Niall Ferguson]] — Ferguson's thesis of empire as net positive directly clashes with Schama's "wrong empire" argument in Chapter 6
- [[The Anglo-Saxons - Marc Morris]] — For the deep background on the "ancient constitution" that figures so prominently in parliamentary rhetoric
- [[The Norman Conquest - Marc Morris]] — For the "Norman Yoke" mythology that the Levellers invoked
- [[The Plantagenets - Dan Jones]] — For the medieval parliamentary traditions that the Long Parliament claimed to defend
