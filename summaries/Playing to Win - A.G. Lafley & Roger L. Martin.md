# Playing to Win: How Strategy Really Works — A.G. Lafley & Roger L. Martin

> Lafley and Martin reduce strategy to its skeleton: five interconnected choices that form an iterative cascade.
> Their thesis is that strategy is not a vision, not a plan, and not a set of goals — it is an integrated set of choices about where to compete and how to win.
> Built on a decade of Procter & Gamble transformation that doubled sales and quadrupled profits, the book demystifies strategic thinking from a rarefied executive activity into a repeatable process that works at every level of an organisation.
> The most powerful idea is the simplest: most organisations fail at strategy not because they lack intelligence but because they refuse to make choices, preferring to keep all options open until options are all they have left.
> The book's most immediately useful tool is the reverse-engineering question — "What would have to be true for this to be a great choice?" — which transforms advocacy battles into productive inquiry.
> It is one of the clearest strategy books written in the last two decades, though its evidence draws almost entirely from a single company and its framework assumes the reader holds decision-making authority.

---

## About the Authors

**A.G. Lafley** spent 33 years at Procter & Gamble, serving as Chairman and CEO from 2000 to 2009, and then returning for a second stint from 2013 to 2015.
Under his first tenure, P&G's sales doubled from roughly $39 billion to $79 billion, profits quadrupled, and market capitalisation more than doubled — during a period when the S&P 500 declined.
He was deeply influenced by Peter Drucker, particularly Drucker's emphasis on understanding the customer's reality, and by Michael Porter's competitive strategy work.
Before becoming CEO, Lafley ran P&G's beauty business and its Asian operations, giving him both category-level and geographic strategic experience.
He was known inside P&G for radically simplifying strategy conversations, stripping out corporate jargon, and insisting that strategy fit on a single page.

**Roger L. Martin** served as Dean of the Rotman School of Management at the University of Toronto for fifteen years.
Before that, he worked as a strategy consultant at Monitor Company, the firm founded by Michael Porter.
Martin developed the **strategy logic flow** and the **reverse-engineering methodology** that form the book's analytical backbone — tools he refined across hundreds of consulting engagements before bringing them into academia.
Lafley hired Martin as his ongoing strategy partner for nearly a decade, a collaboration both men credit as transformative for P&G's strategic capability.
Martin is also the author of *The Opposable Mind* and *Creating Great Choices*, which explore integrative thinking — the ability to hold two opposing ideas in tension and forge a creative resolution, a theme that runs beneath the surface of this book as well.

## The Big Idea

Strategy is **choice**.
Not aspiration, not planning, not goal-setting — choice.
The word "strategy" is among the most overused and least understood terms in business.
Lafley and Martin argue that most of what passes for strategy — mission statements, long-range plans, budget exercises, competitive analyses — is not strategy at all.
Strategy is an integrated set of choices that uniquely positions an organisation to create value and sustain competitive advantage.
The emphasis is on "integrated" and "choices": each word carries load.

The authors call their framework the **Strategic Choice Cascade**, and it consists of five questions that must be answered as a mutually reinforcing system.
First: *What is our winning aspiration?* — the purpose and ambition, framed around what you do for customers, not around financial targets.
Second: *Where to play?* — the specific playing field across geography, product category, customer segment, distribution channel, and vertical stage of production.
Third: *How to win?* — the method of competitive advantage, which comes down to either cost leadership or differentiation (the authors insist there is no third way).
Fourth: *What capabilities must be in place?* — the mutually reinforcing activity system that delivers the how-to-win choice.
Fifth: *What management systems are required?* — the structures, measures, processes, and review cadences that embed strategy into daily operations.

The cascade is iterative, not linear.
You do not answer question one first and then proceed neatly to question five.
Choices at each level constrain and reinforce choices at every other level, and the whole system must be revisited regularly as competitive conditions shift.
The cascade also nests: a company has a corporate-level cascade, each business unit has its own, each brand has its own, and even internal functions can have their own version.
Every layer must reinforce the layers above and below it.

The book's most provocative claim is about what strategy is NOT.
It is not a vision statement tacked to a conference room wall.
It is not a list of initiatives that happens to be called a "strategic plan."
It is not a budget exercise dressed in strategic language.
And it is emphatically not the comfortable decision to keep all options open.
The most common strategic failure, the authors argue repeatedly, is not making bad choices — it is refusing to make choices at all.
An organisation that tries to serve every segment, compete in every geography, and be excellent at everything has no strategy.
It has a wish list.

## Key Concepts at a Glance

- **The Strategic Choice Cascade** — Five interconnected questions (winning aspiration, where to play, how to win, capabilities, management systems) that form an iterative system.
No single question can be answered in isolation; each constrains and enables the others.
The cascade applies at every organisational level, from corporate strategy down to individual brand and function.

- **Playing to win vs playing to play** — Modest aspirations are more dangerous than ambitious ones because they never generate the tough investments required for sustainable advantage.
An organisation that aspires merely to participate will be outinvested and outmanoeuvred by competitors who aspire to lead.

- **The two ways to win** — Cost leadership (lower cost than all competitors for a comparable offering) or differentiation (offering perceived as distinctively more valuable, commanding a price premium).
Both produce the same result: a wider margin than competitors can match.
The authors acknowledge that the best strategies often blend elements of both but insist the primary orientation must be one or the other.

- **Activity systems** — Core capabilities are best understood not as isolated strengths but as a system of reinforcing activities.
The power comes from how they interact, not from any single capability.
A competitor who copies one capability gains little if the rest of the system is missing.

- **Reverse engineering** — The seven-step "what would have to be true?" process for making strategic choices.
It transforms advocacy battles into collaborative inquiry by asking participants to specify the conditions under which each option would succeed, rather than arguing for their preferred answer.

- **Assertive inquiry** — The communication discipline of clearly stating your view AND genuinely exploring others'.
Neither pure advocacy nor pure surrender, it creates the conditions for honest strategic dialogue.

- **The Strategy Logic Flow** — Seven analytical questions across four dimensions (industry, customer, relative position, competitive reaction) that structure the analysis behind where-to-play and how-to-win choices.

- **Six strategy traps** — Do-it-all, Don Quixote, Waterloo, Something-for-everyone, Dreams-that-never-come-true, Program-of-the-month.
Common failure patterns that result from avoiding genuine strategic choice.

- **Six signs of winning** — A distinctive activity system, customers who adore you, competitors who profit doing something different, more resources to spend, competitors who attack each other instead of you, customers who look to you first for innovation.

## Chapter 1: Strategy Is Choice

The book opens with a declaration that would have made Michael Porter proud: strategy is not about what you want or what you plan, but about the choices you make and the trade-offs those choices force.
Lafley describes arriving as CEO of P&G in June 2000 during what he calls a "crisis" — the company had just issued its third profit warning in a year, the stock had lost half its value, and morale was shattered.
His response was not to launch a turnaround programme or a cost-cutting exercise.
It was to ask five questions.

The **Strategic Choice Cascade** is introduced here as the book's central intellectual contribution.
Lafley explains that every P&G business, from the $10 billion laundry division down to a single brand in a single country, was asked to answer the same five questions.
The genius of the cascade, he argues, is not any individual question — none of them is original — but the insistence that the answers must fit together as an integrated, mutually reinforcing system.
A winning aspiration without a where-to-play choice is a dream.
A where-to-play choice without a how-to-win is a gamble.
A how-to-win without the right capabilities is a fantasy.
And capabilities without management systems to sustain them are a temporary advantage waiting to erode.

The chapter's signature case study is **Oil of Olay**, which Lafley uses to illustrate how all five questions work together.
Oil of Olay was a fading $6 mass-market brand when Lafley and his team began rethinking its strategy.
The traditional approach would have been to invest in advertising and hope for the best, or to acquire an existing prestige skin-care brand (which would have cost billions).
Instead, the team asked the five questions and arrived at a revolutionary answer.
The winning aspiration was to become the leader in facial skin care — not a participant, the leader.
The where-to-play choice was radical: mass-market retail channels (not department stores), women aged 35 and older (not teenagers), at a price point of $18-25 (not the $6 Oil of Olay was selling for, and not the $50+ that prestige brands commanded).
The how-to-win was what the team called **"masstige"** — prestige-quality product at a mass-market price, leveraging P&G's unmatched R&D capability and its dominant retail relationships.
The capabilities required included P&G's consumer research (to identify the underserved segment), its R&D (to create a product that actually matched prestige efficacy), and its retail partnerships (to get prime shelf space in drugstores and supermarkets).
The management system was a review cadence that tracked consumer satisfaction, retail sell-through, and competitive response in near real-time.
Neither the where-to-play nor the how-to-win would have worked in isolation.
A prestige-quality product in prestige channels at prestige prices would have failed — P&G had no department-store relationships and no prestige brand equity.
A mass-quality product at $18-25 would have failed — consumers would not pay a premium for a commodity.
The integrated choice — prestige quality through mass channels at a middle price — created a new market segment and turned Oil of Olay into a multi-billion dollar brand.

Lafley also introduces what he calls the **"comfortable trap"** of refusing to choose.
He describes how many P&G businesses, before the transformation, had strategies that were really just lists of goals — grow revenue, increase market share, expand internationally — with no prioritisation, no exclusion, and no integrated logic.

"Strategy is choice," Lafley writes.
"Specifically, an integrated set of choices."

## Chapter 2: What Is Winning

This chapter tackles the first question in the cascade — the **winning aspiration** — and makes the provocative argument that most organisations set their aspirations too low, not too high.

The critical distinction Lafley and Martin draw is between **playing to win** and **playing to play**.
Playing to win means aspiring to lead your chosen space — to be the best, not merely present.
Playing to play means aspiring merely to participate — to be in the game without committing to dominate it.
The authors argue that playing to play is the more dangerous posture, because it never triggers the hard choices and significant investments that winning requires.

The **Saturn** story is the chapter's central cautionary tale.
General Motors invested over $20 billion across twenty years to create Saturn as its answer to Japanese small cars.
But GM never truly aspired to win in small cars — it aspired to participate, to have an entry in the segment.
The investment was spread across too many compromises: Saturn had its own factory and distribution network but was never given the freedom to truly differentiate.
Meanwhile, Toyota, Honda, and Nissan were playing to win.
They invested in manufacturing excellence, product quality, and customer loyalty with the explicit intention of dominating the segment.
The result was inevitable: Saturn died, and GM's two decades of investment yielded nothing.
The lesson is not that GM lacked resources — it had more resources than any competitor.
The lesson is that a participation aspiration never generates the focus and commitment that winning demands.

"A too-modest aspiration is far more dangerous than a too-lofty one."

By contrast, Lafley tells the story of **P&G's Global Business Services (GBS)** unit, which is perhaps the most surprising example in the book.
GBS was P&G's internal shared-services function — IT, facilities, employee services.
It would have been natural for GBS to aspire merely to competent service delivery.
Instead, under Filippo Passerini, GBS adopted a playing-to-win aspiration: to deliver world-class services at lower cost than any external provider, while simultaneously driving innovation.
Passerini's team created an outsourcing model that partnered with best-of-breed providers, offered services so good that business units CHOSE to buy from GBS rather than going to market, and promised "17 cents on the dollar" savings for every new service.
GBS went from a cost centre to a strategic innovation engine — and it happened because someone in an internal function had the audacity to define winning, not just serving.

The chapter also insists that winning aspirations must be framed around the **customer**, not around financial metrics.
"Maximise shareholder value" is not a winning aspiration — it is a financial outcome that results from having a winning aspiration.
P&G's home-care winning aspiration was not "grow revenue by 8%" but "reinvent cleaning experiences, taking the hard work out of household chores."
That aspiration led to **Swiffer** (replacing the mop and bucket with a disposable cleaning pad), **Mr. Clean Magic Eraser** (a cleaning tool that required no chemical cleaner at all), and **Febreze** (eliminating odours rather than masking them).
None of these products would have emerged from a financial aspiration.
They emerged because someone asked: what does the consumer actually need?

Another important example is P&G's aspiration in beauty care.
Lafley describes how the team defined winning not as "selling more shampoo" but as "helping women feel beautiful."
This consumer-centric aspiration opened the door to categories P&G had never competed in — professional hair colour (through the Clairol acquisition), luxury fragrances, and prestige skin care.
If the aspiration had been product-centred ("be the best shampoo company"), none of those expansions would have seemed logical.

The chapter also addresses the question of **who defines winning**.
Lafley argues that winning must be defined against the best competitor in your space, not against traditional rivals.
He tells the story of P&G's home-care team, which initially benchmarked against Unilever and Colgate-Palmolive — the usual suspects.
When pushed to identify who was actually performing best in the space, they discovered **Reckitt Benckiser**, a smaller, more focused company that was outperforming even P&G's strong results.
Benchmarking against the best competitor, regardless of size or familiarity, revealed the true competitive frontier and forced a much more aggressive strategic response.

## Chapter 3: Where to Play

The where-to-play question is about defining the playing field — and, crucially, about defining where you will NOT play.
Lafley and Martin identify five dimensions of the where-to-play choice: geography, product or service offering, customer segment, distribution channel, and vertical stage of production (which parts of the value chain you own versus outsource).

The chapter opens with the principle that strategy without explicit exclusion is not strategy.
Choosing where not to play frees capital, talent, and attention for the fields where you can actually win.
An organisation that tries to play everywhere plays nowhere well.

The **Bounty paper towels** turnaround is the chapter's most detailed example of subtraction as strategy.
In the late 1990s, Bounty was struggling.
The brand had expanded internationally and diversified into adjacent products — food containers, paper cups, other paper goods.
Resources were spread across too many geographies and too many product lines.
The turnaround came not from expanding but from contracting.
P&G sold off Bounty's international assets and redirected R&D away from tangential products back to the core: paper towels in the United States.
Deep consumer research revealed three distinct segments of paper-towel users with different needs, and Bounty refocused its product development and marketing around those segments.
The result was a revitalised brand with sharper positioning and stronger market share — achieved by doing less, not more.

The **P&G laundry business** provides another illustration of the power of subtraction.
P&G had accumulated fifteen detergent brands over the decades, each with its own team, its own budget, and its own strategy.
The result was internal competition, resource fragmentation, and consumer confusion.
The strategic choice was to go from fifteen brands to five — concentrating all resources on the brands that could win.
The remaining brands received enough investment to dominate their segments, while the eliminated brands freed up capital and talent for the winners.

The chapter also tells the story of P&G's **fine-fragrance** business, which illustrates the concept of attacking along the line of least resistance.
When P&G entered the fragrance business, the established players — LVMH, Estee Lauder, Chanel — were dominant in high-end women's fragrances.
A head-on assault would have triggered massive competitive retaliation.
Instead, P&G's fragrance team chose to enter through men's fragrances (Hugo Boss) and sporty, lifestyle fragrances (Lacoste) — segments the luxury houses cared less about defending.
This gave P&G time to build fragrance capabilities, distribution relationships, and consumer understanding without facing the full competitive response.
Once the beachhead was established, P&G could expand into more competitive segments from a position of strength.

Lafley also uses **Citrus Hill** as a cautionary tale about choosing the wrong field.
P&G entered the orange juice market with Citrus Hill, believing its consumer-marketing capabilities would translate.
But the orange juice market was dominated by Coca-Cola (Minute Maid) and PepsiCo (Tropicana), both of which had entrenched distribution and supply-chain advantages that P&G's marketing capabilities could not overcome.
The where-to-play choice was fatally disconnected from P&G's how-to-win capability.
Citrus Hill was eventually killed, having never returned its cost of capital.

The broader lesson is that where-to-play and how-to-win must be considered together.
A brilliant playing field on which you cannot win is worthless.
A powerful competitive advantage on the wrong field is equally useless.
The playing field determines what capabilities are needed; the available capabilities determine which fields are winnable.

## Chapter 4: How to Win

This is the chapter where Lafley and Martin present their adaptation of Michael Porter's competitive strategy framework, reduced to its starkest form: there are exactly two ways to achieve sustainable competitive advantage.

**Cost leadership** means delivering a comparable offering at a structurally lower cost than all competitors.
The margin advantage comes from the cost side.
The key word is "structurally" — this is not about cutting costs through efficiency drives or layoffs but about building a cost position that competitors cannot match because of differences in scale, process, or business model.

**Differentiation** means delivering an offering perceived as distinctively more valuable by customers, commanding a price premium they willingly pay.
The margin advantage comes from the revenue side.
The key word is "perceived" — the offering must be SEEN as more valuable, not just BE more valuable in some objective sense.

Both strategies produce the same fundamental outcome: a wider gap between revenue and cost than competitors can sustain.
There is no third way, the authors insist — though they acknowledge that the best strategies often find ways to incorporate elements of both.
Toyota, for instance, is primarily a differentiator (quality, reliability, resale value) but also achieves extraordinary cost discipline through its manufacturing system.
The distinction is about primary orientation, not absolute purity.

The chapter's signature example is the **Olay "masstige" strategy**, which is explored in greater depth than in Chapter 1.
The Olay team conducted extensive consumer research and identified a large segment of women aged 35 and older who wanted prestige-quality skin care but found department-store brands too expensive and mass-market brands too ineffective.
This was the "white space" — a gap in the market that no competitor was serving.
The product itself needed to deliver prestige-level efficacy, which required P&G's world-class R&D.
The pricing needed to sit in a specific sweet spot — $18 to $25 — above the mass market but below the prestige tier.
The distribution needed to go through mass retail channels (drugstores, supermarkets, big-box retailers) where P&G had dominant relationships, not through department stores where P&G had no presence.
Every element of the strategy — the product, the price, the channel, the marketing — was designed as a reinforcing system.
The masstige strategy turned Oil of Olay from a declining brand into a multi-billion-dollar franchise and created an entirely new market segment that competitors struggled to follow.

The **Glad/Clorox joint venture** is another instructive story.
P&G was struggling in the plastic-wrap and trash-bag category, competing against Hefty and store brands with no clear competitive advantage.
Rather than investing billions to build advantage in an inherently low-margin category, P&G chose to partner with Clorox, which had complementary strengths — specifically, strong brand equity in household cleaning and an existing relationship with the channel.
The joint venture combined P&G's polymer technology and innovation capability with Clorox's brand strength and distribution, creating a how-to-win that neither company could have achieved alone.
The partnership produced Glad ForceFlex and Glad Press'n Seal, both of which became category leaders.
This is the book's clearest example of how a how-to-win choice can involve creative structural solutions, not just internal capability building.

The chapter also discusses the microeconomic foundations of the two-ways-to-win framework in an appendix that is worth summarising.
In a commodity market where all offerings are identical, the only way to earn above-average returns is to have a lower cost structure than competitors.
In a differentiated market where customers perceive meaningful differences between offerings, the player with the most valued differentiation commands a price premium.
The appendix uses simple supply-and-demand diagrams to show why these are the only two sustainable positions and why a "stuck in the middle" position — neither the cheapest nor the most differentiated — is structurally unprofitable.

## Chapter 5: Play to Your Strengths — Capabilities and Activity Systems

This chapter moves from what you choose (where and how) to what you need in order to execute those choices.
The central argument is that sustainable advantage comes not from individual capabilities but from a **system of mutually reinforcing capabilities** — an idea Lafley and Martin adapt from Michael Porter's activity-system concept.

An individual capability can be copied.
If your advantage is great R&D, a competitor can hire scientists.
If your advantage is efficient manufacturing, a competitor can buy machines.
But a bundle of capabilities that reinforce each other creates compounding returns that are exponentially harder to replicate.
A competitor would need to copy not just one capability but the entire system simultaneously — and the connections between capabilities are often invisible from the outside.

P&G's **five core capabilities** formed such a system:

1. **Deep consumer understanding** — P&G invested more in consumer research than any competitor.
Living It and Working It programmes sent researchers to live with consumers in their homes and shop with them in stores, generating insights that no focus group could produce.
2. **Brand building** — P&G was arguably the world's most sophisticated brand-building machine, with proprietary approaches to brand positioning, consumer communication, and category management.
3. **Innovation** — broadly defined to include not just product innovation but business-model innovation and partnership innovation.
The Connect + Develop programme, which sourced innovations from outside P&G, was a direct result of this capability.
4. **Go-to-market and retailer partnerships** — P&G had the deepest retail relationships in consumer goods.
Its collaboration with Walmart, for instance, pioneered category management and supply-chain integration in ways that benefited both companies.
5. **Global scale** — P&G operated in over 180 countries, giving it scale advantages in procurement, manufacturing, and knowledge transfer.

Each capability made the others stronger.
Consumer understanding fed innovation by revealing unmet needs.
Innovation fed brand building by providing genuinely differentiated products.
Brand building fed go-to-market by creating consumer pull that gave retailers incentive to partner.
Go-to-market generated revenue that funded the global scale needed to invest in more consumer understanding.
The system was circular and self-reinforcing.

The **Gillette acquisition** is the chapter's most important story about capability systems in action.
When P&G acquired Gillette in 2005 for $57 billion, the strategic logic was not just about adding a strong brand to the portfolio — it was about how Gillette's capabilities would reinforce P&G's existing system.
Gillette had three capabilities that P&G needed: superior blade and razor technology (innovation in a category P&G did not serve), strong male-grooming brand equity (extending P&G's consumer base), and a different geographic strength profile (Gillette was stronger in markets where P&G was weaker, particularly in India and Latin America).
Crucially, Gillette's capabilities did not compete with P&G's existing system — they reinforced it.
P&G's consumer understanding could improve Gillette's innovation targeting.
P&G's retail relationships could expand Gillette's distribution.
P&G's global scale could reduce Gillette's manufacturing costs.
The acquisition worked because the capability fit was genuine, not cosmetic.

By contrast, Lafley discusses P&G's **acquisition spree of the 1980s** as an example of what happens when acquisitions are made without capability-system logic.
P&G bought Orange Crush, Bain de Soleil, and a string of other brands that had no connection to P&G's existing capabilities.
The result was a "mishmash of acquisitions that never returned the cost of capital."
The company was buying growth rather than choosing growth — using acquisitions to avoid the hard strategic work of building advantage in its existing fields.
The lesson is sharp: acquisition is a poor substitute for strategic discipline.
An organisation that cannot think strategically in one context will not suddenly develop that capability in a new one.

The chapter proposes a simple test for whether a capability system is genuine: **if removing one capability would weaken the others** (not just itself), the system is real.
If capabilities can be removed without affecting the rest, you have a list of strengths, not a system.

## Chapter 6: Manage What Matters — Management Systems

This is the chapter most people skip and the one Lafley argues is most important.
The fifth box in the cascade — management systems — is the most neglected and, paradoxically, the most decisive.
A brilliant strategy without management systems to embed it is a wish list.

Lafley's central story is the **transformation of P&G's strategy review process**.
When he became CEO, strategy reviews were what he calls "corporate theatre."
Twenty-five people would gather in a conference room.
A business president would present a PowerPoint deck that had been rehearsed for weeks.
The CEO and senior staff would ask questions.
The business president would defend the deck.
At the end, everyone would congratulate each other and move on.
The problem was that these reviews produced performances, not decisions.
The presenting team optimised for a smooth presentation, not for honest disclosure of strategic problems.
Difficult issues were hidden or glossed over.
And no one felt they could challenge the strategy because the room was too large, the stakes too high, and the culture too adversarial.

Lafley redesigned the process completely.
Strategy reviews were reduced to four or five people — the CEO, the relevant business leader, and two or three key advisers.
There were no PowerPoint decks.
Instead, strategic questions were agreed in advance, and the three to four hours were spent in genuine dialogue.
The business leader was not selling a strategy — they were working through a strategic problem with the CEO.
The old system incentivised defence.
The new system incentivised discovery.
Lafley reports that the quality of strategic choices improved markedly, and that business leaders who initially resisted the new format came to prefer it because they received better strategic guidance.

The chapter also introduces the **OGSM framework** — Objectives, Goals, Strategies, Measures — as P&G's standard for translating strategy into operational plans.
OGSM forces alignment between the strategic cascade and the daily actions of every team and individual.
The objectives state what you will do (derived from the winning aspiration).
The goals state how you will measure success (specific metrics).
The strategies state how you will achieve the goals (derived from where-to-play and how-to-win).
The measures state how you will track progress.
The point is that every person in the organisation should be able to trace their daily work back to a strategic choice.

However, even Lafley acknowledges that transforming management systems is extremely difficult.
An anonymous P&G division president, quoted in a sidebar, admits that the old sell-and-defend culture "got in the way" even after Lafley tried to create openness.
People who were being evaluated by the person across the table were naturally reluctant to expose problems.
The management system had to fight against deeply ingrained organisational behaviour — and the fight was never fully won.

The chapter includes a broader discussion of what management systems must do.
They must create **regular cadence** for testing strategy against reality — without it, strategies drift.
They must create **aligned measures** — without them, people optimise for the wrong things.
They must create **communication norms** — without them, problems go unaddressed until they become crises.
They must create **talent development systems** — without them, the organisation cannot develop the strategists needed to sustain advantage over time.

## Chapter 7: Think Through Strategy — The Strategy Logic Flow

This chapter provides the analytical toolkit for developing where-to-play and how-to-win choices.
While the cascade tells you what questions to ask, the **Strategy Logic Flow** tells you how to generate and evaluate the answers.

Martin presents seven analytical questions organised across four dimensions:

1. **Industry analysis** — What is the structure and attractiveness of the industry or segment you are considering?
What are the competitive forces?
Where are the profit pools?
2. **Customer value analysis** — What do channel customers (distributors, retailers) and end consumers value?
What are the unmet needs?
What trade-offs are they making?
3. **Relative position analysis** — How does the company fare against competitors on the dimensions that matter?
What are its relative strengths and weaknesses?
4. **Competitive reaction analysis** — What will competitors do in response to your strategic choices?
Will they respond aggressively, passively, or not at all?

The key insight is that most organisations over-analyse.
They conduct comprehensive SWOT analyses, generate mountains of data, and still fail to make a clear strategic choice.
The Strategy Logic Flow is designed to prevent this by focusing analysis on the specific questions that drive the where-to-play and how-to-win decision.
Analysis that does not change the answer to one of the four questions is wasted effort.

Martin illustrates this with examples from P&G's R&D portfolio reviews.
Instead of reviewing every project against a generic scorecard, Martin pushed the teams to ask: which of these projects, if successful, would materially change our where-to-play or how-to-win position?
Projects that would not change the strategic position — even if they were technically interesting — were deprioritised.
Projects that could shift the competitive landscape received disproportionate investment.
The Logic Flow transformed R&D from a capability looking for applications into a strategic weapon aimed at specific competitive outcomes.

## Chapter 8: Shorten Your Odds — Reverse Engineering Strategic Choices

This is the book's most immediately actionable chapter and, by many readers' accounts, its most valuable contribution.
The reverse-engineering process takes the strategy logic flow and turns it into a structured decision-making methodology.

The core idea is deceptively simple: instead of arguing about which strategic option is best, ask **"What would have to be true for this option to be a great choice?"**
This single question transforms strategy conversations from unproductive advocacy ("I'm right, you're wrong") into productive inquiry ("under what conditions would each option succeed?").

Martin presents a **seven-step process**:

1. **Frame the choice** as mutually exclusive options.
An issue cannot be resolved until it is framed as an explicit choice between at least two real alternatives.
Until alternatives are articulated, teams talk endlessly without making progress.

2. **Generate diverse strategic possibilities** — not just two options but several.
The more creative the range of possibilities, the more likely the final choice captures real strategic insight.

3. **For each possibility, specify what would have to be true** for it to be a great choice.
This shifts the conversation from "is this true?" to "what would NEED to be true?" — a far less threatening question that opens rather than closes minds.

4. **Identify which conditions are least likely to hold** — these are the real barriers.
Not every condition matters equally.
Some are near-certain; others are speculative.
The barriers are the conditions that are both critical to the option's success and uncertain in their truth.

5. **Design tests for those barriers — and let the biggest sceptic design the test for each condition.**
This is the genius of the process.
When the person most resistant to an option controls the standard of proof, two things happen.
First, the test is more rigorous than any advocate would design.
Second, if the test passes, the sceptic is genuinely committed to the outcome — because they controlled the criteria.

6. **Conduct tests, starting with the biggest barrier first** (what Martin calls "the lazy person's approach").
If the biggest barrier fails, you do not need to test the rest — the option is eliminated, saving time and resources.

7. **Choose** based on test results.

Martin illustrates the process with a story from his consulting days.
A **mining company's** leadership team was deadlocked between two options for an underperforming mine: shut it down or invest heavily in modernisation.
Both sides had been arguing for months, each presenting data that supported their position and dismissing data that contradicted it.
The conversation was going nowhere.
Martin intervened by asking each side to specify what would have to be true for the OTHER side's option to be a great choice.
The shutdown advocates had to articulate the conditions under which investment would work.
The investment advocates had to articulate the conditions under which shutdown would be best.
This simple reframing unlocked the conversation within hours.
Both sides could engage with the other option's conditions without feeling they were conceding their position.
The team identified two critical barriers, designed tests for each, and reached a decision that both sides committed to — all in a fraction of the time they had already spent arguing.

The chapter also returns to the **Olay masstige decision** as a reverse-engineering case study.
The team identified three critical barriers that would need to hold for the masstige strategy to succeed: (1) P&G's R&D could actually produce a product that matched prestige efficacy at a mass price point, (2) mass-market retailers would give the product premium shelf positioning, and (3) consumers aged 35+ would actually pay $18-25 for a skin-care product in a drugstore.
Each condition was tested with increasing rigour.
The R&D test involved blind product comparisons against prestige competitors.
The retail test involved conversations with key retail partners.
The consumer test involved concept testing and in-market pilots.
All three barriers were cleared, and the team moved forward with confidence.

The **"mutually assured destruction" dynamic** that makes the sceptic-designs-the-test principle work is worth understanding in detail.
Martin explains that the process creates a natural check on gaming.
If you set an unfair standard for my option, I will do the same for yours.
In practice, this mutual accountability pushes all participants toward fair, rigorous testing.
No one wants to be the person who killed their own option by setting a precedent of unfair tests.
The result is a testing process that all parties trust — which is the precondition for genuine commitment to the outcome.

## Assertive Inquiry

Woven through several chapters but discussed most explicitly in Chapter 6, **assertive inquiry** is the communication discipline that Lafley and Martin argue is essential for productive strategic conversation.

The concept comes from Chris Argyris's organisational learning theory.
Argyris observed that most organisations are trapped in what he called **Model I behaviour**: advocate your position, defend it against challenge, control the conversation, and suppress information that contradicts your view.
The result is entrenchment, not clarity.
Model I produces meetings where the loudest voice wins, where dissent is punished, and where the organisation's best information never makes it to the surface.

Argyris proposed **Model II behaviour** as the alternative: advocate your position AND inquire into others', test your assumptions publicly, and treat disagreement as a learning opportunity.
Lafley and Martin operationalise this as assertive inquiry — the stance of "I have a view worth hearing, but I may be missing something."

The discipline has two moves.
First, **state your position clearly**, including the reasoning behind it.
This is the "assertive" part.
Do not soften your view into meaninglessness.
Do not hide behind questions when you actually have a strong opinion.
Put your thinking on the table so others can engage with it.

Second, **ask genuine questions** — not rhetorical questions designed to lead others to your conclusion, but questions whose answers might actually change your mind.
This is the "inquiry" part.
"What am I not seeing?" is a genuine question.
"Don't you think we should focus on the consumer?" is not.

Lafley describes embedding assertive inquiry across P&G's strategy processes, from board reviews to brand-team discussions.
The norms shifted from "sell your strategy and defend it" to "share your thinking and explore alternatives together."
He credits this shift with improving the quality of strategic choices across the organisation, though he acknowledges it took years and was never fully adopted.

The reason assertive inquiry matters for strategy is that strategy requires the best available information, and the best available information is always distributed across many people.
Pure advocacy — where each person fights for their view — ensures that only the most politically powerful information surfaces.
Pure inquiry — where no one commits to a position — ensures that no one takes responsibility for pushing the conversation forward.
Assertive inquiry produces better decisions because more information enters the conversation and less gets suppressed by hierarchy or politics.

## The Six Strategy Traps

In the conclusion, Lafley and Martin identify six common patterns of strategic failure, all of which stem from the same root cause: the failure to make genuine choices.

1. **The Do-It-All Strategy** — Everything is a priority.
Every market is served, every product is invested in, every opportunity is pursued.
The organisation is busy everywhere and winning nowhere.
This is the most common trap and the most seductive, because it feels responsible — surely it would be irresponsible NOT to pursue a promising opportunity?
But resources are finite, and spreading them evenly across all possibilities guarantees that no position reaches the threshold of competitive advantage.

2. **The Don Quixote Strategy** — Attacking the strongest competitor head-on in their strongest segment without a genuine advantage.
Don Quixote charged at windmills he believed were giants.
The strategic equivalent is a company that attacks the market leader in the leader's strongest segment, without a differentiated how-to-win, and is surprised when the leader's entrenched advantages overwhelm it.
P&G's Citrus Hill orange juice — attacking Tropicana and Minute Maid in their core market — is the book's example.

3. **The Waterloo Strategy** — Fighting on too many fronts simultaneously.
Napoleon's defeat at Waterloo was preceded by years of overextension — too many campaigns, too many enemies, too few resources concentrated at any single point.
The business equivalent is the company that enters multiple new markets, launches multiple new products, and restructures its organisation simultaneously, exceeding its capacity to execute effectively on any single front.

4. **The Something-for-Everyone Strategy** — Trying to serve all customer segments simultaneously, satisfying none deeply.
This is a where-to-play failure: refusing to choose which customers to serve and which to walk away from.
The result is a generic offering that creates no differentiation and commands no loyalty.

5. **The Dreams-That-Never-Come-True Strategy** — Grand aspirations with no concrete where-to-play or how-to-win choices beneath them.
The organisation has a beautiful vision statement and a stirring mission, but no one can explain what specific choices those aspirations require.
The gap between aspiration and action is filled with hope, not strategy.

6. **The Program-of-the-Month Strategy** — Adopting whatever management trend is currently fashionable — Six Sigma, digital transformation, agile, AI — without connecting it to a clear strategic choice.
The result is a strategy that looks identical to every competitor's, because every competitor is adopting the same fashionable programme.
Differentiation is impossible when your strategy is a copy of someone else's playbook.

## The Six Signs of Winning

How do you know your strategy is working?
Lafley and Martin offer six diagnostic indicators:

1. **Your activity system looks different from competitors'.**
If your operations, capabilities, and investment patterns look like those of your major competitors, you do not have a strategy — you have a shared industry recipe.
Genuine strategy produces a distinctive operating model that others cannot or will not replicate.

2. **Customers adore you — and non-customers genuinely cannot understand why.**
Strong differentiation creates polarisation.
Loyal customers become advocates who would not dream of switching.
Non-customers look at the offering and think it is overpriced, unnecessary, or confusing.
This polarisation is a SIGN of strength, not weakness.
Universal appeal usually means weak differentiation.

3. **Competitors make good profits doing what they do.**
A winning strategy does not require competitors to fail.
It creates a space where you win and competitors can still thrive — by doing something different.
If you find yourself in a zero-sum war where your gain requires your competitor's loss, you are probably fighting on the same field with the same weapons, which means you have not made a genuine strategic choice.

4. **You have more resources to spend than competitors on an ongoing basis.**
The margin advantage from a winning strategy compounds over time.
Higher margins fund more R&D, which produces better products, which command higher margins.
Lower margins force competitors into a resource squeeze, limiting their ability to invest in the future.
This flywheel is the mechanism through which strategic advantage becomes self-sustaining.

5. **Competitors attack each other, not you.**
When you have defined a distinctive space, competitors recognise they cannot contest it effectively.
Instead, they compete among themselves for the remaining market positions.
If all your competitors are attacking you, it may feel flattering, but it means you are fighting on a field that others believe they can win — which raises questions about your strategic positioning.

6. **Customers look to you first for innovation.**
When customers expect you to define the future of the category — when they come to you before anyone else with their toughest problems and their most ambitious ideas — you own the future of the space in their minds.
This is the strongest possible market position, because it means your competitors are, at best, following your lead.

## The Verdict

*Playing to Win* is the clearest, most actionable strategy framework published in the last two decades.
Its greatest contribution is ruthless simplicity: five questions, two ways to win, and a reverse-engineering process that works in virtually any context.
The cascade gives strategists a shared vocabulary that cuts through the fog of corporate jargon, and the "what would have to be true?" methodology provides a concrete tool for turning contentious debates into productive conversations.
The P&G case studies are genuinely compelling — this is not theory divorced from practice but a framework tested across an $80B+ company over a decade of measurable results, during a period when most large companies were losing ground.

The book has real limitations that an honest reader should weigh.
The case material is drawn almost entirely from P&G, creating significant survivorship bias — the framework is illustrated only by its successes, with failures relegated to an appendix that few readers will study carefully.
The "two ways to win" binary (cost leadership or differentiation) oversimplifies reality; the authors themselves acknowledge that Toyota and others blend both, but they do not adequately address how a strategist should think when the answer is genuinely "both."
The microeconomic appendix is rigorous but narrow — it treats competitive advantage as if it were purely a function of cost and willingness-to-pay, ignoring network effects, platform dynamics, and ecosystem lock-in that drive many of today's most important competitive battles.

The most significant gap is the absence of political dynamics.
The book describes strategic decision-making as if it happens in a rational, collegial vacuum where the best argument wins.
Even Lafley's own anonymous contributor admits that the old sell-and-defend culture persisted because people were being evaluated by the person across the table.
Strategy does not happen in a vacuum; it happens in a political environment where stakeholders have conflicting interests, hidden agendas, and varying degrees of power.
The book offers no guidance for navigating these realities — it assumes the reader has the authority to simply choose, which is true for CEOs and division presidents but not for the vast majority of strategists who must influence choices they do not control.

Despite these gaps, anyone who internalises the five questions and the discipline of "what would have to be true?" will think more clearly about competition, positioning, and resource allocation than most of their peers.
The book is most valuable for mid-career and senior leaders who need a practical framework for articulating and testing strategic choices — and it is most powerful when combined with an understanding of organisational politics and influence that the authors themselves do not provide.
Read alongside Porter's *Competitive Advantage* for deeper economic foundations, and alongside something that addresses the human and political dimensions of organisational decision-making for a more complete picture.
