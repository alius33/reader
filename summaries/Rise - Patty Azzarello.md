# Rise — Patty Azzarello

> Patty Azzarello's *Rise* is a blunt corrective to the belief that hard work speaks for itself. Her thesis: the most talented people stall because they burn all their energy on execution and neglect visibility and relationships. Advancing requires deliberate, simultaneous progress across three dimensions — delivering exceptional results on the *right* things (DO Better), building credibility with the people who matter (LOOK Better), and assembling a network of mentors and allies (CONNECT Better). Drawing on her own trajectory from engineer to CEO by 38, Azzarello provides a practical, sometimes uncomfortable framework for the reality that merit alone is never sufficient. The book is at its best when it names the traps that hardworking people fall into — the workhorse who gets exploited, the expert who cannot communicate value, the lone wolf who has no champions in the rooms where decisions are made.

---

## About the Author

Patty Azzarello rose through Hewlett-Packard to become the youngest General Manager in the company at 33.
She ran a $1 billion software business at 35 and was a CEO at 38.
Her career trajectory was not smooth or linear — she took roles she felt unqualified for, navigated hostile political environments, managed teams in areas where she had no technical expertise, and learned many of the book's principles through painful failure before codifying them.
Her perspective is shaped by large-company corporate politics, product and marketing leadership, and the particular dynamics of Silicon Valley tech advancement from the 1990s through the 2010s.
After her corporate career, she became a leadership consultant and executive coach, working with Fortune 500 leaders on the same problems she once navigated herself.
The book reads like field notes from someone who climbed the ladder, looked back, and realised that the ladder was not where most people thought it was.

## The Big Idea

Azzarello's central argument is that career advancement is a **three-legged stool**.
Most people obsess over one leg — execution — and wonder why they topple over.

The three legs are **DO Better** (work on the right things at the right level), **LOOK Better** (build credibility and visibility with stakeholders), and **CONNECT Better** (build mentors, allies, and access to decision-makers).
Neglect any one and your career stalls, no matter how strong the other two are.

The uncomfortable implication is that working harder on execution is often the *wrong* response to a stalled career.
If nobody who matters sees your work, if no one is championing you in rooms you are not in, and if you have no relationships with the people who decide promotions — then more hours at your desk will only dig the hole deeper.

The model also explains why certain people who seem less talented keep getting ahead.
It is not that they are gaming the system — or rather, it is not *only* that.
It is that they are investing in all three legs while their more diligent colleagues are pouring everything into one.
The brilliant engineer who never talks to senior leadership, the tireless project manager who has no mentor, the subject-matter expert who cannot translate their work into business language — all of these people have the same problem.
They are doing a third of the job and wondering why they get a third of the reward.

The shift Azzarello demands is from personal output to **outcomes created through leadership, strategic focus, and network leverage**.
This is not a book about working less.
It is a book about working on the right things — and ensuring the right people know about it.

## Key Concepts at a Glance

- **The DO-LOOK-CONNECT Model** — three interdependent pillars that must advance simultaneously; neglecting any one produces a specific failure mode (workhorse, self-promoter, or disconnected politician)
- **Ruthless Priorities** — select 1-3 "Can't Fail" outcomes that have the biggest business impact and tolerate risk on everything else; trying to do everything equally guarantees mediocrity across the board
- **The Workhorse Trap** — being known for tireless personal output makes you valuable as a workhorse, not promotable as a leader; the organisation has no incentive to promote its best execution resource
- **The Level Dilemma** — each promotion fundamentally changes what "good performance" means; the habits that made you excellent at one level will make you a failure at the next
- **Inside Voice vs Outside Voice** — your internal team jargon is invisible to stakeholders; translate your work into the vocabulary your audience already cares about, or you are by definition irrelevant
- **The List** — for any desirable role, an informal shortlist exists before applications open; if you are not on it, your qualifications are irrelevant, because the filter happens before evaluation begins
- **The Experience Paradox** — you cannot get the job without the experience, but you can get the experience without the job; proactively seek out work at the level you aspire to
- **Credibility as Infrastructure** — credibility is inversely proportional to organisational obstacles; high credibility removes friction, low credibility creates it, and neither is accidental
- **Personal Brand** — people form impressions whether you manage them or not; define what you want to be known for and practise those behaviours consistently until they stick
- **Energy Management** — burnout is not a badge of honour but a failure to protect your most critical resource; more energy multiplies time, and a well-rested leader working 40 strategic hours outperforms an exhausted one working 70 tactical hours
- **The Extra Team** — people who do not report to you but contribute to your outcomes; the most successful people get the most help
- **Fearlessness Over Confidence** — confidence comes from experience, but you cannot get the experience without the leap; the cycle breaks only through willingness to act before you feel ready

## Part I: DO Better — Ruthless Priorities and the Right Level of Work

The first pillar is not about working harder.
It is about working on the **right things** at the **right altitude**.
Azzarello argues that most hardworking professionals are drowning not in laziness but in undifferentiated effort — treating everything on their plate as equally important, saying yes to every request, and mistaking busyness for impact.
The DO Better section is a sustained argument for strategic ruthlessness about where you invest your time.

### Chapter 1: Rise Above the Work

Azzarello opens the book with the story that crystallises her entire thesis.
Early in her career, she took on the turnaround of a struggling R&D organisation.
She worked punishing hours, rebuilt processes, improved delivery, and by any objective measure, rescued the division.
At the end of the year, she received no raise, no bonus, and no recognition.
Her mentor's feedback was three devastating words: **"Nobody knows you."**

She had done everything right inside the building and nothing right outside it.
The people who made compensation and promotion decisions had no idea who she was or what she had accomplished.
She had confused personal output with organisational impact — and the organisation, predictably, ignored what it could not see.

The principle that emerges from this story runs through the entire book: **delivering work is not the same as adding value**.
Managers are paid for the quality of outcomes they create, not the hours they invest.
A twenty-minute walk that produces a strategic insight can add more value than a twelve-hour day of email triage.
But most people never take the walk, because the email feels urgent and the walk feels indulgent.

Azzarello makes a crucial distinction between working *in* the business and working *on* the business.
Working in the business means doing tasks — writing reports, attending meetings, responding to requests, handling escalations.
Working on the business means stepping back to ask: What are the three things that will matter most six months from now?
Am I spending my time on those things, or am I spending it on everything else?

The shift she demands is uncomfortable: you must accept that some things will not get done, some people will be disappointed, and some balls will drop.
The alternative — doing everything at a mediocre level — is worse, because mediocre performance across the board produces neither forgiveness nor standout results.

### Chapter 2: Ruthless Priorities — Choose the Can't Fails

This is where Azzarello gets specific.
She introduces the concept of **"Can't Fail" outcomes** — the 1-3 things with the biggest business impact that you must deliver at an exceptional level.
Everything else gets managed for acceptable risk, not excellence.

The psychology behind this is counterintuitive.
Most people resist prioritisation because they fear being blamed for what they drop.
But Azzarello argues that remarkable success on the critical few items generates **forgiveness** for gaps on the rest.
The inverse — mediocre performance across everything — generates neither forgiveness nor praise.
**"Being forgiven requires actually succeeding,"** she writes.

She tells the story of a COO who inherited a sprawling agenda of infrastructure, technology, and operational projects.
Rather than spreading resources thinly, he identified one critical initiative — rolling out a new point-of-sale system that would open an entirely new revenue channel.
He delayed infrastructure improvements, deferred technology upgrades, and concentrated his team on the single project that would transform the business.
The point-of-sale system launched on time and exceeded revenue projections.
The delayed projects were eventually completed with no lasting damage.
The COO was promoted.
Had he tried to do everything simultaneously, Azzarello argues, nothing would have been remarkable and nothing would have failed spectacularly — the worst possible outcome for a career.

A second story illustrates the inverse.
A software company was haemorrhaging customers due to quality problems while also trying to add new features to attract new customers.
The CEO made the ruthless call to freeze all new feature development and redirect every engineer to quality and stability.
The product team was furious — they saw their roadmap gutted.
But the existing customer base stabilised, churn stopped, and the company survived.
Trying to do both — new features and quality improvement — with limited resources would have achieved neither.

Azzarello introduces the **21-times rule** to support ruthless priorities: a message must be repeated roughly twenty-one times before it truly lands with an organisation.
Once you have chosen your Can't Fail outcomes, you must overcommunicate them — in meetings, in emails, in one-on-ones, in town halls — until people stop asking you to do other things because they already know what you are focused on.
This creates what she calls **social norms** around your priorities: the organisation learns that requesting your time for non-priority work will be declined, and it stops asking.

The practical mechanics: list all current work, ask "How bad is it if we fail?" for each item, and let the answer reveal the true Can't Fails.
Then ratify them with your boss — not as a unilateral declaration, but as a negotiated agreement.
Without your boss's buy-in, ruthless prioritisation looks like insubordination.

### Chapter 3: Energy Management

Azzarello interrupts the execution discussion with a chapter that feels like it belongs in a different book — until you realise it is the foundation for everything else.

Her argument: **energy is your most finite resource**, more so than time.
You can always find more hours (skip lunch, work weekends, wake up earlier).
You cannot find more energy.
And a burned-out person working seventy hours produces less value than a well-rested person working forty, because the quality of thought, the quality of decisions, and the quality of relationships all degrade when energy is depleted.

Azzarello has an unusual vantage point here.
She has a health condition that limited her working hours for much of her career.
Rather than seeing this as a handicap, she frames it as a competitive advantage: because she could not outwork her peers, she was forced to outthink them.
She had to be ruthless about which meetings to attend, which problems to solve personally, and which hours of the day to protect for strategic thinking.
The result was that she consistently outperformed people who worked longer hours, because her hours were better.

She introduces the concept of **prime time** — the window during the day when you think most clearly.
For most people, this is the first two to three hours of the morning.
Azzarello's prescription is fierce: **never spend prime time on email, low-value meetings, or administrative work**.
Protect it for strategic thinking, difficult problems, and creative work.
Move email and meetings to your low-energy hours.

A story she uses to illustrate: a director she coached was working twelve-hour days but feeling increasingly ineffective.
When they audited his calendar, they found that his mornings — his clearest thinking time — were consumed by standing meetings and inbox triage.
By the time he got to strategic work, it was 3pm and his brain was spent.
They restructured his calendar to protect mornings for deep work and batch meetings into the afternoon.
Within a month, he reported feeling like he had gained an extra day per week — not because his hours changed, but because the alignment of energy to task had changed.

She also makes the broader argument that energy is not just about sleep and exercise.
Resentment drains energy.
Workhorse mode drains energy.
Doing work you know is low-value drains energy.
Conversely, doing meaningful work, feeling visible, and having supportive relationships all *build* energy.
The DO-LOOK-CONNECT model is itself an energy argument: people who are stuck in DO-only mode are often exhausted precisely because they are doing the wrong work with no recognition and no support.

### Chapters 4-5: The Workhorse Trap

This is Azzarello's sharpest diagnostic, and she gives it extended treatment.

The **workhorse trap** works like this: you are smart, diligent, and responsive.
People learn that if they give you a problem, you will solve it.
So they give you more problems.
You solve those too.
Your reputation becomes "the person who gets things done."
This sounds like a compliment.
It is a cage.

The mechanism: the organisation has no incentive to promote its best workhorse.
Doing so would remove its most productive execution resource.
The workhorse is someone whose value can be replaced by a **"black box"** — the organisation cares about the throughput, not the person.
If a machine could do your job as well as you do it, your value is in the function, not in your leadership.
Leaders are promoted because they demonstrate they can build systems, develop people, and think strategically.
Workhorses are kept because they are useful exactly where they are.

Azzarello tells the story of a supply chain manager who was brilliant at crisis resolution.
Every time inventory went wrong, every time a supplier failed, every time a shipment was delayed — he fixed it.
He worked nights and weekends.
He was the first call for every emergency.
He was also passed over for promotion three times.
Why?
Because his value was entirely personal.
He was the system.
If he was promoted and moved to a different role, the supply chain would collapse.
The organisation could not afford to promote him.

The escape came when he shifted his approach.
Instead of personally resolving the next crisis, he built a prioritisation framework — a system that categorised inventory issues by severity and routed them to the appropriate team member with clear escalation criteria.
He trained his team to use it.
He stopped being the first call.
Within six months, the supply chain ran more smoothly than it ever had under his personal heroics, and he had freed himself to work on strategic initiatives.
He was promoted within the year — not because he had become less useful, but because he had demonstrated a fundamentally different kind of value: the ability to build capability rather than provide it personally.

A contrasting story: a marketing director who was an exceptional analyst.
She could dissect any campaign, identify exactly what was working, and produce reports that were the envy of the department.
Senior leadership loved her analysis.
They also never considered her for VP roles.
When she asked why, the answer was revealing: "We need you doing what you are doing."
Her analytical excellence had made her indispensable *at her current level*.
The skills that made her valuable — personal analytical throughput — were individual contributor skills, not leadership skills.
She was the best player on the field, but nobody saw her as a coach.

Azzarello's prescription is not to work less, but to **shift what you work on**.
Stop solving problems; start building systems that solve problems.
Stop doing the work; start building the team that does the work.
Stop being the expert; start developing experts.
The transition is painful because it feels like you are abandoning the thing you are best at.
You are.
That is the point.

### Chapter 6: The Level Dilemma — What "Good" Means Changes

This chapter addresses the transition problem directly.
Each time you step up, the definition of "good performance" changes completely — and most people fail transitions because they keep performing at the old level's definition of excellence.

Azzarello maps three levels:
- **Individual contributors** are valued for expertise and personal output.
They are the people who do the work.
- **Managers** are valued for team outcomes and delegation.
They are the people who get work done through others.
- **Executives** are valued for strategy, network leverage, and building organisational capability.
They are the people who build the machines that get work done through others.

The transition from each level to the next requires *abandoning* the behaviours that made you successful at the previous level.
The brilliant engineer who becomes a manager and still writes all the code is not demonstrating dedication — they are failing to do the new job.
The exceptional manager who becomes a VP and still runs every team meeting is not showing leadership — they are competing with their own subordinates.

Azzarello tells the story of **Natalie**, a regional sales director who personally reviewed every proposal her team submitted.
Natalie was meticulous, experienced, and had excellent instincts about what clients wanted.
Her reviews improved every proposal.
But Natalie was working at the wrong level.
By spending her time on proposal review — work her team should have been doing — she was failing to do the things only she could do: building relationships with key accounts, developing her team's capability, and thinking about regional strategy.
Her team was not growing because they never had to.
Natalie caught every mistake before it reached the client, which meant her team never learned from mistakes.
She was the best individual contributor on her team — and the worst leader.

The lesson: your value shifts from what you personally produce to what you **cause to happen** through others.
If you cling to the detail, you compete with your own subordinates, fail to grow the team, miss strategic opportunities, and signal that you are not ready for a bigger role.

Azzarello frames this as a question every leader should ask regularly: **"Am I working ON the business or IN the business?"**
If the answer is mostly "in," you are at the wrong altitude.

### Chapter 7: Delegation as Value Creation

Delegation gets its own chapter because Azzarello sees it as the primary mechanism for escaping both the workhorse trap and the level dilemma.

Her core reframe: **delegation is not about offloading tasks you do not want to do**.
It is about building capability in your team so the highest-value work gets done at the right levels.
If you have ten people on your team, developing each of them to work at your level creates ten times more value than adding yourself as an eleventh worker at their level.

She learned this lesson in an unusual way.
Early in her career, she was given responsibility for an R&D organisation that worked on technology she did not understand.
She could not do the team's work even if she wanted to — she lacked the technical knowledge.
She was forced to lead at the right level: setting direction, removing obstacles, coaching people through problems, and trusting the team to deliver.
The result surprised her.
Her team told her she was the best manager they had ever had.
Not because she was the smartest person in the room — she was not — but because she gave them room to grow, held them accountable for outcomes, and focused her energy on clearing their path rather than doing their work.

Azzarello codifies this into **eight keys to effective delegation**:

1. **Resist the temptation to do it yourself.** The work will be imperfect.
Accept that 80% quality from a team member is better than 100% quality from you, because it frees you for higher-value work.
2. **No guilt about giving people work.** Delegation is not exploitation.
It is development.
You are giving people opportunities to grow.
3. **Do not cover for poor work.** If someone does a bad job, address it directly.
Covering for them teaches nothing and enables mediocrity.
4. **Do not kill planned hires by absorbing their work yourself.** If you need to hire someone but keep doing their work in the meantime, the organisation will conclude you do not actually need the hire.
5. **Keep outcome ownership.** Delegate the task, not the accountability.
You are still responsible for the result.
6. **Let the delegate define their own measures and milestones.** People are more committed to plans they create than plans they receive.
7. **Inspect and measure regularly.** Delegation is not the same as abandonment.
Check in, review progress, course-correct.
8. **Always be teaching.** Every delegated task is a teaching opportunity.
Explain why the work matters, share context, and debrief afterward.

The critical distinction she draws: **delegation is not the same as abandonment**.
Handing off a task and hoping for the best is as damaging as micromanagement.
Effective delegation requires clear outcomes, regular inspection, and ongoing coaching.
The midpoint between micromanagement and abandonment is where leadership lives.

She also introduces the idea of **delegating to a process** when you cannot delegate to a person.
If no one on your team can handle a particular task, you can still systematise it — create a checklist, a decision tree, a template — so that the task no longer requires your personal judgment every time.
The process becomes the delegate.

## Part II: LOOK Better — Credibility, Visibility, and Personal Brand

The second pillar addresses the gap between doing good work and being known for doing good work.
Azzarello is blunt: **good work does not stand on its own**.
If the people who make decisions do not know your name, your results are invisible.
And invisible results, no matter how impressive, produce exactly zero career advancement.

This section is where Azzarello's advice is most uncomfortable for people who believe that self-promotion is distasteful.
She does not deny that it can be.
She argues that the alternative — doing great work in silence and hoping someone notices — is not modesty.
It is career negligence.

### Chapter 8: "Do What You Love" Is Bad Advice

Azzarello opens the LOOK Better section with a provocative contrarian argument.
She challenges the popular wisdom that you should follow your passion and the career will follow.

Her counterargument: passion without strategic positioning produces a starving artist, not a successful professional.
The people who advance are not necessarily doing what they love — they are doing what the organisation values, and they are doing it visibly.
Love your work if you can, she says, but do not use "doing what you love" as an excuse to avoid the uncomfortable work of visibility and positioning.

The nuance she introduces is the distinction between *loving the work* and *loving the outcomes*.
You may not love the process of stakeholder management, but you can love the outcome of having influence and resources.
Effective professionals learn to love the game, not just the craft — and part of the game is making sure the right people see you playing it well.

### Chapter 9: Credibility as Infrastructure

**Credibility** is not a soft concept in Azzarello's framework — it is a concrete organisational asset, as tangible as budget or headcount.
She introduces a powerful inverse formulation: **"Credibility is inversely proportional to obstacles."**

High credibility means fewer questions, faster approvals, better resources, and the benefit of the doubt when things go wrong.
When a credible leader proposes an initiative, people say "yes" faster, allocate resources more willingly, and forgive mistakes more readily.
Low credibility means endless defence, blocked budgets, and invisible contributions.
When a leader with low credibility proposes the same initiative, people ask for more data, question the assumptions, and attribute any failure to incompetence rather than circumstance.

The mechanism is self-reinforcing.
High credibility reduces friction, which makes it easier to deliver results, which builds more credibility.
Low credibility increases friction, which makes it harder to deliver results, which further erodes credibility.
The rich get richer and the poor get poorer — not because of talent differences, but because of credibility differences.

Azzarello tells the story of a VP of engineering who had built an exceptional product but could never get budget for the initiatives he proposed.
Every budget cycle, he would present detailed technical proposals that demonstrated the value of his projects.
And every cycle, his proposals would be deprioritised in favour of proposals from other VPs.
The problem was not his proposals.
The problem was that the executives who controlled the budget did not know him, did not understand what his team did, and had no reason to trust his judgment.
He had credibility with his team — but not with the people who mattered.

The fix was not a better proposal.
It was a six-month credibility campaign: meeting with each budget decision-maker individually, learning what they cared about, translating his team's work into their vocabulary, and demonstrating relevance to their priorities.
When the next budget cycle came, his proposals sailed through — not because they were better, but because the people reviewing them now trusted the person behind them.

Credibility is built by being **relevant to business outcomes**, not by educating people about your function.
The moment you find yourself explaining what your team does and why it matters, you have already lost.
**"If you need to educate them, you are not relevant,"** Azzarello writes.

### Chapter 10: Inside Voice vs Outside Voice

This is one of the book's most immediately useful concepts, and Azzarello develops it with several examples.

Your **"inside voice"** is the language you use with your team — functional jargon, internal metrics, technical vocabulary.
It is precise, efficient, and completely incomprehensible to anyone outside your function.
Your **"outside voice"** is business outcomes language — the vocabulary that stakeholders, executives, and clients already use and care about.

The principle: **always translate your work into the vocabulary of the person you are talking to**.
If you need to educate someone about what you do, you are by definition not relevant to them — you are a foreign object in their world, requiring effort to understand.
Relevance is a gift; education is a tax.

Azzarello tells the story of a marketing VP who consistently struggled to get budget approval.
His presentations were excellent by marketing standards — detailed campaign analytics, click-through rates, engagement metrics, conversion funnels.
But the CFO who controlled the budget did not care about click-through rates.
The CFO cared about revenue growth, customer acquisition cost, and margin.
The marketing VP was speaking his inside voice to an audience that only heard outside voice.
When he learned to translate — "Our Q3 campaign reduced customer acquisition cost by 14% and contributed $2.3 million in incremental revenue" instead of "Our click-through rate improved 23% and engagement is up across all segments" — the budget conversations transformed.

The practical method Azzarello recommends is what she calls **"replaying the tape"**.
Interview your stakeholders about their business.
Listen for the specific words they use to describe their priorities, challenges, and goals.
Then use those exact words when you present your work to them.
The stakeholder hears their own language coming back to them and immediately registers you as relevant, aligned, and worth supporting.

She extends this to written communication: emails to senior executives should never require the reader to translate.
If a sentence contains jargon that is specific to your function, rewrite it.
If a metric is meaningful only within your team, either translate it to a business metric or leave it out.
The test is simple: would a senior executive in a different function understand every word?
If not, you are speaking inside voice.

### Chapter 11: Personal Brand — Consistency Over Brilliance

People form a specific impression of you whether you manage it or not.
Azzarello's advice is to take deliberate control of that process rather than leaving it to chance.

Her method: define **3-5 attributes** you want to be known for, identify the **"brandable behaviours"** that convey each attribute, and practise those behaviours consistently in every interaction.

She uses a striking example of what she calls **"applied clever."**
A leader she coached chose "clever" as one of his brand attributes.
Rather than trying to seem clever through intellectual showmanship, he identified specific brandable behaviours: asking the unexpected question in meetings, finding the non-obvious connection between two problems, and offering reframes that shifted how people thought about challenges.
He practised these behaviours deliberately until they became habitual.
Over time, people started describing him as "the clever one" — not because he announced it, but because he had built the pattern through consistent behaviour.

**"Brand equals consistent behaviours over time."**
People grant your brand based on their experience of you, not what you claim about yourself.

Azzarello makes a counterintuitive observation that deserves emphasis: **consistency builds trust, and inconsistency destroys it faster than bad behaviour does**.
It is better to be consistently mediocre than inconsistently excellent, because inconsistency creates anxiety and unpredictability in the people around you.
When people cannot predict how you will show up — brilliant one day, distracted the next — they stop trusting you, even when your best days are extraordinary.
Predictability is a form of respect for the people you work with.

Her practical process: gather feedback from 5-15 people on what they see as "always true" about you.
Look for patterns.
These patterns are your brand, whether you chose them or not.
If the patterns do not match what you want to be known for, the gap is your brand development work.
Define the attributes you want, identify the behaviours that would convey them, and begin practising — understanding that brand-building is measured in months, not days.

### Chapter 12: Stakeholder Management — Be Visible, Not Annoying

Promotions happen in rooms you are not in.
If the people in that room do not know your name, your boss cannot sell you for the role.
Decision-makers default to candidates they know, even if unknown candidates are more talented.
This is not corruption — it is human cognition.
People trust what is familiar.

Azzarello recommends creating a **stakeholder communication plan** — a deliberate map of who matters, what they care about, and how often you should be in front of them.
This includes your boss, your boss's peers, your boss's boss, key clients, and people she calls **"influencers"** — people who may not have formal decision-making authority but whose opinions carry weight in the rooms where decisions are made.

She tells the story of her own visibility transformation.
After the "nobody knows you" wake-up call early in her career, she created a list of thirty executives she needed to know.
For each, she identified what they cared about, what she could offer them, and a plan for getting in front of them.
Over six months, she systematically built relationships with every person on that list.
The result was not just visibility — it was a network of people who understood her value and could advocate for her when opportunities arose.

One specific technique she introduces is the **"7-minute meeting"**.
Instead of requesting an hour with a senior executive — which will never get scheduled because executives guard their calendars fiercely — ask for seven minutes.
The brevity is disarming.
The time commitment is trivial.
And a sharp seven-minute conversation, where you come prepared with a specific question or insight relevant to their priorities, creates more impact than a diluted hour ever could.

The 7-minute meeting works because it inverts the usual dynamic.
Most people who request executive time want something from the executive: a decision, a resource, an approval.
The 7-minute meeting offers something *to* the executive: a relevant insight, a connection to something they care about, or a question that demonstrates you are thinking at their level.
When you offer value in a compact format, executives remember you — and they start saying yes when you ask for time.

Azzarello warns against the most common mistake in stakeholder management: **communicating without relevance**.
An irrelevant touchpoint is worse than silence, because it teaches the stakeholder to tune you out.
Every interaction must answer the stakeholder's implicit question: "Why should I care about this?"
If you cannot answer that question, do not have the meeting.

## Part III: CONNECT Better — Mentors, the Extra Team, and Getting on the List

The third pillar addresses the network of relationships that makes advancement structurally possible.
Azzarello's position is direct: **the most successful people get the most help**, and asking for help is an asset, not a liability.
The lone-wolf myth — the idea that truly talented people succeed on their own — is not just wrong, it is actively harmful.

### Chapter 13: The Power of Getting Help

Azzarello opens the CONNECT Better section by dismantling the self-reliance myth.
She argues that reluctance to ask for help is one of the most common career-limiting behaviours among talented professionals.

The resistance comes from multiple sources: fear of appearing weak, belief that you should be able to figure things out yourself, pride in self-sufficiency, and anxiety about being indebted.
Azzarello addresses each one.

Appearing weak: in practice, people who ask for help are perceived as *confident*, not weak.
The request signals that you know what you do not know and are secure enough to admit it.
Figuring things out yourself: you can, but it takes five times longer and produces worse results than learning from someone who has already solved the problem.
Self-sufficiency: admirable as a value, counterproductive as a career strategy.
Indebtedness: the best mentoring relationships are reciprocal, and most senior people *want* to help — it satisfies their desire to teach and builds their own network.

She tells the story of how she became known as the best deal-maker in her company — not because she was naturally gifted at deal-making, but because she sought help from the corporate development team.
They had the expertise she lacked: financial modelling, legal structuring, precedent analysis.
By recruiting them as informal advisors and bringing them into her deals, she delivered better outcomes than anyone else.
The credit went to her, but the capability came from the network.
This, she argues, is the essence of leadership: building a coalition of capability that exceeds what any individual can produce.

### Chapters 14-15: Mentors, Advisors, and the Extra Team

Azzarello distinguishes between three types of career supporters, each serving a different function.

**Formal mentors** are career advocates who actively champion you.
They know your name, your work, your ambitions, and they are willing to spend their own political capital on your behalf.
They recommend you for roles, introduce you to decision-makers, and vouch for you in rooms you are not in.
A formal mentor is someone who, when asked "Who should we consider for this role?", says your name.

**Informal mentors** are people you learn from without a formal arrangement.
You observe them, ask occasional questions, and absorb their patterns.
They may not even know they are mentoring you.
The informal mentor relationship is low-commitment for both parties, which makes it easier to maintain — but it also means you cannot rely on them for advocacy.

**Business advisors** are people you consult on specific, discrete problems.
They are not career advocates; they are subject-matter experts who help you solve particular challenges.
You might have a finance advisor, a technology advisor, and a legal advisor — people you call when you need deep expertise in a specific domain.

Azzarello's **recruitment formula**: aim for 10 informal mentors per year, 1 formal career advocate every 1-3 years, and 3 regular business advisors.
The approach is what she calls **flattery and brevity**: "I admire how you handle X — would you be willing to spend 20 minutes helping me think through Y?"
This formula works because it is specific (people know exactly what you are asking), complimentary (people enjoy being recognised for their strengths), and low-commitment (twenty minutes is not threatening).

The **"extra team"** is a concept distinct from mentoring.
It refers to people who do not report to you but contribute to your outcomes.
They expand your capacity beyond your formal resources.
The extra team might include peers in other departments who share information, former colleagues who provide external perspectives, people you have helped who now feel motivated to reciprocate, or specialists in other functions whose expertise complements yours.

Azzarello tells the story of a product manager who was struggling to get engineering support for her projects.
The engineering team reported to a different VP and had their own priorities.
Rather than escalating through the hierarchy — a move that would have created political friction — she built an extra team.
She identified two sympathetic engineers, helped them with their own projects (giving them visibility they needed), and in return, they became informal allies who prioritised her requests.
She never had formal authority over them.
She had something better: reciprocal relationships built on mutual benefit.

The extra team concept is powerful because it acknowledges a reality of modern organisations: **your formal authority rarely matches your actual need for resources**.
The people you need to get things done rarely report to you.
Building an extra team is how you close that gap.

### Chapter 16: The Networking Paradox — Build by Giving

Azzarello addresses networking directly, and her take is less transactional than most.

She introduces what she calls the **networking paradox**: the way to build a powerful network is not to ask for things, but to give them.
Help people with their problems.
Make introductions.
Share information.
Be useful before you need to be useful.
The network you build through generosity is the network that responds when you need help — because people feel genuine motivation to reciprocate, not obligation.

She contrasts this with **extractive networking** — the approach of collecting business cards, requesting informational interviews, and asking people for favours before you have established a relationship.
Extractive networking feels efficient but produces brittle connections.
People helped through extractive networking feel used and avoid future contact.
People helped through generous networking feel grateful and seek future contact.

Her practical advice: whenever you meet someone new, ask yourself "How can I help this person?" before you ask "How can this person help me?"
When you hear about someone's challenge, send them an article, make an introduction, or share a resource — with no expectation of return.
Over time, these deposits create a network that is eager to help you, not because they owe you, but because they like you.

### Chapters 17-18: Fearlessness, the Experience Paradox, and Getting on the List

Azzarello makes a distinction that reframes how most people think about readiness.
**Confidence** comes from experience — you feel confident when you have done something before and know you can do it again.
**Fearlessness** is the willingness to act before you feel ready — to step into a role you are not sure you can handle, because waiting for confidence means waiting forever.

The problem: you cannot build the experience that creates confidence without first taking the leap that requires fearlessness.
Confidence and experience are locked in a circular dependency.
The only way to break the circle is to leap first and learn second.

She tells the story of her own career trajectory: every major role she took felt premature.
She became a General Manager at 33 and felt she had no idea how to run a general management function.
She ran a $1 billion business at 35 and felt she lacked the financial sophistication the role demanded.
She became a CEO at 38 and felt she was faking it.
Each time, she learned fast enough to succeed — but the learning happened *inside* the role, not before it.
**"Everyone who is a CEO has been a CEO for the first time,"** her mentor told her.

Less talented people who are willing to leap ahead get the roles, while more talented people who wait for readiness watch from below.
The gap between the leap and the learning is where growth happens.
Azzarello's advice is not to be reckless — it is to recognise that the feeling of unreadiness is universal, permanent, and not a reason to stop.

The **Experience Paradox** follows directly from this.
You cannot get the job without the experience, but you *can* get the experience without the job.
Azzarello's method: find people who are currently in the role you want.
Learn what they do.
Offer to take on work they are not getting done — every busy leader has projects they cannot get to.
Practice making decisions at their level before you are formally there.
When the opportunity appears, you have real experience to point to — not hypothetical readiness.

She tells the story of a woman who wanted to become a nonprofit president but lacked second-level management experience — she had only managed individual contributors, never managed other managers.
Rather than waiting for that experience to appear in her current job, she volunteered to lead a community organisation where she would manage other volunteer leaders.
It was unpaid, part-time, and unglamorous.
It was also exactly the experience she needed.
When she interviewed for the nonprofit president role, she had genuine stories about managing managers — not hypothetical claims about what she would do.
She got the job.

### Chapters 19-20: Getting on "the List"

This may be the most strategically important concept in the entire book, and Azzarello gives it full treatment.

For any desirable role, there is an informal shortlist of candidates that the decision-maker will consider.
This list is not generated by HR systems, applications, or performance reviews.
It is generated by **relationships with the decision-maker's inner circle**.

The mechanism: when a role opens, the decision-maker does not post a job ad and wait for applications.
They turn to their trusted advisors — direct reports, peers, mentors, and confidants — and ask: "Who should we consider?"
The names those advisors produce become the list.
If you are not on it, your qualifications are irrelevant — you are filtered out before evaluation begins.
The filter is not malicious.
It is cognitive.
Decision-makers operate under time pressure and trust constraints.
They default to known quantities because evaluating unknown quantities is expensive and risky.

Azzarello tells the story of how she got the role that made her career — running a $1 billion software business.
She would never have been considered based on her resume alone.
Her background was atypical for the role, and the hiring manager did not know her.
But a mentor who *was* in the hiring manager's inner circle put her name forward.
Once she was on the list, she competed and won on merit.
But she could not have competed without the initial nomination.
The list was the gate, and the mentor was the key.

A contrasting story: a highly qualified director who applied for a VP position through the formal process.
He had excellent credentials, strong performance reviews, and a clear track record.
He did not get an interview.
When he investigated why, he learned that the decision-maker had already identified three candidates through her network before the formal posting went live.
The posting was a formality — the list was already set.
His application arrived after the decision had effectively been made.

The practical implications are uncomfortable but clear.
You get on the list by:
1. Identifying who the decision-maker listens to — their inner circle
2. Developing a relationship with at least one person in that inner circle — long before the role opens
3. Making your aspirations explicitly known — people cannot nominate you for a role if they do not know you want it
4. Ensuring the inner circle member associates you with the role — through relevant conversations, demonstrated capability, and sustained visibility

This is not last-minute lobbying.
It is long-term relationship investment.
By the time the role opens, the nomination should be almost automatic — your name should be the first one that comes to mind, not one that requires persuasion.

## Part IV: Putting It All Together

### Chapter 21: The Action Plan

Azzarello closes the book with a practical integration chapter.
She acknowledges that the DO-LOOK-CONNECT model can feel overwhelming — three simultaneous workstreams on top of an already demanding job.
Her answer: start small, but start immediately.

She recommends a phased approach:
- **Week 1:** Audit your current state across all three pillars.
Where are you strong? Where are you weak? Which pillar is most neglected?
- **Month 1:** Choose one action from each pillar.
One Ruthless Priority to define.
One stakeholder relationship to build.
One mentor to recruit.
- **Quarter 1:** Build the habits.
The 21-times rule for communicating priorities.
Regular touchpoints with your stakeholder map.
Monthly mentoring conversations.

The key insight of the closing chapter is that the three pillars are not independent workstreams — they are **mutually reinforcing**.
Better execution on the right priorities gives you better stories for stakeholder conversations (DO feeds LOOK).
Better stakeholder relationships give you intelligence about what the organisation values, which helps you choose better priorities (LOOK feeds DO).
Better mentoring relationships give you coaching on both execution and visibility (CONNECT feeds DO and LOOK).

Azzarello warns against a common failure mode: people read a book like this, feel inspired, create a plan, and then let the plan die under the weight of daily urgency.
Her advice: **do not add the three pillars on top of your current workload**.
The whole point of Ruthless Priorities is that you must drop things to create space.
If you do not make room by eliminating low-value work, you will never have the energy or time for the LOOK and CONNECT work that your career requires.

## The Verdict

*Rise* is the book you need when you are delivering strong results and feeling invisible, overlooked, or stuck.
Its greatest contribution is the **DO-LOOK-CONNECT diagnostic** — a simple model that reveals which leg of the stool is missing.
Most stalled careers can be traced to one neglected pillar, and Azzarello names the traps with uncomfortable precision.
The **inside voice vs outside voice** concept is immediately actionable for anyone who struggles to communicate their value to non-technical stakeholders.
The **"Getting on the List"** framework is a cold-eyed corrective to anyone who believes that applications and interviews are how people get promoted.
These are not abstract ideas — they are diagnostic tools that produce specific, concrete changes in behaviour.

The book's weaknesses are context and depth.
Azzarello's career was in Silicon Valley tech during the 1990s-2000s boom — a world of high talent mobility, rapid company growth, and constant role creation.
In more rigid organisations with fixed band structures, budget committees, and slower promotion cycles, some of the advice ("just renegotiate your job") underplays how much organisational inertia constrains individual agency.
Her advice on toxic bosses is also thin — she counsels "just leave," without addressing the more common situation of a boss who is not toxic but merely unhelpful or politically weak.
The "Do What You Love Is Bad Advice" chapter raises a valid contrarian point but then overcorrects, dismissing the genuine strategic advantage that intrinsic motivation provides in competitive environments.
And her networking advice, while sound in principle, shows its age — the examples feel pre-2020, and the book would benefit from a stronger emphasis on digital visibility and thought leadership.

Who benefits most: mid-career professionals in large organisations who have strong execution records and weak visibility.
If you are the person who always delivers but never gets promoted, *Rise* will show you exactly what you are missing — and more importantly, it will make you uncomfortable enough to change.
The book is less useful for people already in executive roles (the advice is pitched at the climb, not the summit) or for people in small organisations where the political dynamics are simpler.

How it compares: *Rise* occupies a practical middle ground between the philosophical depth of Robert Greene and the tactical specificity of career manuals like Carla Harris's *Expect to Win*.
Greene tells you that the world runs on power and perception; Azzarello tells you what to do about it on Monday morning.
Harris provides sharper tools for the specific challenge of perception management and sponsorship; Azzarello provides a broader diagnostic model that includes execution and energy management alongside visibility.
Marshall Goldsmith's *What Got You Here Won't Get You There* tackles the same Level Dilemma transition problem but from a behavioural coaching angle rather than a strategic positioning angle.
Read Azzarello for the diagnosis, Harris for the sponsor playbook, and Goldsmith for the behavioural change work.
