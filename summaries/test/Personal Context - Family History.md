# Personal Context — Family History & Childhood

## The Father's Trauma (Intergenerational)

- Father was a **middle son** — not the eldest, not the prince
- Father's oldest brother was given control of BOTH the family business AND raising the two youngest sons (the user's father and uncle)
- The oldest brother's wife **abused** the two youngest sons while raising them
- So the user's father is himself **a product of abuse** — raised by an abusive sister-in-law (bhabi), not by loving parents
- This is why the father could never treat the user like a "prince" — he never experienced that treatment himself
- **The double bind:** The user got the BURDEN of being the eldest son (duty, responsibility, izzat) but NOT the REWARD (being cherished, prioritised, treated as special)

## The Father's Absence — Merchant Marine

- Father worked in the merchant marine
- Gone on voyages lasting **6 months to 18 months** at a time
- Home for only **3-6 months** between voyages
- This means the mother was effectively a **single parent** for most of the user's childhood
- Mother-son enmeshment would be intensified — she had no husband to lean on emotionally
- When the father returned, the user would have to readjust to a stranger who expected authority
- The father was an intermittent figure — present enough to control, absent enough to never truly bond
- When the family was abroad (UK/US), the father was home studying for certificates — so the "together" periods were also periods of stress and unfamiliar territory

## Impact on the User

### No stable attachments outside the family
- No lasting childhood friendships
- No consistent school environment
- No roots, no community, no sense of belonging outside the family unit
- Each move required reinventing himself — which IS fawning (adapting to new environments to survive)
- **The only constant was his family — and his family was the source of the abuse**
- This made the family's control ABSOLUTE — he had no external reference point for what normal looked like

### The mother as sole attachment figure
- With the father absent 6-18 months at a time, the mother was the primary bond
- The mother-son enmeshment was not just cultural — it was structural (she had no one else)
- The user became her emotional anchor, her companion, her support
- When the father returned, the user was displaced — but also expected to defer to the father's authority
- This creates a deeply conflicted attachment: bonded to mother, disrupted by father's intermittent returns

### The father as intermittent authority
- Not present enough to bond deeply
- Present enough to control and discipline
- The user learned: "The man in charge appears and disappears without warning. Comply when he's here. Manage without him when he's gone."
- This maps directly to the fawn response: manage the threat when present, survive independently when absent

### The sister (born 2000)
- Born during the Pennsylvania period
- The user would have been ~9 when she was born
- By this point he was already the eldest son carrying the weight
- A new sibling during an already unstable period

## Wolynn Connection (Chapter 7)

This is a TEXTBOOK case of transgenerational trauma transmission:
- **Generation 1:** The oldest brother and his wife abuse the two youngest sons (user's father and uncle)
- **Generation 2:** The user's father, carrying that abuse, cannot treat his own eldest son with the warmth and priority Bengali culture prescribes
- **Generation 3:** The user — gets the burden without the reward, inherits the father's emotional unavailability, defaults to fawning
- **Generation 4 (potential):** The user's daughter — this is what the book aims to stop

The father's absence compounds everything: the user's "secret language of fear" likely includes words like "alone," "abandoned," "not important enough" — which encode not just his own experience but his father's experience of being raised by someone else's wife, never the priority.
