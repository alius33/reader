# Bengali Cultural Abuse Patterns

Research on South Asian and Bengali cultural patterns of family abuse, control, and dysfunction — with a focus on the eldest son's experience in British Bangladeshi families.

---

## 1. The "Baro Chele" (Eldest Son) Burden

### The Role and Its Weight

In Bengali, "baro chele" literally means "eldest son." The role carries expectations that go far beyond birth order — it is a structural position within the family system that assigns disproportionate responsibility, emotional labour, and obligation to one child.

- The eldest child in South Asian immigrant families is "often expected to set an example for younger siblings, excel academically, and fulfill responsibilities that extend beyond their years."
  - **Source:** Vishwani Sahai-Siddiqui, MD, "The Burden of Being the 'Good Child': How South Asian Kids Suppress Mental Health Struggles," *Behavioral Health News* (2024). [Link](https://behavioralhealthnews.org/the-burden-of-being-the-good-child-how-south-asian-kids-suppress-mental-health-struggles/)
  - **Credibility:** Expert article by a psychiatrist and medical writer. Published in a US behavioural health publication.

- "In many South Asian households, the eldest child takes on a pseudo-parental role (the parentified child), managing younger siblings and assisting with household responsibilities."
  - **Source:** Same article (Sahai-Siddiqui, 2024).

- "Eldest children often feel an intense obligation to protect their family's reputation and protect younger siblings from parental disappointment. This role leaves little room for them to express vulnerability, as their own struggles might be seen as a burden to the family."
  - **Source:** Same article (Sahai-Siddiqui, 2024).

### Parentification in the South Asian Diaspora

Parentification — the reversal of the parent-child role — is widespread in South Asian immigrant families and falls disproportionately on the eldest child.

- Parentification is defined as "a parent-child role reversal in which the child performs caretaking responsibilities for the parent" due to parental failure to fulfill those duties.
  - **Source:** Vandan Jhaveri, "Understanding Parentification in the South Asian Diaspora," *Medium / Long Way Home* (2023). [Link](https://vandanjhaveri.medium.com/understanding-parentification-in-the-south-asian-diaspora-b5c6cb944303)
  - **Credibility:** Personal essay by a South Asian diaspora writer drawing on therapeutic literature.

- Two types identified:
  - **Instrumental parentification:** Children handle financial/practical tasks — paying bills, supporting siblings, running errands parents won't do themselves.
  - **Emotional parentification:** Children provide developmentally inappropriate emotional support — mediating parental conflicts, serving as confidants for adult struggles.

- Children become "cultural tour guides, translators and interpreters, best friends, mediators, marriage counsellors, financial advisors, and tech support." Many rationalize this as "It's just easier if I do it."
  - **Source:** Same article (Jhaveri, 2023).

- The "damning logic" internalized by South Asian children:
  1. Well-behaved kids don't burden their parents.
  2. Well-behaved kids deserve parental love.
  3. Therefore, asking for anything risks losing their love.
  - This creates lifelong low self-esteem and the inability to ask for help.
  - **Source:** Same article (Jhaveri, 2023).

- "The parentification of South Asian children is so commonplace in South Asian immigrant households that many South Asian adults wouldn't even recognize it as dysfunction."
  - **Source:** Same article (Jhaveri, 2023).

- Instrumental parentification tends to occur with the eldest son, while emotional parentification tends to occur with the eldest daughter — though in practice, eldest sons in British Bangladeshi families often carry both.
  - **Source:** Springer Nature, "A Phenomenological Study of Parentification Experiences of Asian American Young Adults," *Contemporary Family Therapy* (2024). [Link](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10591-024-09723-x)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed academic journal.

### The "Good Son" Expectation

- In many South Asian families, "first-born children are expected to absorb tension during periods of stress or conflict, taking on a stabilising role within the household. Over time, this can limit their perceived freedom to express vulnerability, as emotional restraint becomes tied to duty."
  - **Source:** "The Quiet Cost of Emotional Silence Among British Asian Men," *DESIblitz* (2024). [Link](https://www.desiblitz.com/content/the-quiet-cost-of-emotional-silence-among-british-asian-men)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based South Asian media outlet, with named personal testimonies.

- Personal testimony — Imran (unnamed surname): "As eldest son, felt obligated to maintain composure regardless of personal overwhelm."
  - **Source:** Same article (DESIblitz, 2024).

## 2. Izzat (Family Honour) as a Silencing and Control Mechanism

### How Izzat Functions

Izzat is the South Asian concept of family honour. In abusive family systems, it operates not as a cultural value but as a weapon of silence.

- "In the vast majority of domestic violence cases in South Asian communities, the honour principle operates as a silencing factor — the violence or abuse is not motivated by the need to maintain honour, but honour is invoked to silence women in the face of such violence and abuse."
  - **Source:** "The use and abuse of honour based violence in the UK," *openDemocracy* (2012). [Link](https://www.opendemocracy.net/en/5050/use-and-abuse-of-honour-based-violence-in-uk/)
  - **Credibility:** Independent global media platform with a focus on human rights.

- Izzat is "rooted in patriarchal structures" and "assigns primary responsibility for its preservation to women, whose perceived chastity, obedience to family authority, and conformity to practices like arranged marriages directly impact the honour of male kin and the extended lineage."
  - **Source:** "Izzat (honour)," *Wikipedia* / synthesis of academic sources. [Link](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Izzat_(honor))
  - **Credibility:** Encyclopedia article drawing on multiple academic sources.

- "Because izzat is dependent on women's behaviour, protecting the izzat of the family can also be seen as exercising social control over women's bodies and behaviour."
  - **Source:** Same article (Wikipedia).

### Izzat and Male Children

While most literature on izzat focuses on its impact on women, it operates powerfully on eldest sons too:

- The eldest son is expected to uphold the family's public image through professional success, obedience, and marriage within community norms.
- Failing to meet these expectations — through career choice, romantic relationship, mental health disclosure, or boundary-setting — is framed as bringing shame on the family.
- "Many women frequently invoke honour to explain why they cannot leave a violent or abusive relationship" — the same mechanism applies to sons who cannot leave abusive family dynamics.
  - **Source:** openDemocracy article (2012), cited above.

### Izzat and Help-Seeking

- "Cultural concepts of izzat have implications for strategies in managing or silencing of psychological distress."
  - **Source:** "Support needs of south Asian adult survivors of childhood sexual abuse in the UK," *PLOS Mental Health* (2024). [Link](https://journals.plos.org/mentalhealth/article?id=10.1371/journal.pmen.0000454)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed open-access journal (Public Library of Science).

- Izzat "leads to hesitation among victims to report abuse within their families, with an additional burden of respecting the older generation forcefully inducing clients' silence."
  - **Source:** Same article (PLOS Mental Health, 2024).

- Research by Natcom (National Communication Association) examined how two generations of South Asian Indian Americans navigate izzat, finding significant intergenerational tension between izzat as identity and izzat as silencing.
  - **Source:** "Uncovering Face: How Two Generations of South Asian Indian Americans Navigate 'Izzat,'" *National Communication Association* (publication). [Link](https://www.natcom.org/publications-library/uncovering-face-how-two-generations-south-asian-indian-americans-navigate/)
  - **Credibility:** Academic communication studies association.

## 3. Mother-Son Enmeshment in South Asian Culture

### Definitions and Dynamics

- "An extremely unhealthy mother-son relationship where there are few or perhaps no boundaries between a mother and her son... The mother is overly involved in her adult son's life and controls his actions and decisions."
  - **Source:** Bina Freilich, "Understanding Mother-Son Enmeshment in South Asian Culture," *Medium* (2023). [Link](https://medium.com/@p_k47/are-you-a-mamas-boy-understanding-mother-son-enmeshment-in-south-asian-culture-d66476daa51b)
  - **Credibility:** Personal/clinical essay by a South Asian writer.

- Maternal control manifests as:
  - Excessive involvement in adult son's decisions regarding clothing, food, and social activities
  - Violation of privacy (entering bedrooms without permission, intruding on intimate time with spouse)
  - Belief that the son should prioritize time with mother over all other relationships

- "This can impact the son's personal development, independence and his relationship with his friends and spouse."
  - **Source:** Same article (Freilich, 2023).

### Cultural Roots

- "In South Asian families, closeness is a virtue, a cultural hallmark. Yet, there's a fine line between togetherness and enmeshment."
  - **Source:** "Is enmeshment trauma a cultural norm in South Asian families?", *Burnt Roti* (South Asian lifestyle publication). [Link](https://www.burntroti.com/blog/is-enmeshment-trauma-a-cultural-norm-in-south-asian-families)
  - **Credibility:** South Asian cultural publication focused on generational issues.

- Root causes identified:
  - Parents with unrealistically high expectations who ignore their child's actual abilities
  - Emotionally distressed caregivers who rely on children as emotional support
  - Narcissistic parental behaviour combined with cultural emphasis on unquestioning respect
  - Survival instincts in children who learn that pleasing parents ensures safety
  - **Source:** Same article (Burnt Roti).

- "This is a topic that most people in the Asian culture don't like to address."
  - **Source:** Same article (Burnt Roti).

### The Son as Extension of the Mother

- Sons are "culturally elevated as 'carriers of family honour,' leading mothers to form emotionally dependent relationships with them. This deep enmeshment creates vulnerability when sons marry, as mothers may view daughters-in-law as competitors rather than family additions."
  - **Source:** "'The Son Belongs to His Mother': South Asian Daughters-in-Law and the Mental Health Toll of Family Hierarchy," *Behavioral Health News* (2024). [Link](https://behavioralhealthnews.org/the-son-belongs-to-his-mother-south-asian-daughters-in-law-and-the-mental-health-toll-of-family-hierarchy/)
  - **Credibility:** Behavioral health publication.

## 4. The Mother-in-Law vs. Daughter-in-Law Dynamic

### The Patriarchal Bargain

- "Young brides are made to accept deprivation, hardship and subservience to their husband and mother-in-law in exchange for economic security through their husband and married sons and control over their own daughters-in-law in due time."
  - **Source:** "An Apology to our Daughters-in-law: Patriarchal Family Dynamics in South Asian Households," *Brown Girl Magazine*. [Link](https://browngirlmagazine.com/apology-to-our-daughters-in-law-patriarchal-family-dynamics/)
  - **Credibility:** Prominent South Asian diaspora publication.

- This is a cyclical system: the oppressed daughter-in-law becomes the oppressive mother-in-law, replicating the abuse she endured — because it is the only power she was ever given.

### Power Structure

- "The mother-in-law often acts as the gatekeeper of tradition, family honor, and daily decision-making. The daughter-in-law is positioned at the bottom of the hierarchy, expected to fulfill duties without question."
  - **Source:** "Trapped by Tradition: The Anxiety of Being a 'Good' South Asian Daughter-in-Law," *Behavioral Health News* (2024). [Link](https://behavioralhealthnews.org/trapped-by-tradition-the-anxiety-of-being-a-good-south-asian-daughter-in-law/)
  - **Credibility:** Behavioral health publication.

- "Speaking up is equated with rebellion or disrespect."
  - **Source:** "'The Son Belongs to His Mother'" article, *Behavioral Health News* (2024), cited above.

### The Son's Position

The eldest son in this dynamic is caught between two women who are structurally set against each other by the patriarchal system:

- The mother demands loyalty and continued enmeshment.
- The wife needs autonomy and partnership.
- The son is culturally prohibited from "choosing" his wife over his mother — doing so is framed as betrayal, ingratitude, or being "controlled" by his wife.
- This is a no-win position that generates chronic guilt, anxiety, and marital conflict.

### Statistics

- 43% of South Asian immigrant women reported psychological abuse from in-laws
- 70% identified mothers-in-law as primary abuse sources
- 61% experienced mental health decline post-marriage in multigenerational households
- Over 50% avoided seeking mental health care due to stigma
  - **Source:** "'The Son Belongs to His Mother'" article, *Behavioral Health News* (2024), cited above. (Statistics cited within the article from broader research.)

## 5. "Respect for Elders" Weaponized as Obedience

### The Mechanism

- "Filial piety is not merely a cultural ideal but a tool used to force obedience and subjugate the child. Through invoking the cultural duty to obey and serve familial elders, parents and authority figures can exert immense psychological pressure to coerce their children into conforming to their expectations."
  - **Source:** "A Critique of Filial Piety and the Traditional East Asian Family Model," *The Commoner*. [Link](https://www.thecommoner.org.uk/a-critique-of-filial-piety-and-the-traditional-east-asian-family-model/)
  - **Credibility:** Independent publication on social issues.

- "Children must show firm respect and politeness and prioritize the needs and desires of the elders over their own. This can create an environment where children or witnesses of the abuse overlook, tolerate, or ignore the parents' abusive behavior."
  - **Source:** "11 Cultural Factors That May Contribute to Child Abuse in Asian Families," *Hopeful Panda* (2022). [Link](https://hopefulpanda.com/asian-cultural-factors-abuse/)
  - **Credibility:** Mental health blog by an author drawing on clinical literature. Cited in multiple academic contexts.

### Colonial Legacy

- "The British weaponized caste under colonial rule, reinforcing its hierarchies. When a colonizer teaches that submission is survival, that lesson outlives empire."
  - **Source:** "Breaking generational curses: How colonialism, caste and silence feed domestic violence in South Asian communities," *Real Change News* (2025). [Link](https://www.realchangenews.org/news/2025/10/29/breaking-generational-curses-how-colonialism-caste-and-silence-feed-domestic-violence)
  - **Credibility:** Independent news outlet focused on social justice.

- "Breaking cycles of abuse involves dismantling the systems that taught families to equate obedience with safety."
  - **Source:** Same article (Real Change News, 2025).

### How It Plays Out

- Any challenge to a parent's authority — setting a boundary, expressing disagreement, declining a demand — is reframed as "disrespect."
- The accusation of disrespect carries enormous weight because it invokes both cultural shame (izzat) and religious guilt (birrul walidayn).
- The child is trapped: obey and suffer, or resist and be labelled ungrateful, Westernized, or sinful.

## 6. Financial Control Through Inheritance and Land

### The British Bangladeshi Land Dynamic

- "Long-settled British-Bangladeshis have accumulated capital to invest in the home country and invested in businesses, purchased extra land and built spacious houses as visible symbols of their material success."
  - **Source:** "Transnational land and property disputes: the British-Bangladeshi experience," *Contemporary South Asia* 29(3), 2021 (published by Taylor & Francis). [Link](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09584935.2021.1886249)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed academic journal article.

- The Swadhinata Trust published the full research paper documenting how British-Bangladeshi diaspora families face "ongoing transnational tensions and disputes around land and property ownership in rural Sylhet."
  - **Source:** Swadhinata Trust / same study. [PDF Link](https://www.swadhinata.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2021/05/Transnational-land-and-property-disputes-the-British-Bangladeshi-experience.pdf)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based Bangladeshi heritage charity; peer-reviewed research.

### How Land Is Used as Control

- Approximately 7 out of 10 families in rural South Asia experience land disputes (World Bank).
- Nearly 60% of land-related disputes result in family estrangement or complete breakdown.
  - **Source:** "How Land & Inheritance Disputes impact South Asian Families," *DESIblitz*. [Link](https://www.desiblitz.com/content/how-land-inheritance-disputes-impact-south-asian-families)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based South Asian media outlet.

- In Bangladeshi families with land in Bangladesh (especially in Sylhet), parents use the promise or threat of inheritance as a control mechanism:
  - Obedient children are promised land or property.
  - Disobedient children are threatened with disinheritance.
  - The eldest son is often expected to manage the land, creating a financial tether to the family.
  - Disputes over land regularly destroy sibling relationships and are used to maintain parental authority well into adulthood.

### Gender and Islamic Inheritance Law

- Under Muslim inheritance law in Bangladesh, "male members of a family inherit lawfully more properties than their female counterparts."
- Women's ownership of land in Bangladesh: 10% by area; among landowners, 22.7% are women and 77.3% are men.
  - **Source:** "Muslim Inheritance Law in Bangladesh," *Justice Corner BD*. [Link](https://justicecornerbd.com/blogs/muslim-inheritance-law-in-bangladesh)
  - **Credibility:** Bangladeshi legal information website.

- Despite Islamic inheritance law specifying fixed shares, "cultural norms and patriarchal attitudes often override legal provisions." Daughters frequently receive smaller shares or nothing.
  - **Source:** DESIblitz article, cited above.

## 7. Narcissistic Parenting in South Asian Families

### How Culture Enables Narcissism

- "Narcissistic parents [view] their children as extensions of themselves rather than as separate individual beings with their own needs and desires."
  - **Source:** "Understanding the Impact of Narcissistic Parents: A South Asian Therapist's Perspective," *Begin Boundless* (NYC therapy practice). [Link](https://www.beginboundless.com/post/understanding-the-impact-of-narcissistic-parents-a-south-asian-therapist-s-perspective)
  - **Credibility:** Clinical practice blog by a South Asian therapist.

- "It's easy for narcissistic parenting to hide behind the 'tiger parent' label, so children of narcissistic parents have a hard time realizing this because they believe it's just part of the cultural norm for Asian parents to be strict."
  - **Source:** "Is Your Asian Parent a Narcissist? Here Are 6 Classic Signs," *NextShark* (2018). [Link](https://nextshark.com/6-signs-asian-narcissist-parent)
  - **Credibility:** Asian American media outlet.

### Golden Child / Scapegoat Dynamic

- "In Asian cultures, family traditions may make the eldest son the golden child."
- The golden child receives special praise and treatment; parents see this child as proof they are good parents.
- The scapegoat "gets blamed for problems" and "faces criticism, rejection, and emotional pain."
- These roles can shift — the eldest son may be the golden child when obedient and the scapegoat when he resists.
  - **Source:** "The Golden Child and Scapegoat Dynamic: Healing from a Narcissistic Family," *Gaslighting Check*. [Link](https://www.gaslightingcheck.com/blog/the-golden-child-and-scapegoat-dynamic-healing-from-a-narcissistic-family)
  - **Credibility:** Mental health education website.

### Emotional Blackmail

- Emotional blackmail uses "fear, obligation and guilt (FOG)" as transactional dynamics between controller and controlled (term coined by Susan Forward).
  - **Source:** Susan Forward, *Emotional Blackmail* (1997). Referenced in [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotional_blackmail).
  - **Credibility:** Foundational clinical text.

- "Indian parents operate on large sums of guilt, a gift from their own parents. They constantly feel like they're not enough, not doing enough and haven't given enough to their children."
  - **Source:** "Surviving Indian Parents: 18 Ways to Stand Up to Insults, Criticism, and Emotional Abuse," *Vishnu's Virtues*. [Link](https://www.vishnusvirtues.com/surviving-indian-parents-emotional-abuse/)
  - **Credibility:** Personal blog drawing on therapeutic literature.

## 8. Emotional Manipulation Tactics in South Asian Households

### Common Patterns

- "Discussing personal struggles is seen as self-indulgent or even disrespectful."
  - **Source:** Sahai-Siddiqui, *Behavioral Health News* (2024), cited above.

- Bengali-specific emotional manipulation includes parents saying things like "you're gonna understand when I die," implying the child's behaviour is causing them mortal anguish.
  - **Source:** "Emotional Manipulation and Expectations in South Asian Households," *The Detroit Writing Room* (2022). [Link](https://www.detroitwritingroom.com/2022-journalism-camp-stories/emotional-manipulation-expectations-south-asian-households)
  - **Credibility:** Student journalism project with personal testimony.

### The Silence Compact

- South Asian culture is "group-based, meaning that saving face is paramount, and parents often keep marital, financial, familial, and health problems private and within the nuclear family."
  - **Source:** Jhaveri, *Medium* (2023), cited above.

- This creates what researchers call a "conspiracy of silence" — parents who experienced trauma avoid discussing it, and children avoid asking about it.
  - **Source:** "Intergenerational Communication about Historical Trauma in Asian American Families," *Adversity and Resilience Science* (PMC, 2022). [Link](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9170877/)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal (Springer Nature).
