# Islam and Parental Abuse

Research on how Islamic concepts are weaponized in abusive families — and what Islam actually teaches about parental rights, obedience, and the limits of filial duty.

---

## 1. Birrul Walidayn: The Quranic Injunction and Its Distortion

### What the Quran Actually Says

**Surah Al-Isra 17:23-24** — the primary text on parental rights:

> "And your Lord has decreed that you not worship except Him, and to parents, good treatment. Whether one or both of them reach old age [while] with you, say not to them [so much as] 'uff,' and do not repel them but speak to them a noble word. And lower to them the wing of humility out of mercy and say, 'My Lord, have mercy upon them as they brought me up [when I was] small.'"

- **Source:** Quran.com, Surah Al-Isra 17:23-24. [Link](https://quran.com/al-isra/23-24)

**Key scholarly commentary on this verse:**

- Imam al-Qurtubi states that "Allah has made it necessary (wajib) to respect and treat parents well by combining it with the command to worship Him. This proves that after the obligation of worshipping Allah, obeying parents is most important."
  - **Source:** Tafsir al-Qurtubi, cited in Maarif ul-Quran commentary. [Link](https://quran.com/en/17:23/tafsirs/en-tafsir-maarif-ul-quran)
  - **Credibility:** Classical Islamic tafsir (Quranic commentary), widely authoritative.

**Surah Luqman 31:15** — the critical exception verse:

> "However, if they force you to ascribe partners to Me about whom you [can] have no [source of] knowledge, then do not obey them. Remain with them in this world with due fairness, but follow the way of the one who has turned himself towards Me."

- Commentary: "The matter of ascribing anyone other than Allah with Him is serious. In it, even the (otherwise essential) obedience to parents is haram (unlawful)."
  - **Source:** Tafheem ul-Quran (Maududi), Surah Luqman 31:12-19. [Link](https://www.islamicstudies.info/tafheem.php?sura=31&verse=12)
  - **Credibility:** Major 20th-century tafsir by Sayyid Abul A'la Maududi.

- Crucially, even when disobeying parents on matters of faith, the verse commands: "Remain with them in this world with due fairness." This establishes a distinction between **obedience** (conditional) and **good treatment** (unconditional) — a distinction abusive parents deliberately collapse.
  - **Source:** Same tafsir (Maududi).

### How Abusive Parents Distort Birrul Walidayn

Abusive parents weaponize these verses by:

1. **Collapsing the distinction between ihsan (good treatment) and ta'ah (obedience):** Islam commands unconditional good treatment but conditional obedience. Abusive parents present ALL disobedience — including boundary-setting, disagreement, or independent decision-making — as a sin equivalent to cursing one's parents.

2. **Omitting the exception clauses:** They quote Al-Isra 17:23-24 without ever mentioning Luqman 31:15 or the hadith limiting obedience.

3. **Equating their desires with God's commands:** By placing parental obedience immediately after worship of Allah, they imply that disobeying *them* is disobeying *God*.

4. **Using the threat of divine punishment:** They invoke the hadith about the severity of 'uquq al-walidayn (disobedience to parents) being among the major sins, without acknowledging any limits.

## 2. What Islam Actually Says: The Limits of Parental Obedience

### Scholarly Consensus: Obedience Is Not Absolute

- "It is not an obligation upon children to obey both of their parents in every matter they command or prohibit, by agreement of the scholars."
  - **Source:** Justin Parrott, "Obedience To Parents And Its Limits," *MuslimMatters.org* (2023). [Link](https://muslimmatters.org/2023/03/03/obedience-to-parents-and-its-limits/)
  - **Credibility:** Major English-language Islamic scholarship platform. Justin Parrott is a research librarian at NYU Abu Dhabi specializing in Islamic ethics.

- "The scholars came to a consensus that obedience to parents is not a limitless right, but they disagreed over the definition of disobedience."
  - **Source:** Same article (Parrott, 2023).

### The Foundational Hadith

- The Prophet Muhammad said: **"There is no obedience to a creature when it involves disobedience to the Creator."**
  - (Musnad Ahmad 1098, graded sahih)
  - **Source:** Abu Amina Elias, "Obedience to Parents and its Limits." [Link](https://www.abuaminaelias.com/obedience-to-parents-limits/)
  - **Credibility:** Islamic research website run by Justin Parrott (Abu Amina Elias), citing primary hadith sources.

- Another version: "There is no obedience in misconduct, obedience is only in good deeds."
  - **Source:** The Muslim Vibe, "Parental Obedience: Religion vs Culture." [Link](https://themuslimvibe.com/muslim-lifestyle-matters/parental-obedience-religion-vs-culture)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based Muslim media platform.

### Ibn Taymiyyah's Rulings (Hanbali School)

Ibn Taymiyyah (1263-1328), one of Islam's most influential scholars, made several rulings directly relevant to abusive parental dynamics:

- "A person must obey his parents as long as it does not involve disobedience, even if his parents were disobedient."
  - **Source:** Cited in Parrott (2023) and The Muslim Vibe article.

- **On forced marriage:** "Parents do not have the right to force their son or daughter to marry someone of their choice. As a result, if the child does not agree to the marriage, then he/she will not be called disobedient."
  - **Source:** The Muslim Vibe article, cited above.

- **On parents sabotaging marriages:** Ibn Taymiyyah considered a parent who maliciously tries to ruin their child's marriage to be committing a grave sin, comparing them to those who "followed Harut and Marut" (angels who taught sorcery to separate spouses — Quran 2:102).
  - **Source:** The Muslim Vibe article, cited above.

- **On forced divorce:** When asked about obeying a parent's command to divorce one's spouse, scholars including Ibn Taymiyyah ruled it is not obligatory to obey.
  - **Source:** IslamWeb fatwa. [Link](https://www.islamweb.net/en/fatwa/131695/conditions-under-which-disobeying-ones-parents-is-permitted)
  - **Credibility:** Major Islamic fatwa website operated by the Qatari Ministry of Awqaf.

### Al-Ghazali's Position (Shafi'i School)

- Al-Ghazali "related the opinion of most scholars that it is an obligation to obey parents and to agree with them in doubtful matters."
  - **Source:** Parrott (2023), cited above.
  - Note: Even al-Ghazali, who takes a more conservative position, limits obedience to "doubtful matters" — not clearly harmful or sinful commands.

### Imam Shatibi's Framework (Maliki School)

- Imam Shatibi established that acts which are individually permissible may collectively constitute sin when combined — meaning a pattern of individually "reasonable" parental demands can become oppressive when taken together.
  - **Source:** The Muslim Vibe article, cited above.

### Hanafi Jurisprudence (via SeekersGuidance)

- SeekersGuidance (Hanafi perspective) distinguishes between:
  - **Obedience (ta'ah):** Limited to lawful commands.
  - **Kindness and respect (ihsan):** Unconditional obligation toward parents as individuals.
- Even when disobeying parental commands that contradict Islamic teachings, "children must maintain respectful behavior and good treatment toward parents."
  - **Source:** "When May Parents Be Disobeyed, and How?", *SeekersGuidance*. [Link](https://seekersguidance.org/answers/hanafi-fiqh/when-may-parents-be-disobeyed-and-how/)
  - **Credibility:** Major Islamic education platform, Hanafi fiqh section.

## 3. When Disobedience to Parents Is Islamically Justified

### Specific Conditions Identified by Scholars

The following conditions for permissible disobedience emerge from classical and contemporary scholarship:

**1. Commands involving sin (haram):**
- If parents command anything that Allah has forbidden, obedience is not just unnecessary — it is itself sinful.
- "If parents forbid one from one's fard, wajib or confirmed sunna duties, one may not listen to them, as is the case if they order one to do something haram."
  - **Source:** SeekersGuidance, cited above.

**2. Commands motivated by unreasonableness or ignorance:**
- "If parents are telling their child to do something because of a lack of reason or knowledge, or because of being unreasonable and stubborn, and obeying them will be detrimental to the interests of the child, then the scholars are of the view that it is not obligatory to obey them in this case."
  - **Source:** IslamWeb fatwa. [Link](https://www.islamweb.net/en/fatwa/461938/limits-of-obeying-parents)
  - **Credibility:** Major Islamic fatwa website.

**3. Commands that cause harm to the child:**
- "Children should obey their parents in matters that benefit both of them, but parents may not order their children to harm themselves or others."
  - **Source:** IslamWeb fatwa, cited above.

**4. Parents hostile to the child's wellbeing:**
- The Muslim Vibe article identifies an exception for "parents hostile to a child's life, wealth, and honor."
  - **Source:** The Muslim Vibe article, cited above.

**5. Commands that are "completely foolish and harmful":**
- **Source:** The Muslim Vibe article, cited above.

**6. Violence or intolerable abuse:**
- "Only in the most extreme circumstances, such as violence or intolerable abuse, should the parental-child relationship be severed."
- "If parents are abusive, demanding sinful acts, or harming your health, emotionally or physically, then Islam allows you to limit contact."
  - **Source:** "Islam's Guidance On Dealing With Toxic Parents," *ShunChild*. [Link](https://shunchild.com/article/what-does-islam-say-about-toxic-parents)
  - **Credibility:** Islamic guidance website.

### The Critical Distinction: Obedience vs. Good Treatment

This is the single most important point that abusive parents deliberately obscure:

- **Obedience (ta'ah)** — following parental commands — is conditional and has limits.
- **Good treatment (ihsan/birr)** — treating parents with kindness and respect — is unconditional.

You can refuse to obey an unjust command while still treating your parent with dignity. These are not the same thing, and Islam does not collapse them into one.

## 5. Religious Guilt as a Control Mechanism in Muslim Families

### How Guilt Is Weaponized

- "Emotional manipulation is a key indicator of spiritual abuse, where abusers control behavior by using guilt, fear, or shame, often distorting religious teachings and using fear of divine punishment to serve their own agendas."
  - **Source:** "Processing Spiritual Abuse In Islam: A Comprehensive Guide," *MuslimMatters.org* (2024). [Link](https://muslimmatters.org/2024/11/18/processing-spiritual-abuse-in-islam-a-comprehensive-guide-for-individuals-communities-and-organizations/)
  - **Credibility:** Major English-language Islamic scholarship platform.

- "Abusers exploit emotions like guilt, using religious rhetoric to justify their actions and may imply that struggling to follow their guidance signifies weak faith or ingratitude."
  - **Source:** Same article (MuslimMatters, 2024).

- "At an individual level, victims grapple with spiritual dilemmas surrounding exposing the faults of others and the internalized guilt felt in relation to this."
  - **Source:** Same article (MuslimMatters, 2024).

### Specific Tactics in Muslim Families

- Misrepresenting Quranic verses to demand unconditional obedience
- Using interpretations of Islamic law to isolate victims
- Invoking religious duty to suppress dissent or questioning
- Framing abuse as "Islamic discipline" or spiritual correction
- Suggesting that questioning them equals "doubting Allah"
- Implying abuse is necessary for spiritual progress
- Framing silence about abuse as spiritually elevating
  - **Source:** "Spiritual Abuse in Muslim Communities: Recognition, Accountability, and Healing," *Amaliah* (2024). [Link](https://www.amaliah.com/post/69839/how-to-recognise-heal-spiritual-abuse-trauma-muslim-communities)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based Muslim women's platform with editorial standards.

### Impact on Victims

- "Spiritually abused children or adults may feel worthless and consider themselves as bad Muslims and sinners."
  - **Source:** "Spiritual Abuse," AMINA (Muslim Women's Resource Centre, Scotland). [PDF Link](https://mwrc.org.uk/wp-content/uploads/2023/03/amina-resources-Spiritual-Abuse-2021.pdf)
  - **Credibility:** Scottish charity focused on Muslim women's rights.

- "Fear, shame and guilt induced in God's name can deeply affect the child's relationship with Allah."
  - **Source:** Same source (AMINA).

- Victims experience:
  - Spiritual crisis and loss of faith
  - Damaged trust in religious communities
  - Identity confusion between authentic Islam and abusive interpretations
  - Long-term difficulty distinguishing healthy religious practice from manipulation
  - **Source:** MuslimMatters (2024) and Amaliah (2024), cited above.

### The "Backbiting" Trap

One of the most insidious forms of religious silencing: abusive parents claim that speaking about their abuse constitutes **gheebah** (backbiting), which is a sin in Islam.

- However, Islamic scholars distinguish between sins that violate others' rights (huqooq al-'ibad) — which should be exposed for justice — and sins against Allah alone, which may be concealed.
- The Hurma Project's research establishes that abuse falls into the category of violations against others' rights, making disclosure not only permissible but potentially obligatory.
  - **Source:** The Hurma Project, referenced in MuslimMatters (2024). [Link](https://hurmaproject.com/sample-spiritual-harm-abuse/)

## 6. Islamic Counter-Arguments to Abusive Interpretations

### What Authentic Islamic Teaching Emphasizes

For anyone writing about this topic, these are the Islamic principles that directly contradict the abusive distortion:

1. **Mercy (rahmah) is central to the faith.** The Prophet described himself as a mercy to all creation.

2. **"There is no harming nor reciprocating harm"** (la darar wa la dirar) — a foundational legal maxim.
   - **Source:** Hadith of the Prophet, cited in MuslimMatters (2024).

3. **Justice is obligatory regardless of the perpetrator's identity.** "Stand firm for justice as witnesses for Allah even if it is against yourselves" (Quran 4:135).

4. **Accountability applies to everyone.** No one — including parents — is exempt from the obligation to treat others with justice.

5. **Individual agency in religious matters.** The Quran repeatedly addresses the individual's conscience and relationship with God — it does not grant intermediaries (including parents) absolute authority over another person's faith.

6. **The prohibition against oppression (dhulm).** Oppression is one of the gravest sins in Islam, and it does not become permissible simply because the oppressor is a parent.

### The Core Reframe

The Islamic position, properly understood, is:

- You owe your parents **good treatment** (birr/ihsan) — always.
- You owe your parents **obedience** (ta'ah) — in lawful matters that do not harm you.
- You do NOT owe your parents **submission to abuse** — ever.
- Setting a boundary is not 'uquq (disobedience) — it is self-preservation, which Islam protects.
