# The Eldest Son: What They Took From You and How to Take It Back

*A Guide to Surviving Narcissistic Parents*

## PART ONE: THE INHERITANCE — What Was Done to You

*Before you can protect yourself, you have to understand the system that shaped you. Not to excuse it. Not to make peace with it. To see it clearly, without the fog of loyalty and guilt that your family spent years installing. What follows in these seven chapters is the map of the territory — the specific forces that formed you and the precise mechanisms through which they operated. Read this like an intelligence briefing. Knowing the enemy is not disloyalty. It is survival.*

## Chapter One: The Baro Chele

> *"Eldest children often feel an intense obligation to protect their family's reputation and younger siblings from parental disappointment. This role leaves little room for them to express vulnerability, as their own struggles might be seen as a burden to the family."*
> — Vishwani Sahai-Siddiqui, MD, *Behavioral Health News* (2024)

You were born with a sentence hanging over you. Not a crime you committed — a role you were assigned. In Bengali, the word for it is *baro chele*: eldest son. Two simple words that, in your family and in thousands of families like yours across Britain, Bangladesh, and the diaspora, carry the weight of a small nation.

Before you spoke your first word, decisions had already been made about what your life would mean. You would be the standard-bearer. The one who reflected well on the family. The proof that the sacrifice of immigration — the dislocation, the cold winters, the grinding work, the loneliness of a foreign country — had been worth something. You were not so much a person as a project. A proof of concept.

This is not your parents' invention. It is centuries old. The *baro chele* in South Asian culture carries a structural role that goes well beyond birth order. He is the family's public face and private pillar. He handles the practical burdens that would otherwise fall on his parents. He sets the example for younger siblings. He upholds *izzat* — the family honour — with every grade, every career choice, every relationship, every breath he takes in public. In immigrant families, he also carries the specific weight of a family that has crossed an ocean and needs to prove it made the right choice. His success is their vindication. His failure is their humiliation.

None of this, taken alone, is necessarily monstrous. Many eldest sons carry this burden and emerge from it with genuine resilience and purpose. The burden becomes a sentence only when the two things that should accompany it — the crown and the crown-maker — are both absent.

### The Burden Without the Crown

Here is what healthy eldest-son culture looks like: the boy carries significant responsibility, yes. But in return, he receives the *reward* of that position. He is cherished. He is the pride of the household. When relatives visit, he is brought forward and shown off. His opinion is sought. His achievements are celebrated. He is treated as special — not just obligated, but *seen*. The burden and the crown are inseparable. They are two sides of the same coin.

You received the burden. The crown never came.

You were the *baro chele* in terms of what was taken from you: your autonomy, your childhood, your right to be ordinary, your right to fail without consequence, your right to have feelings that were inconvenient. But you were not the *baro chele* in terms of what that status was supposed to provide. The warmth, the priority, the sense of being treasured — these things are conspicuously absent from the file.

This is the foundational double bind of your life. You were held to an eldest son's obligations while being denied an eldest son's inheritance. And because the cultural script insisted that this was normal — because everyone around you treated the arrangement as simply how things were — you had no reference point to see it for what it was. You just knew that you were failing. That no matter what you did, it wasn't enough. That love, in your family, was a debt you were perpetually behind on.

The question worth asking is: why? Why did the crown never come? The answer, when you trace it honestly, is not about you at all.

### The Father Who Couldn't Crown You

Your father was not the eldest son. He was the middle one — a fact that, in a Bengali household of his generation, carried its own specific weight. When his oldest brother married and took control of the family, it was that brother's wife who stepped in to raise your father and your uncle. She was abusive. Not the dramatic, film-worthy kind of abuse that people find easy to condemn — the quiet, grinding kind that teaches a child that love comes with cruelty attached, that the people responsible for your care will use you as they see fit, that you are not the priority.

Your father learned early that he was not the prince. He was the property.

A man who was never treated as a cherished son cannot model that treatment for his own children. He simply does not have the software. He can provide — financially, practically — because that he learned. He can enforce authority, because that was the only form of power he ever received. But the specific warmth of a father who looks at his eldest son and thinks *you are mine and I am proud of you and I will protect you* — that requires a template he was never given. He was giving you the best version of what he had. And what he had was broken.

This is not a defence of your father. Explanations are not excuses. But it is important to understand, with precision, why the crown never came. It was not because you were unworthy of it. It was because the man who should have placed it on your head had never held one.

### The Man Who Left

Your father's response to a family system that had damaged him was the only escape available to a Bengali man of his generation and background: he left. He joined the merchant marine. He went to sea.

For months at a stretch — six, sometimes twelve, sometimes eighteen — he was on the other side of the world. When he came home, he stayed for a few months, and then he left again. The pattern repeated throughout your childhood. Your mother was, for most of the years that mattered, a single parent. You were her eldest child. The structural consequence of this, in a household already shaped by a narcissistic mother, was enmeshment at the highest possible degree of intensity. She had no husband to lean on emotionally. She had you.

When your father returned from a voyage, you had to quickly recalibrate. A stranger had arrived who expected authority — authority he had not earned through presence, because he had not been present. You learned to manage this as you managed everything: by reading the room, adjusting your behaviour, becoming whoever the moment required. Comply when he's here. Survive without him when he's gone.

This is, it turns out, a perfect rehearsal for the fawn response. But we are getting ahead of ourselves.

What matters now is this: you grew up in a household where the primary attachment figure was the narcissistic mother, where the secondary figure appeared and disappeared without warning, and where neither of them gave you what you actually needed — which was, simply, to be seen.

### Seven Countries Before Puberty

You were born in Chandpur. You moved to England at four or five when your father was completing his second officer's certificate in Lowestoft. You went back to Bangladesh. You came back to England — Blackpool this time, 1999 — and you were eight years old and had already lived in two countries and spoken in two different social registers and adapted to two different versions of what was expected of you. The following year you were in Pennsylvania. Then back to England. Then Bangladesh again. Then England for good, at thirteen.

That is at least seven or eight international moves across three countries before you were old enough to sit a GCSE. After that, it continued: Blackpool, then Newcastle, then South Shields, then Stafford, then Southampton, then London.

There is a particular kind of person that this kind of childhood produces. Not a globally curious adventurer — that is the version of the story that sounds good at dinner parties. The real version is quieter and harder. It is someone who learned, in the laboratory conditions of extreme instability, that survival requires constant environmental scanning. Every new school, every new city, every new country, demanded a version of yourself that fit. A new accent, or the suppression of an old one. New cultural codes, or the erasure of the old ones. New friendships, built fast and abandoned faster because you knew from experience that you would leave again.

Each move was not just logistical displacement. It was a rehearsal for the erasure of self. You became expert at reading what the environment needed you to be, and becoming it. This is not resourcefulness in the ordinary sense. This is the *fawn response* in its purest developmental form — the systematic training of a child to adapt his identity to whatever will ensure acceptance. You were learning fawning before Pete Walker had named it, before the word existed in any book you would ever read.

And here is the thing that makes it a trap rather than a skill: unlike your peers, who had stable friendships and communities outside the family to give them alternative models of relationship, you had nothing. Every time you built something outside the family, the family moved. The only constant across all those countries and cities and schools was your family. And your family was the source of the harm.

When the prison is the only home you have ever known, escape is not a concept that makes sense. You adapt to the prison. You learn its rules. You become very, very good at surviving inside it.

That is the baro chele. Not the prince you were supposed to be. The prisoner who learned to call the cell home.

## Chapter Two: The Narcissistic Mother

> *"A narcissistic mother sees her son as an extension of herself — more than just a child, a proof of her worth. She does not love him. She owns him. The distinction is everything."*
> — Dr. Karyl McBride, *Will I Ever Be Good Enough?* (2008)

Let us begin with a distinction that your culture worked very hard to prevent you from making. There is a version of love that sees you — that perceives you as a separate person with your own feelings, your own needs, your own emerging identity — and nurtures that separateness as the most precious thing in the world. And there is a version of love that does not see you at all. That sees itself. That keeps you close not because you are cherished but because you are useful. That calls itself love loudly and publicly and in front of witnesses, while privately operating on the logic of ownership.

Your mother practised the second kind. She may not have known she was doing it. That is the cruelty of it: the damage is comprehensive whether it is consciously inflicted or not.

### The Engulfing Mother

Dr. Karyl McBride, who spent over two decades studying narcissistic mothers and their children, identifies two broad styles of narcissistic mothering: the engulfing kind and the ignoring kind. Your mother was primarily an engulfer. She did not ignore you — she smothered you. But the smothering was not in service of your development. It was in service of hers.

The engulfing narcissistic mother keeps her child close because distance threatens her. When you are close, she can monitor you. She can ensure that your identity remains an extension of hers. She can claim your achievements as evidence of her own worth and treat your failures as personal betrayals. She can pour her loneliness, her frustration, her needs for emotional companionship into you — because you are available, because you are pliable, and because you were conditioned from infancy to receive whatever she needed to deposit.

Your father was at sea for the better part of your childhood. Your mother, alone in a foreign country with young children, an absent husband, and her own unresolved wounds, needed an emotional anchor. You were the eldest. You became that anchor. She did not do this strategically — she did it because she was drowning, and you were the nearest thing to grab. But the damage is no less real for being unintentional. You were not her child so much as her husband. Her confidant. Her mirror. Her audience.

This is what the researchers call *mother-son enmeshment*, and in South Asian culture it has a particular cultural cover story that makes it almost impossible to challenge. The dominant cultural script says that closeness between mother and son is a virtue — that a son who is devoted to his mother is a good son, that physical and emotional proximity is simply how Bengali families express love. And within that script, the line between healthy closeness and pathological enmeshment becomes deliberately blurred, especially by mothers who benefit from that blurring.

The sign that tells you which side of the line you were on is this: healthy closeness builds your capacity for separateness. You feel loved *and* free. Enmeshment destroys that capacity. You feel loved only when you are compliant, only when you are present, only when you are providing what she needs. The moment you assert your own preferences — a friendship she didn't approve of, a relationship she hadn't sanctioned, an opinion that contradicted hers — the warmth was withdrawn. Not with a slap. With a chill. With a heaviness in the room that you had been trained from childhood to find intolerable.

### What She Actually Wants

The nine clinical traits of narcissistic personality disorder, when applied to a mother, tell a specific story. She cannot see her son as a separate person — she sees him as a reflection. When he succeeds, she succeeds. When he fails, she is humiliated. His achievements belong to her story; his failures are directed at her personally. She lacks the empathy to ask what *he* is feeling in any given moment, because the question that is always running, in the background of every interaction, is: *what does this mean for me?*

This creates a particular kind of crazy-making in childhood. You receive praise that is not really about you. You receive criticism that is not really about you either. The feedback loop between your actions and your mother's responses has nothing to do with who you actually are. It has to do with how you make her look, how you make her feel, and whether you are currently serving or threatening her sense of herself.

McBride identifies ten recurring dynamics in these relationships — what she calls the "Ten Stingers." A few of them will be familiar to the point of pain. You can never win her approval no matter what you achieve. She is envious of you — not just generally, but specifically, in moments when you are happy or successful in ways she is not. She violates the boundary between her emotional life and yours, using you as a container for feelings that should never have been your responsibility to hold. She talks about you to others in ways that serve her narrative, not yours. She is charming in public and cold in private. She makes you feel responsible for her wellbeing in a way that no child should ever be made to feel.

The result, across years and decades, is a son who has learned one lesson above all others: *your feelings do not matter. Hers do.* Your job is to manage her state, not your own. Your happiness is a threat because it might mean you need less of her. Your grief is inconvenient because it adds to her burden. Your anger is unacceptable because it might reveal the truth that neither of you is allowed to speak aloud.

### The Covert Dimension

There is a layer to your mother's narcissism that makes it harder to name and therefore harder to defend against. She was not the overt kind — not the screaming, obviously self-centred parent who makes everyone in the room uncomfortable and is easy to identify. The overt narcissist's mask is thin. It cracks constantly. People see it.

Your mother's mask was considerably thicker. She could be warm, generous, hospitable. She was beloved by relatives and neighbours who knew her on the surface. She was *nice*. And because she was nice, and because she was your *mother*, and because the cultural scaffolding insisted that mothers were to be honoured and not questioned — every abusive dynamic she ran operated under the cover of plausible deniability. When she withdrew emotionally, she had a reason. When she used guilt, she was just expressing how much she cared. When she inserted herself into your decisions, she was only trying to help.

Debbie Mirza, who interviewed over a hundred survivors of covert narcissistic relationships, captures the essential dynamic: the covert narcissist's greatest weapon is plausible deniability. Every act of cruelty is wrapped in enough ambiguity that you can never quite prove what just happened. You are usually the only person who sees their true nature — friends, family, and even therapists will side with the covert narcissist because they only ever experience the mask.

This is why you spent years wondering if you were imagining it. Why you kept finding yourself in the position of defending your own perceptions to people who looked at your mother and saw only someone devoted to her son.

### The Approval Trap: British Girls and Approved Marriages

Her behaviour around your relationships is where the ownership model becomes impossible to misread.

When you began noticing girls — British girls, girls from outside the community, girls who did not fit the cultural script she had written for your life — her response was not the protective concern of a parent who had genuine worries about cultural compatibility or religious values. It was visceral and disproportionate. It was the response of a woman who was being threatened.

She hated those relationships not because of who those girls were. She hated them because of what they represented: a version of you that was choosing, freely and independently, who to love. That is incompatible with the ownership model. The owned son does not choose his wife. His mother does.

When the semi-arranged marriage finally came, she approved — not because she evaluated your happiness or assessed the compatibility. She approved because the arrangement served her. A wife selected within the parameters she had approved was a wife who would exist within her field of control. Or so the logic went.

What she could not have fully calculated was that the woman she approved of had done a preliminary audit of your wounds — had seen, with the precision of someone who recognised the territory, exactly where you were most vulnerable — and filed that information away for later use. But that is a story for another part of this book.

### What She Couldn't Give

The psychologist Susan Forward puts it in terms that are worth sitting with: if you were a daughter of a mother who couldn't love, you go through your days with a cavernous gap in your confidence, a sense of emptiness, an inability to fully trust your ability to love. The same is true for sons, even if the culture works harder to disguise it. The mother wound in a son presents differently — as hypervigilance rather than insecurity, as over-caretaking rather than under-confidence — but the gap is the same.

You needed a mother who could see you. Who could reflect back to you, in her face and her voice and her choices, that you were valuable simply because you existed. Not because of what you produced. Not because of how you made her look. Simply because you were you, and you were hers, and that was enough.

She could not give you that. Not because you were unworthy of it. Because she did not have it to give.

## Chapter Three: The Controlling Father

> *"The passive parent who permits abuse shares responsibility for it. His absence was a form of consent."*
> — Susan Forward, *Toxic Parents* (1989)

There is a peculiar cruelty in the father who is almost there. The father who is completely absent is at least consistently absent — you can build your life around his absence, grieve it cleanly, and stop waiting. The father who appears and disappears, who is home just long enough to establish authority and then gone again before the authority has any warmth behind it — he is something else. He keeps the door of hope propped open just enough that you can never stop hoping.

Your father was that kind of almost-there.

### The Man He Was Before He Was Your Father

To understand him, you have to understand where he came from. He was the middle son, which in a Bengali household of his generation already meant a diminished status — not the cherished firstborn, not the indulged youngest, but the one in the middle who could be overlooked without anyone noticing. When his oldest brother married and was given control of both the family business and the raising of the younger children, your father and your uncle were effectively handed over to be raised by their sister-in-law.

She was abusive.

Not the dramatic, easily recognisable kind that stamps itself on memory like a photograph. The quiet kind. The kind that teaches a child, slowly and thoroughly over years, that love is conditional on compliance, that authority means cruelty, that you are not the priority and you never will be. Your father grew up in a house where he was not a prince — he was a burden tolerated by a woman who had not asked to raise someone else's children and made sure the children knew it.

This matters for you because of what it cost him. A man cannot give his son a crown he was never given. The warmth, the pride, the specific male tenderness of a father who looks at his boy and sees someone precious — that requires a template, a stored memory of having received something like it. Your father had no such memory. He learned that fathers are intermittent presences who enforce rules. He learned that love is a transaction. He learned that he was not important enough to be cherished. He passed this education on to you, not because he wanted to but because it was all he had.

Understanding this does not require you to forgive him. It does not require you to minimise the damage. It simply requires you to see it accurately. He was not withholding from you out of indifference. He was withholding because his cupboard was empty.

### The Sea as Escape

Your father solved his inherited trauma the way men of his generation and culture often solved their unbearable situations: he found a way out that was also a way in. The merchant marine gave him mobility, income, and legitimate absence. It let him leave the family system that had shaped him without having to officially leave it. He was not abandoning his responsibilities — he was fulfilling them, in the most practical and culturally acceptable way, by sending money home. The physical fact of his absence was reframed as the material proof of his dedication.

But what his absence actually meant, for the child left behind, was something quite different. It meant that the narcissistic mother had you entirely to herself for stretches of six months, twelve months, eighteen months at a time. It meant that when he returned, you had to rapidly readjust to a man who expected authority that he had not earned through presence. It meant that you learned a specific lesson about male power: it arrives without warning, demands compliance, and disappears again. Comply when he's here. Manage without him when he's gone.

This is not an abstract psychological observation. It mapped directly onto how you learned to relate to male authority figures for the rest of your life. The fawn response — the adaptive strategy of reading the threat, making yourself palatable, surviving by management — was as much about him as about her. Just in a different register.

### The Passive Parent's Complicity

Susan Forward is direct about this in a way that most books about toxic parents are not: the passive parent who permits abuse shares responsibility for it. The parent who does not protect when protection is required has made a choice, even if that choice was made out of conflict avoidance or genuine limitation. The result for the child is identical — there is no one standing between you and the harm.

Your father was not present enough to protect you. And on the occasions when he was present, he brought his own controlling tendencies — the authoritarian edge, the financial leverage, the expectation of compliance — rather than the protection that a father is supposed to provide.

The money from the Chandpur land is worth examining in this light. Twenty thousand pounds is not a gift in a context like yours. It is a claim. Land in Bangladesh, in British Bangladeshi families, functions as a long-term control mechanism — a visible, tangible asset that the family can offer or withhold, bestow or threaten to remove. When that money arrived after significant conflict, it arrived with strings attached that didn't need to be articulated because both parties understood the language. It was not generosity. It was a leash with a longer lead.

### What You Needed from Him

You needed a father who stood between you and the narcissistic mother's engulfment. A man who, by his simple male presence and authority, would have limited her ability to pour her emotional life into you. You needed someone who showed you, by example, what it looked like to hold authority without cruelty, to be strong without being cold, to love without conditions. You needed to be protected.

Instead you got an intermittent figure who appeared with expectations already formed and disappeared before the relationship could become anything resembling a bond. You got a man whose own trauma made him unable to see your needs because he had spent a lifetime not being allowed to see his own.

The compassion here is real. What he gave you — within the limits of a broken inheritance — was probably the best he had. But it was not enough. And the gap between what you needed and what you received has been shaping your decisions ever since.

## Chapter Four: The Religion They Weaponised

> *"There is no obedience to a creature when it involves disobedience to the Creator."*
> — Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, Musnad Ahmad 1098

Before we go further, we need to address the argument that has probably followed you your entire adult life — the one that appears whenever you attempt to create any distance from the dysfunction, whenever you assert any need of your own, whenever you decline to sacrifice your wellbeing on the altar of family harmony.

*But they're your parents. What about birrul walidayn?*

This argument deserves a full and precise answer. Not because the people deploying it against you have earned the precision — they have weaponised a sincere religious obligation in order to prevent you from asking legitimate questions, and they do not deserve the courtesy of careful scholarship. But you deserve it. Because there is a version of you that has genuinely internalised the argument, that carries real religious guilt, that is quietly afraid that setting any limit on your parents' treatment of you constitutes a sin against God. That version of you needs to be able to distinguish between what Islam actually teaches and what abusive parents in Muslim families claim it teaches.

They are not the same thing.

### What the Quran Actually Says

The primary Quranic text on parental rights is Surah Al-Isra, verses 23 and 24. Allah commands good treatment (*ihsan*) toward parents, uses the word "noble" (*kariman*) to describe how they should be addressed, and instructs the believer to show them humility and mercy. These verses are genuine and their authority is not in question. The obligation to treat your parents with kindness is real.

But ten chapters later, in Surah Luqman, verse 15, the Quran provides the crucial qualification that abusive parents almost never quote. Addressing the situation where parents command something that contradicts God's commands, the verse says: *do not obey them*. Even in that case — even when you are disobeying a direct parental command — the verse continues: *remain with them in this world with due fairness.*

Do you see what the Quran has done here? It has drawn an explicit distinction between two separate obligations that abusive parents deliberately collapse into one. The first obligation is *ta'ah* — obedience — which is conditional. It applies to lawful commands that do not require harm to yourself or sin before God. The second obligation is *ihsan* — good treatment, kindness, respect — which is unconditional. You owe your parents dignity even when you disobeying their commands.

These are not the same thing. You can refuse to comply with a harmful or unjust command while still treating your parents with respect. Islam does not demand that you submit to abuse in order to honour your parents. The scholar Justin Parrott, writing for MuslimMatters and drawing on the consensus of classical scholarship, states it plainly: "It is not an obligation upon children to obey both of their parents in every matter they command or prohibit, by agreement of the scholars."

### What the Scholars Said

Across all four major Sunni schools of jurisprudence, the same consensus emerges. Ibn Taymiyyah, one of the most authoritative voices in the Hanbali tradition, ruled that parents have no right to force their children into marriages they do not accept, and explicitly stated that a parent who attempts to sabotage their child's marriage commits a grave sin. Al-Ghazali, whose position on parental obedience is relatively conservative, limits the obligation to "doubtful matters" — matters where the harm or benefit is unclear. He does not extend it to clearly harmful commands. The Hanafi platform SeekersGuidance draws the distinction precisely: *ta'ah* is limited to lawful commands; *ihsan* — the kindness and respect — remains unconditional.

The hadith that abusive parents rely on — that filial disobedience is among the major sins — is real. So is the hadith that directly limits its application: "There is no obedience to a creature when it involves disobedience to the Creator." And so is the principle, embedded in classical Islamic legal maxims, that *la darar wa la dirar* — there is no harming and no reciprocating harm. No one — not a parent, not a spouse, not anyone — has an Islamic right to harm you. The prohibition on oppression (*dhulm*) does not have a family exemption.

Nouman Ali Khan, who may be the most widely followed English-language Quran educator alive today, addressed this question directly in a public talk. He was asked about dealing with abusive parents. His answer: "Islam doesn't say that even if parents are monsters, you should still be good to them." He continued: "Every human being has a right to defend themselves and the right to defend their own dignity, and has the right to seek protection."

That is not the message your family delivered to you. The message your family delivered was that *ihsan* and *ta'ah* were identical — that any failure to comply was a failure of faith — and that the threat of *'uquq al-walidayn* (disobedience to parents) as a major sin applied to every time you failed to give them what they wanted. This is a distortion. It is a deliberate conflation of two separate concepts by people who benefit from you not knowing the difference.

### The Six Conditions

Islamic scholars identify specific conditions under which disobeying a parental command is not merely permitted but required. They include: commands that involve sin; commands motivated by unreasonableness or ignorance that harm the child; commands that are completely foolish and harmful; commands hostile to the child's life, wealth, or honour; violence or intolerable abuse; and situations where compliance would constitute oppression of yourself or others.

Read that list again. Not as a theological exercise. As a map.

### The Backbiting Trap

There is one more piece of religious manipulation that deserves naming before we move on. When you speak honestly about what happened to you — to a therapist, to a trusted friend, in a journal, or in a book like this one — the accusation of *gheebah* (backbiting) may arise. To speak ill of someone, even truthfully, is generally prohibited in Islam. Your family may have used this, implicitly or explicitly, to keep you from disclosing the abuse. This is a trap that requires careful Islamic ethics to navigate.

The scholars distinguish between two categories of wrongdoing: sins against God (*haqqullah*) and violations of others' rights (*huqooq al-'ibad*). Sins against God should be concealed — you do not broadcast someone else's private transgressions. But violations of others' rights — abuse, injustice, harm — may not only be disclosed but must sometimes be disclosed in order for justice to be served. The academic ethics project Hurma, which studies abuse in Islamic contexts, is explicit: abuse falls into the category of violations against others' rights, making disclosure not only permissible but potentially obligatory in some circumstances.

You are not sinning by naming what was done to you. You are engaged in the Islamically protected right of seeking protection for yourself and your family.

### The Core Reframe

Here is what authentic Islamic teaching, stripped of its weaponised distortion, actually says about your situation:

You owe your parents *good treatment*. Always. In every circumstance.

You owe your parents *obedience* in lawful matters that do not harm you.

You do not owe your parents *submission to abuse*. Not ever. Not in any school of jurisprudence, not in any era of Islamic scholarship, not in any reading of the Quran that is remotely honest.

Setting a boundary is not *'uquq*. It is self-preservation — and Islam, in its foundational principles, protects the right of every human being to preserve their life, their sanity, their dignity, and their faith.

## Chapter Five: The Operating System

> *"The family system is not something your parents invented. It is accumulated dysfunction passed down from ancestors like a multi-car pileup on the freeway."*
> — Susan Forward, *Toxic Parents* (1989)

Everything that has been described in the preceding chapters — your mother's engulfment, your father's intermittent control, the religious guilt, the baro chele burden — does not operate as a collection of isolated events. It operates as a *system*. A self-reinforcing, internally consistent, extraordinarily effective system for producing one specific outcome: a child who is compliant, self-erasing, and incapable of imagining that his own needs are legitimate.

Understanding the system does not require you to be a psychologist. It requires only that you look at the pattern, name what you see, and stop mistaking the pattern for reality.

### The Architecture

Susan Forward, in her foundational work on toxic parents, describes how every dysfunctional family maintains its dysfunction through a shared architecture: beliefs generate rules, rules generate blind obedience, and blind obedience is enforced by the family's five weapons — denial, projection, sabotage, triangulation, and keeping secrets.

In your family, the beliefs were largely unspoken. They included things like: the family's needs come before any individual's needs; showing negative emotions is weakness; asking for help is selfishness; parents are always right by virtue of being parents; the outside world must see a particular image of this family; and to challenge any of this is to betray everyone who sacrificed to bring you here.

These beliefs were never written down or stated explicitly. They were communicated in the hundred small interactions of everyday life — in the look that your mother gave when you said something that threatened the image, in the silence that fell when your father's authority was even slightly questioned, in the way that family problems were never discussed and never resolved but also never went away.

The rules that emerged from these beliefs were equally unspoken. McBride, drawing on decades of work with narcissistic families, identifies four: *don't show feelings*, *maintain the perfect image*, *keep secrets*, and *never challenge the narcissist*. Your family probably did not call these rules. They did not need to. Everyone in the household knew them with the certainty of carved stone.

Do not show feelings. This is the first and most comprehensive rule. Feelings — specifically your feelings, as distinct from your mother's feelings — were implicitly forbidden. They were inconvenient. They required her to respond empathically rather than reactively. They introduced complexity into the emotional ecosystem that she was managing for her own benefit. When you were distressed, she was more likely to become distressed herself than to attend to yours. When you were angry, the anger had to be about something you had done wrong, not something that had been done to you. You learned very early that having feelings was a liability, and so you buried them.

Maintain the perfect image. The family presented a specific face to the world, and that face required collective maintenance. Your academic performance, your behaviour in public, your relationships, your career trajectory — all of these were never just about you. They were about what they said about the family. A bad result at school was not a learning opportunity; it was an embarrassment to manage. A conflict with a teacher or an employer was not evidence of a legitimate grievance; it was a reputation risk. The image had to be protected because the image was what stood between the family and the admission that something was deeply wrong.

Keep secrets. The other side of the image maintenance was the silence within. What happened inside the family stayed inside the family. This was enforced not by specific command but by atmosphere — by the understanding that to speak honestly about the family dynamics would be to violate an unwritten covenant whose consequences were vague but serious. You learned that the family's private reality was not to be shared. Not with teachers. Not with friends. Not with doctors. Not with yourself, if you could possibly avoid it.

Never challenge the narcissist. This is the rule that undergirds all the others. Your mother's emotional state was the ambient condition that everyone else's life organised around. When she was content, the household breathed. When she was unhappy — and the triggers for her unhappiness were numerous and unpredictable — the household contracted. Everyone moved carefully. Everyone became very skilled at anticipating her needs. And no one, under any circumstances, challenged her version of events or her right to feel however she felt about whatever she felt it about.

### The Four Weapons of Emotional Control

Lindsay Gibson identifies the specific coercions that emotionally immature parents use to maintain the system. They are worth naming precisely because you have been subject to all four and are still, if you examine your current inner landscape carefully, still responding to them in ways that have outlived their usefulness.

*Self-doubt* is the most insidious. Every time you thought independently — every time your own assessment of a situation diverged from the family's assessment — the system punished that divergence by withdrawing warmth, by expressing hurt, by making you feel that your independent perception was a betrayal. Over years, this teaches you to distrust your own perceptions. To defer to the family's version of events. To wonder, in the face of clear evidence, whether you are imagining things. This is not a character flaw. It is the predictable result of a system designed to produce exactly that uncertainty.

*Fear* operated through your mother's emotional states — the volatility, the sudden shifts in temperature, the moments of withdrawal or explosion that created an environment of chronic low-level vigilance. You never knew quite when the atmosphere would change. So you learned to monitor it constantly. This is hypervigilance, and it is one of the defining features of Complex PTSD, which Pete Walker spent thirty years studying and treating.

*Guilt* was deployed with particular sophistication in a Bengali Muslim household, because the cultural and religious frameworks provided it with so much supporting material. The sacrifice of immigration. The suffering of parents who did everything for you. The specific weight of the *baro chele* obligation. The religious command to honour your parents. Every legitimate need you had could be made to feel like an act of ingratitude simply by invoking these frameworks in the right tone.

*Shame* is the deepest weapon and the hardest to dislodge. Shame is different from guilt in a critical way: guilt says *I did something wrong*, while shame says *I am something wrong*. The narcissistic family system installs shame at the identity level. Not just *your behaviour is unacceptable* but *you are unacceptable* — too needy, too emotional, too much, not enough. Over years, this shame becomes invisible, internalised so thoroughly that it feels like self-knowledge rather than installed programming. It feels like you are simply being accurate about yourself.

You are not. You are reading the programme.

### The Role-Self and the True Self

Susan Forward describes what she calls the "role-self" — the identity the child builds to survive inside the toxic family system. It is not a lie, exactly. It is a necessary adaptation. But it is not the whole person. Behind the compliant eldest son who manages everyone's emotions and asks for nothing and never challenges anyone and maintains the image — behind that person — there is someone else. Someone who has needs. Someone who has opinions. Someone who is angry, sometimes, about things that deserve anger. Someone who wants things — not just the things the family approves of, but his own things, chosen by his own lights.

The tragedy of the toxic family system is that it is extraordinarily effective at suppressing that second person while convincing the first person that he is the whole. The role-self becomes so practised, so automatic, so much the default mode of engagement with the world, that the child — and then the adult — genuinely cannot distinguish between what he wants and what the system has trained him to want. His own desires are experienced as foreign objects. His own anger is the emotion he is most afraid of. His own needs are the things he is least equipped to name.

Aziz Gazipura, in his work on the psychology of approval-seeking, describes what he calls *nice training* — the childhood process by which parents condition obedience, politeness, and conflict avoidance through approval and withdrawal cycles. Children learn a list of qualities that earn love (agreeable, quiet, achieving) and a list that triggers withdrawal (outspoken, angry, defiant, needy). The approval seeker that emerges from this conditioning carries two objectives into every interaction for the rest of his life: avoid all judgment and criticism, and earn positive regard. These objectives run unconsciously. They are not chosen. They are installed. And they are running right now, in every interaction, as you read these words.

### The Approval Trap

The approval trap is the specific mechanism by which the system perpetuates itself across decades. The logic is simple, elegant, and devastating: compliance equals acceptance. Every time you complied — every time you swallowed your feelings, maintained the image, kept the secrets, failed to challenge — you received a small signal of approval. The temperature in the room improved slightly. Your mother relaxed. Your father was less critical. The peace held, for a little while.

And every time you failed to comply — every time the real self leaked out, every time you said something that didn't fit the script, every time you made a choice that was yours rather than theirs — the warmth was withdrawn. Not usually dramatically. Usually subtly. But you had been trained from infancy to feel that subtle withdrawal as catastrophic, because for an infant, withdrawal of the caregiver's warmth literally is catastrophic. The nervous system does not know that you are an adult now. The nervous system responds to withdrawn approval with the same alarm it felt when you were four years old and entirely dependent on these people for survival.

This is why the trap is so hard to escape even after you understand it intellectually. The understanding operates at the level of cognition. The trap operates at the level of the nervous system. Intellectual understanding is necessary — this book is full of it — but it is not sufficient on its own. That is a subject for later chapters.

For now, it is enough to name the system clearly. You did not choose this programme. It was chosen for you, before you had the vocabulary or the life experience to evaluate or resist it. The choices you made from inside the programme — the relationships you entered, the opportunities you declined, the person you slowly became — were not free choices in any meaningful sense. They were the logical outputs of a system that was designed to produce exactly the person it produced.

The first act of genuine freedom is seeing the system for what it is.

You are seeing it now.
