## 1. DARVO (Deny, Attack, Reverse Victim and Offender)

### Origin and Research

DARVO was first identified and named by **Dr. Jennifer Freyd**, a professor of psychology at the University of Oregon, in a 1997 publication connected to her primary research focus on **betrayal trauma theory**. The acronym describes a three-stage reaction pattern that perpetrators of interpersonal violence use when confronted about their abusive behaviour.

- **Deny** -- The perpetrator denies the behaviour occurred at all ("That never happened," "You're making things up")
- **Attack** -- The perpetrator attacks the credibility, character, or motives of the person confronting them ("You're crazy," "You're just trying to destroy me")
- **Reverse Victim and Offender** -- The perpetrator assumes the victim role and reframes the actual victim as the aggressor ("Look what you're doing to me," "You're the abusive one")

Sources:
- [Jennifer Freyd's DARVO page](https://www.jjfreyd.com/darvo)
- [DARVO - Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DARVO)

### Key Research Findings

**Harsey, Zurbriggen, & Freyd (2017) -- Journal of Aggression, Maltreatment & Trauma:**

An experiment with 316 university students assessed the effects of a perpetrator's use of DARVO on perceptions of credibility and responsibility. Key findings:

- Participants exposed to DARVO **perceived the victim as less believable**, more responsible for the violence, and more abusive
- DARVO led participants to judge the **perpetrator as less abusive and less responsible**
- The more DARVO a perpetrator used during confrontation, the more **victims reported feeling blameworthy** for the wrongdoing
- DARVO is empirically confirmed as an effective strategy for discrediting victims

Source: [Deny, Attack, and Reverse Victim and Offender (DARVO): What Is the Influence on Perceived Perpetrator and Victim Credibility? -- Tandfonline](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/10926771.2020.1774695)

**Harsey & Freyd (2023) -- Journal of Interpersonal Violence:**

This follow-up study found that DARVO-educated participants (compared with a control group) **rated the perpetrator as less believable**, suggesting that education about DARVO can mitigate its manipulative power.

Source: [The Influence of DARVO and Insincere Apologies on Perceptions of Sexual Assault -- PubMed](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37154429/)

### How DARVO Operates in Close Relationships

In intimate and family relationships, DARVO is particularly devastating because:

- The victim already has an emotional bond with the perpetrator, making them more susceptible to self-blame
- Repeated use over years rewires the victim's perception of reality (intersection with gaslighting)
- Children who grow up with DARVO-using parents internalise self-blame as a default response
- When a spouse uses DARVO, the victim learns that raising concerns about mistreatment will result in punishment, training them into silence

### Clinical Significance

DARVO is now widely recognised in domestic violence research as a hallmark behaviour of abusers. Its effectiveness relies on the victim's attachment to the perpetrator and their desire to maintain the relationship -- the stronger the bond, the more powerful the manipulation.

## 2. The JADE Trap (Justify, Argue, Defend, Explain)

### Origin

JADE has its roots in **Al-Anon literature** (the support group for families of alcoholics). The concept was later adopted by the personality disorder and narcissistic abuse recovery communities, particularly through the website **Out of the FOG** (outofthefog.website).

- **Justify** -- Explaining your reasons for a decision or boundary
- **Argue** -- Engaging in logical debate to prove your point
- **Defend** -- Protecting your position against accusations
- **Explain** -- Providing context or rationale for your behaviour

Sources:
- [Out of the FOG -- JADE](https://outofthefog.website/what-not-to-do-1/2015/12/3/jade-dont-justify-argue-defend-explain)
- [Sherry Gaba, LCSW -- Stop JADE-ing](https://sherrygaba.com/stop-jade-ing-why-you-cant-justify-argue-defend-or-explain-with-a-narcissist/)

### Why JADE Fails with Narcissistic Abusers

The core insight is that **logic, reasoning, and heartfelt explanations do not work with someone who is committed to misunderstanding you**. With a narcissist:

- Arguments are a source of **narcissistic supply** -- they feed off your emotional reactions
- Every justification becomes proof that they can control your emotions
- Every defence you offer confirms they still have psychological power over you
- Circular arguments are fuelled by one party's emotional reasoning and perpetuated by the other party's JADEing (Dr. Tara Palmatier, Shrink4Men)

Source: [Circular Arguments, Emotional Reasoning and JADE -- Shrink4Men](https://shrink4men.com/2018/04/27/circular-arguments-emotional-reasoning-and-jade-justify-argue-defend-explain/)

### How to Break the JADE Trap

The recommended approach is to **state your position once and only once**, then disengage:

- Replace justification with a simple statement: "This is my decision"
- Replace arguing with silence or repetition of the boundary (the "broken record" technique)
- Replace defending with acknowledgement without engagement: "I hear that you see it differently"
- Replace explaining with acceptance that they do not need to understand for your decision to be valid

The key shift: **You do not need the other person's agreement, understanding, or permission to set a boundary.**

Source: [Avalon Malibu -- Breaking Your JADE Habit](https://www.avalonmalibu.com/blog/breaking-your-jade-habit-to-find-self-empowerment)

## 3. Emotional Blackmail -- FOG (Fear, Obligation, Guilt)

### Clinical Research and Origin

The term **emotional blackmail** was defined and popularised by **Dr. Susan Forward** (clinical therapist) and Donna Frazier in their 1997 book *Emotional Blackmail: When the People in Your Life Use Fear, Obligation and Guilt to Manipulate You*.

**FOG** is a contrived acronym -- a deliberate play on the word "fog" -- describing something that **obscures and confuses a person's thought processes**:

- **Fear** -- "If you don't do what I want, something terrible will happen"
- **Obligation** -- "After everything I've done for you, you owe me this"
- **Guilt** -- "How can you be so selfish/ungrateful/cold?"

Sources:
- [BPD Family -- Emotional Blackmail: Fear, Obligation and Guilt](https://www.bpdfamily.com/content/emotional-blackmail-fear-obligation-and-guilt-fog)
- [Emotional Blackmail -- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emotional_blackmail)

### Forward's Framework

Forward's framework is largely **cognitive-behavioural**: she demonstrates how certain underlying dysfunctional beliefs make a person vulnerable to an emotional blackmailer, and how changing one's thinking can lead to healthier emotional reactions.

**Four Types of Blackmailers:**

| Type | Strategy | Example |
|------|----------|---------|
| **Punishers** | Let you know exactly what they want and the consequences if you refuse | "If you leave, I'll make sure you never see the kids again" |
| **Self-Punishers** | Turn threats inward -- what they'll do to *themselves* if you don't comply | "If you go out tonight, I don't know what I'll do to myself" |
| **Sufferers** | Talented blamers and guilt-peddlers who make you figure out what they want | Heavy sighs, silent treatment, "I guess I'll just sit here alone" |
| **Tantalizers** | Hold out promises of something wonderful if you comply | "If you just do this for me, I'll finally be the partner you want" |

Source: [Amazon -- Emotional Blackmail by Susan Forward](https://www.amazon.com/Emotional-Blackmail-People-Obligation-Manipulate/dp/0060928972)

### FOG in Narcissistic Parent-Child Dynamics

FOG is especially potent in parent-child relationships because:

- Children are biologically wired to attach to their caregivers regardless of treatment
- The obligation component is culturally reinforced ("Honour thy father and mother")
- Fear is primal -- a child's survival depends on parental approval
- Guilt is installed early: "I sacrificed everything for you"
- In **collectivist and honour cultures** (South Asian, East Asian, Middle Eastern), FOG operates with cultural reinforcement that makes it nearly invisible as abuse

### FOG in Marriage

In a marriage with a covert narcissist, FOG often operates as:

- **Fear**: Fear of explosive anger, withdrawal of affection, financial punishment, or custody threats
- **Obligation**: "I provide for this family, the least you can do is..." / "A good wife/husband would..."
- **Guilt**: "You're tearing this family apart" / "The children need both parents under one roof"

## 4. Covert Narcissism vs. Overt Narcissism

### Research Background

The distinction between grandiose (overt) and vulnerable (covert) narcissism is well-established in clinical literature. A landmark paper on the "Two Faces of Narcissism" by Paul Wink (1991) identified these as distinct but related constructs.

Source: [Scott Barry Kaufman -- Two Faces of Narcissism (PDF)](https://scottbarrykaufman.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/Two-faces-of-Narcissism.pdf)

### Key Differences

| Dimension | Overt Narcissist | Covert Narcissist |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Presentation** | Grandiose, loud, commanding | Quiet, self-effacing, "nice" |
| **Entitlement** | Openly demands special treatment | Passive-aggressively expects it |
| **Empathy deficit** | Visible -- dismisses others' feelings | Hidden -- appears empathetic but is performing |
| **Emotional stability** | More stable (high self-esteem) | More neurotic, higher emotional dysregulation (2017 study) |
| **Response to criticism** | Rage, counterattack | Sulking, withdrawal, silent treatment, victimhood |
| **Attachment style** | Dismissive | Anxious/fearful (fear of rejection) |
| **Relationship pattern** | Openly controlling | Subtly controlling through guilt, martyrdom, helplessness |

Sources:
- [Psychology Today -- How Covert Narcissism Differs From Overt Narcissism](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/blog/understanding-ptsd/202601/how-covert-narcissism-differs-from-overt-narcissism)
- [HelpGuide -- How to Identify and Deal with a Covert Narcissist](https://www.helpguide.org/mental-health/personality-disorders/covert-narcissism)

### How Covert Narcissists Operate in Marriages

Covert narcissists are often the more dangerous type in intimate relationships precisely because their abuse is difficult to identify:

- **Idealize-Devalue-Discard Cycle**: They cycle between idealizing their partner early in the relationship and devaluing them later when the partner no longer fulfills the narcissist's need for validation
- **Emotional Distance**: They can be emotionally distant and self-centred, leading to strained relationships over time
- **Passive Aggression**: Instead of overt demands, they use silence, sulking, "forgetting," and withdrawal as punishment
- **Martyr Complex**: "I do everything for this family and no one appreciates me" -- positions themselves as the victim while controlling through guilt
- **Plausible Deniability**: Their abuse is subtle enough that when the victim tries to describe it to others, it sounds trivial -- "He just gets quiet sometimes" does not elicit the same response as "He screams at me"

Source: [Center for Shared Insight -- Covert Narcissism: Recognizing Subtle Signs](https://www.centerforsharedinsight.com/blog/covert-narcissism-recognizing-subtle-signs-relationships/46143)

### Why Covert Narcissism Is Harder to Identify

- The covert narcissist often appears to outsiders as kind, generous, and selfless
- Victims frequently doubt their own reality because the abuse doesn't match the cultural image of "an abuser"
- Therapists may initially side with the covert narcissist because they present as the "reasonable" one
- The victim's anger and frustration (a natural response to years of covert abuse) may be misidentified as the problem

## 5. Moving the Goalposts

### What It Is

Moving the goalposts is a **manipulation tactic in which the narcissist changes the criteria for success, approval, or resolution after the target has already met the original conditions**. It is classified as a logical fallacy when used in argument, but in relationships it functions as a control mechanism.

Source: [Neurolaunch -- Narcissists' Goal Post Moving](https://neurolaunch.com/moving-the-goal-post-narcissist/)

### How It Works in Practice

The pattern typically follows this cycle:

1. The narcissist states a demand or condition: "If you just did X, everything would be fine"
2. The target works hard to meet the condition
3. Upon meeting it, the narcissist shifts to a new demand: "Well, you did X but you didn't do Y"
4. The target works to meet Y
5. The narcissist shifts again: "Y was okay, but now the real problem is Z"
6. The cycle repeats indefinitely

### Specific Manifestations

- **Downplaying achievements**: When the victim meets the narcissist's demands, the narcissist minimises the accomplishment ("That's not what I meant" / "You only did it because I told you to")
- **Combined with gaslighting**: The narcissist denies previous discussions, agreements, or promises, leaving the victim questioning their own memory
- **Creating a lose-lose situation**: No matter what the victim does, they remain in an endless cycle of failure and blame
- **Maintaining perpetual insecurity**: The target can never achieve the approval or validation they seek, keeping them in a constant state of striving

Sources:
- [Flying Monkeys Denied -- Moving the Goalposts](https://flyingmonkeysdenied.com/glossary/moving-the-goalposts/)
- [Our Levitated Minds -- 7 Ways Narcissists Shift the Goalposts](https://ourlevitatedminds.com/7-ways-narcissists-shift-the-goalposts-to-manipulate-and-control-you/)

### Why Victims Stay Trapped

The trap works because:

- The initial demand sounds **reasonable** -- "If you just communicated better" / "If you were more organised"
- Each individual shift is small enough to seem like a valid concern
- The victim is trained (often from childhood) to believe that if they just try harder, things will improve
- The pattern exploits the victim's **fawn response** -- their deep need to earn love through performance

## 6. Weaponized Incompetence

### Definition

**Weaponized incompetence** (also called **strategic incompetence**) is when someone knowingly or unknowingly performs tasks so poorly that others stop asking them to help, thereby shifting the entire burden of responsibility onto the other person.

Source: [Psychology Today -- Weaponized Incompetence](https://www.psychologytoday.com/us/basics/weaponized-incompetence)

### How It Functions as Abuse

While occasionally unintentional, weaponized incompetence crosses into emotional abuse when:

- It is part of a **repeated pattern** used to manipulate or control
- It creates a **severe imbalance of power** where one partner carries all domestic, emotional, and logistical labour
- It is combined with other tactics: if the victim complains, the abuser uses DARVO ("You're so controlling") or guilt ("I can never do anything right for you")

Sources:
- [Therapist.com -- Weaponized Incompetence: Meaning, Signs, and Impact](https://therapist.com/behaviors/weaponized-incompetence/)
- [South Lake Counseling -- Weaponized Incompetence in Relationships](https://southlakecounseling.com/weaponized-incompetence-in-relationships-what-it-is-and-how-to-handle-it/)

### Research on Gendered Patterns

A 2024 study found that **domestic cognitive labour** (planning, remembering, organising household tasks) functions as another form of "doing gender":

- Mothers hold most daily family tasks while fathers handle episodic maintenance
- This unequal distribution is often maintained through strategic incompetence
- The pattern is reinforced by childhood socialisation: boys raised to prioritise academic/financial achievement while domestic competence is neglected

Source: [Find My Therapist -- Weaponized Incompetence: What It Is and How to Respond](https://findmytherapist.com/resources/relationships/weaponized-incompetence/)

### Connection to Covert Narcissism

In marriages with a covert narcissist, weaponized incompetence serves multiple functions:

- **Maintains control** while appearing helpless
- **Forces the victim into a caretaker role** (reinforcing fawn patterns from childhood)
- **Creates plausible deniability**: "I'm not controlling -- I'm just not good at that stuff"
- **Generates resentment** that the narcissist then uses against the victim: "You're always angry"

## 7. The Cycle of Abuse

### Origin and Research

The **cycle of abuse** (also called the **cycle of violence**) was developed in 1979 by **Dr. Lenore E. Walker**, a psychologist who interviewed 1,500 women who had experienced domestic violence. She identified a recurring four-phase pattern.

Source: [Cycle of Abuse -- Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycle_of_abuse)

### The Four Phases

**Phase 1: Tension Building**
- Stress and conflict gradually escalate
- The abuser becomes increasingly irritable, critical, or emotionally withdrawn
- The victim walks on eggshells, trying to prevent the explosion
- Minor incidents of verbal abuse, put-downs, or passive aggression increase

**Phase 2: The Incident (Acute Abuse)**
- The abuser's anger reaches a boiling point
- This can involve physical, emotional, sexual, or financial abuse
- In covert narcissistic abuse, this phase may look like: explosive rage episodes, extended silent treatment, cruel verbal attacks, or major acts of sabotage

**Phase 3: Reconciliation (The "Honeymoon" Phase)**
- The abuser may apologise, minimise their actions, or show affection
- Promises of change: "It will never happen again"
- May agree to counselling, buy gifts, or create a seemingly normal atmosphere
- The abuser may blame external factors: stress, alcohol, the victim's behaviour

**Phase 4: Calm**
- The relationship enters a relatively peaceful period
- The abuser may be genuinely pleasant and attentive
- The victim begins to believe the worst is over
- This phase reinforces the victim's hope and makes leaving more difficult

Sources:
- [Cleveland Clinic -- What Is the Cycle of Abuse and How Do You Break It?](https://health.clevelandclinic.org/cycle-of-abuse)
- [Psych Central -- The 4 Stages of the Cycle of Abuse](https://psychcentral.com/health/cycle-of-abuse)

### Why the Cycle Traps Victims

- The calm/honeymoon phase creates **intermittent reinforcement** -- the most powerful form of psychological conditioning (same mechanism as gambling addiction)
- Each cycle erodes the victim's self-trust: "Maybe it really was my fault" / "See, they can be loving"
- Over time, the calm phase typically **shortens** while the tension and incident phases **lengthen**
- The cycle trains victims to manage the abuser's emotions (hypervigilance) rather than attend to their own needs

### Important Criticisms

Walker's model has faced academic criticism:

- It was based on research with a specific population and may not apply universally
- Not all abusive relationships follow a clear cyclical pattern -- some involve constant low-grade abuse without dramatic incidents
- The model may overemphasise physical violence and underrepresent emotional/psychological abuse patterns
- Covert narcissistic abuse often lacks a clear "incident" phase -- the abuse is continuous and ambient

Source: [DVSN -- The Cycle of Abuse: Imperfect Model or Useful Tool?](https://www.dvsn.org/november-2022-the-cycle-of-abuse-imperfect-model-or-useful-tool/)

## 8. How Abusers Use Children as Leverage

### Research Findings

Research consistently shows that abusers use children as instruments of control both during relationships and after separation.

**During the Relationship:**
- Children are used as emotional pawns: "Look what you're doing to the kids"
- The abuser triangulates: confiding in children about the other parent, creating loyalty conflicts
- Children are weaponised to enforce compliance: "If you don't do what I want, I'll tell the kids what you really are"
- The abuser positions themselves as the "fun parent" while the victim handles all discipline and logistics

**Post-Separation Coercive Control:**
- **Parental alienating behaviours**: bad-mouthing, lying, guilting, or rewarding children for rejecting the other parent
- Research shows parental alienation and coercive control are "two sides of the same coin" -- they are the same dynamic expressed through the parent-child relationship
- **Litigation abuse**: repeated, frivolous family court filings used to maintain control, drain resources, and punish the victim for leaving
- **False parental alienation claims**: abusive parents may weaponise the concept of parental alienation to discredit protective parents

Sources:
- [Colorado State University -- Parental Alienation, Partner Abuse: Two Sides of Same Coin](https://natsci.source.colostate.edu/parental-alienation-partner-abuse-two-sides-of-same-coin-says-csu-social-psychologist/)
- [Taylor & Francis -- Endless Litigation in Family Court as Post-Separation Coercive Control](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/09649069.2025.2530882)
- [End Coercive Control USA -- Parental Alienation, Abuse by Proxy](https://endcoercivecontrolusa.com/parental_alienation_abuse_by_proxy_and_parent_child_relationship_sabotage/)

### Power Asymmetries in Custody

Research found that about **80% of situations studied involved asymmetries of power** that could be described as "games of chicken," providing only two narrow choices to the parent with less power. These asymmetries were most prevalent when the perpetrating parent had primary or sole custody.

False claims of parental alienation in domestic violence contexts are deeply problematic when **weaponised by abusers to discredit victims**, obscure histories of violence, and shift blame onto protective parents. Research demonstrates that this tactic is far more powerful when used by men accused of abuse than by women.

Source: [GWU Scholarship -- Child Custody Outcomes in Cases Involving Parental Alienation](https://scholarship.law.gwu.edu/cgi/viewcontent.cgi?article=2712&context=faculty_publications)

## 9. Financial Abuse as a Control Mechanism

### Scope and Definition

Financial abuse (also called **economic abuse**) is a form of intimate partner violence that involves controlling a victim's ability to acquire, use, and maintain financial resources.

Source: [PMC -- Examining the Impact of Economic Abuse on Survivors](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9121607/)

### Forms of Financial Abuse

| Category | Tactics |
|----------|---------|
| **Economic Control** | Preventing access to bank accounts, credit cards, and shared assets; requiring approval for all purchases; giving an "allowance" |
| **Employment Sabotage** | Preventing the victim from working; sabotaging job interviews; calling the victim's workplace repeatedly; creating childcare emergencies on important work days |
| **Economic Exploitation** | Stealing from the partner; gambling joint money; opening credit lines without permission; refusing to pay bills to ruin the victim's credit |
| **Information Restriction** | Keeping the victim uninformed about household finances; hiding assets; refusing to discuss money |

### Research Findings

- Studies found significant associations between economic abuse and **mental health, physical health, financial outcomes, parent-child interactions, and quality of life**
- **Three-quarters of IPV victims** in a 2012 survey cited financial insecurity as the primary reason they stayed with or returned to an abuser
- Financial abuse is one of the strongest predictors of whether a victim can successfully leave an abusive relationship

Sources:
- [University of Maryland -- Recognizing Financial Abuse](https://www.ssw.umaryland.edu/fall-2024-connections/alumni-and-faculty-making-an-impact-in-ipv/recognizing-financial-abuse-a-growing-weapon-in-intimate-partner-violence-/)
- [Springer -- Addressing Economic Abuse in IPV Interventions](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10896-023-00639-y)

### Financial Abuse by Narcissistic Parents

Financial abuse from narcissistic parents takes additional forms:

- Using financial support as leverage: "I paid for your education, so you owe me obedience"
- Threatening to cut off inheritance or financial support if boundaries are set
- Demanding financial contributions from adult children while spending lavishly on themselves
- Sabotaging an adult child's financial independence (refusing to release tax documents, keeping them on shared accounts to maintain control)
- In collectivist cultures: the expectation of financial "repayment" for the cost of raising the child

## 10. Research from Dr. Lundy Bancroft

### Core Framework from *Why Does He Do That?* (2002)

Lundy Bancroft spent over 15 years working directly with abusive men in court-mandated programmes. His central finding: **abuse is not a loss of control -- it is a deliberate strategy rooted in entitlement, ownership, and the desire for power**.

Source: [Lundy Bancroft Official Site](https://lundybancroft.com/)

### The Anatomy of Abuse (Tree Model)

Bancroft pictures the anatomy of abuse as a tree:

- **Roots = Ownership**: The abuser treats the partner as property. This leads to profound disrespect.
- **Trunk = Entitlement**: The abuser believes they have the right to control. This leads to justification: "It's your fault I'm responding this way."
- **Branches = Control**: The specific tactics deployed -- lying, blame-shifting, twisting everything into its opposite, isolating the partner, gaslighting.

Sources:
- [Shortform -- Why Does He Do That? Summary](https://www.shortform.com/summary/why-does-he-do-that-vb13747-g-summary-lundy-bancroft)
- [Palomar -- Book Review: Why Does He Do That?](https://palomarokc.org/book-review-why-does-he-do-that/)

### Key Findings on Abuser Behaviour

1. **Abuse is about beliefs, not feelings**: Abusers do not abuse because they are angry, stressed, or mentally ill. They abuse because they believe they are entitled to control their partner.

2. **The "Garden of Eden" phase**: The charm at the beginning of the relationship is often genuine -- the abuser desires a seemingly perfect future where their demands are met without resistance. The early kindness is not a lie; it is the abuser's vision of how things should be when the partner is fully compliant.

3. **Abusers are not out of control**: They choose whom to abuse (partner, not boss), when to abuse (in private, not in public), and how far to go (breaking the partner's belongings, not their own).

4. **Therapy rarely works for abusers**: Traditional couples therapy is contraindicated because the abuser uses it as another arena for manipulation. Individual therapy for the abuser often provides them with more sophisticated language for their abuse.

### Applicability to Female Abusers

While Bancroft's book focuses on male abusers (reflecting his clinical population), **the underlying framework applies regardless of the abuser's gender**:

- The entitlement-ownership-control dynamic is not gender-specific
- Female abusers may employ different tactics (more emotional manipulation, less physical violence) but the core architecture is identical
- Covert narcissistic mothers use the same tree structure: ownership of children ("I gave you life"), entitlement to obedience ("After everything I've sacrificed"), and control through FOG, DARVO, and the cycle of abuse
- Narcissistic wives employ these patterns through emotional withholding, weaponized incompetence, financial control, and using children as leverage

Source: [Life-Saving Divorce -- Lundy Bancroft Interview](https://lifesavingdivorce.com/lundy/)

## Cross-References Between Tactics

These manipulation tactics rarely operate in isolation. In a typical narcissistic abuse dynamic, they reinforce each other:

| When the victim... | The abuser uses... |
|--------------------|--------------------|
| Tries to set a boundary | DARVO + FOG |
| Tries to explain their feelings | JADE trap + Moving goalposts |
| Tries to share household responsibilities equally | Weaponized incompetence |
| Threatens to leave | Children as leverage + Financial abuse |
| Stays and endures | Cycle of abuse (calm phase as intermittent reinforcement) |
| Seeks outside help | DARVO (reframing victim as the abuser) |

Understanding that these tactics form an **interlocking system of control** -- not isolated incidents -- is the first step toward recognising the pattern and breaking free.
