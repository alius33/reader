# South Asian Men and Mental Health

Research on mental health challenges specific to South Asian men — with a focus on British Bangladeshi men, the eldest son's burden, cultural masculinity, intergenerational trauma, and available resources.

---

## 1. Stigma Around Mental Health in South Asian Muslim Communities

### The Scale of the Problem

- "South Asian Bangladeshi people in the UK under-use mental health services in comparison with White Britons, at times being described as 'hard to reach'."
  - **Source:** "British-Bangladeshi Muslim men: removing barriers to mental health support and effectively supporting our community," *The Cognitive Behaviour Therapist* (Cambridge University Press, 2023). [Link](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/the-cognitive-behaviour-therapist/article/britishbangladeshi-muslim-men-removing-barriers-to-mental-health-support-and-effectively-supporting-our-community/8914770E83EC4FB85953B45043A6DECB)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal published by Cambridge University Press.

- "British Bangladeshi men face self- and community stigma surrounding mental health negatively impacting help-seeking behaviours."
  - **Source:** Same article (Cambridge, 2023).

- "In England, Bangladeshi men are amongst the lowest number of people referred to primary care NHS Talking Therapies services and amongst the most likely to have deteriorated."
  - **Source:** Same article (Cambridge, 2023).

- "People in Bangladeshi, Pakistani, Mixed and Other Ethnic Groups were less likely to experience improvement and recovery after mental health treatment than those identifying as White British, Indian, African or Caribbean."
  - **Source:** Tower Hamlets Children and Young People's Mental Health Needs Assessment (2025), referenced in search results. [Link](https://www.towerhamlets.gov.uk/Documents/Public-Health/JSNA/Tower-Hamlets-CYP-MH-Needs-Assessment-2025.pdf)
  - **Credibility:** Official local government public health assessment.

- A 2024 study found that "over 60% of South Asian men avoided professional mental health support, associating help-seeking with weakness rather than self-care."
  - **Source:** Cited in "The Quiet Cost of Emotional Silence Among British Asian Men," *DESIblitz* (2024). [Link](https://www.desiblitz.com/content/the-quiet-cost-of-emotional-silence-among-british-asian-men)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based South Asian media outlet.

### Specific Barriers Identified

Research on British Bangladeshi Muslim men specifically identifies these barriers:

1. **Shame and stigma:** Mental illness viewed as personal weakness or spiritual failing
2. **Mistrust in government and service providers:** Historical and ongoing experiences of racism and discrimination create reluctance to engage with institutions
3. **Misconceptions and superstitions regarding mental illness:** Including beliefs that mental illness is caused by jinn (spirits), evil eye, or lack of faith
4. **Language and accessibility barriers:** Many first-generation Bangladeshis have limited English; services are rarely offered in Sylheti or standard Bengali
5. **Gender-specific barriers:** "Traditional notions of masculinity within the Bangladeshi community discourage men from relying on female family members and emphasise their role as providers, putting them under a lot of mental toll"
6. **Community stigma:** Fear that seeking help will become known in the community and bring shame on the family

- **Source:** Cambridge University Press article (2023) and National Elf Service summary. [National Elf Service link](https://www.nationalelfservice.net/populations-and-settings/black-and-minority-ethnic/british-bangladeshi-men-mental-health/)
- **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed research; National Elf Service is an evidence-based mental health analysis platform.

### Mental Health Stigma in Bangladesh

- "Mental illness stigma has been found to be widespread in Bangladesh that often overwhelms mental health care services."
  - **Source:** "Mental illness stigma in Bangladesh: Findings from a cross-sectional survey," *Cambridge Prisms: Global Mental Health*. [Link](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/global-mental-health/article/mental-illness-stigma-in-bangladesh-findings-from-a-crosssectional-survey/03290B03AC48848918673DA1CB636FB9)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal (Cambridge University Press).

- Estimates of the prevalence of depression in Bangladesh range from 7% for people over 20. A 2013 study reported that 25% of adolescents in urban schools experienced depressive symptoms.
  - **Source:** "Mental health challenges in Bangladesh and the way forwards," *PMC* (2022). [Link](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9372593/)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed, PubMed Central.

## 2. Cultural Expectations of Masculinity

### The "Man Up" Culture

- "Phrases such as 'man up' or 'don't cry like a girl' frame emotion as feminine and therefore undesirable" in British Asian households.
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024), cited above.

- Personal testimony — Shafiq: "I was only taught what not to be. Don't be soft, don't cry, don't act like a girl."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

- Personal testimony — Rahim (19, Birmingham): "After a while, you stop showing anything. You learn it's embarrassing to feel."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

- "In many South Asian cultures, emotional expression is often viewed as a vulnerability, especially for men, as many are raised in environments where toughness and masculinity are prized over emotional openness."
  - **Source:** "Why is it difficult for South Asian men to express their emotions?", Jas Hundal (therapist blog). [Link](https://www.jashundal.com/why-is-it-difficult-for-south-asian-men-to-express-their-emotions/)
  - **Credibility:** Blog by a practising South Asian therapist.

### The Provider Trap

- "Men are often conditioned from a young age to see themselves as providers, which makes it harder for them to acknowledge vulnerability or seek help."
  - **Source:** "Unspoken pain: Why South Asian men struggle to seek help," *Asian Voice*. [Link](https://www.asian-voice.com/Lifestyle/Food/Health-Diet/Unspoken-pain-Why-South-Asian-men-struggle-to-seek-help)
  - **Credibility:** UK-based South Asian newspaper.

- "The pressure to meet societal expectations — whether it's excelling academically, being the primary financial provider, or maintaining the family's honor — can further strain emotional wellbeing."
  - **Source:** Same article (*Asian Voice*).

### Historical Roots of Emotional Silence

- "South Asian migrants arriving from the 1950s onward faced racism and economic insecurity, making emotional restraint a survival mechanism."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024), cited above.

- "First-generation parents postponed emotional needs to keep families stable. Silence became normalized and transmitted across generations through behavior rather than explicit instruction."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

- Boys "learn masculinity through correction: being told not to 'act like a girl.'" Parents "rarely modeled emotional expression, making vulnerability feel unfamiliar and risky."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

### Consequences of Emotional Suppression

- "Bottled emotions often manifest in unhealthy ways. Anxiety, depression, and even anger can simmer beneath the surface."
  - **Source:** Hundal, therapist blog, cited above.

- South Asian men are documented as "less likely to reach out for mental health support," with some turning to "alcohol, drugs, gambling, and behavioral addictions as coping mechanisms."
  - **Source:** Hundal, therapist blog, cited above.

- Personal testimony — Aaliyan (sixth-form student): Struggled naming emotions, defaulting to "I'm tired" when distressed.
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

## 3. Statistics on Mental Health in British Bangladeshi Communities

### Tower Hamlets: A Key Population Centre

- 58% of children in Tower Hamlets are from Bangladeshi backgrounds.
- Around 32-34% of Tower Hamlets' total population is of Bangladeshi origin — the single largest ethnic group in the borough.
  - **Source:** Tower Hamlets CYP Mental Health Needs Assessment (2025). [Link](https://www.towerhamlets.gov.uk/Documents/Public-Health/JSNA/Tower-Hamlets-CYP-MH-Needs-Assessment-2025.pdf)
  - **Credibility:** Official local government public health document.

### Service Engagement

- Bangladeshi men are among the lowest referred to NHS Talking Therapies and among the most likely to have deteriorated after treatment.
  - **Source:** Cambridge University Press article (2023), cited above.

- Research recruited British-Bangladeshi Muslim men aged 22-59 (mean age 32) who "self-reported symptoms of anxiety and/or depression, with no contact with mental health services."
  - **Source:** Cambridge University Press article (2023).

### Broader UK Statistics

- "People from ethnic minority groups in the UK are more likely to have untreated or undiagnosed" mental health difficulties.
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

- The House of Commons Library provides ongoing data on mental health prevalence, services, and funding in England, though it does not always disaggregate by specific South Asian subgroup.
  - **Source:** "Mental health statistics: prevalence, services and funding in England," House of Commons Library. [Link](https://commonslibrary.parliament.uk/research-briefings/sn06988/)
  - **Credibility:** UK Parliament research.

### Social Determinants

- A 2025 study in the *British Journal of General Practice* examined "Social determinants of distress in South Asian men with long-term conditions" in primary care.
  - **Source:** *British Journal of General Practice* 75(755), 2025. [Link](https://bjgp.org/content/75/755/e397)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal of the Royal College of General Practitioners.

## 4. The Eldest Son's Specific Mental Health Burden

### Compounded Pressures

The eldest son in a British Bangladeshi family faces a unique intersection of pressures:

- **Parentification:** Expected to be a co-parent, translator, mediator, and financial advisor from childhood. See detailed research in the Bengali Cultural Abuse Patterns file.

- **Provider expectations:** Expected to be the primary financial support for parents, often before his own career or family is established.

- **Emotional pillar role:** "Family structures expect men — especially eldest sons — to serve as emotional 'pillars' while suppressing their own needs."
  - **Source:** Hundal, therapist blog, cited above.

- **Cultural representation:** Expected to represent the family's success to the community — his achievements are the family's achievements; his failures are the family's shame.

- **Marital pressure:** Expected to marry within community norms, often to a partner his parents approve of, and to manage the mother-in-law/wife dynamic without ever being seen to "choose" his wife.

### Psychological Consequences

- "Heightened fear of failure, as their identity is shaped around being dependable, and any shortcoming can trigger guilt and self-criticism."
  - **Source:** Sahai-Siddiqui, *Behavioral Health News* (2024). [Link](https://behavioralhealthnews.org/the-burden-of-being-the-good-child-how-south-asian-kids-suppress-mental-health-struggles/)
  - **Credibility:** Expert article by a psychiatrist.

- Documented consequences include:
  - Anxiety and depression from unaddressed stress
  - Hyper-independence and difficulty requesting support
  - Impaired self-compassion and persistent guilt
  - Fear of failure linked to family reputation
  - Difficulty forming reciprocal adult relationships
  - **Source:** Same article (Sahai-Siddiqui, 2024).

- Personal testimony — Haroon: Described feeling he would "disappoint parents by expressing anxiety or complaints."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

## 5. Intergenerational Trauma in South Asian Diaspora Families

### Mechanisms of Transmission

- "Intergenerational trauma represents the ripple effect of trauma across multiple generations. In immigrant contexts, ancestral trauma from war, displacement, persecution, or famine shapes subsequent generations without requiring explicit memory or acknowledgment."
  - **Source:** "Exploring Intergenerational Trauma in Immigrant Families," *South Asian Therapists* (directory/blog). [Link](https://southasiantherapists.org/blog/understanding-intergenerational-trauma-in-immigrant-families/)
  - **Credibility:** Therapist directory with clinical blog content.

- Transmission occurs through:
  - **Biological mechanisms:** Epigenetic changes affecting stress responses.
  - **Psychosocial pathways:** Family dynamics, cultural narratives, normalized patterns of behaviour.
  - **Nervous system adaptation:** Children internalize parental hypervigilance, learning that constant alertness is normal — manifesting as "reactivity, threat-scanning, and guilt during rest."
  - **Source:** Same article (*South Asian Therapists*). Cites Sangalang & Vang (2017), Bryant et al. (2018), Yehuda & Lehrner (2018).

- "When parents are under undue chronic stress, their emotional availability narrows" (Bryant et al., 2018), causing children to minimize needs and overfunction.
  - **Source:** Same article.

### South Asian-Specific Factors

- "Historical trauma from war, colonization, and forced migration intertwines with cultural values emphasizing hierarchy, duty, obligation, and collective responsibility."
  - **Source:** Same article.

- "This creates protective family structures that simultaneously feel deeply loving and yet burdensome and rigid."
  - **Source:** Same article.

- Common intergenerational patterns:
  - Parentification (children assuming parental roles)
  - Blurred emotional boundaries
  - Excessive achievement pressure
  - Emotional restraint and stoicism
  - Dislocation, nostalgia, and persistent non-belonging
  - **Source:** Same article.

### The Partition Legacy

- "The Partition of India caused the disruption of social networks for a number of families, physical and psychological abuse, and economic destruction."
  - **Source:** "The partition of India through the lens of historical trauma: Intergenerational effects on immigrant health in the South Asian diaspora," *ScienceDirect* (2023). [Link](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2666560323000610)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal article.

- While the 1947 Partition primarily affected the India-Pakistan border, Bangladesh's own liberation war (1971) created its own legacy of trauma — mass displacement, genocide, and family separation — that has been even less studied in the diaspora.

### The "Conspiracy of Silence"

- "Families affected by historical trauma often find themselves in a 'conspiracy of silence' in which parents who directly experience trauma avoid discussing the trauma and their children avoid asking about it."
  - **Source:** "Intergenerational Communication about Historical Trauma in Asian American Families," *Adversity and Resilience Science* (PMC, 2022). [Link](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9170877/)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal (Springer Nature).

### Research Gap

- "Historical trauma related to major events, such as the India/Pakistan Partition, have not been extensively examined among Asian Americans."
  - **Source:** Same article (PMC, 2022).

- The 1971 Bangladesh Liberation War and its intergenerational effects on the British Bangladeshi diaspora remain dramatically under-researched. This is a significant gap given the scale of the community in the UK.

### Healing Approaches

- "Healing involves making the unknown known and naming the patterns that shape us, without abandoning cultural identity."
  - **Source:** South Asian Therapists blog, cited above.

- Effective interventions include:
  - Emotionally attuned home environments
  - Intentional family conversations
  - Parental vulnerability and sharing hardships
  - Flexible role expectations
  - Cultivating reflective capacity
  - **Source:** Same article. Cites Yehuda & Lehrner (2018).

## 6. Resources and Organizations That Help South Asian Men

### UK-Based Organizations

**Bangladeshi Mental Health Forum (BMHF)**
- Focus: Community mental health support in Tower Hamlets and surrounding London boroughs.
- Offers: Information and advice, mental health wellbeing workshops, support groups, activities, awareness events, signposting to local services.
- Workshops are "delivered in an interactive way which will be culturally and religiously sensitive."
- [Website](https://bangladeshimentalhealth.org/)
- **Credibility:** Registered charity operating in Tower Hamlets.

**Taraki**
- Focus: British Pakistani mental health.
- Works to destigmatize mental health in Punjabi communities.
- Referenced in *DESIblitz* (2024) as a leading community organization.
- **Credibility:** Registered community organization.

**The Black, African and Asian Therapy Network (BAATN)**
- "The UK's largest independent organisation to specialise in working psychologically, informed by an understanding of intersectionality, with people who identify as Black, African, South Asian and Caribbean."
- Provides therapist directory and training.
- [Website](https://www.baatn.org.uk/)
- **Credibility:** Major UK therapeutic network, registered organization.

**Brown Therapist Network**
- "A learning hub promoting knowledge of south Asian mental health and offers therapeutic services from a range of accredited professionals."
- "The team is all south Asian and includes professionals from a range of disciplines in the therapeutic field."
- Has a specific "Trauma X South Asians" article series by Dr Rima Lamba addressing intergenerational trauma.
- [Website](https://www.browntherapistnetwork.com/)
- **Credibility:** Professional network of accredited South Asian therapists in the UK.

**South Asian Health Action (SAHA)**
- Charity that "seeks to empower the South Asian communities to make better health choices."
- [Referenced via AMHP](https://amhp.org.uk/member/south-asian-health-action/)
- **Credibility:** Registered charity.

**South Asian Health Foundation**
- "Founded in 1999 by Dr Kiran Patel to address health inequalities in the UK, particularly with respect to the UK South Asian population."
- [Website](https://www.sahf.org.uk/)
- **Credibility:** Established charity, medical leadership.

**Sharing Voices (Bradford)**
- Charity in Bradford that aims to "reduce mental health and related inequalities for BAME communities."
- **Credibility:** Registered charity.

**Bradford District and Craven Mind / Leeds Mind**
- Run focus groups to identify barriers to mental health support in South Asian communities.
- [Link](https://www.mindinbradford.org.uk/news/report-on-mental-health-support-for-south-asian-communities/)

**BESEAN (British East and Southeast Asian wellbeing)**

### Faith-Based Approaches

- Mosques and gurdwaras increasingly host mental health awareness sessions.
- Some organizations frame emotional well-being through faith concepts like balance and collective care.
- The Bangladeshi Mental Health Forum specifically designs workshops to be "culturally and religiously sensitive."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024) and BMHF website.

### What the Research Recommends

- Stigma could be addressed through "'role models' speaking about mental health, visible interventions using social media and community promotion, and community talks on social media and Bengali TV channels."
  - **Source:** Cambridge University Press article (2023), cited above.

- Culturally tailored interventions that "specifically address the unique needs and barriers faced by British Bangladeshi men rather than treating them as part of a broader 'South Asian' category."
  - **Source:** Same article (Cambridge, 2023).

- "Some parents [are] actively creating conversation opportunities, shifting from 'how did you do?' to 'how did you feel?'"
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

- Universities including the University of Birmingham and SOAS "offer peer-support groups and culturally-aware mental health workshops."
  - **Source:** *DESIblitz* (2024).

## 7. Accessing Mental Health Services: Systemic Barriers

### The Research

- A systematic review and meta-ethnography examined "the Experiences of South Asian Service Users in the UK" when accessing mental health services.
  - **Source:** "Accessing Mental Health Services: a Systematic Review and Meta-ethnography of the Experiences of South Asian Service Users in the UK," *PMC* (2022). [Link](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8897382/)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed, PubMed Central.

- A 2025 systematic review examined "Explanatory models of common mental disorders among South Asians in high-income countries."
  - **Source:** *Transcultural Psychiatry* (Sage Journals, 2025). [Link](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/13634615241296302)
  - **Credibility:** Peer-reviewed journal.

### Key Systemic Issues

1. **Language barriers:** Services rarely available in Sylheti, the dialect most British Bangladeshis actually speak (as opposed to standard Bengali).

2. **Cultural competency gaps:** Therapists without understanding of izzat, collectivist family structures, religious frameworks, and community dynamics cannot effectively engage South Asian men.

3. **Referral patterns:** GPs may not recognize culturally-specific presentations of distress. South Asian men may present with somatic symptoms (headaches, fatigue, pain) rather than naming emotional distress.

4. **Service design:** Group therapy models that assume willingness to share personal information with strangers conflict with the izzat-driven need for privacy.

5. **Waiting times and deterioration:** Bangladeshi men are "amongst the most likely to have deteriorated" while waiting for or during treatment — suggesting current service models are actively failing this group.
