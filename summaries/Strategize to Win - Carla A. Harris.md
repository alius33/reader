# Strategize to Win — Carla A. Harris

> Carla Harris spent over thirty years climbing the ranks at Morgan Stanley, eventually becoming Vice Chairman, Managing Director, and Senior Client Advisor. Along the way she led landmark transactions across technology, media, and telecom, was named multiple times to *Fortune*'s "Most Powerful Women in Business" list, and became one of the most sought-after speakers on leadership and professional strategy. *Strategize to Win* distils that experience into a field manual for navigating large organisations. Its central argument is deceptively simple: doing excellent work is necessary but never sufficient. Organisations make their most consequential decisions about people — who gets promoted, who gets the stretch assignment, who gets protected in a downturn — based on subjective judgment, and subjective judgment is shaped far more by relationships than by performance metrics. The book provides a practical operating system for three career phases: starting out, stepping up, and starting over. It is not a theory book. It is a playbook for people who want clear moves.

## About the Author

Carla A. Harris is Vice Chairman, Managing Director, and Senior Client Advisor at Morgan Stanley, where she has spent more than three decades.
She has led landmark transactions worth billions across technology, media, and telecom sectors.
*Fortune* has named her to its "Most Powerful Women in Business" list multiple times, and she has served on numerous corporate and non-profit boards.
She is also a certified gospel recording artist — a fact that reflects her conviction that professional identity should not be one-dimensional.
Her perspective is shaped by a specific and revealing vantage point: navigating the highest levels of Wall Street as a Black woman in a predominantly white, male industry.
That experience gives her a sharp, unsentimental eye for the gap between how organisations say they work and how they actually work.

## The Big Idea

Harris's central thesis rests on a distinction that most professionals grasp too late.
There are two forms of capital in any organisation: what you deliver and who trusts you.
She calls these **performance currency** and **relationship currency**, and the entire book is built around the argument that most people overinvest in the first and catastrophically neglect the second.

At junior levels, performance is relatively easy to measure.
You can compare the analyst who builds a better model to the one who does not.
You can count the deals closed, the reports delivered, the bugs fixed.
But as you rise, the competitive field narrows.
Everyone competing for the same senior role already has strong performance.
They would not be in the running otherwise.
The differentiator becomes something harder to quantify: who has relationships with the people making the decision?
Who has been seen operating under pressure?
Who would the decision-maker trust with a difficult client, a failing project, a politically sensitive initiative?

Harris is not arguing that performance does not matter.
She is arguing that performance alone creates a ceiling.
The people who break through that ceiling are the ones who invested in relationships at the same time they were building their track record — not as an afterthought, not as a reward for good work, but as a parallel, ongoing discipline.

The book is structured around three career phases.
**Starting Out** covers how to choose and land a role, how to interview, and how to position yourself for entry.
**Stepping Up** covers how to build both currencies, how to read the unspoken signals that reveal your real standing, and how to manage your professional profile.
**Starting Over** covers how to navigate career transitions, manage disruption, and reposition after setbacks.
The thread connecting all three phases is that your career is a strategic project — not something that happens to you, but something you actively design.

## Key Concepts at a Glance

- **Performance Currency** — the capital earned by consistently delivering excellent work; calculated as intellect plus experience plus strong execution, multiplied by repetition over time; essential at every level but depreciates as a differentiator at senior ranks where everyone already has it
- **Relationship Currency** — the capital earned through deliberate, sustained investment in people; becomes the dominant factor in advancement decisions at senior levels, where judgment is subjective and familiarity breeds trust
- **The Sponsor** — someone who spends their own political and social capital on your behalf behind closed doors; distinct from a mentor (who gives advice) and a supporter (who backs you publicly but lacks decision-making power); Harris argues you need two to three sponsors at any given time
- **Career Modules** — the modern career planned as six to eight blocks of roughly five years each, potentially across multiple companies; no single employer will remain an industry leader long enough to justify a thirty-year tenure
- **Signal Reading** — the discipline of interpreting what organisations communicate through behaviour rather than words; the gap between what is said and what is done carries more information than any performance review
- **Compensation Anchoring** — the principle that your starting salary creates a gravitational pull on every future offer; starting below market rate creates a deficit that annual raises almost never close and that future employers perpetuate by anchoring to your current number
- **The Seat / The House Diagnostic** — a framework for diagnosing dissatisfaction: is it the role (the seat), the company (the house), or a specific person? If neither the seat nor the house is the problem, the answer is almost always a person
- **Frequency of Touch** — the practice of building relationships through repeated, deliberate, positive interactions over time; if you wait for relationships to happen organically, they will not
- **Can Do / Will Do / Fit** — the three dimensions every hiring decision evaluates: technical capability, motivation and tenacity, and cultural alignment (the most subjective and often most decisive)
- **Evolutionary vs Revolutionary Ideas** — a framework for categorising proposals: evolutionary ideas build on what exists and are easier to sell; revolutionary ideas require behaviour change and demand more political capital
- **The Five Professional Profiles** — five workplace archetypes (Good Soldier, Yes Man, Arguer, Safe Pair of Hands, Chief) that shape how others perceive you; only The Chief is consistently associated with senior advancement
- **Imbalance of Trade** — the principle that the most effective way to build a relationship is to invest first, creating a surplus of goodwill that the other person will instinctively want to balance

## Part I: Starting Out

### Chapter 1: The Content-Jobs-Skills Triad

Harris opens the book with a framework designed to cut through the fog of career planning.
Most people, she observes, know they want a "better job" but cannot articulate what that means.
They chase titles, prestige, and compensation without asking what activities actually energise them.
The result is that they land roles that look impressive on paper but leave them miserable in practice — and miserable people do not build the kind of performance currency that compounds over time.

Her solution is a three-worksheet exercise.
The first worksheet is the **Content Page**: list everything you genuinely enjoy doing.
Not what job title you want, not what industry sounds prestigious, but what actual activities make you lose track of time.
Do you like analysing data?
Persuading people?
Building things?
Solving complex problems under time pressure?
The content page forces honesty about what you actually want to spend your days doing.

The second worksheet is the **Jobs Page**: what roles contain that content?
This is where most people start — and that, Harris argues, is the mistake.
Starting with jobs instead of content means you are fitting yourself into someone else's definition of a role rather than building from your own preferences outward.

The third worksheet is the **Skills/Experience/Education Page**: what credentials do people in those roles actually have?
Not what the job description says (job descriptions are aspirational wish lists), but what the people who actually hold those positions brought to the table.
This worksheet reveals gaps — and gaps can be closed through deliberate acquisition of experience, not just formal education.

Harris tells the story of a young professional she mentored who wanted to move into venture capital.
He had been applying to VC firms for months with no success, increasingly frustrated by the lack of response.
When Harris walked him through the three worksheets, the content page revealed that what he actually enjoyed was evaluating business models and meeting entrepreneurs — activities that existed in several roles beyond VC.
The skills page revealed that successful VC associates typically had either operating experience in startups or deep expertise in a specific industry.
He had neither.
The exercise did not kill his ambition, but it redirected it: he took a role at a startup to build operating experience, with the explicit intention of using that as a launching pad into VC two years later.

The framework forces a separation between desire and reality that most career planning skips entirely.
It is simple, but its power lies in the sequence.
Content first.
Then jobs.
Then skills.
Most people reverse the order and wonder why they feel stuck.

### Chapter 2: Know What the Buyer Is Buying

Harris's second chapter addresses a problem she has seen derail thousands of interviews: people pitch what they want to say rather than what the buyer wants to hear.

Every hiring decision, she argues, evaluates three things: **Can Do** (intelligence, credentials, technical capability), **Will Do** (motivation, tenacity, self-starting capacity), and **Fit** (cultural alignment, personality compatibility).
Of these three, Fit is the most subjective and often the most decisive.
Two candidates may be equally qualified on Can Do and equally motivated on Will Do, but the one who feels like a better cultural fit will get the offer.

The implication is that job descriptions are incomplete maps of what the employer actually values.
The stated requirements list hard skills and qualifications.
The unstated requirements — the ones that actually determine the outcome — are about whether the candidate will fit into the team, whether they can be trusted with ambiguity, whether they will make the hiring manager's life easier or harder.

Harris illustrates this with her own experience entering Wall Street.
She arrived at Morgan Stanley with only one line of professional experience on her resume: managing a McDonald's restaurant.
On paper, this was disqualifying for an investment banking role.
But Harris understood that what the firm was really buying was not finance expertise — they could teach that.
They were buying commercial orientation: had the candidate ever been responsible for a P&L?
They were buying the ability to earn peer respect: could the candidate lead a team of people who did not report to them?
They were buying self-motivation: could the candidate produce under pressure without constant supervision?

She reframed her McDonald's experience around these underlying criteria.
Managing a restaurant meant running a business with real revenue, real costs, and real consequences for failure.
It meant managing a staff of teenagers and adults who had no obligation to respect her authority.
It meant solving problems at two in the morning when no one was available to help.

> "Figure out what the buyer is buying, then sell that."

The story is a masterclass in reframing.
Harris did not pretend to have finance experience she lacked.
She translated her actual experience into the language of what the firm was genuinely evaluating.
The buyer was buying capability, not credentials — and once she understood that, the McDonald's experience became an asset rather than a liability.

She extends this principle beyond interviews to every professional interaction.
When pitching an idea to a sceptical executive, the question is not "what do I want to say?" but "what does this person actually care about?"
When negotiating compensation, the question is not "what do I think I deserve?" but "what does the employer value enough to pay for?"
The discipline of understanding the buyer's real criteria — as opposed to their stated criteria — is one of the most transferable skills in the book.

### Chapter 3: The Entry Point Problem

One of the book's most sobering arguments appears early: **where you enter an organisation largely determines where you end up**.

Organisations operate on precedent.
If no one has ever moved from your department to the role you want, the company cannot "see" you in that role — regardless of your capability.
The path from accounting to strategic finance may look obvious on paper, but if no one has ever made that transition at your firm, you are not walking a path.
You are trying to create one.
And creating a new path requires a senior leader willing to spend significant political capital on your behalf.
It is not impossible, but it should never be assumed.

Harris tells the story of Ted, an MBA graduate who entered a financial services firm in the accounting department.
His plan was to transfer into strategic finance after proving himself.
He was excellent in accounting — fast, accurate, well-liked.
But when he applied for the strategic finance role two years later, he was not even considered.
The reason was not performance.
The reason was that no one in the firm's history had ever moved from accounting to strategic finance.
The hiring managers could not picture an accounting person in that role.
Ted's capability was irrelevant because the precedent did not exist.
He eventually left the firm and entered strategic finance at a competitor — but he lost two years and had to start his relationship currency from zero.

The same gravitational pull applies to compensation.
Harris introduces the concept of **compensation anchoring**: your starting salary creates a gravitational field that is extraordinarily difficult to escape.
Companies budget at market rate but offer below it when they can.
Annual raise percentages are capped by policy — typically three to five per cent.
So even stellar performance cannot close a gap that opened on day one.
And the problem compounds: prospective employers use your current salary as an anchor for their offer, propagating the deficit through every subsequent role.

Harris tells the story of Linda, who spent twelve years at her company delivering excellent work.
When she finally explored external opportunities, she discovered that her salary was roughly fifty per cent below market rate.
But the damage went deeper than lost income.
When a prospective employer saw her current compensation, they questioned her competence.
The reasoning was circular but powerful: if she were truly exceptional, surely she would be paid more?
Her low salary had become a signal — and not the signal she intended.

> "Every job you take is a five-year bet."

The practical advice is direct.
Research market rate before accepting any offer.
Negotiate in terms of the role's value rather than your current salary.
If you accept below market for strategic reasons — to enter a new industry, to gain specific experience — insist on a written review date and specific milestones that will trigger a compensation adjustment.
Never allow a starting deficit to compound unchallenged.

## Part II: Stepping Up

### Chapter 4: Performance Currency — Putting Points on the Board

Harris defines **performance currency** with a formula: intellect plus experience plus strong execution, multiplied by consistent repetition over time.
It is the capital you earn by delivering excellent work product — the deal closed, the project shipped, the analysis that changed the decision.

She is emphatic: you cannot advance without it.
Performance currency is the price of admission.
Without a track record of strong delivery, no amount of relationship-building will save you.
But she is equally emphatic about its limitations.
Performance currency has diminishing returns.
At senior levels, everyone competing for the same role already delivers strong results.
Performance currency gets you into the conversation; it does not win the conversation.

The chapter's most practical contribution is the principle of **defining success before you execute**.
Harris learned this lesson the hard way.
Early in her career, she was assigned to arrange a meeting with a major potential client — a meeting that had never happened before in the firm's history.
She pulled it off.
The client agreed to meet.
Harris walked into her boss's office expecting congratulations.
Instead, her boss shrugged.
The client had not bought anything.
In her boss's mind, the meeting was not the success — the sale was.

The problem was that Harris and her boss had never agreed on what "success" looked like.
She had assumed that arranging an unprecedented meeting was a significant win.
He had assumed that the only metric that mattered was revenue.
Both interpretations were reasonable.
But because they had never been aligned, Harris earned zero performance currency for what was, by any objective standard, an impressive accomplishment.

From that point on, Harris made it a discipline to negotiate success criteria before starting any assignment.
What does success look like?
What deliverables are within my control?
What outcomes depend on factors I cannot influence?
She separates controllable milestones — "I arranged the meeting, I prepared the pitch, I delivered the presentation" — from uncontrollable outcomes — "the client bought the product."
By defining success in terms of what she could control, she created discrete, defensible units of performance currency that could not be dismissed because the uncontrollable variables went the wrong way.

The chapter also introduces the principle of **early wins**.
When entering any new environment — a new role, a new team, a new company — perceptions form fast.
If you are seen struggling early on, the organisation decides you are a poor fit before you have had a chance to prove otherwise.
The halo effect works in reverse: early negative impressions disproportionately weight all subsequent evaluations.

Harris's tactical advice is concrete.
If asked for a deliverable by 2:00 PM, deliver by 11:00 AM.
If asked for a summary of one company, deliver a comparative analysis of two.
Identify the easiest wins in the first twenty-four to forty-eight hours and execute them immediately.
The goal is not to show off.
The goal is to create an early positive anchor that buys you tolerance for the inevitable mistakes that come with learning any new environment.

She tells the story of a new hire at an investment bank who was given a routine research assignment on his first day.
Most new hires would have produced a competent summary by the deadline.
This hire produced the summary by lunch, then added a comparative analysis of two competitors and a one-page strategic implications memo.
His manager mentioned it at the next team meeting.
Within a week, senior people were requesting him for projects.
The extra three hours of work on day one created a reputation that took months for his peers to match.

> "Under-promise and over-deliver. Score your points early."

The principle is cognitive anchoring applied to professional reputation.
Early positive impressions become the lens through which all subsequent performance is evaluated.
A strong first week buys forgiveness for a weak third month.
A weak first week makes even strong subsequent performance look like an exception to the rule.

### Chapter 5: Relationship Currency — The Other Half of the Equation

If Chapter 4 establishes the floor, Chapter 5 establishes the ceiling.

**Relationship currency** is the strength and leverage of your professional relationships.
It is what determines who gets promoted when multiple candidates have comparable performance.
It is what gets your name mentioned in rooms you are not in.
It is what makes the difference between being a strong contributor and being someone the organisation invests in, protects, and advances.

Harris observes a pattern she has seen repeated across thirty years: many professionals — and women in particular — want their performance to be beyond reproach before they invest in relationships.
They treat relationship-building as something you earn the right to do after proving yourself.
Meanwhile, others begin building relationships from day one, in parallel with performance.
This creates a compounding gap.
By the time both groups have equivalent performance currency, the relationship-builders have years of accumulated trust, familiarity, and social capital.
At promotion time, when decisions are subjective and multiple candidates are qualified, the relationship gap is decisive.

The mechanism Harris identifies is **frequency of touch** — the practice of building relationships through repeated, deliberate, positive interactions over time.
Relationships do not happen organically.
They are built through scheduled, intentional contact.
Coffee meetings.
Shared commutes.
Brief check-ins that serve no immediate purpose beyond maintaining the connection.
Each positive interaction deposits a small amount into the relationship account.
Over months and years, these deposits compound into trust.

Harris tells the story of a trader on Morgan Stanley's trading floor who initially refused to work with her.
He was dismissive, unresponsive, and actively avoided her.
Rather than accepting the situation or escalating it, Harris began a campaign of frequency of touch.
Every morning, she brought him coffee.
Not every week — every day.
She did not ask for anything.
She did not pitch ideas.
She brought coffee, exchanged a few words, and left.
After several weeks, the trader began acknowledging her.
After a few months, he was actively helping her with trades.
When she needed emergency assistance on a critical deal — the kind of help that required someone to stay late and absorb personal inconvenience — he was the first to volunteer.

The daily coffee was not manipulation.
It was investment.
Each interaction was small, but the cumulative effect was a relationship with genuine trust on both sides.

Harris introduces a related concept she calls the **imbalance of trade**.
When building a new relationship, invest first.
Create value for the other person before you ask for anything.
Over time, the relationship accumulates a surplus — an imbalance of trade — that the other person will instinctively want to balance.

The mechanism is reciprocity.
People track social debts intuitively, even unconsciously.
If you consistently help someone — sharing insights, making introductions, solving their problems — they feel an obligation to reciprocate.
The imbalance is not a trick.
It is a genuine investment that creates genuine goodwill.
But it only works if the investment is authentic.
Transparently transactional behaviour destroys trust faster than no investment at all.

> "Performance currency gets you in the door; relationship currency gets you to the top."

Harris also addresses the **trust factor** in this chapter.
Trust, she argues, is the ultimate form of relationship currency.
It is earned slowly and lost quickly.
The professionals who advance fastest are not necessarily the most talented — they are the ones who have built the deepest reservoirs of trust with the people who make decisions.
Trust is built through reliability (doing what you say you will do), consistency (behaving the same way regardless of audience), and vulnerability (being willing to admit mistakes and ask for help).

The chapter closes with a warning about the asymmetry of the two currencies.
Performance currency is visible.
It shows up in deliverables, metrics, and reviews.
Relationship currency is invisible.
It operates behind closed doors, in hallway conversations, in the split-second judgments that determine who gets the call when a critical assignment needs to be staffed.
Because it is invisible, it is easy to neglect.
And because it compounds slowly, the cost of neglect is not apparent until it is too late — typically at the moment you are passed over for a promotion you believed you had earned on performance alone.

### Chapter 6: Sponsors, Supporters, and the Four Players

This chapter contains one of the book's sharpest contributions: a taxonomy of the people who affect your trajectory, and a clear-eyed analysis of who actually matters.

Harris identifies **four types of players** in every organisation.

**The Sponsor** is the most important person in your professional life.
A sponsor carries your name into rooms you are not in.
They spend their own credibility advocating for you.
They are not merely supportive — they are invested.
When a promotion decision is being made behind closed doors, the sponsor is the person who says "I have worked with this person, I trust their judgment, and I am willing to stake my reputation on their readiness."
The cost to the sponsor is real: if the person they championed fails, the sponsor's judgment is questioned.
That is what makes sponsorship valuable — it is expensive social capital, spent deliberately.

> "Your sponsor carries your paper into the room."

Harris is adamant that you need two to three sponsors at any given time.
Relying on a single sponsor is dangerous because sponsors change roles, leave companies, fall out of favour, or simply move on.
If your sole sponsor departs and you have no replacement, you are suddenly invisible in the rooms where decisions are made.

She tells the story of Kelly, a high-performing professional whose boss was enthusiastic about her work but lacked political capital within the organisation.
Kelly's boss praised her constantly, advocated for her loudly, and submitted her name for every promotion cycle.
But the boss had no real influence with the senior committee that made promotion decisions.
Kelly watched peers with weaker performance records get promoted — because those peers had sponsors who sat on the committee, or who had the ear of people who did.
Kelly's boss was a supporter, not a sponsor.
The distinction cost Kelly years.

**The Supporter** is someone who backs you informally and publicly.
They will say nice things about you, recommend you for projects, and generally be in your corner.
But they lack decision-making authority.
A supporter without power is like a reference letter without a signature — well-intentioned but insufficient.

**The "Say No to Everything" Person** is a reflexive sceptic.
They are not malicious.
They simply default to no.
Every proposal, every new initiative, every change is met with reasons it will not work.
Harris advises against fighting them.
They are not worth the energy.
The correct strategy is to route around them — build consensus with others first, then present the SNEP with a fait accompli.

**The Saboteur** is someone who actively works against you.
They are rare but real.
They may feel threatened by your success, bear a grudge from a past interaction, or simply be the kind of person who tears others down to elevate themselves.
Harris advises identifying saboteurs early.
The counter-strategy is not confrontation but relationships: build strong connections with people above and around the saboteur so that their opposition is neutralised by the weight of support from other quarters.

She tells the story of a mid-level executive who discovered that a peer was systematically undermining him in meetings with senior leadership — not through overt attacks but through subtle questioning of his judgment and quiet suggestions that his projects were behind schedule.
The executive's first instinct was to confront the saboteur directly.
Harris advised against it.
Instead, the executive invested heavily in relationships with the senior leaders the saboteur was trying to influence.
He made sure they heard directly from him, not through the saboteur's filter.
Within six months, the saboteur's influence had evaporated — not because he had been defeated, but because his audience had been co-opted.

The distinction between sponsor and mentor is worth lingering on because most professionals confuse them.
Mentors are plentiful and cheap.
Anyone can give advice.
Sponsors are rare and expensive.
They put their own reputation on the line.
The question is not "who is advising me?" but "who is spending capital on me when I am not in the room?"
If you cannot answer the second question, you have a mentorship problem disguised as a sponsorship problem.

### Chapter 7: Reading the Signals

Harris argues that organisations communicate their most important career information **non-verbally**.
Corporate cultures are allergic to direct negative feedback.
Legal liability, HR protocols, and cultural norms all conspire to ensure that the most consequential information about your standing is never stated explicitly.
Instead, it is signalled through patterns: who gets the stretch assignments, who is invited to present, who is mentioned for promotion — and, crucially, who is not.

> "What is not said tells you more than what is said."

The gap between what is said and what is done is the single most important career diagnostic.
If your manager says "you are doing a great job" but you are not being promoted, given new responsibilities, or included in strategic conversations, the words are noise.
The actions are the signal.

Harris offers several stories that illustrate the principle.

Michael was a mid-level professional who received consistently positive feedback from his manager.
"Great work."
"Keep it up."
"You are a valuable member of this team."
But Michael noticed something troubling: his peers were being given stretch assignments — high-visibility projects that put them in front of senior leadership.
Michael was not.
He asked his manager about it and received reassurance: "Your time will come."
It did not.
When the next promotion cycle arrived, Michael was passed over.
His peers — the ones who had received the stretch assignments — were promoted.
The positive verbal feedback had been a cushion, not a signal.
The real signal was the absence of stretch assignments.

Cole's story is different but equally instructive.
Cole's boss kept suggesting that he consider a lateral move to a different department.
The suggestions were casual, almost offhand.
"Have you thought about the marketing team? They could use someone with your skills."
Cole dismissed the suggestions — he liked his current role.
What Cole did not know, and what his boss was not allowed to say directly, was that his department was being eliminated.
The lateral move suggestion was not career advice.
It was a lifeline thrown in the only language the corporate culture permitted.
By the time Cole understood the signal, the department had been dissolved and the marketing role had been filled.

Harris herself provides a third example.
Early in her career, she was never invited to present to senior clients, even though her work was consistently strong.
She assumed the invitations would come with seniority.
They did not.
When she finally asked her boss directly why she was never included, the answer was uncomfortable: her boss thought she was "verbose" — that she talked too much in presentations.
This was actionable feedback that had been available for years but was never communicated directly.
The signal — the absence of presentation invitations — had been there all along.
She had simply failed to read it.

The practical discipline Harris teaches is to track actions, not words.
Who is being given what?
Who is being included in what?
Who is being asked to present, to travel, to meet clients?
And when you detect a gap between verbal feedback and actual behaviour, ask direct, clarifying questions.
Do not accept vague reassurances.
Press for specifics: "What would need to change for me to be considered for X?"
The answer — or the evasion of the answer — is itself a signal.

### Chapter 8: Communication and Professional Profile

Harris devotes a full chapter to how you are perceived — not what you deliver, but what people think of you when you are not in the room.
She introduces the **Five Professional Profiles**, a taxonomy of workplace archetypes that shape how others evaluate your readiness for advancement.

**The Good Soldier** follows orders, executes reliably, and rarely challenges authority.
Good Soldiers are valued.
Every organisation needs people who can be counted on to do what is asked, when it is asked, without drama.
But Good Soldiers are rarely promoted to leadership.
They are seen as implementers, not decision-makers.
The organisation trusts them with tasks, not with strategy.

**The Yes Man** agrees with everything and never dissents.
They are comfortable to have around — they do not create friction, do not challenge assumptions, do not rock the boat.
But they are not trusted for independent judgment.
When a difficult decision needs to be made, no one turns to the Yes Man, because the Yes Man's opinion is indistinguishable from whoever spoke last.

**The Arguer** always plays devil's advocate.
Arguers are intellectually stimulating — they see angles others miss and they force rigour into sloppy thinking.
But they are exhausting to work with.
Every meeting becomes a debate.
Every proposal receives a ten-minute critique before it receives consideration.
The Arguer's contribution is real, but the cost of that contribution — in time, in energy, in morale — is often judged too high.

**The Safe Pair of Hands** executes meticulously but never innovates.
Safe Pairs of Hands are trusted with routine — give them a process and they will run it flawlessly.
But they are not trusted with ambiguity.
When the situation requires creative problem-solving, risk-taking, or a decision made with incomplete information, the Safe Pair of Hands is not the person the organisation calls.

**The Chief** has strong vision, is comfortable with risk, and challenges authority when necessary — but with enough judgment to know when challenging is productive and when it is merely disruptive.
The Chief is the profile most consistently associated with senior advancement.
Chiefs are the people organisations put in charge of things that are broken, new, or important.

Harris is direct about the hierarchy: the profiles are not equal.
Only The Chief is consistently associated with leadership advancement.
But the profile must be managed.
A Chief who challenges authority without tact becomes an Arguer.
A Chief who takes risk without delivering results becomes reckless.
The skill is in calibrating the profile — showing enough independent judgment to be seen as a leader, but not so much that you are seen as unmanageable.

She also addresses **communication across generations** in this chapter, noting that different generational cohorts have different expectations about formality, directness, and the role of hierarchy.
What reads as respectful deference to a Boomer executive may read as passivity to a Gen X manager.
What reads as refreshing directness to a Millennial peer may read as insubordination to a senior partner.
The discipline is reading your audience and calibrating accordingly — the same message, delivered differently, can produce opposite reactions.

### Chapter 9: Evolutionary vs Revolutionary Ideas

When attempting to influence your environment — to change a process, propose a new initiative, or redirect a team's strategy — Harris advises categorising your idea before pitching it.

**Evolutionary ideas** build on something that already exists.
They are extensions, improvements, enhancements.
They are easier to sell because they do not require anyone to change their fundamental behaviour.
The strategy for evolutionary ideas is to co-opt the original author — position your contribution as a natural extension of their work.
This gives the original author a stake in your idea's success and removes the threat that you are trying to replace what they built.

Harris tells the story of a junior analyst who wanted to improve the firm's client reporting process.
The existing process was inefficient but had been designed by a senior partner who was protective of his systems.
Rather than proposing a replacement — which would have been received as an attack — the analyst framed his improvement as a "Phase 2 enhancement" of the senior partner's original design.
He credited the partner's framework as the foundation.
The partner, feeling validated rather than threatened, championed the improvement himself.

**Revolutionary ideas** require a change in behaviour.
They do not build on what exists — they replace it.
Revolutionary ideas are harder to sell because they implicitly criticise the status quo, and the status quo has defenders.
They require either a **positive incentive** (a clear reward for adoption that outweighs the discomfort of change) or a **negative incentive** (a clear cost for non-adoption that makes the status quo untenable).

The distinction is practical, not philosophical.
The same idea can be framed as either evolutionary or revolutionary depending on how it is presented.
A new data analytics platform can be pitched as "a replacement for our current system" (revolutionary — threatening) or as "an upgrade that builds on what we have learned from the current system" (evolutionary — inviting).
The content may be identical.
The framing determines the reception.

Harris advises defaulting to evolutionary framing whenever possible.
Revolutionary framing should be reserved for situations where the status quo is so clearly broken that everyone already agrees it needs to change — and even then, giving the defenders of the old system a role in building the new one reduces resistance dramatically.

## Part III: Starting Over

### Chapter 10: The Seat, the House, or the Person

When professionals feel stuck, unhappy, or ready to leave, Harris argues that the first step is diagnosis, not action.
Most people conflate three distinct problems, and the solution for each is different.

**The Seat** is your specific role.
If the problem is the seat, you dislike the actual work you are doing.
The content does not match your interests, the scope is too narrow or too broad, the responsibilities do not play to your strengths.
The solution is to change roles — but not necessarily companies.
Internal mobility may solve a seat problem without the cost of losing your accumulated currency.

**The House** is the company or organisation.
If the problem is the house, the issue is cultural, structural, or strategic.
The company's values do not align with yours, the industry is declining, the leadership is dysfunctional.
The solution is to leave — but to leave for the right reason, not because of a problem that will follow you.

**The Person** is a specific individual — a manager, a peer, a senior leader — who is making your professional life difficult.
Harris is blunt about this.

> "If neither the seat nor the house is the problem, it's the person."

And her advice is equally blunt: you should almost never leave a company because of one person.
People change roles, get promoted, transfer, or leave.
The person who is blocking you today may be gone in six months.
If you like the seat and the house, leaving surrenders all your accumulated performance and relationship currency — years of investment — because of one individual.

The correct response to a person-problem is to build alternative relationships.
Invest in your boss's boss.
Build connections in adjacent departments.
Create multiple pathways to visibility and advancement so that no single person has veto power over your trajectory.
Every person can be navigated, routed around, or outlasted — as long as you have not abandoned the organisation out of frustration.

Harris tells the story of a woman who was ready to resign because her manager consistently took credit for her work.
The manager would present her analyses to senior leadership as his own, would volunteer her for projects and then present the results himself, and would systematically keep her away from direct interaction with the people who made decisions.
Harris's advice was not to confront the manager — that would have created a war she could not win — but to build direct relationships with the senior leaders herself.
She began attending industry events where the senior leaders were present.
She volunteered for cross-functional task forces that gave her visibility outside her manager's control.
Within a year, the senior leaders knew her name, knew her work, and knew her capabilities — independently of her manager's representations.
When the manager eventually left the firm, she was the natural choice for his replacement.

The only exception Harris acknowledges is when the blocking person has absolute control over your career and no counter-balance exists.
In a very small organisation, or under a manager who reports directly to the CEO with no peers and no oversight, the "work around" strategy may genuinely be impossible.
In those cases, leaving is rational — but it should be a last resort, not a first instinct.

### Chapter 11: Compensation and the Risk Premium

Harris returns to compensation in the context of career transitions, expanding on the anchoring principle introduced earlier.

When changing organisations, she argues, you are always taking on risk.
You are entering a new culture where you have no track record.
You have zero performance currency — everything you built at your previous company stays there.
You have zero relationship currency — every connection, every champion, every person who knows your work must be rebuilt from scratch.
You are, in economic terms, starting over.

This risk has a cost, and Harris argues that it should be priced into any offer.
Moving laterally in compensation while changing companies is not a lateral move — it is a step backward, because the intangible capital you are surrendering has real value.

> "Never move for the same salary."

She advises quantifying the risk explicitly.
What are you giving up?
Known culture, established relationships, a track record that has been observed and validated, a reputation that precedes you into every meeting.
What are you gaining?
An unknown culture, an unproven team, a manager you have never worked with, and a set of expectations you may or may not be able to meet.
The gap between what you are giving up and what you are gaining is the risk premium, and it should be reflected in compensation.

The one exception she acknowledges is the industry switch.
When you are entering a new field where you have no relevant experience, both you and the employer are sharing the risk.
The employer is gambling that your transferable skills will translate.
You are gambling that the new industry will suit you.
In this scenario, lateral or even slightly reduced compensation can be rational — but it should be explicitly time-bounded.
Harris advises negotiating a written review date, typically six months, with specific milestones that will trigger a compensation adjustment if met.

### Chapter 12: Managing Through Change and Disruption

The final section of the book addresses the reality that careers do not unfold in straight lines.
Industries contract.
Companies merge.
Managers change.
Projects get cancelled.
The professionals who sustain long careers are not the ones who avoid disruption — disruption is unavoidable — but the ones who manage through it effectively.

Harris offers several principles for navigating career disruption.

First, maintain your performance currency during transitions.
When an organisation is in flux — during a merger, a restructuring, a leadership change — many professionals freeze.
They stop producing.
They wait for clarity.
Harris argues this is exactly wrong.
Disruption is when visibility is highest and competition is lowest.
While everyone else is waiting, the professional who continues to deliver creates a stark contrast that decision-makers notice.

Second, protect your sponsors during disruption.
In a merger, your sponsor may lose power.
In a restructuring, your sponsor may be eliminated.
When your sponsor's position changes, your position changes — whether you realise it or not.
Harris advises maintaining awareness of your sponsor's standing and beginning to cultivate replacement sponsors before you need them, not after.

Third, use disruption as an opportunity to reset.
A new leader, a new structure, a new strategy — these are all openings.
The precedents that constrained you may no longer apply.
The people who blocked you may no longer be in position.
Disruption dissolves the gravitational pull of the status quo, and for a brief window, mobility increases.
The professionals who recognise this window and move through it gain ground.
The ones who wait for the dust to settle find that by the time it does, new precedents have hardened and new constraints have formed.

Harris tells the story of a division that was merged into a larger business unit during a corporate restructuring.
Most of the division's employees spent months worrying about whether their jobs were safe.
One mid-level manager took a different approach.
She identified the incoming leadership team, researched their priorities, and began producing work that directly addressed those priorities — before anyone asked her to.
When the merger was complete and the new leaders were deciding who to keep, who to promote, and who to let go, she was the person who had already demonstrated alignment with their vision.
She was promoted.
Several of her peers, who had been waiting anxiously for instructions, were let go.

## The Verdict

*Strategize to Win* is a clear, practical operating system for navigating large corporate organisations. Its greatest contribution is the **two-currency model**, which reframes the relationship between performance and advancement in a way that is immediately actionable. The insight that performance currency is necessary but has diminishing returns — and that relationship currency becomes the dominant factor precisely when the stakes are highest — is one of those ideas that seems obvious in retrospect but that most professionals internalise too late. The **signal-reading framework** is equally valuable: the discipline of tracking what organisations do rather than what they say is a skill that, once developed, changes how you interpret every meeting, every review, and every casual conversation with your manager. The **compensation anchoring** principle alone could save a professional tens of thousands of pounds over a career.

The book's weakness is intellectual depth. Harris provides no citations to research, studies, or data beyond her own extensive but anecdotal experience. The illustrative stories may be composites; they are certainly not documented case studies. The five-year module model is asserted as fact without empirical backing — why five years and not three or seven? The advice assumes a fundamentally functional corporate environment where strategic manoeuvring will be rewarded. It does not meaningfully address what to do when the system itself is broken — when sponsors do not exist, when the culture is actively hostile, when the entire concept of "relationship currency" is undermined by structural dysfunction. Harris acknowledges systemic barriers — particularly around gender and race — but her solutions remain stubbornly individual. The gap between "here is a systemic problem" and "here is what you, personally, can do about it" is never fully bridged.

The reader who will benefit most from this book is the mid-career professional in a large organisation who has been relying on performance alone and wondering why advancement has stalled. If you have ever thought "my work should speak for itself" and been frustrated that it did not, this book will explain why — and give you a concrete alternative. It is less useful for people in small companies, startups, or non-corporate environments where the dynamics Harris describes may not apply. It is also less useful for people who are already sophisticated about organisational politics; for them, the two-currency model will feel familiar even if the terminology is new.

Compared to other books in this space, *Strategize to Win* occupies a practical middle ground. It is less theatrical and less historically grounded than Robert Greene's work on power dynamics, but more actionable for day-to-day corporate life. It is less research-heavy than Herminia Ibarra's *Act Like a Leader*, but more prescriptive — Harris tells you what to do, not just what to think about. It pairs well with Chris Voss's *Never Split the Difference* for the negotiation chapters and with Cialdini's *Influence* for the psychological mechanisms (reciprocity, anchoring, social proof) that underpin Harris's relationship model. Read it as a field manual, not a theory book. The operating system is sound even if the theoretical foundations are thin.
